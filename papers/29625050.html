TL;DR

Sanchez-Vega et al. integrated somatic mutations, copy-number alterations, mRNA expression, gene fusions and DNA methylation across 9,125 TCGA PanCanAtlas tumors from 33 cancer types (64 molecular subtypes) to map alterations in 10 canonical oncogenic signaling pathways: cell cycle, Hippo, Myc, Notch, NRF2/oxidative stress, PI3K/Akt, RTK-RAS, TGFβ, p53, and WNT/β-catenin. They report that 89% of tumors carry at least one driver alteration in these pathways, 57% have at least one alteration potentially targetable by an existing drug, and 30% have multiple targetable alterations — establishing a quantitative pan-cancer rationale for both pathway-level precision oncology and combination therapy. All alteration calls and curated pathway templates are accessible via cBioPortal (http://cbioportal.org/pancanatlas) and PathwayMapper.

Cohort & data

  • 9,125 tumors drawn from the TCGA PanCanAtlas whitelist (started from 11,276 patients; required all four platforms — mutations, copy number, methylation, mRNA — with QC/pathology filtering).
  • 33 cancer types, stratified into 64 genomically distinct subtypes per individual TCGA analysis working groups.
  • Sample composition: 8,602/9,125 (94%) primary solid tumors; 162/9,125 (2%) blood tumors from the LAML subset; 361/9,125 (4%) metastatic samples from melanoma (SKCM) patients.
  • Cancer types covered include (non-exhaustive): GBM, LGG, BRCA, SKCM, COAD, READ, KIRC, KIRP, KICH, LIHC, LUAD, LUSC, PAAD, PRAD, STAD, ESCA, CESC, UCEC, UCS, OV, THCA, HNSC, BLCA, ACC, CHOL, MESO, LAML, PCPG, SARC, TGCT, THYM, UVM.
  • Corresponding cBioPortal studies (subset present in the wiki): gbm_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, brca_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, coadread_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, luad_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, blca_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, cesc_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, chol_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, laml_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, prad_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, thca_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018.
  • Assays: somatic mutations from whole-exome sequencing using the MC3 MAF v2.8 call set; copy-number from Affymetrix SNP6 arrays; gene expression from RNA-Seq; DNA methylation from Illumina Infinium HM450 (hm450-methylation-array) arrays.
  • Driver-discovery and annotation methods: MutSigCV for mutation recurrence, GISTIC 2.0 for copy-number recurrence (confidence 0.95; thresholds +2 and −2 for amplification/deep deletion), cancer-hotspots (2D linear and 3D protein-structure mutational hotspots), OncoKB for oncogenicity/actionability annotation, and RESET for CDKN2A promoter methylation calling (probe cg13601799). Fusions from RNA-Seq via STAR-Fusion, EricScript and BreakFast. Mutual-exclusivity / co-occurrence testing via SELECT. Pathway templates visualized via PathwayMapper.

Key findings

  • Pan-cancer driver burden: 89% of 9,125 tumors carry at least one driver alteration in one of the 10 canonical pathways; 57% have at least one clinically actionable alteration (including BRCA1/2 and IDH1/2 outside the 10 pathways); 30% carry two or more potentially targetable alterations PMID:29625050.
  • RTK-RAS is the most-altered pathway pan-cancer, with a median frequency of 46%. Tumor subtypes with the highest RTK-RAS alteration rates: SKCM 95%, colorectal GS 88%, BRCA Her2-enriched 82%, PAAD 78%, LGG IDHwt 82%, LUAD 74%, THCA 84%.
  • PI3K pathway is dominant in squamous and uterine cancers: LUSC 68%, EBV+ esophagogastric 80%, non-hypermutated UCEC CN-high 86% / CN-low 95%.
  • NRF2/oxidative-stress pathway had the lowest overall alteration rate (4%), peaking in LUSC (25%) and esophagogastric squamous (ESCC) (23%).
  • Cell-cycle pathway is broadly altered but rarely in UVM, THYM, TGCT, or LAML.
  • MSI-H and POLE-mutant subtypes (GI and uterine) carry both the highest mutation burden and the highest overall pathway alteration frequency, dominated by inactivating mutations.
  • 416 oncogenic alterations were tested for co-occurrence/exclusivity via SELECT, yielding 152 mutually exclusive pairs and 116 co-occurring pairs (Table S5).
  • Within-pathway mutual exclusivity is strongest in p53, cell cycle, RAS, and PI3K — consistent with one alteration being functionally sufficient.
  • Cross-pathway interactions: RTK activation is mutually exclusive with downstream RAS or PI3K alterations (because RTKs alone activate both); notable exception is significant co-occurrence between FGFR2/FGFR3 alterations and PI3K-pathway alterations.
  • p53 and cell-cycle pathways are frequently co-altered — including co-occurrence of TP53 mutation with CCNE1 amplification, CDKN2A mutation, RB1 loss, and CDK6 or E2F3 amplification. TP53 mutation is mutually exclusive with CDKN2A deletion (because the CDKN2A locus encodes both p16 and ARF/p53-regulating). MDM2 amplification is mutually exclusive with RB1 and CDKN2A loss, but MDM2 and CDK4 are almost always co-amplified due to genomic proximity.
  • PI3K–NRF2 axis: the strongest cross-pathway co-occurrence. NFE2L2 GoF/amplification co-occurs with PIK3CA amplification and tends to co-occur with PIK3CA mutation and PIK3CB amplification. NFE2L2 amplification is mutually exclusive with STK11 loss, but STK11 loss co-occurs with loss-of-function KEAP1 mutation. Co-occurring NRF2–PI3K alterations are concentrated in lung (squamous and adenocarcinoma), esophagogastric, head and neck, and uterine tumors.
  • EGFR alteration biology: EGFR is involved in more significant mutual-exclusivity pairs than any other gene. EGFR amplification is mutually exclusive with ERBB2 activation and with BRAF/KRAS mutation and NF1/RASA1 loss. In GBM and IDHwt LGG, however, EGFR amplification co-occurs with EGFR mutation/fusion and with focal 4q12 amplification of KIT/PDGFRA.
  • Top-altered RTK-RAS genes pan-cancer: KRAS 9%, BRAF 7%, EGFR 4%. KRAS hotspots concentrated in PAAD (72%), colorectal GS (69%), LUAD (33%). BRAF hotspots in SKCM (51%) and THCA (62%). EGFR alterations in GBM (50%), LGG IDHwt (52%), HPV− HNSC (13%), LUAD (13%), esophagogastric squamous (14%). ERBB2 alterations cluster in BRCA, chromosomally-unstable esophagogastric (STAD CIN 26%), and CESC (23%).
  • Novel cancer gene flagged: recurrent hotspot (SOS1 A90V/T, N233Y/S) and other activating mutations (M269I/V, G434R, R552S/K/G/M, E846K) in SOS1 — present in ~1% of LUAD, ~1% of uterine carcinomas independent of subtype, and at lower rates in several other cancer types. Demonstrates that rare but functional drivers emerge at PanCanAtlas sample sizes.
  • Standard-of-care actionability: apart from HER2+ breast cancer (most have standard-care HER2-targeted therapy), SKCM leads with 46% of samples carrying an OncoKB Level 1 or 2A alteration (driven by BRAF hotspots), followed by esophagogastric (ERBB2 amplifications). Luminal-A breast cancer leads in OncoKB Level 3A frequency (driven by PIK3CA, AKT1, ERBB2 mutations).
  • Lowest actionability: UVM 2.5% and testicular non-seminoma 8.5%; THYM, MESO, and KIRC also have low actionable-alteration rates.
  • Combination-therapy opportunities (frequency of co-occurring actionable pairs): CDK4 + MDM2 inhibitors in 78% of dedifferentiated liposarcomas; HER2 + PI3K inhibitors in 17% of HER2-enriched BRCA, 7% of UCS, 7% of UCEC CN-high, 7% of cervical adenocarcinoma; PI3K + MEK in 10% of EBV+ stomach; CDK4 + PI3K in 7% of GBM; HER2 + MEK in 7% of PAAD; PI3K + RAF in 12% of SKCM; IDH + PI3K in 14% of IDH-mutant LGG.

Genes & alterations

  • KRAS — most-altered RTK-RAS gene pan-cancer (9%); hotspot mutations dominate; concentrated in PAAD (72%), colorectal genomically-stable (69%), LUAD (33%).
  • BRAF — 7% pan-cancer; hotspot mutations in SKCM 51%, THCA 62%; primary Level-1/2A driver in melanoma actionability.
  • EGFR — 4% pan-cancer; involved in the most significant SELECT pairs; mutated/amplified/fused in GBM 50% and IDHwt LGG 52%; amplification co-occurs with EGFR mutation/fusion and with 4q12 (KIT/PDGFRA) amplification in glioma, but is mutually exclusive with ERBB2 activation and with KRAS/BRAF mutation or NF1/RASA1 loss elsewhere.
  • ERBB2 — alterations concentrated in BRCA/HER2-enriched and esophagogastric CIN (26%) and CESC (23%); mutually exclusive with EGFR amplification.
  • NF1, RASA1 — RAS-pathway negative regulators; loss is mutually exclusive with EGFR amplification, suggesting an antagonistic interaction.
  • SOS1 — newly nominated RTK-RAS driver: recurrent hotspots A90V/T and N233Y/S plus activating M269I/V, G434R, R552S/K/G/M, E846K; ~1% of LUAD and uterine carcinoma.
  • FGFR2 / FGFR3 — exceptional co-occurrence with PI3K-pathway alterations (versus the typical mutual exclusivity between RTK and downstream RAS/PI3K).
  • TP53 — co-occurs with cell-cycle alterations (CCNE1 amp, CDKN2A mut, RB1 loss, CDK6/E2F3 amp); mutually exclusive with CDKN2A deletion (because CDKN2A deletion removes both p16 and ARF).
  • MDM2 — mutually exclusive with RB1 and CDKN2A loss but co-amplified with CDK4 (genomic proximity); 78% of dedifferentiated liposarcomas have CDK4+MDM2 co-amplification.
  • CDKN2A, RB1, CCNE1, CDK4, CDK6, E2F3 — frequent cell-cycle drivers across the cohort; many co-altered with TP53.
  • PIK3CA, PIK3CB, PIK3R1, PTEN, AKT1 — core PI3K pathway drivers; PIK3CA activation + PTEN inactivation dominate in head-and-neck, breast, GI and gynecologic tumors.
  • STK11 — loss co-occurs with KEAP1 loss in lung cancer; mutually exclusive with NFE2L2 amplification.
  • NFE2L2, KEAP1, CUL3 — NRF2-pathway drivers (NFE2L2 GoF + KEAP1/CUL3 LoF); concentrated in squamous lung/esophagus, head and neck, and uterine cancers.
  • MYC — pathway driven mainly by 8q amplification; most common in BRCA, OV, and others.
  • IDH1, IDH2 — outside the 10 pathways but enumerated as actionable in LGG (IDH + PI3K inhibitor combination indicated in 14% of IDH-mutant LGG).
  • BRCA1, BRCA2 — outside the 10 pathways; included in the 57% pan-cancer actionability figure (vs 52% restricted to the 10 pathways).
  • POLE — POLE-mutant tumor subtype (with MSI-H) carries the highest mutation burden and the highest pathway alteration frequency.

Clinical implications

  • The paper claims a quantitative pan-cancer rationale for targeted therapy planning by pathway, not just by gene: 57% of TCGA tumors carry at least one OncoKB-actionable alteration once BRCA1/2 and IDH1/2 are included; 30% carry multiple.
  • Standard-of-care biomarkers remain concentrated in a few tumor types — BRAF in SKCM (46% Level 1/2A), ERBB2 in HER2-enriched BRCA and esophagogastric. Most other actionable hits are still investigational (OncoKB Level 3A/3B).
  • Combination-therapy hypotheses that the data directly nominate:
    • CDK4 + MDM2 inhibitors — supported by 78% co-amplification in dedifferentiated liposarcoma.
    • HER2 + PI3K inhibitors — 17% co-actionable in HER2-enriched BRCA, 7% in UCS/UCEC CN-high/cervical adeno.
    • PI3K + MEK in EBV+ STAD (10%); CDK4 + PI3K in GBM (7%); HER2 + MEK in PAAD (7%); PI3K + RAF in SKCM (12%); IDH + PI3K in IDH-mutant LGG (14%).
  • NRF2 inclusion criteria for trials: alterations in NFE2L2 and KEAP1 are noted as enrollment criteria for a phase-2 TORC1/2 inhibitor trial — direct example of pathway-level biomarker stratification.
  • Resistance reasoning: mutual-exclusivity patterns (e.g. EGFR amp ⟂ BRAF/KRAS mut ⟂ NF1/RASA1 loss) double as resistance hypotheses — secondary acquisition of any of these could bypass single-agent EGFR targeting (cf. [Sun et al. 2014, Unni et al. 2015] cited in the paper).
  • Not all mutations are targets: the authors caution that MSI-H and POLE-mutant tumors have inflated apparent actionability counts because most of their alterations are passengers; clinical interpretation requires functional filtering (OncoKB-style).

Limitations & open questions

  • Tumor-type scope: most hematologic cancers (other than LAML) are not included; metastatic and post-treatment disease is largely absent (only 4% metastatic, all melanoma).
  • Power for rare drivers: 9,125 samples still underpowers tumor-type-specific drivers below ~1% frequency; the SOS1 finding illustrates both the opportunity and the limitation. Larger tumor-type-specific cohorts are needed.
  • Pathway scope: restricted to 10 canonical mitogenic-signaling pathways; DNA repair, spliceosome, ubiquitination, metabolic, and epigenetic-modifier pathways are deferred to other PanCanAtlas papers. Cross-pathway interactions with these processes are not assessed here.
  • Filtering bias: filtering by OncoKB “oncogenic / likely / predicted oncogenic” status — alterations not yet annotated as oncogenic are removed, which can underestimate alteration frequency for under-studied genes.
  • Pathway membership is curated, not derived: the 10 pathway templates are union sets from prior TCGA marker papers plus expert curation. Membership disagreements between curators are not quantified.
  • Combination-therapy claims are genomic, not pharmacologic: the paper identifies co-occurring actionable alterations; whether the corresponding drug combinations are tolerable or synergistic is not tested.
  • No germline analysis: germline contributions to the 10-pathway landscape (and their interaction with somatic alterations) are deferred to other work.
