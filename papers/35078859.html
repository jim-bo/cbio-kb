Bone marrow surveillance of pediatric cancer survivors identifies clones that predict therapy-related leukemia

TL;DR

Spitzer and colleagues at MSKCC profiled 199 serial bone marrow samples from 52 high-risk neuroblastoma patients (17 who developed therapy-related MDS/AL, 14 with transient cytogenetic abnormalities, 21 controls) using HemePACT targeted DNA capture and ArcherDX FusionPlex Pan-Heme RNA sequencing. They detected at least one t-MDS/AL-associated mutation in 13/17 transformation patients a median of 15 months before overt leukemia diagnosis, identified two novel KMT2A fusion partners (PRDM10, DDX6), and showed that — unlike adults — TP53 clones in pediatric patients were only detectable after initiation of cytotoxic therapy. In a separate cohort of 657 pediatric solid-tumor patients profiled with MSK-IMPACT, only 1.05% had oncogenic clonal hematopoiesis (CH-PD) in myeloid genes, supporting longitudinal (rather than single-timepoint) surveillance for pediatric survivors.

Cohort & data

  • Primary neuroblastoma cohort: 52 patients with NBL treated at MSKCC (diagnosed 1988–2009, enrolled on institutional protocol #00-109 / NCT00588068), contributing 199 serial bone-marrow samples.
    • Transformation cohort (developed TMN / AML / MDS): 17 patients, 88 samples.
    • Transient cohort (cytogenetic abnormality without t-MDS/AL): 14 patients, 74 samples.
    • Neuroblastoma-treated control cohort: 21 patients, 37 samples.
  • Extended pediatric solid-tumor cohort: 657 patients ≤25 years old seen in MSKCC Pediatrics (144 neuroblastoma + 513 other solid tumors), profiled by MSK-IMPACT. Subset deposited in cBioPortal as msk_ch_ped_2021.
  • Assays:
    • HemePACT v3/v4 targeted DNA capture (585 / 576 hematologic-malignancy genes; median coverage 705× on BM samples).
    • ArcherDX FusionPlex Pan-Heme RNA panel for recurrent fusions.
    • MSK-IMPACT (341/410/468-gene tumor + matched-blood panel) for CH detection in the extended cohort.
    • Droplet digital PCR for TP53 hotspot backtracking (C135S, V172F, R175H, R213P, R249S, L257Q, G266R) on 28 additional time points.
  • Reference genome: NCBI build 37 (BWA v0.7.17); variant calling with CaVEMan, Mutect, Strelka, Pindel.

Key findings

  • At t-MDS/AL diagnosis, every case (17/17) had at least one disease-defining alteration detectable by integrated capture-based sequencing, vs. 13/17 (76.5%) by conventional cytogenetics — 39 putative oncogenic events total (median 2/patient, range 1–4) plus 32 VUS.
  • Most frequent drivers at transformation: KMT2A rearrangements (n=6 patients) and TP53 mutations (n=9 variants in n=5 patients); these were mutually exclusive (1 exception with a clonal KMT2A translocation and a subclonal TP53 mutation).
  • KMT2A fusion partners: canonical ELL and AFF1, less common FRYL and GAS7, plus two novel partners — PRDM10 (captured by both DNA and cytogenetics) and DDX6 (RNA-capture only). Both novel partners are involved in histone methyltransferase activity.
  • Backtracking identified t-MDS/AL-associated mutations in 13/17 (76%) transformation patients at a median of 15.0 months before diagnosis (range 1.3–32.4) — and 26.9 months after initial neuroblastoma diagnosis. Median VAF at initial detection 0.08 (range 0.03–0.33).
  • Cytogenetic surveillance caught only 2/6 KMT2A rearrangements before overt disease; RNA-capture caught 5/6 (median 28.5 months earlier).
  • Acquired mutations were rare in the transient (4/14) and control (1/21) cohorts. Relative risk for t-MDS/AL given an oncogenic mutation = 8.8 (95% CI 1.3–57.8, p<0.001) for transformation vs. transient.
  • Mean VAF in the transient cohort was 5× lower than in t-MDS/AL diagnostic samples (0.042 vs. 0.22, p<1e-5) and 2.5× lower than in pre-malignant samples from transformation patients (0.10, p<1e-4).
  • In all 5 transformation patients with TP53 mutations, the earliest ddPCR-detectable timepoint was after initiation of cancer therapy; the burden expanded preferentially during chemotherapy and/or radiation windows.
  • Pediatric solid-tumor MSK-IMPACT cohort: 26/657 (3.9%) had any CH (VAF ≥0.02); only 7/657 (1.05%) had a CH-PD variant in a myeloid gene — vs. 17.6% in patients >25 years old in the same dataset. 4 patients later developed t-MDS/AL, none with detectable CH-PD at MSK-IMPACT time (median 17.5 months prior).

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — somatic mutations (hotspots C135S, V172F, R175H, R213P, R249S, L257Q, G266R) in 5/17 transformation patients; acquired post-therapy in pediatric NBL (distinct from adult CH); associated with later transformation and allelic imbalance / LOH at the locus. Co-occurrence with PPM1D mutations in 2 patients (118730, 118731) consistent with parallel/convergent DDR-pathway clones.
  • KMT2A — canonical breakpoint-region rearrangements in 6/17 transformation patients; partners include ELL, AFF1, FRYL, GAS7, and the novel PRDM10 and DDX6. Associated with topoisomerase-II exposure and earlier transformation.
  • NPM1 — clonal hotspot mutation 36 months before transformation in patient 118725 (followed by IDH1 R132H 12 months later).
  • IDH1 — R132H acquisition in patient 118725’s evolution toward t-AML.
  • IDH2 — transient R140Q clone 1 year before transformation in patient 118729 (in context of pre-leukemic KMT2A rearrangement).
  • PTPN11, NRAS, KRAS — RAS-pathway mutations among transformation drivers; patient 118728 had multiple RAS-pathway hits.
  • PPM1D — DDR-pathway clones at transformation and in the transient cohort.
  • CUX1, STAG2, WT1 — myeloid-malignancy driver mutations at transformation.
  • DNMT3A — non-R882 oncogenic variant in the transient cohort.
  • BCOR — transient clonal oncogenic mutation 2 years before transformation in patient 118731 (fell below detection at t-MDS); also one control-cohort BCOR clone (patient died of neuroblastoma).
  • TET2 — independent clone with IDH1 in patient 118725 (parallel evolution); also among transient-clone genes.
  • RUNX1, RB1 — observed as transient molecular abnormalities in the transformation cohort.
  • CHEK2 — cited as a DDR-pathway gene whose CH clones expand under therapeutic pressure in adult cohorts (context; not a primary finding here).

Clinical implications

  • Integrated DNA + RNA capture-based BM surveillance detects t-MDS/AL-associated alterations a median of 15 months before clinical leukemia diagnosis — substantially earlier than conventional cytogenetics, which misses ~67% of pre-transformation KMT2A rearrangements.
  • An oncogenic mutation in a serial BM sample carries an 8.8-fold relative risk of subsequent t-MDS/AL in this neuroblastoma population, supporting molecular surveillance as a risk-stratification tool for pediatric survivors.
  • Unlike adult oncology, pediatric CH at a single time point (e.g. MSK-IMPACT matched-normal) has low sensitivity for predicting t-MDS/AL — only 1.05% of pediatric solid-tumor patients had CH-PD, and none of the 4 patients who later developed t-MDS/AL had detectable CH-PD at MSK-IMPACT time. Recommendation: longitudinal BM (or peripheral-blood) surveillance for high-risk pediatric patients rather than single-timepoint screening.
  • Distinct molecular natural histories suggest different intervention windows: KMT2A-driven cases may be detectable years earlier by RNA fusion capture; TP53-driven cases acquire mutations post-therapy and expand preferentially during chemo/radiation windows.
  • Authors propose that emerging surveillance protocols should integrate deep molecular profiling and serial sampling to enable risk-adapted interventions before overt t-MDS/AL.

Limitations & open questions

  • Single-institution referral cohort — primary neuroblastoma patients may represent higher-than-average risk; though between-cohort comparisons within the study should be unaffected.
  • Small longitudinal sample size (n=17 transformation) limits power to build a fully predictive VAF / VAF-change model for t-MDS/AL progression.
  • BM-centric (not PB) — though authors note BM and PB are comparable for MDS, acute leukemia, and CH evaluation in prior work.
  • Mutations were called at clinical-grade VAF ≥0.02; 3 of the 4 transformation patients without pre-leukemia detection at ≥0.02 had variants at VAF 0.0012–0.006 retrospectively, suggesting error-corrected sequencing might shift the detection horizon further but with unclear clinical utility.
  • Earlier MSK-IMPACT panel versions lack PPM1D and SRSF2, and never include SRCAP, BRCC3, ZNF318 — true CH prevalence in the pediatric solid-tumor cohort may be underestimated.
  • Whether pre-natal or very-early-life clones contribute to pediatric t-MDS/AL (as suggested for adult JAK2 MPNs) cannot be excluded; the cohort lacked pre-therapy samples from all patients.
  • Open question: do pediatric CH clones detected post-therapy reflect therapy-induced de novo mutations or selection of pre-existing rare clones below detection? The post-therapy-only emergence of TP53 mutations in this cohort favors the former but doesn’t exclude the latter.
