Proteogenomic Characterization Reveals Therapeutic Vulnerabilities in Lung Adenocarcinoma

Authors

Michael A. Gillette

Shankha Satpathy

Song Cao

Saravana M. Dhanasekaran

Suhas V. Vasaikar

Karsten Krug

Francesca Petralia

D. R. Mani

Karl R. Clauser

Steven A. Carr

Doi

10.1016/j.cell.2020.06.013

PMID: 32649874 · DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2020.06.013 · Journal: Cell (2020)

TL;DR

The CPTAC consortium performed comprehensive proteogenomic characterization of 110 treatment-naïve LUAD tumors and 101 matched normal adjacent tissues (NATs), integrating whole-exome and whole-genome sequencing, RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, DNA methylation, and deep-scale proteomics, phosphoproteomics and acetylproteomics. The cohort intentionally balanced smokers with self-reported never-smokers and included patients from multiple countries (notably USA, Vietnam, China). Multi-omics NMF clustering yielded four subgroups (C1–C4) anchored on driver mutations, geography, and gender. Phosphoproteomics revealed therapeutic vulnerabilities downstream of EGFR, KRAS, and EML4-ALK drivers — most notably outlier PTPN11/Shp2 phosphorylation in EGFR-mutant and ALK-fusion tumors, and SOS1 S1161 phosphorylation in KRAS-mutant tumors. STK11 mutation was strongly associated with an immune-cold phenotype, with proteomic evidence pointing to neutrophil degranulation as a potential immunosuppressive mechanism. The dataset is released as the cBioPortal study luad_cptac_2020.

Cohort & data

  • 110 treatment-naïve LUAD tumors and 101 paired normal adjacent tissues (NATs); complete multi-omics data for 101 tumors and 96 NATs.
  • 111 participants (73 males, 38 females; age range 35–81); samples collected from 13 tissue source sites across 8 countries, with deliberate enrichment for never-smokers and non-Western populations relative to TCGA.
  • Assays: whole-exome sequencing (~150× coverage), whole-genome sequencing (~15× coverage), RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, EPIC DNA methylation array, TMT-based isobaric labeling for deep-scale proteomics, phosphoproteomics and acetylproteomics.
  • Released as cBioPortal study luad_cptac_2020; raw proteomics at the CPTAC data portal (S056), genomics via Genomic Data Commons (dbGaP phs001287.v5.p4).

Key findings

  • Multi-omics NMF clustering identified four LUAD subgroups (C1–C4) that overlapped previously described mRNA subtypes (proximal-inflammatory, proximal-proliferative, terminal respiratory unit) but split proximal-proliferative into two distinct clusters:
    • C1 (proximal-inflammatory): enriched for TP53 mutants, STK11 wild-type, CIMP-high.
    • C2 (proximal-proliferative subset): enriched for Western (especially USA) patients, TP53/EGFR wild-type, intermediate CIMP.
    • C3 (dominant proximal-proliferative): enriched for Vietnamese patients and STK11 mutation.
    • C4 (terminal respiratory unit): enriched for EGFR mutations, female sex, Chinese nationality; essentially devoid of KRAS or STK11 mutations. Most EML4-ALK fusion tumors assigned to C4.
  • Kinase fusion landscape: rearrangements involving ALK, ROS1, RET, and PTK2 were most recurrent; oncogenic kinases generally formed in-frame fusions while tumor-suppressor kinases (STK11, STK4, ATM, FRK, EPHA1) showed disruptive out-of-frame events. Seven in-frame ALK fusions used 5′ partners EML4 and novel HMBOX1 and ANKRD36B.
  • ALK phosphosite Y1507 was specifically and dramatically increased in ALK-fusion tumors (validated by IHC with phospho-Y1507 antibody); fusion-specific phosphorylation of partner-side residues EML4-Y226 and HMBOX-S141 was also seen only in fusion-positive samples.
  • Cis/trans CNA effects: 6,043 RNA, 2,354 protein, and 244 phosphoprotein significant positive cis-correlations; only 156 cis-effects overlapped across all three layers. Twelve cancer-associated genes (CREBBP, KMT2B, PSIP1, AKT2, EGFR, GMPS, IL6ST, IRF6, NFKB2, PHF6, YES1, ZBTB7B) showed overlapping cis-regulation.
  • Methylation cis-effects: 120 methylation-driven cis-effects coordinated across RNA, protein, and phosphoprotein (85/120 supported by promoter-region probes), including LUAD-relevant CLDN18, ANK1, and PTPRCAP.
  • Driver-mutation effects on the proteome: TP53 and EGFR mutations elevated cognate protein/phosphosite abundance, while STK11, RBM10, RB1, NF1, and KEAP1 mutations reduced them.
  • TP53 mutant tumors showed proteomic upregulation of mismatch repair (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2) and DNA-damage-response (ATM, ATR, BRCA1) proteins, elevated EZH2 protein relative to RNA, and downregulation of Wnt-signaling proteins (AXIN1, TCF7L2).
  • KEAP1/NFE2L2 axis: 12 tumors carried KEAP1 mutations that did not affect RNA but generally reduced KEAP1 protein and increased NFE2L2 phosphorylation at S215 and S433 (FDR <0.05). A BTB-domain missense KEAP1 G511V mutation preserved KEAP1 protein but produced amongst the highest NFE2L2 phosphorylation — proposed to disrupt KEAP1–NFE2L2 binding rather than KEAP1 stability.
  • KRAS-mutant tumors: extreme outlier phosphorylation of SOS1 S1161 (a GEF that activates KRAS), nominating SOS1 inhibition as a therapeutic strategy.
  • EGFR-mutant tumors: highly consistent outlier tyrosine phosphorylation of PTPN11/Shp2 at Y62 (in the N-SH2/PTP interface) without changes in RNA or protein abundance — the first such report in primary treatment-naïve LUAD. ALK-fusion tumors showed PTPN11 phosphorylation at C-terminal Y546/Y584. The authors nominate EGFR-mutant and ALK-fusion LUAD as promising target populations for PTPN11/Shp2 inhibitors.
  • Outlier kinase analysis (FDR <0.20) identified driver-specific hyperphosphorylated kinases, often without corresponding CNA/RNA/protein changes: EGFR in EGFR-mutants, PRKCD in KRAS-mutants, BRAF in TP53-mutants, WEE1 in EML4-ALK fusions. 27 additional putatively druggable kinases were nominated with known but non-FDA-approved inhibitors.
  • Immune landscape: three immune clusters (Hot-tumor-enriched HTE, Cold-tumor-enriched CTE, NAT-enriched). HTE was enriched for multi-omics cluster C1 and CIMP-low status, with upregulated PD1, PD-L1, CTLA4 (FDR <1E-10), FOXP3 (FDR <0.0001), TGF-β, and IL-10. IDO1 protein was upregulated in HTE and IFNG-high clusters, supporting IDO1 inhibition as an opportunity in immune-hot LUAD.
  • STK11 immune-cold signature: STK11-mutant tumors showed the most dramatic immune downregulation, with reductions in xCell-derived dendritic-cell, NK T-cell, and macrophage signatures (FDR <0.1); ESTIMATE immune scores were lowest in KRAS-wildtype STK11 mutants. A deep-learning model trained on histopathology predicted STK11 mutation status with 94% slide-level accuracy.
  • Neutrophil degranulation emerged as the proteomic pathway most strongly associated with STK11 mutation; all 16 measured neutrophil-degranulation proteins (CAMP, LTF, BPI, MMP8, MMP9, MPO, LCN2, ELANE, ARG1, others) were coherently overexpressed in STK11-mutant tumors despite no enrichment in neutrophil counts — proteins are stored in granules so the signal was undetectable at the RNA level.
  • Smoking signature: ~50% of mutations were GG→TT/CC→AA dinucleotide changes; smoking-high samples correlated with polycyclic-aromatic-hydrocarbon environmental signatures (DBADE, DBA, 5-Methylchrysene). Tumors had higher RNA stemness than NATs, with HSS samples higher than LSS within both tumors and NATs (field cancerization effect).
  • Never-smoker biology: ARHGEF5 protein and its Y1370 phosphosite were elevated in non-smokers, highest in ALK-fusion patients; the negative Rho-GTPase regulator SRGAP1 was suppressed — hypothesized to drive tumorigenesis through Rho-GTPase hyperactivation in this subset.
  • Tumor–NAT biomarker candidates: 289 proteins upregulated at the protein level using stringent thresholds (log2 FC >2, FDR <0.01, differential in ≥90% of pairs); 60 were also RNA-differential, including 5 (GFPT1, BZW2, PDIA4, P4HB, PMM2) up in all tumors vs paired NATs. GREM1 (log2 FC >5) and OCIAD2 (log2 FC >4) were highlighted as poor-prognosis markers.
  • Cancer-testis antigens / neoantigens: 44 CT antigens recurrently overexpressed; 9 in ≥10% of samples. KIF2C was the most ubiquitous (63% of samples). 2,481 mRNA-validated and 49 peptide-validated somatic mutation–derived neoantigens identified across 104 patients. 97 samples showed evidence of CT antigens or neoantigens.

Genes & alterations

  • EGFR — activating mutations enriched in cluster C4 (terminal respiratory unit), associated with female sex and Chinese nationality. EGFR-mutant tumors showed elevated cognate protein/phosphosite abundance, outlier PTPN11/Shp2 Y62 phosphorylation, elevated CTNNB1 protein/phosphoprotein (despite reduced RNA), MET overexpression, and ROCK1 pathway activation.
  • KRAS — mutant tumors showed outlier SOS1 S1161 phosphorylation, hyperphosphorylated PRKCD, MAPK1/TAK1 pathway activation, upregulation of complement/coagulation cascades, and enrichment for chemokine CXCL8 and immune target THY1.
  • ALK / EML4 — seven in-frame ALK fusions (EML4-ALK, novel HMBOX1-ALK, ANKRD36B-ALK) clustered in C4 with miRNA cluster enriched for miR-494/495/496. Outlier ALK Y1507 phosphorylation specific to fusion-positive samples, validated by IHC. Hyperphosphorylated WEE1 and PTPN11 (Y546, Y584).
  • STK11 — predominantly disruptive out-of-frame fusion events plus point mutations; enriched in cluster C3 and in Vietnamese patients. Strongly associated with immune-cold phenotype, low ESTIMATE immune scores, neutrophil-degranulation protein signature, and amino-acid-metabolism/urea-cycle perturbation. STK11 mutation was associated with increased SMAD4 S138 phosphorylation.
  • TP53 — mutant tumors enriched in C1; elevated cognate protein/phosphosite, upregulation of MMR (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2) and DDR (ATM, ATR, BRCA1) proteins, elevated EZH2 protein, downregulated Wnt-signaling proteins (AXIN1, TCF7L2). High expression of CCNA2, TOP2A, PLOD2, ANLN, MMP12, CDK1, CCNB1. Outlier BRAF hyperphosphorylation.
  • KEAP1 / NFE2L2 — 12 KEAP1-mutant tumors; KEAP1 mutation generally reduced KEAP1 protein and increased NFE2L2 S215/S433 phosphorylation. KEAP1 G511V (BTB domain) preserved KEAP1 protein but produced high NFE2L2 phosphorylation, suggesting binding disruption rather than degradation.
  • RB1 — mutations associated with increased CDK4 protein abundance, potentially conferring CDK4/6-inhibitor resistance.
  • SMARCA4 — mutations led to increased SMAD2 protein expression.
  • RBM10 / NF1 — mutations reduced cognate protein and phosphosite abundance.
  • PTPN11 — Y62 phosphorylation in EGFR-mutants, Y546/Y584 phosphorylation in ALK-fusions; nominated as priority therapeutic target.
  • SOS1 — S1161 C-terminal phosphorylation in KRAS-mutant tumors.
  • IDO1 — protein upregulated in immune-hot and IFNG-high clusters.
  • ARHGEF5 — protein and Y1370 phosphosite elevated in never-smokers, peaking in ALK-fusion patients; nominated as a therapeutic target via auto-inhibitory peptides.
  • Methylation cis-effect targets with LUAD relevance: CLDN18, ANK1, PTPRCAP, BCLAF1, GSTP1, MGA, TBX3.

Clinical implications

  • PTPN11/Shp2 inhibition is nominated for EGFR-mutant and EML4-ALK-fusion LUAD based on consistent outlier phosphorylation; multiple Shp2 inhibitors are already in clinical trials.
  • SOS1 inhibition is proposed for KRAS-mutant LUAD downstream of the SOS1 S1161 phosphorylation signal.
  • IDO1 inhibition is highlighted as a candidate in immune-hot LUAD where IDO1 protein is upregulated.
  • Anti-CTLA4 therapy may benefit HTE tumors given high CTLA4/FOXP3 expression and T-reg infiltration.
  • CDK4/6 inhibitor resistance in RB1-mutant LUAD is suggested by elevated CDK4 protein.
  • VTE management may be stratifiable by mutation type — coagulation cascades were upregulated in KRAS- and downregulated in EGFR-mutant tumors, consistent with NSCLC literature showing lower VTE risk in EGFR-wildtype patients.
  • Driver-specific outlier kinases with FDA-approved drugs include EGFR (EGFR-mutants), PRKCD (KRAS-mutants), BRAF (TP53-mutants), and WEE1 (EML4-ALK fusions).
  • Biomarker candidates for early detection or prognosis include GFPT1, BZW2, PDIA4, P4HB, PMM2, GREM1, OCIAD2, DHFR, HYOU1, LDHA, CBX8, AKAP4, MORC1, NUF2.
  • Histopathology-based deep learning predicted STK11 mutation with 94% slide-level accuracy, suggesting a low-cost screening route for immune-cold LUAD identification.

Limitations & open questions

  • Heavy interdependence between mutational status, ethnicity/geography, gender, and smoking status; cohort size (110 tumors) limited the effectiveness of linear-model adjustment for these confounders, frustrating clean attribution of gender- or smoking-specific effects.
  • Bulk-tumor analyses lack spatial and cellular resolution needed to disambiguate tumor-epithelium versus microenvironment contributions; single-cell or spatial proteogenomic approaches were called out as future directions.
  • The associations described are explicitly hypothesis-generating and require functional validation before being treated as biological conclusions.
  • Strong correlations between cigarette-smoking PAH signatures and apparently unrelated environmental signatures (Figure S6C) caution against over-interpretation of mutational-signature attributions and motivate richer environmental/occupational annotation.
  • The neutrophil-degranulation signal in STK11-mutant tumors was detected only at the proteome level (granule storage explains the RNA invisibility); the functional contribution of these proteins to immune-cold biology remains to be tested.
  • Several novel kinase fusions (HMBOX1-ALK, ANKRD36B-ALK) and CT antigens (MORC1, NUF2 in LUAD) require validation of oncogenic / immunogenic roles.
