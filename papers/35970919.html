TL;DR

Chatila and colleagues profiled DNA and/or RNA from 738 patients with rectal adenocarcinoma — pooling the ACOSOG Z6041 (n=25), TIMING (n=71), MSK-Research (n=163), and MSK-Clinical (n=479) cohorts — to define genomic and transcriptomic correlates of response to neoadjuvant therapy (NAT) in locally advanced rectal cancer (LARC). They found that APC mutations are significantly less frequent in lower-rectum and anal tumors, providing a molecular basis for the more aggressive behavior of distal disease. No somatic alteration was associated with complete response in a treatment-agnostic manner, but KRAS mutations predicted faster relapse specifically in patients treated with chemoradiation followed by consolidative chemotherapy (CRT-CNCT). Transcriptomically, overexpression of IGF2 and L1CAM marked incomplete responders, and RNA-seq immune deconvolution identified a small subset of pMMR/MSS “immune-hot” tumors with prolonged disease-free survival and overexpression of immune-checkpoint genes — a candidate population for future ICI trials.

Cohort & data

  • 738 rectal adenocarcinoma patients from four sources: ACOSOG Z6041 (NCT00114231, n=25), TIMING (NCT00335816, n=71), MSK-Research (MSK-R, n=163), MSK-Clinical (MSK-C, n=479) PMID:35970919.
  • Cancer type: READ — rectal adenocarcinoma; cBioPortal study rectal_msk_2022.
  • 692 pre-treatment primary tumors sequenced with the MSK-IMPACT panel (341–505 cancer-related genes). Stage I=78, II=77, III=375, IV=162. Tumors located in lower (LR, 0–4 cm from anal verge, n=257), middle (MR, 4–8 cm, n=197), and upper rectum (UR, 8–12 cm, n=204); detailed location unknown for 34.
  • MMR status: 652/692 (94.2%) pMMR/MSS, 36/692 (5.2%) dMMR/MSI, 4/692 (0.6%) POLE hypermutants.
  • Whole-exome sequencing: 97 tumors for mutational-signature analysis.
  • RNA-seq: 114 pre-treatment endoscopic biopsies; 97 with NAT outcome data; 87 with paired DNA, RNA, and outcomes.
  • Outcome-focused subset: 346 stage II–III LARC patients (290 pMMR/MSS with MSK-IMPACT data) from TIMING (n=71), MSK-R (n=127), MSK-C (n=148); median follow-up 45.2 months.
  • Comparator cohort: 178 proximal colon (PCA) + 204 distal colon (DCA) tumors from the published MSK-Colon cohort, sequenced with identical MSK-IMPACT methods.
  • Independent validation cohorts: Kamran et al. LARC RNA-seq (n=15) for IGF2/L1CAM; TCGA LARC subset (n=42) for immune-cluster replication.

Key findings

  • Mutational landscape of pMMR/MSS rectal cancer (n=652): most frequently altered genes were APC (81%), TP53 (81%), KRAS (42%), FBXW7 (14%), and PIK3CA (12%) PMID:35970919. Top altered pathways: WNT (85%), TP53 (82%), RAS (51%).
  • Co-occurrence / mutual exclusivity: KRAS and AMER1 significantly co-occurred (q<0.05); TP53 was mutually exclusive with PIK3CA and KRAS (q<0.1). C-terminal APC mutations co-occurred with KRAS and AMER1. Oncogenic PIK3CA mutations were subclonal in ~1/3 of cases where they co-occurred with APC or TP53.
  • Clonality / WGD: 82% of driver mutations were clonal. dMMR/MSI tumors had fewer TP53 mutations (38% vs 81%, p<0.001) and no whole-genome duplication events (0% vs 40.1%, p=0.026). Biallelic TP53 inactivation correlated with higher fraction-genome-altered (FGA).
  • KRAS hotspots: Oncogenic KRAS alterations in 274/652 (42%) of pMMR/MSS tumors, majority clonal, predominantly at codons G12 / G13.
  • Actionability per OncoKB: 28% of pMMR/MSS tumors harbored ≥1 actionable alteration. Level 1: BRAF V600E (1%); Level 2: ERBB2 amplification (3%); Level 3A: KRAS G12C (2%); Level 3B: PIK3CA (12%), NRAS (4%), ATM (2%).
  • APC by rectal location: WNT-pathway alteration frequency declined from upper to lower rectum (UR 92% / MR 90% / LR 77%, p<0.001), driven by APC. Median tumor location of APC-altered cases was 6.85 cm from the anal verge vs 4 cm for APC-wild type (p<0.001). Replicated in an independent 157-patient MSK-OtherRectal cohort (p<0.001). Anal adenocarcinomas (n=9) had only 1/9 (11%) WNT-altered tumors. Biallelic APC inactivation was also less frequent in LR and anal tumors (p<0.001).
  • Rectal vs colon: Compared with MSK-Colon (178 PCA + 204 DCA), rectal tumors had higher rates of lung metastasis and lower peritoneal/abdominal metastasis; PCAs had higher TMB among pMMR/MSS tumors, more RAS/PI3K alterations (q<0.001); rectal tumors had more TP53-pathway alterations (q<0.005). 20q amplification was more frequent in DCAs (q<0.05). Rectal and DCA APC mutations were predominantly C-terminal; PCAs predominantly N-terminal.
  • Genomic predictors of NAT response — null in pooled analysis: Among 290 pMMR/MSS LARC patients, multivariate logistic regression detected no significant clinical or genomic association with complete response (CR). Multivariate Cox proportional-hazards for disease-free survival (DFS) found a borderline association of KRAS with shorter DFS (p=0.04).
  • Treatment-stratified KRAS effect: After stratification, KRAS mutations were associated with significantly shorter DFS in CRT-CNCT-treated patients (p=0.004) but not in INCT-CRT-treated patients (p=0.122). KRAS was not associated with different CR rates in CRT-CNCT (25% vs 41%, p=0.277), and showed a borderline-non-significant improved CR in INCT-CRT (33% vs 15%, p=0.056).
  • Transcriptomic predictors: Differential expression in CR vs incomplete-response (iCR) identified 7 down- and 8 up-regulated genes in iCR; iCR-overexpressed included IGF2 and L1CAM. Strong IGF2 overexpression was mutually exclusive with PI3K-pathway alterations. High L1CAM correlated with CMS4, lower CR, shorter DFS, and higher metastasis rates. Both validated in the independent Kamran et al. cohort (n=15).
  • CA9 (hypoxia marker): Increased in KRAS- or PIK3CA-mutant tumors, particularly double-mutants (p<0.001).
  • CMS distribution and outcomes: CMS4 patients had the worst DFS; CMS2 had slightly better DFS than CMS1/CMS3.
  • Immune clustering (ssGSEA, n=101): Hierarchical clustering on pMMR/MSS tumors (n=96) identified IG1 (low, n=52), IG2 (intermediate, n=37), IG3 (immune-hot, n=7); dMMR/MSI tumors formed IG4 (n=5). Immune-hot IG3 (~8% of pMMR/MSS) had higher inflammatory response ssGSEA, higher Th1 cells and regulatory T cells, lower DNA-repair signature, and overexpression of PDCD1 (PD-1), CD274 (PD-L1), CTLA4, HAVCR2 (TIM3), and LAG3.
  • IG3 + IG4 outcomes (n=87 with DNA/RNA/outcomes): Better response and DFS than IG1/IG2. APC and TP53 mutations were less frequent in IG3/IG4; KRAS mutations less frequent in IG2.
  • CD3/CD4 IHC validation: CD3 IF correlated with ssGSEA T-cell scores (p<0.001); CD4 IF correlated with Th-helper RNA signatures (p<0.01).
  • External replication: Immune-cluster findings replicated in a 42-sample LARC subset of TCGA.

Genes & alterations

  • APC — Altered in 81% of pMMR/MSS rectal tumors. Frequency decreases toward the anal verge (UR 92% / MR 90% / LR 77% WNT-pathway, p<0.001); APC-altered cases sit a median 6.85 cm vs 4 cm from the anal verge (p<0.001). Replicated in MSK-OtherRectal (n=157). C-terminal in rectum/DCA; N-terminal in PCA.
  • TP53 — Altered in 81% of pMMR/MSS; only 38% of dMMR/MSI (p<0.001). Biallelic inactivation correlated with higher FGA. Truncating > missense for FGA. Less frequent in immune-hot IG3/IG4 tumors.
  • KRAS — Oncogenic alterations in 42% (274/652) of pMMR/MSS, mostly clonal at G12/G13. G12C in 2% (Level 3A). Borderline shorter DFS overall (p=0.04). Significantly shorter DFS in CRT-CNCT–treated patients (p=0.004); not significant in INCT-CRT. Borderline improved CR in INCT-CRT (33% vs 15%, p=0.056).
  • FBXW7 — Altered in 14% of pMMR/MSS rectal tumors.
  • PIK3CA — Altered in 12% (Level 3B); mutually exclusive with TP53; subclonal in ~1/3 of co-occurrent cases with APC/TP53.
  • AMER1 — Co-occurs significantly with KRAS and with C-terminal APC mutations (q<0.05).
  • BRAF — V600E in 1% (only Level 1 actionable finding).
  • ERBB2 — Amplification in 3% (Level 2).
  • NRAS — Mutated in 4% (Level 3B in CRC).
  • ATM — Mutated in 2% (Level 3B).
  • POLE — Hypermutant in 4/692 (0.6%) tumors.
  • MUTYH — Strong SBS36 signature observed in one pMMR/MSS patient (single case).
  • IGF2 — Overexpressed in incomplete-responder (iCR) patients. Strong overexpression mutually exclusive with PI3K-pathway alterations. Validated in Kamran et al. independent cohort.
  • L1CAM — Overexpressed in iCR patients; higher levels associated with CMS4, lower CR, shorter DFS, and higher metastasis rates. IHC confirmed L1CAM-expressing tumor cells pre- and post-NAT. Validated independently.
  • CA9 — Hypoxia-induced; expression elevated in KRAS-/PIK3CA-mutant tumors, especially double-mutants (p<0.001).
  • PDCD1 (PD-1), CD274 (PD-L1), CTLA4, HAVCR2 (TIM3), LAG3 — Overexpressed in IG3 immune-hot (and to a lesser extent IG4) tumors, nominating these patients for ICI trials.

Clinical implications

  • Organ preservation candidates: Rate of organ preservation increased over time (modern era), while overall CR (pathological + clinical) rate remained stable. KRAS status and immune profile may help risk-stratify patients considered for non-operative management.
  • KRAS as treatment-specific biomarker: KRAS mutations predict shorter DFS specifically when patients receive chemoradiation followed by consolidative chemotherapy (CRT-CNCT). The authors interpret KRAS as having treatment-specific predictive value rather than a treatment-agnostic prognostic signal, and call for prospective randomized validation.
  • L1CAM and IGF2 as candidate biomarkers / drug targets: L1CAM overexpression and IGF2 overexpression mark poor responders to NAT in LARC. The authors cite L1CAM-inhibitory molecules and IGF2 inhibitors as candidate therapeutic strategies for non-responders, pending prospective validation.
  • Immune-hot pMMR/MSS subset for ICI trials: A ~8% subset of pMMR/MSS LARC tumors (IG3) exhibit immune-hot biology with checkpoint-target overexpression — overall trials of ICI in unselected pMMR/MSS LARC have failed, but selecting this subset could re-enable immunotherapy strategies. Consistent with the “Immunoscore” prognostic concept.
  • Anatomic location matters molecularly: Tumors close to the anal verge harbor less frequent APC/WNT activation. This provides a genomic basis for previously anatomy-only explanations of more aggressive distal-rectum behavior and supports recording precise rectal location in future trials.
  • Actionable alterations are rare: Only 28% of pMMR/MSS rectal tumors harbor any OncoKB-actionable alteration, with Level 1 (BRAF V600E) and Level 2 (ERBB2 amp) at 1% and 3% respectively.

Limitations & open questions

  • The majority of samples were sequenced with targeted exome panels (MSK-IMPACT); broader sequencing platforms (WES, WGS, methylation) might reveal additional drivers.
  • RNA-seq was only available for a small subset (114/738 patients), limiting power for transcriptomic analyses.
  • Cohorts are non-overlapping in calendar time, conflating treatment paradigm shifts (CRT, CRT-CNCT, INCT-CRT) with patient accrual era — KRAS-DFS treatment-specific effects may be confounded by changes in surgical timing, dose, and management.
  • Surgical vs non-operative management timing (interval between NAT completion and surgery) may also affect both response and recurrence patterns.
  • The borderline KRAS / INCT-CRT improved-CR finding (p=0.056) needs prospective testing — direction is opposite to the CRT-CNCT shorter-DFS finding.
  • Authors anticipate that molecular data from OPRA, TNT, and PROSPECT trials will be needed to robustly validate genomic / transcriptomic / immune predictors.
  • Only 7 immune-hot IG3 tumors were identified in the n=96 pMMR/MSS RNA cohort — the cluster is robust but small; replication required.
