Pan-cancer analysis of whole genomes

Author

ICGC/TCGA Pan-Cancer Analysis of Whole Genomes Consortium

Doi

10.1038/s41586-020-1969-6

PMID: 32025007 · DOI: 10.1038/s41586-020-1969-6 · Journal: Nature (2020)

TL;DR

This is the flagship marker paper of the ICGC/TCGA Pan-Cancer Analysis of Whole Genomes (PCAWG) Consortium. It reports an integrative analysis of 2,658 high-quality matched tumor–normal whole genomes spanning 38 tumor types, uniformly aligned and variant-called across three core pipelines on a globally distributed cloud-computing infrastructure. The cohort yielded ~43.8 million somatic SNVs, ~2.4 million indels, 288,416 SVs, 19,166 retrotransposition events, and 8,185 de novo mtDNA mutations. Cancer genomes contain an average of 4.6 coding+non-coding driver mutations per tumor (2.6 coding); 91% of tumors had ≥1 identified driver, but ~5% had no identifiable driver after accounting for technical and biological causes. The paper anchors a series of 22 companion papers covering chromothripsis, mutational signatures, evolutionary timing, transcriptomic consequences, germline determinants, and telomere maintenance — establishing PCAWG as the canonical pan-cancer WGS reference resource (cBioPortal studyId: pancan_pcawg_2020).

Cohort & data

  • 2,658 white- and grey-listed donors with matched tumor/normal WGS, drawn from 2,834 donors after QC exclusion of 176 (PMID:32025007).
  • 38 tumor types including breast, prostate, ovary, colorectal, hepatocellular, pancreatic, pancreatic neuroendocrine, chromophobe RCC, glioblastoma, lung squamous, liposarcoma, medulloblastoma, MPN, thyroid, bladder, head-and-neck, esophagus, CLL, DLBCL, cutaneous melanoma, and acral melanoma.
  • 2,605 primary tumors + 173 metastases/local recurrences; mean read coverage 39× normal, bimodal 38×/60× tumor.
  • RNA-seq for 1,222 donors. Mean age 56 (range 1–90); 55% men, 45% women.
  • Pipelines: three core SNV/indel/SV/CNA callers (Sanger, [DKFZ], Broad MuTect) plus bespoke algorithms for retrotransposition, mtDNA, and telomere length. SVs called with DELLY/SvABA; CNAs/purity-ploidy by ABSOLUTE-class tools; significantly mutated regions by GISTIC2.0; signatures via SigProfiler.
  • Benchmarking: 63 representative tumor–normal pairs validated by deep custom-bait sequencing; consensus SNV calls reached 95% sensitivity (90% CI 88–98%) and 95% precision (90% CI 71–99%); consensus indel sensitivity 60% (34–72%), precision 91% (73–96%); consensus SV sensitivity ~90%, precision ~97.5%.
  • Compute: alignment and variant calling distributed across 13 data centres on 3 continents; pipelines packaged as Docker containers and published on Dockstore.
  • Dataset page: pancan_pcawg_2020.

Key findings

  • Genome-wide mutation burden across 2,583 white-listed donors: 43,778,859 somatic SNVs, 410,123 MNVs, 2,418,247 indels, 288,416 SVs, 19,166 retrotransposition events, 8,185 mtDNA mutations.
  • Age correlates with somatic burden across most variant classes: ~190 SNVs/year (P = 0.02), ~22 indels/year (P = 5×10⁻⁵), ~1.5 SVs/year (P < 2×10⁻¹⁶; linear regression).
  • Driver landscape: 91% of tumors carried ≥1 driver; mean 4.6 drivers per tumor (2.6 coding). Across PCAWG only 13% (785/5,913) of driver point mutations are non-coding, but 25% of tumors carry ≥1 non-coding driver, one-third of which (237/785) hit the TERT promoter (9% of PCAWG tumors).
  • Tumor-type SV vs SNV mix: driver SVs dominate breast adenocarcinoma (6.4 ± 3.7 SVs vs 2.2 ± 1.3 point mutations; P < 1×10⁻¹⁶) and ovary adenocarcinoma (5.8 ± 2.6 vs 1.9 ± 1.0; P < 1×10⁻¹⁶), whereas point mutations dominate colorectal adenocarcinoma (2.4 ± 1.4 SVs vs 7.4 ± 7.0 point muts; P = 4×10⁻¹⁰) and mature B-cell lymphomas.
  • Biallelic TSG inactivation is the rule for tumor-suppressor drivers: of 954 TP53-driver tumors, 736 (77%) had both alleles mutated, 96% via a somatic SNV + somatic deletion of the other allele.
  • Germline contribution: 17% of patients carried rare germline protein-truncating variants in cancer-predisposition / DDR / somatic-driver genes; biallelic inactivation by germline+somatic combined was 4.5% overall, 81% of which affected BRCA1, BRCA2, or ATM.
  • Tumors with no apparent driver: 181/2,658 (≈6.8%) had no identifiable driver. After triage of technical causes (tumor-in-normal contamination, low coverage at hotspots — e.g. only ~50% of PCAWG tumors had ≥90% power at the two TERT-promoter hotspots due to GC bias) and biological causes, 5.3% of PCAWG tumors still had no driver identified. Chromophobe RCC (44%; 19/43) and pancreatic neuroendocrine (22%; 18/81) tumors were most enriched for missing drivers — both showing recurrent aneuploidy patterns.
  • Bioinformatic failure mode: PCAWG missed the canonical JAK2 V617F driver in 35 myeloproliferative neoplasms because panels-of-normals built from blood samples filtered out the mutation (2–5% of healthy individuals carry occult haematopoietic clones).
  • Novel driver: recurrent CNA spanning SETD2 in driverless medulloblastomas; SETD2 loss occurred exclusively in group-4 medulloblastomas (P < 1×10⁻⁴) and significantly decreased SETD2 expression (P = 0.002).
  • Clustered mutational processes (Fig. 4):
    • Chromoplexy in 467/2,658 (17.8%) samples; prominent in prostate adenocarcinoma and lymphoid malignancies; unexpectedly enriched in thyroid adenocarcinoma where ≥31% of fusion/enhancer-hijacking events were caused by chromoplexy, generating RET and NTRK3 fusions and juxtaposition of IGF2BP3 with regulatory elements.
    • Kataegis in 60.5% of cancers, dominated by APOBEC C>N at TpC (81.7% of foci; correlates with APOBEC3B expression, SV burden, age); 5.7% T>N error-prone polymerase signature; 2.3% alternative GpC/CpC deaminase signature in sarcomas.
    • Chromothripsis in 587/2,658 (22.3%) samples; most frequent in sarcoma, glioblastoma, lung squamous, melanoma, and breast. TP53 is the strongest associated driver (pan-cancer OR = 3.22, P = 8.3×10⁻³⁵; q < 0.05 in breast lobular OR = 13, colorectal OR = 25, prostate OR = 2.6, hepatocellular OR = 3.9). Chromothripsis coincided with 3.6% of all PCAWG drivers and ~7% of CNA drivers; 58% of coincident events were amplifications, 34% homozygous deletions.
  • Evolutionary timing: chromothripsis tends to be clonal/early in liposarcoma, prostate adenocarcinoma, and lung squamous. In acral melanoma, chromothripsis-induced co-amplification of TERT + CCND1 occurred very early (few/no high-VAF SNVs predated amplification), whereas SOX2 amplification in lung squamous followed many earlier SNVs (late event). Melanoma had 89 chromothripsis events across 66 tumors (61%); 47/89 affected genes recurrently altered in melanoma. Chromosome-11 involvement including CCND1 was seen in 21 cases (10/86 cutaneous, 11/21 acral/mucosal), nearly always with amplification (19/21).
  • Germline GWAS of mutational signatures:
    • 22q13.1 locus (rs12628403) tags a ~30-kb germline deletion fusing APOBEC3B coding into APOBEC3A 3’ UTR; minor allele protective against APOBEC3B-like mutagenesis (β = −0.43, P = 5.6×10⁻⁹, MAF 8.2%, n = 1,201 EUR donors). The deletion reduces APOBEC3B mutagenesis in low-APOBEC cancers and increases APOBEC3A-like mutagenesis in high-APOBEC cancers.
    • Novel 22q13.1 locus rs2142833 (β = 0.23, P = 1.3×10⁻⁸) associated with APOBEC3B-like mutagenesis, replicated in East Asian cohorts (β = 0.58, P = 8×10⁻¹⁵); cis-eQTL for APOBEC3B (β = 0.19, P = 2×10⁻⁶).
  • Germline rare-variant associations:
    • Germline BRCA1 PTVs ↔︎ excess small tandem duplications (P = 6×10⁻¹³); germline BRCA2 PTVs ↔︎ excess small SV deletions (P = 1×10⁻⁸). 20/21 BRCA1-deficient tumors with the “templated insertion SV phenotype” had biallelic BRCA1 hits, linking biallelic BRCA1 inactivation to the templated-insertion phenotype.
    • Germline MBD4 PTVs ↔︎ elevated somatic CpG > T mutagenesis (P < 2.5×10⁻⁶), replicated in TCGA exome cohort (n = 8,134; P = 7.1×10⁻⁴). MBD4 encodes a DNA repair gene that removes thymine from T:G mismatches at methylated CpGs.
  • Active germline LINE-1 source elements: 114 germline L1 source elements capable of somatic retrotransposition, 70 representing insertions vs the human reference. 16 hot-L1 elements account for 67% (2,440/3,669) of all L1-mediated transductions, split into “Strombolian” (common, frequently active, modest output) and “Plinian” (rare MAF ≤ 2%, aggressive output) classes; only 38% of donors carry ≥1 Plinian element.
  • Telomere maintenance: 16% of PCAWG tumors have somatic mutations in at least one of ATRX, DAXX, or TERT. Twelve telomere-sequence features cluster tumors into 4 groups, with ALT-pathway clusters C1 enriched for RB1 alterations + ATRX SVs (P = 3×10⁻⁵ for RB1; P = 6×10⁻¹⁴ for ATRX SVs) and C2 enriched for ATRX/DAXX point mutations (P = 6×10⁻⁵). C3 (predominantly thyroid adenocarcinomas) had frequent TERT promoter mutations (30%; P = 2×10⁻⁶). Telomere maintenance abnormalities inversely correlated with tissue stem-cell division rates (P = 0.01, Poisson regression).

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — driver in 954 tumors; 736 (77%) biallelic, 96% somatic-SNV + somatic-deletion. Pan-cancer associated with chromothripsis (OR = 3.22, P = 8.3×10⁻³⁵) (PMID:32025007).
  • CDKN2A — biallelic inactivation in 475 tumors; co-deleted in chromothripsis events in glioblastoma (PMID:32025007).
  • ARID1A — driver in 316 tumors (PMID:32025007).
  • KRAS — driver in 287 tumors (PMID:32025007).
  • PTEN — driver in 269 tumors (PMID:32025007).
  • TERT — driver in 263 tumors; promoter mutations are the dominant non-coding driver (237/785 non-coding drivers; 9% of PCAWG). In acral melanoma, chromothripsis-induced TERT amplification occurs as a very early event; in chromophobe RCC, breakpoints immediately adjacent to TERT increase expression 80-fold (P = 0.0004) (PMID:32025007).
  • SMAD4 — driver in 181 tumors (PMID:32025007).
  • PIK3CA — driver in 177 tumors (PMID:32025007).
  • RB1 — driver in 167 tumors; enriched in ALT-pathway telomere cluster C1 (P = 3×10⁻⁵); links RB1 loss to ALT activation independent of ATRX/DAXX (PMID:32025007).
  • BRAF — driver in 162 tumors (PMID:32025007).
  • CCND1 — recurrently co-amplified with TERT via chromothripsis in acral melanoma (11/21 acral/mucosal melanomas affected on chr 11) (PMID:32025007).
  • MDM2 — universally amplified in liposarcoma chromothripsis events (PMID:32025007).
  • EGFR — focally amplified in glioblastoma chromothripsis events (PMID:32025007).
  • SOX2 — amplified in lung squamous cell carcinoma chromothripsis, but as a late event (many predating SNVs are amplified) (PMID:32025007).
  • SETD2 — novel recurrent CNA-driven loss in driverless group-4 medulloblastoma (P < 1×10⁻⁴); decreased expression (P = 0.002) (PMID:32025007).
  • ATRX / DAXX — 128 tumors with alterations in either; 71 truncating. Cluster differently across telomere phenotype groups (ATRX SVs in C1, ATRX/DAXX point muts in C2) (PMID:32025007).
  • BRCA1 — germline PTVs associated with somatic tandem-duplication burden (P = 6×10⁻¹³) and templated-insertion SV phenotype (P = 4×10⁻¹⁵); 20/21 templated-insertion tumors had biallelic BRCA1 inactivation (PMID:32025007).
  • BRCA2 — germline PTVs associated with somatic small-deletion burden (P = 1×10⁻⁸) (PMID:32025007).
  • ATM — alongside BRCA1/BRCA2, one of the three genes accounting for 81% of germline+somatic biallelic combinations (PMID:32025007).
  • MBD4 — germline PTVs cause elevated somatic CpG > T mutagenesis (P < 2.5×10⁻⁶ in PCAWG; replicated P = 7.1×10⁻⁴ in TCGA n = 8,134) (PMID:32025007).
  • APOBEC3A / APOBEC3B — germline ~30-kb APOBEC3A–APOBEC3B fusion deletion (rs12628403) and independent rs2142833 cis-eQTL modulate APOBEC mutagenesis pan-cancer (PMID:32025007).
  • JAK2 — V617F driver missed in 35 MPNs because panels-of-normals from blood samples filtered the mutation; illustrates a systematic bioinformatic failure mode (PMID:32025007).
  • RET, NTRK3, IGF2BP3 — chromoplexy-mediated fusions/enhancer-hijacking events in thyroid adenocarcinoma (PMID:32025007).
  • FGFR2, FANCA, PCM1 — among representative cancer-associated genes inside significant copy-number-loss peaks (GISTIC2.0) in driverless cases (PMID:32025007).

Clinical implications

  • The paper is a methods/resource marker; it makes no direct treatment recommendations but explicitly frames clinical applications:
    • Quality-of-call argument for clinical WGS: PCAWG documents that single-pipeline calls miss ≥10% of true SNVs and have wider precision bounds; the authors argue that “careful and critical appraisal of the whole pipeline — including sample acquisition, genome sequencing, mapping, variant calling and driver annotation, as done here — should be required for laboratories that offer clinical sequencing of cancer genomes” (PMID:32025007).
    • Non-coding driver discovery (TERT promoter, others) demonstrates that exome-only sequencing systematically misses 13% of point-mutation drivers, with implications for biomarker assays in cancers where TERT promoter mutation is therapeutically actionable.
    • Germline biomarkers: rare germline MBD4 PTVs and the APOBEC3 22q13.1 deletion are candidate inherited modifiers of somatic mutation landscape that may inform predisposition screening and signature-based therapy stratification.
    • Knowledge-bank thesis: authors argue that delivering precision medicine requires tens-of-thousands-scale knowledge banks with matched clinical outcomes (ICGC-ARGO).

Limitations & open questions

  • 5.3% residual driverless tumors even after technical/biological triage — suggests undiscovered driver elements, especially in chromophobe RCC and pancreatic neuroendocrine, and the authors speculate that aneuploidy alone may suffice in some lineages.
  • Coverage/GC-bias gaps: only ~50% of tumors had ≥90% power at the two TERT promoter hotspots — true non-coding driver prevalence may be higher.
  • Panel-of-normals failure for JAK2 V617F in MPN highlights a general risk of filtering recurrent driver mutations that appear in healthy clonal haematopoiesis.
  • Indel sensitivity of consensus calls is 60% (much lower than SNV); indel-mediated drivers are likely systematically under-counted.
  • Population bias: germline GWAS was performed in European-ancestry donors with independent replication in East Asians; non-European associations may be missed.
  • Cohort imbalance: tumor types vary 50-fold in donor count (1–317), limiting per-type discovery power for rare drivers and complicating cross-type comparisons.
  • Sample-collection era: PCAWG was largely assembled from primary, treatment-naive ICGC/TCGA cases — does not address treated/resistant or metastatic biology except via the 173 metastasis subset.
  • Open question: how heritable patterns of clustered mutational processes (chromothripsis, kataegis, chromoplexy) map onto specific cell-cycle/repair defects across lineages.
