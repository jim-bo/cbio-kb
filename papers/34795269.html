HER2 + breast cancers evade anti-HER2 therapy via a switch in driver pathway

Author

Smith AE, Ferraro E, Safonov A, Bernado Morales C, Arenas Lahuerta EJ, Li Q, Kulick A, Ross D, Solit DB, de Stanchina E, Reis-Filho J, Rosen N, Arribas J, Razavi P, Chandarlapaty S

Doi

10.1038/s41467-021-27093-y

PMID: 34795269 · DOI: 10.1038/s41467-021-27093-y · Journal: Nature Communications (2021)

TL;DR

Smith et al. sequenced 733 ERBB2-amplified primary and metastatic breast cancers (BRCA) from Memorial Sloan Kettering (brca_mapk_hp_msk_2021) using MSK-IMPACT and identified enrichment of somatic MAPK pathway alterations — most commonly biallelic NF1 loss (8% of metastatic tumors) and activating ERBB2 mutations (7% of metastatic tumors) — in tumors that progressed rapidly on anti-HER2 therapy. In 145 patients receiving first-line trastuzumab/pertuzumab-based therapy, MAPK-altered tumors had median PFS of 9.9 months vs 21 months for MAPK-WT (HR 2.03, 95% CI 1.18–3.51; p=0.011 multivariate). Functional studies in cell lines and a HER2+/NF1-null patient-derived xenograft show these mutations cause a switch in driver pathway from AKT to MEK/ERK with marked sensitization to trametinib (MEK inhibitor), mediated by MEK-dependent activation of CDK2 kinase.

Cohort & data

  • 733 ERBB2-amplified breast tumors from 664 patients with HER2+ metastatic breast cancer (BRCA), prospectively sequenced April 2014–February 2021 at MSKCC under IRB protocol NCT01775072 (PMID:34795269).
  • Dataset: brca_mapk_hp_msk_2021 (cBioPortal study, 385 metastatic + 348 primary samples).
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT hybridization capture NGS (341–505 cancer-associated genes; average coverage 663X). Copy number called with FACETS; mutations annotated with OncoKB.
  • Survival cohort: 145 patients receiving first-line docetaxel + trastuzumab + pertuzumab (THP) with pre-treatment tumor sequencing.
  • Preclinical models: SKBR3, BT-474, MDA-MB-361, HCC1954 cell lines (shRNA, CRISPR/Cas9 NF1 depletion); BT-474 subcutaneous xenografts; a HER2+/NF1-null patient-derived xenograft from a trastuzumab-refractory patient.

Key findings

  • MAPK alterations enriched in metastatic HER2+ disease (PMID:34795269, Fig. 1a-b): NF1 loss in 8% of metastatic vs 4% of primary tumors; ERBB2 activating mutations in 7% vs 3%; EGFR 0.9% vs 1%; KRAS, BRAF, MAP2K1 each ≤0.3%. Enrichment significant (p=0.020, two-sided Fisher exact).
  • MAPK alterations predict poor PFS on first-line anti-HER2 therapy (n=145): median PFS 9.9 months (95% CI 5.5–17, n=23 MAPK-altered) vs 21 months (95% CI 17–30, n=122 MAPK-WT); HR 2.03 (1.18–3.51), univariate log-rank p=0.023, multivariate p=0.011. After adjusting for PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN alterations: HR 2.25 (1.29–3.93), p=0.0043 — independent of PI3K pathway lesions.
  • PI3K/AKT pathway co-alterations in the cohort: PIK3CA 30%, PTEN 2.6%, AKT1 0.3%; no mutual exclusivity with MAPK alterations (OR 1.20 [0.74–1.92], p=0.42).
  • Case example (Fig. 1d-e): de novo metastatic HER2+ invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC) patient acquired an NF1 intragenic inversion (c.1527+970:NF1_chr17:g.57371683inv) at lymph-node progression after 31.5 months of THP/HP; the rearrangement bisects NF1 across the RAS-GTPase domain.
  • NF1 loss confers anti-HER2 resistance in vitro: shNF1 and CRISPR/Cas9 NF1-knockout SKBR3, BT-474, MDA-MB-361, HCC1954 cells are resistant to 500 nM lapatinib, 50 nM neratinib, and 100 nM tucatinib. Re-expression of NF1 in HER2i-resistant SKBR3 restores HER2i sensitivity.
  • ERBB2 L755S, BRAF V600E, KRAS G12V expressed in SKBR3 each phenocopy NF1 loss in driving resistance to lapatinib and tucatinib (Fig. 2f).
  • Driver pathway switch from AKT to MEK/ERK: in shNF1 HER2i-R SKBR3, trametinib IC50 falls from 738 nM (control) to 13 nM (~57-fold sensitization); ERK inhibitor SCH772984 IC50 from 2,851 to 250 nM. AKT inhibitor MK-2206 IC50 rises from 197 to 4,475 nM (~23-fold resistance). KRAS G12V and HER2 L755S expressing cells similarly sensitized to MEK inhibition (IC50 16–24 nM).
  • In vivo MEK sensitivity: BT-474 shNF1 HER2i-R xenografts show 37% growth inhibition with 1 mg/kg trametinib (p=0.0059); shRenilla control xenografts unaffected. A HER2+/NF1-null PDX shows 70% growth inhibition with trametinib monotherapy vs no effect of trastuzumab (p<0.001).
  • Mechanism — MEK-dependent CDK2 activation: in shNF1 HER2i-R cells, the G1/S checkpoint is regulated by MEK rather than AKT; MEK inhibition reduces pRb, cyclin E2/A2/D1, and induces p27. CDK2 immunoprecipitation kinase assays show MEK inhibition reduces CDK2 activity by ~80% in resistant cells with 6-fold induction of p27, while control cells are unaffected.
  • CDK2 not CDK4/6 mediates resistance: shNF1 HER2i-R cells are resistant to CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib IC50 292→857 nM; abemaciclib/LY-2835219 2,816→2,981 nM) but sensitive to selective CDK2 inhibition (MK-8776 IC50 7,883→597 nM) and CDK2/4/6 inhibition (PF-06873600 IC50 45→15 nM). CCNE2 shRNA knockdown phenocopies CDK2 inhibition in restoring HER2i sensitivity.

Genes & alterations

  • ERBB2 — Amplification is the cohort entry criterion (all 733 tumors). Activating mutations (e.g., L755S) in 7% of metastatic vs 3% of primary samples drive anti-HER2 resistance via MAPK reactivation.
  • NF1 — Biallelic loss-of-function (truncating mutations, deep deletions, intragenic inversions) in 8% of metastatic vs 4% of primary tumors; most common MAPK alteration; sufficient to confer resistance to lapatinib/neratinib/tucatinib and MEK hypersensitivity.
  • KRAS — Activating mutations (e.g., G12V) in 0.3% of both groups; G12V phenocopies NF1 loss in driving HER2i resistance with MEK sensitization.
  • BRAF — V600E in 0.3% of both groups; expressed BRAF V600E drives HER2i resistance in SKBR3.
  • EGFR — Alterations in 0.9% of metastatic, 1% of primary.
  • MAP2K1 — Activating mutations in 0.3% of metastatic samples (0% in primary).
  • PIK3CA, PTEN, AKT1 — Co-occurring PI3K-pathway alterations (30%, 2.6%, 0.3%); MAPK resistance signal is independent of these.
  • CDK2 — Effector of MEK-driven resistance; targetable in MAPK-activated HER2+ disease.
  • CCNE2 — Cyclin E2 knockdown rescues anti-HER2 sensitivity in shNF1 HER2i-R cells, confirming CDK2/cyclin E axis as the proximal cell-cycle driver.

Clinical implications

  • Resistance biomarker: pre-treatment MAPK-pathway alterations on MSK-IMPACT (NF1 LoF, ERBB2 activating, KRAS/BRAF/MAP2K1 activating) predict ~2-fold worse PFS on first-line anti-HER2 therapy; effect is independent of PI3K-pathway status.
  • Therapeutic vulnerability: MAPK-altered HER2+ tumors are profoundly sensitized to MEK inhibition (trametinib) in vitro and in vivo, suggesting MEK/ERK inhibitor addition to anti-HER2 regimens for patients with MAPK pathway alterations who have progressed on standard therapy.
  • CDK2 over CDK4/6: HER2+ tumors with MAPK activation acquire CDK4/6-inhibitor resistance but become CDK2-dependent — the authors speculate CDK2 inhibition may offer greater benefit than palbociclib/ribociclib/abemaciclib in treatment-refractory HER2+ disease.
  • Sequencing recommendation: The data support pre- and post-progression genomic profiling to identify acquired MAPK lesions and stratify candidates for MEK or CDK2 inhibitor-based combinations.

Limitations & open questions

  • Single-institution cohort: 145-patient PFS analysis is restricted to MSKCC patients on first-line THP; external validation in independent cohorts pending.
  • Rare individual alterations: BRAF, KRAS, MAP2K1, EGFR each ≤1%; aggregated MAPK signal is driven primarily by NF1 loss and ERBB2 mutations.
  • MEK/ERK inhibitor clinical efficacy unproven: preclinical sensitization is striking but no prospective clinical trial of MEK inhibition in MAPK-altered HER2+ breast cancer is reported here.
  • Metastatic phenotype causation: whether MAPK activation downstream of HER2 also promotes metastatic progression itself, independent of treatment resistance, is unresolved.
  • CDK2 inhibitor maturity: selective CDK2 inhibitors (e.g., PF-06873600) are early-phase agents; clinical translation of the CDK2-dependence finding awaits durable, selective drugs.
  • Sample type adjustment: multivariate model adjusts for sample type (metastatic vs primary) and hormone receptor status — residual confounding from treatment history before sequencing is possible.
