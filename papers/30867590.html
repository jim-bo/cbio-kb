TL;DR

Rueda et al. developed a non-homogeneous semi-Markov multistate model that follows breast cancer patients through loco-regional recurrence (LR), distant recurrence (DR) and cancer death while accounting for competing risks. Applied to 3,240 patients (median 14-year follow-up among survivors), including 1,980 from METABRIC with copy-number and expression data, the model recovers the 11 integrative (IntClust) subtypes and shows that four ER+/HER2− subgroups (IntClust 1, 2, 6, 9) — together 26% of ER+ tumours — have a 42–56% probability of relapse persisting up to 20 years post-diagnosis, each defined by a characteristic copy-number driver (e.g., CCND1/RSF1 at 11q13, ZNF703/FGFR1 at 8p12, RPS6KB1 at 17q23, MYC at 8q24). The integrative subtypes add prognostic information beyond IHC and standard clinical covariates for late distant relapse in ER+/HER2− disease.

Cohort & data

  • Full Dataset (FD): 3,240 breast cancer patients (15,394 state transitions from 3,147 patients) diagnosed 1977–2005 in five UK/Canada tumour banks, median follow-up 9.77 years overall and 14 years among survivors.
  • Molecular Dataset (MD): 1,980 patients (9,512 transitions from 1,962 patients) from the brca_metabric cohort with IHC subtype (ER/HER2), PAM50 intrinsic subtype, and IntClust integrative subtype assignments.
  • Recurrent event Dataset (RD): 618 of 1,079 FD relapsers with complete dates of every metastatic site, enabling spatio-temporal metastasis analysis.
  • External validation metacohort: 1,380 breast cancer patients from eight cohorts, median 8-year follow-up.
  • Cancer type: invasive breast carcinoma (BRCA). Genomic data are copy-number and gene-expression arrays previously published as part of METABRIC; raw data deposited at EGA accession EGAS00000000083.

Key findings

  • Bias in disease-specific death estimates. Treating non-cancer deaths as censored overestimates DSD in ER+ tumours (0.46 vs 0.37 at 20 years) because the diagnosis age is higher (median 63.9 vs 53.0 years; p < 1×10⁻⁶); a proper competing-risks framework is required.
  • Four late-recurring ER+/HER2− IntClust subgroups. IntClust 1, 2, 6 and 9 together comprise 26% of ER+ cases and carry mean 20-year relapse probabilities of 0.42–0.56. IntClust2 has the worst prognosis (0.56, SE 0.02), second only to the HER2-enriched IntClust5 (0.65, SE 0.04 at 20 years).
  • TNBC splits into two prognostic groups. Among ER−/HER2− tumours, IntClust10 (Basal-like enriched) has most relapses within the first 5 years (probability 0.33 at 5 years, only 0.37 at 20 years), whereas IntClust4ER− shows persistent late risk (0.30 at 5 years rising to 0.49 at 20 years).
  • IntClust improves late-relapse prediction over IHC + clinical variables in ER+/HER2− patients relapse-free at 5 years. C-index for clinical + IHC: 0.63 (10 y) / 0.62 (15 y) / 0.61 (20 y); adding IntClust: 0.70 (P = 0.00011) / 0.67 (P = 0.0016) / 0.66 (P = 0.0017). Trends replicated in the external validation metacohort.
  • Metastatic patterns. ER− cases had more visceral disease (brain/meningeal 27% vs 11%; pulmonary 50% vs 41%); ER+ cases had more bone metastases by proportion (71% vs 43%) and more often presented with first metastasis in bone (76% vs 61%), but cumulative incidence of bone metastasis was similar because ER− patients tend to die of other metastases first.
  • Subtype dictates metastasis pace. ER− patients suffered rapid sequential relapses early; ER+ patients typically had a single early relapse (often bone), but after a second relapse the rate of further relapses increased.
  • Most cancer deaths follow distant metastasis (83% in ER+, 87% in ER−); brain metastasis is the single most predictive site for death after recurrence.

Genes & alterations

  • CCND1, FGF3, EMSY, PAK1, RSF1 — co-amplified on chromosome 11q13; defines IntClust2 (4.5% of ER+ cases). 96% of IntClust2 tumours have RSF1 amplification vs 0–22% of other IntClust groups.
  • ZNF703, FGFR1 — focal 8p12 amplification defines IntClust6 (5.5% of ER+ tumours); 100% of IntClust6 cases vs 2–21% of others.
  • RPS6KB1 (S6K1, mTOR effector) — 17q23 gain/amplification defines IntClust1 (8% of ER+ tumours); gained or amplified in 96% / 70% of IntClust1 cases vs 0–25% amplification in other groups.
  • MYC — 8q24 amplification defines IntClust9 (8% of ER+ cases); amplified in 89% of IntClust9 tumours vs 3–42% of other groups.
  • ERBB2 (HER2) — central to the IHC stratification (ER+/HER2+, ER+/HER2−, ER−/HER2+, ER−/HER2−); IntClust5 is HER2-amplification enriched and has the highest overall relapse probability (0.65 at 20 years).
  • ESR1 (ER) — clinical ER status drives the divergent recurrence trajectories that motivate the analysis; the late-recurring IntClust 1/2/6/9 subgroups are defined within ER+/HER2− disease.

Clinical implications

  • Stratifying ER+/HER2− patients for extended monitoring and therapy. The 26% of ER+ patients in IntClust 1, 2, 6 or 9 carry a chronic relapse risk to 20 years; the authors propose extended endocrine therapy or other interventions as candidates for these subgroups specifically.
  • Targetable copy-number drivers. The authors note that the genomic drivers of the late-recurring subgroups (e.g. CDK4/6 axis via CCND1, FGFR1, mTOR via RPS6KB1) are viable therapeutic targets, motivating biomarker-driven trial design — though the paper itself does not test any specific therapy.
  • TNBC subgroup definition. Patients with IntClust10 TNBC who are disease-free at 5 years have a substantially lower late-relapse risk than IntClust4ER− TNBC; integrative subtyping could refine surveillance intensity within TNBC.
  • Web-based recurrence calculator. A patient-level recurrence predictor is provided at https://caldaslab.cruk.cam.ac.uk/brcarepred for use throughout follow-up (in contrast to PREDICT, which is fixed at diagnosis).

Limitations & open questions

  • Tumour-bank cohort, not a trial. Patients were diagnosed 1977–2005 across five tumour banks; tumour grade and other clinical variables were not centrally reviewed, introducing variability.
  • Treatment heterogeneity not modelled. Trastuzumab was not in widespread use during the cohort era, and the impact of specific adjuvant therapies on IntClust trajectories is not assessed; the authors call out that determining whether specific treatments differentially benefit IntClust subtypes will require randomised trial cohorts.
  • External validation underpowered for 20-year endpoints. The metacohort (n = 1,380; median 8-year follow-up) reproduces qualitative trends but cannot test 20-year C-index claims directly.
  • No prospective interventional data. Extended endocrine therapy and targeted treatment of copy-number drivers in late-recurring IntClust subgroups remain hypothesised but untested in this study.
  • Model simplifications. Continuous covariates (age, size, lymph-node count) were modelled linearly rather than with splines as in prior METABRIC analyses; lymph nodes are capped at 10 to prevent leverage from extreme values.
