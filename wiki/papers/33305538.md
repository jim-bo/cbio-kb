**PMID:** [33305538](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33305538/) · **DOI:** 10.1002/acm2.13121 · **Journal:** Journal of Applied Clinical Medical Physics (2021)

## TL;DR

A systematic review of 111 peer-reviewed deep learning studies (through February 2020) on inter- and intra-modality medical image synthesis with applications in radiology and radiation oncology. The authors categorize methods into three architectural families — auto-encoder (AE), U-net, and generative adversarial network (GAN, including CycleGAN) — and survey their use for MR-to-CT synthesis (for MR-only radiotherapy planning and PET attenuation correction), CT-to-MR, CBCT-to-CT, PET attenuation correction from non-attenuation-corrected PET, low-dose CT restoration, low-count PET restoration, and intra-modality MR synthesis (e.g., 3T-to-7T, undersampled k-space recovery). No cancer genomic data is analyzed; cohorts are organized by anatomical treatment site (brain, prostate, head/neck, liver, lung, breast, pelvis). This is an out-of-scope paper for cBioPortal genomics and is included for radiation-oncology workflow context.

## Cohort & data

- **Type:** Systematic literature review — not a primary cohort study. No patients enrolled by the authors.
- **Corpus:** 111 peer-reviewed journal articles (PubMed search; query in title/abstract: `("pseudo" OR "synth*" OR "reconstruct*" OR "transform" OR "restor*" OR "correct*" OR "generat*") AND "deep" AND "learning" AND ("CT" OR "MR" OR "MRI" OR "PET" OR "SPECT" OR "Ultrasound")`). 681 initial records, manually screened to 70; citation search added 41 more.
- **Earliest study:** 2017. Publication count growing roughly linearly at ~25 articles/year through 2019; first 2 months of 2020 alone surpassed all of 2017.
- **Network distribution:** U-net and GAN studies dominate (~90% combined); AE-only is a minority. 3 of 111 used unsupervised image-translation from unpaired datasets; the remainder are supervised.
- **Anatomical sites covered across the reviewed primary studies:** [BRAIN](../cancer_types/BRAIN.md), [PROSTATE](../cancer_types/PROSTATE.md), [HEAD_NECK](../cancer_types/HEAD_NECK.md), [LIVER](../cancer_types/LIVER.md), [LUNG](../cancer_types/LUNG.md), [BREAST](../cancer_types/BREAST.md), pelvis (mixed prostate/rectum/cervix), abdomen, and whole-body PET.
- **Patient counts in reviewed studies:** typically dozens of patients per training set; hundreds is considered "large"; N-fold or leave-N-out cross-validation dominates due to small sample sizes.

## Key findings

- **Three method families:** AE → U-net → GAN form a hierarchy of increasing complexity. U-net and GAN studies are close in number and together account for ~90% of the 111 articles. GAN use is rising faster than U-net since 2017.
- **MR-to-CT synthesis for radiation therapy (Table 1 of paper):** mean [absolute](../methods/absolute.md) error (MAE) within patient body typically ranges 40–70 HU; some soft-tissue MAE <40 HU. Reported dose differences from synthetic-CT plans vs ground-truth CT plans are typically <1% for photon plans; <1.6% for pelvis prostate (Maspero et al., 2018); PTV V95 difference <0.6% (Largent et al., 2019); PTV D95 difference <1% (Olberg et al., 2019, breast).
- **MR-to-CT synthesis for PET attenuation correction (Table 2):** learning-based methods reduced PET bias to ~2% vs ~5% bias with conventional CT-synthesis methods, an improvement attributable to better bone reconstruction (bone has the highest attenuation due to high density and atomic number).
- **MR-CT inter-modality registration via synthetic CT (Table 3):** McKenzie et al. (2019) reduced average landmark error from 9.8±3.1 mm (direct MR-CT deformable registration) to 6.0±2.1 mm (CycleGAN synthetic-CT as bridge) in head/neck.
- **CycleGAN tolerates training-pair misalignment** via cycle-consistency loss, which is attractive for inter-modality synthesis where paired MR/CT registration is imperfect (especially in pelvis/abdomen due to bladder/rectum filling and respiratory motion).
- **Bone is the hardest tissue to synthesize from MR** because bone and air both appear dark on conventional T1/T2 MRI. UTE and ZTE MR sequences improve bone contrast but lack soft-tissue diagnostic value and are slow to acquire. Multi-channel input (Dixon water/fat + UTE/ZTE; or T1+T2+post-contrast T1) consistently outperforms single-channel input.
- **CBCT-to-CT synthesis (Table 5 in paper):** CycleGAN-based synthetic CTs from on-board cone-beam CT recover Hounsfield-unit accuracy sufficient for adaptive radiotherapy dose recalculation; reported MAE ranges ~40–110 HU depending on site.
- **PET-to-CT synthesis (Table 6):** synthetic CT generated directly from non-attenuation-corrected PET enables PET attenuation correction without an explicit CT scan; bias generally <5% across VOIs.
- **Low-dose CT restoration (Table 7):** learning-based methods restore PSNR to ~36–44 dB at 1/4 dose; preserved fine texture better than total-variation (TV) iterative methods which tend to over-smooth. Double-blinded reader studies validated learning-based restoration in two studies (Yang et al., 2018; Shan et al., 2019).
- **Low-count PET restoration (Table 10):** GAN/CycleGAN can restore full-count PET from as little as 1/100 of full counts (Chen et al., 2019, brain; Ouyang et al., 2019); PSNR ~24–41 dB depending on count fraction and site. Adding MR as a second input channel improved a clinical uptake-status reading accuracy from 83% (PET-only) to 89% (PET+MR) (Chen et al., 2019).
- **Intra-modality MR synthesis (Table 8):** includes 3T-to-7T MR (PSNR ~27–28 dB), low-to-high resolution, T1↔T2 cross-contrast, undersampled k-space recovery (SSIM 0.82 at 8-fold undersampling), and synthesis of DTI from fMRI, FLAIR from multi-parametric MRI, arterial spin labeling from T1w, and MR angiography from 3D-QALAS.
- **Inference is fast:** trained models predict synthetic images in seconds-to-minutes, vs minutes-to-hours for iterative model-based or compressed-sensing methods.

## Genes & alterations

No gene-specific analyses were performed in this review. The paper does not discuss cancer genomics, mutations, expression, or any HUGO-symbol-level molecular content. Cancer sites are referenced only as anatomical contexts for imaging-based treatment planning.

## Clinical implications

- **MR-only radiotherapy planning** is the dominant clinical motivation for MR-to-CT synthesis: it would eliminate the duplicate CT simulation, reduce patient ionizing-radiation exposure (especially for re-simulation), and remove the CT-MR fusion registration step that introduces systematic positioning error. MR-linac platforms further motivate exclusive MR-based planning.
- **PET/MR systems** require synthetic CT (or equivalent attenuation map) for photon attenuation correction at 511 keV, since MR signal derives from hydrogen nuclei and cannot directly yield electron-density / linear-attenuation coefficients. Learning-based synthetic CT reduced PET bias from ~5% (conventional) to ~2% across selected VOIs in the reviewed studies.
- **Adaptive radiotherapy with CBCT** benefits from synthetic-CT generation that corrects the scatter/artifact deficiencies of on-board cone-beam CT, enabling daily dose re-estimation and patient-positioning verification.
- **Dose reduction** in CT (low-mAs or sparse-view) and PET (low-count for pediatric imaging or for response evaluation) becomes safer when learning-based restoration recovers diagnostic-quality images from reduced acquisitions.
- **Proton therapy** is more sensitive to synthetic-CT HU errors than photon therapy because of the sharp Bragg-peak gradient: range differences of 0.19 cm (mean, liver, n=21) and 0.23 cm (mean, prostate, n=17) were reported (Liu et al., 2019).
- **Dosimetric improvement over conventional methods is small (~1%)** for VMAT photon plans and may lack clinical relevance, but matters in SRS/SBRT where small volumes receive very high dose and in proton therapy.
- **No molecular biomarker, prognostic, or therapeutic claim is made**; this paper is methodological/technical.

## Limitations & open questions

- **Small sample sizes:** most reviewed studies use dozens of patients with N-fold cross-validation; lack of independent unseen test sets limits generalizability claims.
- **Single-institution, single-scanner training:** most studies trained on data from one center; model robustness across hardware vendors, MR field strengths (1.5T vs 3T vs 0.35T MR-linac), and acquisition protocols is largely unvalidated. Boni et al. (2020) is one proof-of-concept of leave-one-center-out training.
- **Exclusion of unusual cases:** training datasets typically exclude images with severe artifacts (hip prostheses, medical implants), obesity, or anatomic abnormalities. The effect of these on synthesis quality has not been characterized but is clinically relevant.
- **Misalignment in paired training data:** MR-CT registration errors propagate into pixel-wise loss training and into evaluation MAE. CycleGAN partially mitigates via cycle-consistency loss; Kazemifar et al. proposed mutual-information loss to bypass registration entirely.
- **2D vs 3D models:** GPU memory limits forced many studies to 2D slices, producing inter-slice discontinuities. 3D models give smaller MAE and better bone reconstruction but need substantially more training data.
- **Lack of standardized benchmarks:** studies use different datasets, sites, MR sequences, and metrics, so head-to-head method comparison is rare and a "best" architecture cannot be declared.
- **Quality assurance for clinical deployment:** no QA workflows yet exist to flag unpredictable synthetic-image failures for out-of-distribution inputs.
- **Bone synthesis from MR remains the dominant error source** for both radiotherapy dose calculation and PET attenuation correction. UTE/ZTE help but trade off acquisition time and soft-tissue diagnostic value.
- **Evaluation reporting:** mean±SD obscures rare local failures; the authors argue for full distribution plots or ranges.
- **Out-of-scope for cBioPortal:** this is a radiation-oncology imaging review with no genomic data, no cBioPortal cohort, no mutation calling, no biomarker association. It is retained in the corpus for cross-disciplinary radiation-oncology context only.
