TL;DR

Kim, Hu, and colleagues at Memorial Sloan Kettering report the first systematic collection of patient-derived xenograft (PDX) and patient-derived cell line (PDC) models for upper tract urothelial carcinoma (UTUC). Starting from 34 UTUC tumors collected at radical nephroureterectomy or metastasectomy, they established 17 PDX models (50% take rate) and 6 PDC models (25% take rate), all profiled by MSK-IMPACT and benchmarked against the matched patient tumors. The PDX models recapitulated the genomic landscape, mutational signatures, histology, and chemosensitivity of the source tumors. Using a HER2 S310F-mutant PDX/PDC pair (UCC14), the authors show that HER2-mutant UTUC is largely refractory to the kinase inhibitor neratinib but exquisitely sensitive to the antibody–drug conjugate trastuzumab deruxtecan (DS-8201a), providing a mechanistic rationale for prioritizing ADCs over HER kinase inhibitors in HER2-mutant urothelial cancer.

Cohort & data

  • Genomic cohort: 119 UTUC patients sequenced by msk-impact-panel (37 prospective + 82 retrospective from Sfakianos 2015/Audenet et al.); 118 nephroureterectomies and 1 metastasectomy.
  • Transcriptomic cohort: rna-seq on 80 of the 119 UTUC tumors.
  • Model cohort: 34 surgical UTUC specimens entered the PDX pipeline; 17 patient-derived-xenograft models established (50%) and 6 PDC models (25%, 6/24). Three of three distant/lymph-node metastatic specimens (UCC03 abdominal wall, UCC11 liver, UCC32 lymph node) engrafted; the lone endoscopic biopsy (UCC27) did not.
  • Cancer types: UTUC, with comparative reference to BLCA.
  • Datasets: Released through cBioPortal as utuc_msk_2019 (patient tumors) and utuc_pdx_msk_2019 (PDX/PDC). RNA-seq deposited at GEO GSE134292.
  • Assays: MSK-IMPACT (300/341/410/468-gene versions), whole-exome-seq on UCC03 and UCC30, immunohistochemistry for MMR proteins, masson-trichrome-staining and Picro Sirius red for ECM characterization.

Key findings

  • Mutation landscape in 119 UTUC. Recurrent actionable drivers: FGFR3 47–48%, KDM6A 38%, KMT2D 26%, TERT 26%, TP53 25%, STAG2 22%, ARID1A 20%, CREBBP 20%, HRAS 15% (paper text 12%), PIK3CA 14–16%, TSC1 14%, ERBB2 8–9%, KRAS 8%. 39.3% of all somatic mutations identified were variants of unknown functional significance.
  • Molecular subtypes (n=80 RNA-seq). BASE47 classifier: 70/80 (87.5%) luminal, 10/80 (12.5%) basal. Consensus MIBC classifier: 66/80 (82.5%) luminal–papillary (including all 14 low-grade tumors), 7 LumU (8.75%), 1 LumNS (1.25%), 1 Stroma-rich (1.25%), 5 Ba/Sq (6.25%). No significant subtype difference between high- and low-grade tumors (P=0.5389, chi-square). Somatic FGFR3 mutations occurred only in the luminal subtype.
  • PDX take rate and predictors. 17/34 PDX (50%) and 6/24 PDC (25%) successfully established. Smoking status differed between engrafters and non-engrafters (P=0.039); muscle-invasive (≥pT2) stage trended toward higher take rate (P=0.166). Three of three distant/nodal metastases engrafted. Transcriptomically, the basal/squamous consensus signature correlated with engraftment (P=0.04); cell-cycle / DNA-replication pathways (SMC1, MCM5, MCM7) were enriched in engrafters and ECM-receptor / focal-adhesion pathways (FN1, THBS2, COMP) in non-engrafters.
  • Genomic concordance tumor↔︎PDX. 72.7% concordance of known/likely oncogenic mutations across 17 tumor/PDX pairs. PDX-specific deep deletions of CDKN2A/CDKN2B appeared in 29% of PDX (selection during engraftment). Only the hypermutated UCC34 PDX dropped below 50% concordance. WES on UCC03 and UCC30 confirmed linear and branched evolution; the AID/APOBEC signature (COSMIC Signatures 2 + 13) was preserved in UCC03 across passages but Signature 2 was lost in the UCC30 PDX.
  • MSI / Lynch enrichment. 4/17 engrafted tumors were MSI-H (MSIsensor ≥10), including three Lynch cases with germline MLH1 (UCC15), MSH2 (UCC36, UCC34) and one (MSH2/MSH6 IHC-negative) without identifiable germline cause (UCC17). All four MSI-H tumors carried TMB 20.3–157.2 mut/Mb; MMR/MSI and aging signatures were stable across passages. The FGFR3 R248C hotspot, previously associated with Lynch-related UTUC, was present in 3/4 MSI-H pairs (UCC17, UCC34, UCC36).
  • Chemosensitivity matches patient. PDX responses mirrored the source patients in all three tested cases. UCC03 PDX (patient: partial response to gemcitabine/carboplatin) was sensitive to Gem/Carbo (P=0.0177) and gemcitabine alone (P=0.0065) but not carboplatin alone (P=0.138). UCC19 PDX (patient: durable response, off therapy 15 months) was highly sensitive to Gem/Carbo (P<0.0001) and carried a truncating RECQL4 F987* mutation. UCC17 (MSI-H, patient: pT3 stable disease after neoadjuvant Gem/Cis, no pathologic response) showed minimal PDX growth inhibition by Gem/Cis (P=0.07); UCC36 (MSI-H Lynch) was similarly resistant.
  • MSI vs neoadjuvant chemotherapy in patients. In a separate UTUC cohort of 20 patients receiving NAC before RNU (2 MSI-H, 1 MSI-indeterminate, 17 MSS), pathologic response (≤pT1N0) occurred in 10/17 MSS (59%) and in 0/2 MSI-H patients.
  • HER2 epidemiology across 44,183 prospectively sequenced tumors. Oncogenic/likely-oncogenic ERBB2 mutations: 10.3% of bladder UC (n=1032) and 5.9% of UTUC (n=204), vs 3.2% breast, 2.9% endometrial, 2.6% colorectal, 2.4% NSCLC. ERBB2 amplification: 6.2% bladder (64/1032) and 4.4% UTUC (9/204).
  • HER2 S310F PDX pharmacology. UCC14-PDC (HER2 S310F UTUC) IC50 for neratinib = 508.3 nM, vs 245.9 nM for ERBB2-WT MGHU3, 56.8 nM for the HER2 S310F cervical line CVX-4, and 0.1 nM for HER2-amplified BT-474. Neratinib suppressed p-ERK but not p-AKT in UCC14-PDC, indicating HER2-independent AKT signaling. In vivo, UCC14-PDX was largely refractory to neratinib (P=0.0034) but strongly inhibited by the HER2 ADC DS-8201a / trastuzumab-deruxtecan (P<0.0001), with the ADC significantly outperforming neratinib (P<0.0001).

Genes & alterations

  • FGFR3 — activating hotspots (incl. R248C, S249C) in 47–48% of UTUC and only in luminal-subtype tumors; favorable prognostic marker per prior literature. R248C enriched in Lynch-syndrome UTUC and seen in 3/4 MSI-H PDX pairs.
  • ERBB2 — oncogenic mutations in 8–9% of UTUC and 10.3% of bladder UC; HER2 S310F (UCC14) used to model HER2-mutant UC pharmacology. ADC sensitivity but kinase-inhibitor resistance demonstrated.
  • HRAS, PIK3CA, TSC1, KRAS — recurrent RTK/RAS/PI3K-pathway drivers consistent with prior UTUC genomics (Sfakianos 2015).
  • TP53, RB1, CDKN2A/CDKN2B — tumor-suppressor losses; CDKN2A/2B showed PDX-specific deep deletions in 29% of models (engraftment selection).
  • TERT promoter mutations — 53% of PDX models; consistent with UC biology.
  • KDM6A, KMT2D, ARID1A, CREBBP, STAG2 — chromatin/cohesin alterations frequent in UTUC; STAG2 mutation positively (and ERBB2 negatively) associated with engraftment, though not statistically significant.
  • MLH1, MSH2, MSH6 — Lynch-syndrome germline/somatic MMR loss; defined the MSI-H UTUC subset (UCC15, UCC17, UCC34, UCC36) with TMB 20.3–157.2 mut/Mb.
  • RECQL4 — truncating F987* in UCC19 patient and PDX; offered as candidate explanation for the durable platinum/gemcitabine response (DNA-repair / homologous recombination role).
  • ERCC2 — included in the recurrent-mutation panel (4%) and cited as a platinum-response biomarker per prior advanced-UC literature.
  • PTEN, EGFR — EGFR cited as a basal-marker gene in the BASE47-defined basal/squamous cluster; PTEN low-frequency (2%).

Clinical implications

  • HER2-mutant urothelial carcinoma. Antibody–drug conjugates (DS-8201a / trastuzumab-deruxtecan, and by extension ado-trastuzumab-emtansine) should be prioritized over HER kinase inhibitors such as neratinib for HER2-mutant UC. Authors propose this rationalizes the modest activity of HER kinase inhibitors in basket trials.
  • MSI-H UTUC and neoadjuvant chemotherapy. MSI-H Lynch-associated UTUC may be relatively chemoresistant (0/2 pathologic responses in MSI-H NAC patients vs 10/17 in MSS; both MSI-H PDX models resistant to gemcitabine/cisplatin). Authors suggest MSI status be used to triage MSI-H UTUC patients toward immune checkpoint blockade rather than cytotoxic NAC.
  • FGFR3 and erdafitinib. Although erdafitinib is FDA-approved for FGFR3-altered metastatic bladder cancer, the authors note that targeted therapy impact in UC has been limited to date and frame UTUC PDX as a platform for testing precision strategies.
  • Predictive biomarker hypothesis: DDR alterations and platinum response. UCC19’s exquisite Gem/Carbo response and truncating RECQL4 mutation are offered (with appropriate caution) as a single-case example consistent with DDR-gene-alteration / platinum-sensitivity associations reported in advanced UC.
  • Resource value. All genomic data released to cBioPortal as utuc_msk_2019 and utuc_pdx_msk_2019; models offered to the community as a shared preclinical resource.

Limitations & open questions

  • Author-acknowledged. Biological characterization of the variants of unknown significance identified in the PDX collection exceeds the capacity of any one lab; the authors explicitly invite community use.
  • Author-acknowledged. Models were derived predominantly from large surgical specimens; PDX generation from ureteroscopic biopsies (the pre-RNU diagnostic setting most relevant for guiding neoadjuvant therapy) remains a goal.
  • Statistical power. Engraftment-predictor analyses (e.g., STAG2/ERBB2 positive/negative engraftment association, basal/squamous correlation) operate on N=17 PDX, so even strong signals are underpowered; STAG2/ERBB2 results were not statistically significant.
  • Cross-paper synthesis. The MSI-H chemoresistance signal (n=2 MSI-H NAC patients, 2 MSI-H PDX) is hypothesis-generating; whether MSI status alone is sufficient to redirect UTUC patients from cytotoxic NAC to immunotherapy will need confirmation in larger neoadjuvant cohorts.
  • Genomic drift in PDX. Recurrent CDKN2A/2B deletion specific to PDX (29%) and loss of the AID/APOBEC Signature 2 in the UCC30 PDX flag selection pressure during engraftment that downstream users should account for when interpreting PDX-only findings.
  • HER2 ADC generalizability. The PDX-vs-ADC efficacy claim rests on a single HER2 S310F model (UCC14) and a single ADC (DS-8201a); generalization across HER2 mutation classes (e.g., kinase-domain mutations, amplifications) is not addressed.
