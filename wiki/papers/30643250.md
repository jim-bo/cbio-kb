## TL;DR

This is a methodological review of three-dimensional (3D) cell culture systems for cancer research. It argues that monolayer cultures fail to recapitulate intra- and inter-tumoral heterogeneity, drug resistance, and tumor microenvironment biology, and surveys four families of 3D models — scaffold-free spheroids/tumorspheres/tumoroids, scaffold-based hydrogels and electrospun/porous scaffolds, 3D bioprinting, and microfluidic platforms including droplet systems and organ-on-a-chip — with worked examples in breast, glioblastoma, osteosarcoma, ovarian, pancreatic, Ewing sarcoma, head and neck, and other cancers ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).

## Key findings

- **Spheroid size limits.** Cancer spheroids rarely exceed 1000 µm diameter, far below human tumor sizes — e.g. colon tumors ≈ 4 cm median across 300,386 patients; osteosarcoma 9.14 cm mean length in ≤14-year-olds (n=140) vs. ≈ 200–1000 µm spheroid diameters with 143B/MG63/MNNG-HOS/SAOS-2/U2OS/SJSA1; 38.2% of 4,265 primary breast tumors ≥ 2 cm vs. ≈ 200–900 µm MCF7 spheroids ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md), Table 1).
- **Hypoxia recapitulation.** 3D models develop hypoxic cores similar to tumors and recapitulate the drug-resistance phenotype observed in patients; monolayers cannot model the spatial oxygen gradient. Phenotypic and genotypic effects of hypoxia can irreversibly yield malignant subclones in tumoroid models (Kumano et al., 2024) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Drug-resistance amplification.** IC50 values typically rise 3–5-fold in spheroids vs. monolayers (e.g. therapeutic peptide testing in breast cancer cell-line spheroids, Cavaco et al., 2021). Co-culture of ovarian cancer cell lines with fibroblasts using liquid overlay (Flörkemeier et al., 2024) increased [cisplatin](../drugs/cisplatin.md) susceptibility, demonstrating stromal modulation of drug response in 3D ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Stemness phenotype emergence in 3D.** Breast cancer spheroids upregulate Nanog and OCT3/4 (Dhandapani et al., 2023); glioblastoma cells in chitosan-hyaluronic porous scaffolds upregulate [CD44](../genes/CD44.md), ABCG2, and [MGMT](../genes/MGMT.md) in a pore-size–dependent manner and show greater [temozolomide](../drugs/temozolomide.md) resistance vs. monolayers (Zhou et al., 2023); gelatin-foam glioblastoma tumoroids resist [irinotecan](../drugs/irinotecan.md) and [fluorouracil](../drugs/fluorouracil.md) but are more sensitive to the hypoxia-activated agent [tirapazamine](../drugs/tirapazamine.md), coincident with HIF-1α upregulation (Yang et al., 2015) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Electrospun PCL scaffolds reveal mechanobiology.** Aligned electrospun poly(ε-caprolactone) fibers induce CAV1-driven F-actin polymerization, focal adhesion formation, and nuclear YAP translocation in breast cancer cells; CAV1 silencing reduces invasion into murine tissue (Li et al., 2024) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **High-throughput droplet microfluidics.** MCF-7 spheroid generation in droplet-based chips achieves >1000 droplets/min at 16–20 Hz with droplet diameters up to 250 µm after 2 weeks (Kwak et al., 2018). U87MG glioblastoma spheroids in droplets dropped viability from 91% to 55% under near-infrared irradiation with nanocomposites, supporting photothermal therapy screening (Lee et al., 2020). Anchored droplet chips screen cisplatin + [etoposide](../drugs/etoposide.md) combinations on A673 Ewing sarcoma spheroids without IC50 bias vs. 96-well plates; synergy is greater when etoposide precedes cisplatin (Fevre et al., 2023) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Organ-on-a-chip vascularization.** A neuroblastoma-on-a-chip model using collagen biomaterial at 36 dynes/cm² (143 µL/min) shear stress supports endothelial–NB co-culture capillary formation, while static cultures produce cell death (Villasante et al., 2024). Skin/skin-cancer-on-a-chip platforms achieve uniform 600 µm penetration of doxorubicin-loaded GelMA microneedles to melanoma cells beneath the epidermis (Barros et al., 2025) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Co-culture in droplets enables immune-tumor study.** Anchored droplet co-cultures of T cells with B16-OVA spheroids show that tumor destruction reflects cooperative cytotoxic T-lymphocyte accumulation at the spheroid edge rather than the sum of individual T-cell activity (Ronteix et al., 2022) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).

## Clinical implications

- **Personalized medicine framing.** The authors position patient-derived tumoroids (notably from 3D bioprinted decellularized scaffolds and porous-scaffold-grown tumoroids) as a potential drug-screening platform for individualized therapy selection, especially for cancers where the tumor microenvironment drives drug response (e.g. pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, osteosarcoma) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Minimal residual disease modeling.** Droplet-based microfluidic encapsulation of OVCAR-5/RFP cells enables transcriptomic characterization of chemotherapy-residual vs. chemotherapy-resistant ovarian-cancer populations, supporting MRD-specific therapeutic strategies (Yang et al., 2025, cited herein) ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Drug-screening readiness.** Anchored droplet chips deliver IC50 measurements consistent with conventional 96-well plates for combination chemotherapy (cisplatin + etoposide) in Ewing sarcoma spheroids, suggesting microfluidic formats are viable for translational drug screening ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Transdermal drug delivery.** Skin-cancer-on-a-chip enables localized [doxorubicin](../drugs/doxorubicin.md) delivery via GelMA microneedles with significantly higher efficiency than diffusion through media — a model for testing transdermal melanoma therapy ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).

## Limitations & open questions

- **No standardized 3D nomenclature.** Terms "spheroid", "tumorsphere", "tumoroid", and "organoid" are inconsistently used across the literature; the authors adopt "tumoroid" for patient-tumor-derived 3D cultures to disambiguate ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Size mismatch with patient tumors.** Spheroids rarely exceed 1000 µm even after 20+ days with serial transfer, limiting extrapolation to clinical disease ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **MatrigelTM batch variability.** Composition is poorly defined (≈1851 proteins identified across batches; growth-factor-reduced batch similarity may be as low as 53% between manufacturers), confounding mechanistic interpretation; PubMed shows a downward trend in MatrigelTM publications since 2013 ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Rotating wall vessel introduces microgravity artifacts.** Simulated microgravity alters cell-cycle progression, cell death, and migration in ways that do not reflect in-vivo shear stress experienced by circulating tumor cells ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Magnetic levitation toxicity.** SPIONs and gold nanoparticles can induce reactive oxygen species, apoptosis, and cytoskeletal perturbations, complicating phenotype interpretation ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Scalability vs. resolution trade-off.** Dynamic suspension techniques generate large spheroid batches (e.g. up to 841 spheroids/run) but make it difficult to track individual spheroids over treatment time ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Bioprinting cost and access.** Bioprinter cost, patient-sample availability, and inability of simple bioinks to fully reproduce the tumor microenvironment limit the throughput and fidelity of bioprinted tumor models ([PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)).
- **Frontmatter mismatch in source record.** The raw extraction lists `study_id: gbm_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018`; this appears to be a stale extractor artifact — the paper makes no use of TCGA or any cBioPortal study and is not a cohort analysis. The PMID also does not align with the publication date (paper published August 2025) and may be misassigned at the source.
