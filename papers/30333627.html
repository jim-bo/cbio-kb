TL;DR

The Beat AML program profiled 672 primary tumor specimens from 562 acute myeloid leukemia (AML) patients using whole-exome sequencing (622 specimens / 531 patients), RNA-sequencing (451 specimens / 411 patients), and a 122-compound ex vivo drug sensitivity assay (409 specimens / 363 patients). Integrating these layers, the authors identified novel mutational events not previously detected in AML, mapped drug-response patterns to single and combinatorial mutational events (e.g. FLT3-ITD/NPM1 co-mutation sensitizes to ibrutinib and entospletinib; BCOR/RUNX1 co-mutation sensitizes to JAK inhibitors), and derived expression signatures predicting response. All data and an interactive Vizome browser (www.vizome.org) were released as the aml_ohsu_2018 cohort.

Cohort & data

  • 672 tumor specimens from 562 unique AML patients (AML), bone marrow aspirate or peripheral blood, collected across OHSU and 9 partner sites (Utah, UT Southwestern, Stanford, Miami, Colorado, Florida, NIH, Fox Chase, KUMC).
  • Whole-exome sequencing: 622 specimens / 531 patients (whole-exome-seq) on Illumina Nextera RapidCapture (37 Mb), aligned with BWA MEM, variants from MuTect v1.1.7, VarScan 2 v2.4.1, and Pindel (FLT3-ITD calls); annotated with VEP v83 and converted via vcf2maf. 11.9-Mb custom-capture validation library applied to 96 specimens (94% SNV / 82% indel validation).
  • RNA-sequencing: 451 specimens / 411 patients (rna-seq) using Agilent SureSelect Strand-Specific kit on HiSeq 2500.
  • Ex vivo drug sensitivity: 409 specimens / 363 patients screened against a panel of 122 small-molecule inhibitors (ex-vivo-drug-sensitivity-screen) within 24 hours of sample isolation; AUC summarized per drug.
  • Data release: dbGaP study ID 30641 / accession phs001657.v1.p1; GDC; interactive Vizome browser. cBioPortal study: aml_ohsu_2018.
  • Reference genome: GRCh37 / b37 (Broad bundle 2.8).

Key findings

  • Mutational landscape comparable to TCGA but with distinct rare events: median 13 somatic variants per patient (range 1–80). The top 33 most commonly mutated genes overlapped with TCGA AML at similar frequencies, except SRSF2 which was significantly more frequent in Beat AML (effect preserved in de novo–only comparison). Among low-frequency variants (<2%), 221 mutant genes were shared, 939 were unique to TCGA, ~1,500 were unique to Beat AML; 11 genes mutated in ≥1% of Beat AML patients had not been observed in prior AML sequencing studies (Fig. 1A–C).
  • Co-occurrence patterns: significant co-segregation of frequent variants suggests biological cooperativity (Fig. 1D).
  • De novo vs. transformed drug response: of 122 drugs tested, 64 were significantly more sensitive in de novo samples (FDR <0.1); only panobinostat (HDAC inhibitor) was significantly more sensitive in transformed cases (Extended Data Fig. 4).
  • Single-gene drug-response associations (Fig. 2A, Volcano plot):
    • FLT3-ITD → sensitive to FLT3-pathway inhibitors (proof-of-principle).
    • TP53 or ASXL1 mutation → broad drug resistance, with exceptions: TP53-mutant cases trended sensitive to elesclomol; ASXL1-mutant to panobinostat.
    • NRAS or KRAS mutation → broad resistance but predicted sensitivity to MAPK inhibitors; NRAS showed stronger MAPK association than KRAS.
    • IDH2 mutation → broad sensitivity; IDH1 mutation → broad resistance.
    • RUNX1 mutation → sensitivity to PIK3C/MTOR inhibitors (e.g. BEZ235) and the VEGFR multi-kinase inhibitor cediranib.
    • Spliceosome mutations U2AF1 and ZRSR2 → novel sensitivity to multiple drugs.
  • Combinatorial mutation analysis for ibrutinib (Fig. 2B): cases with FLT3-ITD alone or any NPM1 mutation (including triple FLT3/NPM1/DNMT3A) were significantly more sensitive than wild type, whereas DNMT3A alone or DNMT3A/FLT3 double-mutants were not different from WT. Entospletinib (selective SYK inhibitor) showed the same FLT3-ITD/NPM1 sensitivity pattern, consistent with prior reports of SYK as an FLT3-ITD interacting target.
  • JAK-inhibitor combinatorial signal (Fig. 2C): co-mutation of BCOR with RUNX1 correlated with sensitivity to all four selective JAK inhibitors on the panel (momelotinib, ruxolitinib, tofacitinib, JAK Inhibitor I), whereas BCOR alone or BCOR/DNMT3A and BCOR/SRSF2 co-mutations did not. BCOR-alone cases instead showed sensitivity to the tankyrase/WNT inhibitor XAV-939 and the multi-kinase inhibitor crizotinib.
  • Expression signatures of drug response: 78 of 119 testable drugs (65.5%) had FDR<0.05 expression signatures distinguishing the most- vs. least-sensitive 20% of samples. A 17-gene expression signature stratified ibrutinib response (Fig. 3).
  • Multivariate WGCNA integration: 14 co-expression gene clusters were identified across the cohort; lasso regression linked specific mutation × expression-cluster combinations to drug response. For ibrutinib, a 345-gene “turquoise” cluster (enriched for immune pathways) co-occurred with FLT3-ITD and predicted sensitivity (overlap with the 17-gene signature: representation factor 13.6, P<1.734e-04), while a 110-gene “magenta” subnetwork predicted resistance and tracked with adverse ELN 2017 risk.

Genes & alterations

  • FLT3 — ITD detected by Pindel and an internal PCR/capillary-electrophoresis assay; FLT3-ITD predicted ibrutinib and entospletinib sensitivity (alone or with NPM1).
  • NPM1 — 4-bp insertion mutations detected by internal PCR assay; any NPM1 mutation sensitized to ibrutinib/entospletinib; consensus calls derived by combining internal PCR with CLIA/CAP lab tests (Sequenome, GeneTrails, Foundation Medicine, Genoptix, Illumina) and exome data.
  • DNMT3A — frequent co-mutation with FLT3-ITD and NPM1; DNMT3A alone was not sufficient to confer ibrutinib sensitivity.
  • TP53 — broad drug resistance with trend toward elesclomol sensitivity.
  • ASXL1 — broad resistance with trend toward panobinostat sensitivity.
  • NRAS, KRAS — predicted MAPK-inhibitor sensitivity; NRAS association stronger than KRAS.
  • IDH1, IDH2 — opposing patterns: IDH1 broad resistance, IDH2 broad sensitivity.
  • RUNX1 — PI3K/mTOR and VEGFR-multikinase sensitivity; co-mutation with BCOR drives JAK-inhibitor sensitivity.
  • BCOR — combinatorial effect: alone sensitizes to XAV-939 and crizotinib; with RUNX1 sensitizes to JAK inhibitors.
  • SRSF2 — significantly more frequent in Beat AML than TCGA.
  • U2AF1, ZRSR2 — novel drug-sensitivity correlations for spliceosome mutations.
  • CEBPA — biallelic mutation status (N-terminal + C-terminal) curated as a favorable-risk feature.
  • TET2, KMT2A, BCL2, EZH2, KDM1A, DOT1L — discussed as known therapeutic-target genes in the AML landscape (hypomethylating agents, venetoclax, DOT1L inhibitors for KMT2A-rearranged disease, LSD1/EZH2 inhibitors).

Clinical implications

  • Co-mutation as a therapy-selection biomarker: FLT3-ITD + NPM1 (regardless of DNMT3A status) defines a subset selectively sensitive to ibrutinib and entospletinib ex vivo, suggesting BTK/SYK pathway dependence in this combinatorial-mutation context.
  • BCOR + RUNX1 co-mutation marks a subgroup with ex vivo sensitivity to JAK inhibitors (momelotinib, ruxolitinib, tofacitinib), implicating JAK-pathway dysregulation as a context-specific vulnerability.
  • Adverse-risk subgroup hypotheses: TP53-mutant AML showed trend sensitivity to elesclomol; ASXL1-mutant to panobinostat — candidate avenues for genotype-matched repurposing in poor-prognosis disease.
  • Risk stratification cross-check: a 110-gene “magenta” expression subnetwork associated with ibrutinib resistance was significantly enriched in adverse ELN 2017–risk patients, linking transcriptomic and clinical-risk frameworks.
  • Data resource: the Beat AML cohort (Vizome / dbGaP phs001657 / cBioPortal aml_ohsu_2018) provides a public reference for biomarker-driven trial design and hypothesis generation in AML.

Limitations & open questions

  • The drug-response readout is an ex vivo AUC assay, not an in vivo or clinical-response endpoint; combinatorial sensitivity signals (e.g. FLT3-ITD/NPM1 → ibrutinib, BCOR/RUNX1 → JAK inhibitors) require clinical validation.
  • Mechanisms underlying novel sensitivity correlations (U2AF1, ZRSR2, IDH2, RUNX1, BCOR/RUNX1) “merit further investigation”; the paper does not establish causality.
  • Ibrutinib is described as having “broad off-target effects when maintained in continuous culture with target cells,” so single-drug ex vivo response should be interpreted cautiously.
  • Comparisons of low-frequency variants (~1,500 unique to Beat AML, 939 unique to TCGA) may reflect cohort composition (de novo vs. transformed/secondary AML), sequencing-pipeline differences, and lift-over from NCBI36→GRCh37, not biology alone.
  • 110 patients had multiple specimens (672 specimens / 562 patients), which the analyses do not always disentangle at the patient level.
  • Multivariate WGCNA + lasso model is correlative and observational; the 14 co-expression clusters and their drug associations are hypothesis-generating.
