**PMID:** 29902176 · **DOI:** 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1006144 · **Journal:** PLOS Computational Biology (2018)

## TL;DR

This is an introductory perspective (not primary cancer-genomics research) on cloud computing for biomedical science. The authors survey cloud deployment models (public, private, community, hybrid), service models (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS), and catalog representative biomedical workloads — sequence alignment, NGS pipelines, TCGA/PCAWG-scale analyses, ECG telemonitoring, and medical-image archiving — that have moved to cloud platforms. They argue for a FAIR-aligned, commons-style ecosystem of interoperable clouds with shared APIs, permanent IDs, data peering, and pay-for-compute, and discuss cost, security, and funding-model frictions for biomedical researchers ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).

## Cohort & data

- **Article type:** Education / Perspective in PLOS Computational Biology, June 2018; no patient cohort and no new analyses ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Cancer-relevant case examples cited** (third-party work, not new data here):
  - **PCAWG** — common patterns of mutation studied across **>2,800 cancer whole-genome sequences**; data-coordinating center used AWS and the Cancer Collaboratory (Ontario Institute for Cancer Research / Compute Canada), with 500 of 1,827 samples analyzed in AWS in ~6 weeks while academic resources took >6 months for the rest ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
  - **TCGA** — NCI funded three Cancer Genomics Cloud pilots (Broad Institute FireCloud on Google Cloud; ISB-CGC on GCP; Seven Bridges Genomics CGC on AWS), all loaded with harmonized reference data from the Genomic Data Commons aligned to **GRCh38** ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
  - **[AML](../cancer_types/AML.md) resequencing** via the Bionimbus Protected Data Cloud — referenced as an example of cloud-enabled somatic-variant discovery in adverse-risk primary AML, citing the [CUX1](../genes/CUX1.md) haploinsufficient tumor-suppressor paper (McNerney et al., Blood 2013, PMID:23212519; not in this corpus) ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).

## Key findings

- **Cloud deployment is faster than on-prem for elastic workloads.** In the PCAWG project, 500 cancer whole genomes were analyzed on AWS in ~6 weeks, while 1,827 samples on multi-institution academic resources took >6 months; AWS S3 storage was scaled from 600 TB to multiple [PB](../cancer_types/PB.md) on demand ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Three NCI cancer-genomics cloud pilots are operational** (Broad FireCloud on GCP, ISB-CGC on GCP, Seven Bridges CGC on AWS), all consuming GDC-harmonized reference data and exposing >200 tools/workflows for TCGA reanalysis on Common Workflow Language and WDL ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Cost structure differs fundamentally from on-prem.** Cloud is pay-as-you-go in small time increments, while on-prem hardware is amortized over 3–5 years. A reported transcript-quantification benchmark on ISB-CGC across >12,000 RNA-seq samples found per-sample cost on pre-emptible nodes was less than half the cluster-based cost ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Egress and "lock-in" economics matter.** Upload is typically free, but download costs and overhead-charging policies push the field toward commons-style co-located compute+data, rather than per-lab download-and-analyze ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Security in public cloud is shared responsibility.** Authors point to FedRAMP, FISMA, NIST 800-53, and FIPS as baseline frameworks, and note that healthcare publications often conflate "cloud" with "VM" or "web tool" without meeting NIST cloud definitions ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **FAIR + commons + containers + workflow languages** (Docker, WDL, CWL) are framed as the emerging stack for reproducible cloud-based biomedical analysis ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).

## Clinical implications

- This paper is a perspective/infrastructure piece and makes **no direct clinical biomarker, treatment, or prognostic claim**. Healthcare-adjacent examples it surveys — real-time ECG telemonitoring on AWS EC2, Azure-hosted 12-lead ECG telemedicine, and cloud-hosted PACS for medical imaging — are presented as deployment patterns rather than evaluated clinical interventions ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- For clinical NGS labs, the authors note that CloudMan on AWS has been used as a distribution platform for genetic-variant analysis with reduced storage costs in clinical workflows, but the present paper does not assess diagnostic performance ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).

## Limitations & open questions

- **Not primary research.** No cohort, no new dataset, no analytical results — claims about runtime/cost benefits are inherited from cited third-party reports and not independently reproduced here ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Cost comparisons are weak.** Authors explicitly state that cloud-vs-on-prem cost comparisons are "often difficult to perform effectively due to the lack of good data for actual local costs," and that institutional-overhead policies on cloud spending distort the comparison ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Long-term data sustainability is unsolved.** The authors flag long-term retention of biomedical data in cloud environments as an unresolved policy and economic problem requiring public–private partnership ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Multi-cloud interoperability is aspirational.** "The idea of environments where multiple public cloud providers are used in a collective ecosystem is still mostly on the horizon" — open questions remain on cross-cloud APIs, data peering, and jurisdictional boundaries on genomic data ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
- **Healthcare-cloud literature is muddled.** The cited scoping review (Griebel et al., 2015) found many "cloud" healthcare publications use the term loosely, conflating it with VMs or web tools ([PMID:29902176](../papers/29902176.md)).
