Genomic Differences Between “Primary” and “Secondary” Muscle-invasive Bladder Cancer as a Basis for Disparate Outcomes to Cisplatin-based Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy

TL;DR

Pietzak et al. compared clinical and genomic features of “primary” versus “secondary” muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) — the latter defined as MIBC arising after prior non–muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC) treatment. In a clinical cohort of 288 MSKCC patients receiving cisplatin-based neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC) followed by radical cystectomy, secondary MIBC patients had markedly lower pathologic response rates (26% vs 45%) and worse recurrence-free, cancer-specific, and overall survival than primary MIBC patients. Genomic analysis of a 385-tumor retrospective discovery cohort (TCGA + MSKCC) and an independent 94-tumor MSK-IMPACT validation cohort showed that likely deleterious ERCC2 missense mutations — a known cisplatin-sensitivity biomarker — were significantly enriched in primary versus secondary MIBC (10.9% vs 1.8% in discovery; 15.7% vs 0% in validation). The authors conclude that secondary MIBC patients derive little benefit from standard cisplatin-based NAC and should be considered for upfront cystectomy or clinical trials.

Cohort & data

  • Clinical cohort: 288 MSKCC patients with clinical stage cT2–4aN0M0 bladder urothelial carcinoma (BLCA) treated with three to four cycles of cisplatin-based NAC followed by radical cystectomy between May 2001 and July 2015 — 245 primary MIBC (85%) and 43 secondary MIBC (15%) PMID:30290956.
  • Retrospective discovery genomic cohort: 385 muscle-invasive (≥T2) chemotherapy-naïve pretreatment specimens (330 primary, 55 secondary) drawn from 981 NGS-sequenced tumors — 412 by whole-exome sequencing through the urothelial TCGA and 569 sequenced at MSKCC by WES or targeted exon capture PMID:30290956.
  • Prospective validation genomic cohort: 94 consecutive chemotherapy-naïve MIBC tumors (70 primary, 24 secondary) prospectively sequenced 2014–2015 on the MSK-IMPACT panel (341- or 410-gene versions) in a CLIA-certified laboratory under MSKCC IRB #12–245 / NCT01775072 PMID:30290956.
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT targeted exon capture across three panel generations covering 300, 341, or 410 cancer-associated genes — IMPACT300, IMPACT341, IMPACT410. Data on FANCC were missing for six patients sequenced using the 300-gene panel PMID:30290956.
  • Dataset: blca_msk_tcga_2020 — combined MSK + TCGA bladder cancer cohort with data publicly available at cbioportal.mskcc.org PMID:30290956.

Key findings

  • Lower pathologic response in secondary MIBC. 26% (11/43) of secondary MIBC vs 45% (110/245) of primary MIBC achieved pathologic response (≤pT1N0) following NAC (univariable p=0.02). Multivariable analysis adjusted for age, sex, and T stage gave OR=0.4 (95% CI 0.18–0.84, p=0.02) PMID:30290956.
  • Worse survival outcomes in secondary MIBC. Secondary MIBC was associated with significantly worse recurrence-free survival (RFS) (univariable p<0.001; multivariable HR=2.10, 95% CI 1.23–3.57, p=0.007), cancer-specific survival (CSS) (univariable p=0.004; multivariable p=0.054), and overall survival (univariable p=0.006; multivariable HR=1.66, 95% CI 1.01–2.73, p=0.048) PMID:30290956.
  • NAC actively harmful in secondary MIBC. In multivariable analysis, NAC was associated with improved RFS vs cystectomy alone in primary MIBC (p=0.042) but with worse RFS (p=0.007), CSS (p=0.002), and OS (p=0.013) in secondary MIBC PMID:30290956.
  • Delay to cystectomy in secondary MIBC. Median time from MIBC diagnosis to cystectomy was 5.8 vs 1.7 months (p<0.001), potentially increasing micrometastatic spread in this chemo-refractory population PMID:30290956.
  • ERCC2 missense mutations enriched in primary MIBC (discovery cohort). 10.9% (36/330) primary vs 1.8% (1/55) secondary (p=0.044). Among the four pre-specified DDR/NAC-response genes (ERCC2, ATM, FANCC, RB1), only ERCC2 reached significance PMID:30290956.
  • ERCC2 finding replicated in validation cohort. 15.7% (12/70 — also reported as 17.1% later in text) primary vs 0% (0/24) secondary, p=0.033 in the prospective MSK-IMPACT validation set PMID:30290956.
  • Combined cohort confirms enrichment. Pooling both cohorts: ERCC2 missense in 12% (48/400) primary vs 1.3% (1/79) secondary MIBC (unadjusted p=0.004). Mutations clustered near or within conserved helicase domains PMID:30290956.
  • Other gene-level differences (combined, univariable only). KMT2D alterations were enriched in primary MIBC (23% vs 10%, p=0.013); inactivating STAG2 (7.8% vs 17%, p=0.014) and TSC1 (5.5% vs 14%, p=0.007) alterations were enriched in secondary MIBC. None remained significant after Benjamini-Hochberg correction PMID:30290956.
  • Actionable alterations in secondary MIBC. RTK/MAPK pathway alterations in 42% of secondary MIBC, with FGFR3 (18%) and ERBB2 (15%) among the most commonly altered genes. TP53-pathway and cell-cycle regulatory gene alterations in 61% and 65%, respectively, of secondary MIBC PMID:30290956.
  • FGFR3/CDKN2A coalteration not significantly different. 10% (8/79) secondary vs 5.0% (20/400) primary, unadjusted p=0.08. Coalteration of FGFR3 with any cell-cycle regulatory gene (RB1, E2F2, CDKN1A, CDKN2A, CDKN2B, CCND1, CCNE1) was also not significant (13% vs 6.8%, p=0.07) PMID:30290956.
  • ERCC2-mutant tumors carry higher tumor mutation burden. WES: 14.7 vs 5.5 mutations/Mb (p<0.001); targeted capture: 25.0 vs 8.5 mutations/Mb (p<0.001) for mutant vs wild-type PMID:30290956.
  • Primary MIBC has higher TMB than secondary by targeted capture only. 11.2 vs 7.7 mutations/Mb (p=0.012) by targeted exon capture; not confirmed by WES (6.1 vs 6.1, p=0.8) PMID:30290956.
  • No molecular-subtype enrichment. Unsupervised clustering of TCGA gene expression data using two published luminal/basal classifier sets showed no enrichment of intrinsic molecular subtypes in primary vs secondary MIBC PMID:30290956.

Genes & alterations

  • ERCC2 — Missense mutations clustering near/within conserved helicase domains. Significantly enriched in primary versus secondary MIBC across both cohorts and in combined analysis. Hypothesized mechanism: NER-pathway defect heightens cisplatin sensitivity. Also associated with higher TMB in both WES and targeted capture data PMID:30290956.
  • ATM — Pre-specified DDR/NAC-response gene; no significant difference between primary and secondary MIBC in the discovery cohort PMID:30290956.
  • FANCC — Pre-specified DDR/NAC-response gene; no significant difference. Missing data for 6 patients on the 300-gene MSK-IMPACT panel PMID:30290956.
  • RB1 — Pre-specified DDR/NAC-response gene; no significant difference between groups. Included among cell-cycle regulators tested for FGFR3 co-alteration PMID:30290956.
  • KMT2D — Alterations enriched in primary MIBC (23% vs 10%, univariable p=0.013); did not survive FDR correction PMID:30290956.
  • STAG2 — Inactivating alterations enriched in secondary MIBC (17% vs 7.8%, univariable p=0.014); did not survive FDR correction PMID:30290956.
  • TSC1 — Inactivating alterations enriched in secondary MIBC (14% vs 5.5%, univariable p=0.007); did not survive FDR correction PMID:30290956.
  • FGFR3 — Altered in 18% of secondary MIBC; framed as a potentially actionable target for FGFR-axis small-molecule inhibitors in this chemo-refractory subgroup PMID:30290956.
  • ERBB2 — Altered in 15% of secondary MIBC; highlighted as actionable PMID:30290956.
  • CDKN2A — Tested for co-alteration with FGFR3; no significant difference between primary and secondary MIBC PMID:30290956.
  • TP53 — TP53 pathway alterations in 61% of secondary MIBC (not formally compared between groups) PMID:30290956.

Clinical implications

  • Cisplatin-based NAC may be ineffective or harmful in secondary MIBC. Secondary MIBC patients had worse CSS following NAC than after cystectomy alone (p=0.002), inverting the standard-of-care rationale PMID:30290956.
  • Upfront radical cystectomy or clinical-trial enrollment should be considered for secondary MIBC. The authors argue that, until a molecular biomarker of NAC sensitivity is validated for routine use, prior NMIBC history may serve as a clinical surrogate to identify patients who could benefit from skipping NAC PMID:30290956.
  • ERCC2 missense mutations remain a candidate predictive biomarker for cisplatin response in BLCA. The enrichment of these mutations in primary versus secondary MIBC offers a molecular explanation for the differential chemosensitivity PMID:30290956.
  • Secondary MIBC may be a target population for targeted-therapy trials. FGFR3 (18%) and ERBB2 (15%) alterations and 42% RTK/MAPK pathway activation make secondary MIBC a candidate cohort for FGFR-axis or HER2-directed agents in the neoadjuvant setting PMID:30290956.
  • Possible BCG-mediated selective pressure. The authors speculate that intravesical bacille Calmette-Guérin (BCG) used in NMIBC may preferentially eradicate ERCC2-mutant subclones (via mutational/neoantigen burden-driven immune activation or oxidative DNA damage), enriching for chemo-refractory ERCC2-wild-type clones at MIBC progression PMID:30290956.

Limitations & open questions

  • Retrospective clinical cohort with selection bias. Patients were not randomized to NAC vs cystectomy-alone arms; NAC selection within primary vs secondary MIBC was non-protocolized PMID:30290956.
  • Outside-institution NMIBC management. Most secondary MIBC patients received initial NMIBC treatment elsewhere, so the authors could not control for inadequacy or delay of prior intravesical therapy PMID:30290956.
  • DDR focus may miss other mechanisms. Germline, epigenetic, and transcriptomic factors that modulate or substitute for DDR function were not examined; molecular drivers of cisplatin sensitivity in DDR wild-type patients remain undefined PMID:30290956.
  • Limited power for molecular-subtype analysis. Cohort size was insufficient to detect modest enrichments of luminal/basal subtypes between primary and secondary MIBC PMID:30290956.
  • No significance after multiple-testing correction for non-ERCC2 genes. KMT2D, STAG2, and TSC1 findings remain hypothesis-generating only PMID:30290956.
  • Prospective validation needed. The authors call out SWOG1314 (COXEN) and the Alliance 017301 bladder-sparing trial as venues where these findings could be tested prospectively PMID:30290956.
