The Role of the TP53 Pathway in Predicting Response to Neoadjuvant Therapy in Esophageal Adenocarcinoma

PMID: 35377946 · DOI: 10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-21-4016 · Journal: Clinical Cancer Research (2022)

TL;DR

Sihag and colleagues retrospectively analyzed 237 patients with locally advanced lower esophageal/gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma treated with curative-intent multimodality therapy at Memorial Sloan Kettering from 2014–2020, all of whom underwent prospective MSK-IMPACT tumor sequencing. After excluding microsatellite-instability-high cases, they found that MDM2 amplification was independently associated with poor response to neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy (OR 0.10, 95% CI 0.01–0.55; p=0.032) and that severity of TP53 pathway dysfunction stratified responders cleanly: MDM2-amplified and TP53 truncating bi-allelic tumors had nearly identical poor responses and shorter progression-free survival relative to TP53 pathway wild-type tumors. The cohort is released as cBioPortal study egc_msk_tp53_ccr_2022.

Cohort & data

  • n = 237 patients with lower esophageal adenocarcinoma or gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma (ESCA histology), treated with curative-intent multimodality therapy at MSK 2014–2020.
  • Demographics: 82% male; median age 61 (IQR 55, 68); 78% clinical stage III; 10 patients clinical stage IVB/limited metastatic disease treated with curative intent.
  • Assay: Prospective targeted NGS via MSK-IMPACT (341-gene, 410-gene, or 468-gene versions). Variant filtering used OncoKB annotation. Copy number, tumor purity, ploidy, and bi-allelic status assessed with FACETS. MSI status assessed by MSIsensor (score ≥10 = MSI-high; 14 MSI-high cases excluded from response analyses).
  • Treatments: Neoadjuvant carboplatin + paclitaxel (Carbo-Taxol) in 49%; FOLFOX/FLOT regimens (5-FU/leucovorin/oxaliplatin ± docetaxel) in 32%; 90% received concurrent radiotherapy; 10 patients received neoadjuvant trastuzumab (HER2+); 33 received neoadjuvant immune checkpoint blockade.
  • Tissue: Primary tumor sequenced in 192 (81%); pre-treatment tissue adequate for sequencing in 110 (46%). Higher-purity sample selected when multiples were available.
  • Dataset: Released as cBioPortal study egc_msk_tp53_ccr_2022.
  • Response definition: Major pathologic response (nT-R) = absence of residual nodal disease (pN0) AND ≥90% treatment effect (CAP-TRG score 0 or 1) per CAP tumor regression grading. After removing 14 MSI-high cases, 64 nT-R vs 159 nT-NR (of 223 analyzed). Overall pCR rate was only 12%.

Key findings

  • MDM2 amplification was the only individual gene alteration significantly associated with non-response on univariable testing (p=0.01, q=0.09). On multivariable logistic regression controlling for clinical stage, tumor grade, and chemotherapy regimen, MDM2 amplification remained an independent predictor of poor neoadjuvant response (OR 0.10, 95% CI 0.01–0.55, p=0.032).
  • The TP53 pathway, when stratified by severity of dysfunction, predicted response (p=0.004, q=0.07). The authors defined a 4-tier modified pathway: (1) MDM2 amplification, (2) TP53 truncating bi-allelic mutation (TP53 TruncBi), (3) other TP53 pathway mutation, (4) TP53 pathway wild-type.
  • Multivariable model with the modified TP53 pathway: MDM2 amplification vs WT (OR 0.06, 95% CI 0.00–0.39, p=0.01); TP53 TruncBi vs WT (OR 0.31, 95% CI 0.08–1.03, p=0.06).
  • Recurrent driver alterations (≥8% prevalence, after MSI-H exclusion): TP53 (79%), CDKN2A (22%), KRAS (19%), ERBB2 (16%), ARID1A (14%), SMAD4 (10%), MDM2 (9%), CCNE1 (8%). ERBB2, KRAS, MDM2, and CCNE1 were predominantly affected by oncogenic amplifications.
  • Mutual exclusivity: TP53 TruncBi mutations and MDM2 amplification were almost entirely mutually exclusive (q<0.05), consistent with both lesions producing critical TP53 dysfunction.
  • Progression-free survival: Patients with MDM2 amplification or TP53 TruncBi mutations had significantly shorter PFS than TP53 pathway WT. 24-month cumulative incidence of progression: 61% (95% CI 28–79%) MDM2-amplified, 67% (44–80%) TP53 TruncBi, 42% (19–58%) TP53 pathway WT. Median PFS in cohort 15.9 months (95% CI 13.8–20.9); median follow-up among survivors 20.9 months.
  • Negative findings: TMB, whole-genome doubling, fraction of genome altered, and large-scale transition scores were not associated with neoadjuvant response. Metabolic response on PET (≥35% SUV decrease) did not correlate with pathologic response.
  • Chemotherapy regimen effects: Compared with Carbo-Taxol, FOLFOX was associated with better treatment response (OR 2.38, 95% CI 1.20–4.76, p=0.013), consistent with CALGB 80803 results. Of MDM2-amplified tumors, 21 patients: only 1 (4.8%) classified as nT-R; 16/21 (76%) had clinical or pathologic nodal disease.
  • Of 21 MDM2-amplified patients, 5 had PFS >20 months and 4 of those 5 had concurrent TP53 mutations — consistent with MDM2’s stronger growth-restraint activity on wild-type rather than mutant TP53.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53: altered in 79% of the MSI-stable cohort. Severity of inferred TP53 dysfunction (TruncBi > Other > WT) tracks with worsening pathologic treatment response and shorter PFS. TP53 TruncBi mutations (n=37) had 24-month cumulative progression incidence 67%.
  • MDM2: focal amplification in ~9% (21/237); independently associated with non-response (OR 0.10, p=0.032 multivariable). Mutually exclusive with TP53 TruncBi (q<0.05). Authors propose MDM2 inhibition (e.g., idasanutlin, Nutlin derivatives) combined with cytotoxic chemotherapy as a therapeutic hypothesis in this subgroup.
  • CDKN2A: altered in 22% — high prevalence but not significantly associated with response.
  • KRAS: 19% (predominantly amplification); not significantly associated with response.
  • ERBB2: 16% (predominantly amplification); not associated with response in this cohort. 10 patients received neoadjuvant trastuzumab based on clinical HER2 status.
  • ARID1A: 14%; not associated with response.
  • SMAD4: 10%; not associated with response.
  • CCNE1: 8% (predominantly amplification); not associated with response.
  • ATM, CHEK2: mutations in the TP53 pathway were rare; not driving the pathway signal, which was dominated by TP53 and MDM2.

Clinical implications

  • Predictive biomarker for neoadjuvant resistance. MDM2 amplification and TP53 truncating bi-allelic loss together identify ~26% (57/223) of MSI-stable EAC patients with markedly worse pathologic response to standard neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy and shorter PFS, accounting for a substantial proportion of non-responders. Authors argue this stratification could justify upfront enrollment of these patients onto alternative therapy or trials.
  • Therapeutic hypothesis: MDM2 inhibition. In the ~9% of EAC patients with MDM2 amplification, the authors propose MDM2 antagonists (Nutlin derivatives; specifically named idasanutlin, which has shown preclinical activity combined with chemotherapy in TP53-functional tumors and was tolerable combined with cytarabine in AML trials) combined with cytotoxic chemotherapy as a path to reactivate residual wild-type TP53 function. The few MDM2-amplified patients with concurrent TP53 mutations had marginally better long-term outcomes, consistent with MDM2’s preferential restraint of wild-type rather than mutant TP53.
  • Targeting TP53 TruncBi is harder. PRIMA-1-class small molecules restore conformation in misfolded mutant TP53 and are expected to be less effective against truncating bi-allelic loss; this subgroup may need different strategies.
  • Regimen selection. Consistent with CALGB 80803, FOLFOX was associated with better pathologic response than Carbo-Taxol (OR 2.38, p=0.013). Notably, MDM2-amplified and TP53 TruncBi tumors were disproportionately treated with Carbo-Taxol in this cohort, a potential confounder.
  • PET metabolic response should not substitute for pathologic response. ≥35% SUV decrease did not correlate with pathologic major response.

Limitations & open questions

  • Selection bias toward more advanced disease. Patients with adequate tumor for sequencing tended to have higher-stage and lower-response disease (pCR rate only 12% vs ~40% in other reported series); patients achieving pCR were systematically under-represented because pre-treatment biopsy tissue was unavailable to sequence.
  • Mechanism of MDM2- and TP53-driven resistance is unconfirmed. The authors hypothesize loss of the TP53 negative feedback loop drives resistance but state that intra-tumoral heterogeneity, a plausible alternative driver, cannot be quantified with panel-based sequencing.
  • Multiple-testing margin is narrow. The modified TP53 pathway association achieved q=0.07; the TP53 TruncBi vs WT multivariable comparison (p=0.06, OR 0.31) is suggestive but not significant.
  • Single-institution retrospective design. Findings need prospective external validation, ideally in trial cohorts where regimen assignment is controlled (the regimen-by-genotype confound — Carbo-Taxol over-represented in MDM2/TP53 TruncBi tumors — is a real concern).
  • Heterogeneous neoadjuvant exposure. 10 patients received trastuzumab, 33 received immune checkpoint blockade, and 16% switched regimens post-induction; subgroups are too small to test interactions with the TP53/MDM2 stratification.
  • Therapeutic hypothesis untested in EAC. No EAC-specific MDM2 inhibitor + chemotherapy trial yet; existing idasanutlin data are largely from AML and preclinical xenografts.
