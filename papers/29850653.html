Landscape of Microsatellite Instability Across 39 Cancer Types

Authors

Russell Bonneville

Melanie A. Krook

Esko A. Kautto

Jharna Miya

Michele R. Wing

Hui-Zi Chen

Julie W. Reeser

Lianbo Yu

Sameek Roychowdhury

Doi

10.1200/PO.17.00073

PMID: 29850653 · DOI: 10.1200/PO.17.00073 · Journal: JCO Precision Oncology (2017)

TL;DR

Bonneville et al. applied the MANTIS microsatellite-instability (MSI) caller to 11,139 tumor-normal whole-exome pairs spanning 39 cancer types from TCGA, TARGET, and several external cohorts — to their knowledge the largest pan-cancer MSI analysis to date at the time. MSI was detected in 3.8% of all tumors and was present in 27 of the 39 cancer types, with previously underappreciated MSI in adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC, 4.3%), cervical cancer (CESC, 2.6%), and mesothelioma (MESO, 2.4%). MSI-H ACC and CESC tumors carried significantly higher mutational burden than MSS counterparts, supporting an expanded role for clinical MSI testing beyond colorectal/endometrial/gastric cancers as a biomarker for immune-checkpoint therapy.

Cohort & data

  • 11,139 paired tumor-normal whole-exome sequencing samples from 11,080 patients across 39 distinct cancer types.
  • 10,415 cases from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) (referenced via the pan-can-atlas-2018 freeze for ACC, CESC, MESO, and GBM among others).
  • 280 cases from the TARGET (Therapeutically Applicable Research to Generate Effective Treatments) pediatric initiative.
  • 444 cases from external sources (Landau et al. 2015 CLL, n=338; Choi et al. 2015 CTCL, n=32; Zheng et al. 2016 NPC, n=51; Ong et al. 2012 CHOL, n=8; Chan-On et al. 2013 CHOL, n=15).
  • Assay: whole-exome sequencing. Reads aligned to hg38 with BWA-MEM (except DLBCL, which used hg19); duplicates removed with SAMtools/Picard; in-house L-MAP pipeline orchestrated downloads and processing on the Ohio Supercomputer Center.
  • Assessed cancer types include ACC, CESC, MESO, UCEC, COAD, READ, STAD, GBM, BRCA, OV, BLCA, LUAD, LUSC, SKCM, HNSC, KIRC, KIRP, KICH, LIHC, LGG, PAAD, PRAD, THCA, THYM, UCS, UVM, ESCA, CHOL, PCPG, SARC, TGCT, DLBC, LAML, NBL, NPC, CTCL, CLL, WT, and pediatric AML.

Key findings

  • MSI was detected in 3.8% of all tumors assessed (overall pan-cancer rate) and was present in 27 of 39 cancer types; no MSI was detected in the remaining 12.
  • Disease-specific MSI-H prevalence ranged from 31.4% in endometrial carcinoma (UCEC) at the high end to 0.25% in glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) among the cancer types where MSI was detected at all.
  • The four highest-MSI cancer types were the classic Lynch syndrome–associated tumors: UCEC, COAD, STAD, and READ. Consistent with prior work, colon adenocarcinoma had higher MSI prevalence (19.7%) than rectal adenocarcinoma (5.7%).
  • Previously underappreciated MSI signal was identified in three cancer types: adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC, 4.3%; 4/92 cases), cervical squamous cell carcinoma and endocervical adenocarcinoma (CESC, 2.6%), and mesothelioma (MESO, 2.4%).
  • Across ACC, CESC, and MESO (n=480 combined), kernel-density estimation on MANTIS scores produced clearly separable MSI-H vs MSS distributions, supporting the classifications.
  • Mutational burden was significantly elevated in MSI-H vs MSS tumors within these cancer types: ACC averaged 1,157 mutations (MSI-H) vs 216 (MSS), P = .01; CESC averaged 5,675 vs 639, P = .003; MESO averaged 982 vs 142, P = .10 (~7-fold but not statistically significant). P values calculated by Welch two-sample t-test on log-normalized counts.
  • Of 9,569 tumors outside colorectal, endometrial, or gastric cancer, 77 (0.8%) were MSI-H, of which only 14 fell within ACC, CESC, or MESO — limiting statistical power for downstream mutational-signature analysis.
  • Pooled and per-case mutational-signature analyses with deconstructSigs using the Alexandrov 2013 signature set did not reveal statistically significant differences (after Benjamini-Hochberg correction) between MSI-H and MSS tumors within ACC, CESC, or MESO.
  • Across the 2,530 microsatellite loci tested, the authors identified 22 loci that — within at least five cohorts — showed an MSI-H vs MSS difference score > 0.75 and ≥50% sample coverage. Only two of the standard Bethesda/Promega MSI-PCR-panel loci were assessed by this study’s locus set, and neither fell within the top-22 list, indicating substantial heterogeneity of informative loci across cancer types.
  • Among MSI-H cases in ACC/CESC/MESO, 64% carried at least one predicted-deleterious somatic mutation in an MMR or proofreading gene (MSH2, MSH6, MLH1, PMS2, EXO1, POLD1, POLE) vs 7% of MSS cases; statistical significance could not be formally established due to differing exome captures plus the higher background mutation rate in MSI-H samples.

Genes & alterations

  • MSH2, MSH6, MLH1, PMS2 — core MMR proteins; somatic deleterious mutations enriched in MSI-H tumors of ACC, CESC, and MESO. The authors explicitly screened these genes (along with EXO1) for DANN-pathogenic somatic variants. Loss of these proteins (or germline Lynch alterations) is the canonical cause of MSI.
  • EXO1 — exonuclease component of the MMR pathway; included in the same MMR-gene somatic-mutation screen.
  • POLD1, POLE — DNA proofreading polymerases. Not MMR proteins per se, but somatic mutations in these genes can drive hypermutation and were tallied alongside the MMR genes in MSI-H tumors.

Clinical implications

  • The authors explicitly argue these findings support expanded clinical MSI testing beyond colorectal and endometrial cancers, since MSI-H tumors are predicted to benefit from PD-1 blockade and pembrolizumab had recently received an FDA pan-tumor approval (May 2017) for any MSI-H or MMR-deficient unresectable/metastatic solid tumor.
  • ACC, CESC, and MESO each had ongoing immune-checkpoint clinical trials at the time of writing (ACC: NCT02673333; CESC: NCT02635360; MESO: NCT02784171, NCT02991482, NCT02707666, NCT02399371); the authors suggest these studies could benefit from retrospective MSI-H biomarker analysis.
  • Identification of MSI in ACC reinforces prior anecdotal reports of ACC as a possible extracolonic Lynch syndrome manifestation, which has implications for germline testing referral.
  • The 22 cancer-type-spanning informative loci identified could form the basis of a targeted NGS panel for pan-cancer MSI detection, with the caveat that standard Bethesda/Promega PCR-panel loci performed poorly outside colorectal/endometrial contexts.

Limitations & open questions

  • Statistical power for mutational-signature differences in ACC/CESC/MESO was limited — only 14 MSI-H tumors across the three cohorts combined.
  • Samples were sequenced with potentially different exome capture kits, confounding the somatic-mutation-burden comparison of MMR/proofreading genes between MSI-H and MSS cases.
  • The study cannot distinguish whether MSI in ACC, CESC, and MESO is driven by classical MMR deficiency (e.g., MLH1 promoter methylation, which the authors did not measure) versus other mechanisms.
  • No clinical-outcome correlation was performed; whether MSI-H status is prognostic outside colorectal cancer remains an open question that requires larger MSI-H cohorts with linked clinical data.
  • The microsatellite locus set (2,530 loci) was originally designed from exome-flanking regions and is not directly comparable to the Bethesda/Promega PCR-panel loci — a methodological gap for clinical adoption.
  • The detection threshold (MANTIS average distance ≥ 0.4) was held constant across all cancer types; cancer-type-specific calibration was not explored.
