Regional Differences in Gallbladder Cancer Pathogenesis: Insights From a Multi-Institutional Comparison of Tumor Mutations

TL;DR

Narayan et al. profiled 81 primary gallbladder cancer (GBCA) tumors from three centers — Memorial Sloan Kettering (USA, n=49), Arturo Lopez Perez Foundation Cancer Institute (Chile, n=21), and Yokohama City University (Japan, n=11) — using the MSK-IMPACT targeted DNA-sequencing assay. They report regional differences in clinicopathology and mutation pattern: Japanese tumors lacked ARID1A and PIK3CA mutations, Chilean tumors lacked ERBB3 and ARID2 mutations, and Chilean tumors carried the highest mutation burden (median 7 vs. 6 in Japan vs. 4 in the United States; P-adj = .006). SMAD4 was mutated in roughly one-third of patients across all three sites and was independently associated with worse overall survival (HR 2.01, 95% CI 1.02–3.94; P = .043). Eighty percent of tumors carried at least one mutation in a potentially actionable gene target.

Cohort & data

  • N = 81 patients with primary gallbladder cancer (GBC) treated between September 1999 and March 2016.
  • Distribution by site: 49 from MSK (United States), 21 from Arturo Lopez Perez (Chile), 11 from Yokohama City University (Japan).
  • Only adenocarcinoma and adenosquamous histologies; 14 patients with metastatic-only tissue and 6 with other histologies (neuroendocrine, small cell, sarcomatoid, cholangiocarcinoma) were excluded.
  • Paired tumor + normal DNA sequenced with the MSK-IMPACT hybridization-capture targeted NGS assay (341–410 cancer-associated genes; IMPACT341 / IMPACT410 panel versions). Mutation burden was normalized by panel size (1.2 Mb for IMPACT-341; 1.38 Mb for IMPACT-410).
  • Data deposited as gbc_msk_2018 on cBioPortal.
  • Survival analysis used Kaplan–Meier and Cox proportional-hazards modeling; actionable targets were defined via OncoKB.

Key findings

  • Mutation burden differed by region (P-adj = .006): Chile median 7 (range 3–20), Japan median 6 (range 1–23), United States median 4 (range 0–27).
  • TP53 was the most commonly mutated gene cohort-wide (47/81; 58%). Chilean cohort 76% (16/21), Japanese 55% (6/11), American 51% (25/49); regional differences not statistically significant (P-adj = .45).
  • SMAD4 mutated in 25/81 (31%) — 38% Chile, 36% Japan, 27% US (P-adj = .68 across sites). Univariately associated with worse overall survival (median 10.4 mo vs. 24.8 mo; P-adj = .039). After controlling for stage, gallstone status, and sex in a multivariable Cox model, SMAD4 remained independently associated with worse OS (hazard ratio 2.01, 95% CI 1.02–3.94; P = .043).
  • ARID1A mutated in 20/81 (25%) but absent in all 11 Japanese patients (0% vs. 38% Chile, 25% US; P-adj = .35).
  • PIK3CA mutations were exclusively observed in Chilean (3/21; 14%) and American (7/49; 14%) patients; none in Japanese patients.
  • ERBB3 and ARID2 mutations were absent in Chilean patients but present at 9% in Japanese (1/11 each) and 12%/10% in American patients.
  • Pathway-level recurrence: p53 pathway (73%), RTK-RAS (44%) — the two most commonly altered pathways; no pathway showed regional differences (P-adj = .62 to >.95).
  • 80% of patients carried at least one mutation in a potentially actionable gene target (per OncoKB or prior literature).
  • No mutation or pathway alteration was significantly linked to gallstone-associated GBCA (P = .34 to .92 for individual genes; P-adj = .25 to >.95 for pathways).
  • Median overall survival after complete resection (n=46) was 19.6 months (95% CI 14.5–28.8); 5-year survival 18% (95% CI 8–31%).
  • Exploratory trends toward worse survival (not significant after adjustment) for PIK3CA (median 9.9 mo vs. 24.5 mo; P-adj = .09) and CDKN2A (median 8.5 mo vs. 23.8 mo; P-adj = .09).
  • Mutual exclusivity / co-occurrence: ATM and TP53 appeared mutually exclusive (log-OR 1.92; P-adj = .13, NS); co-occurrence trends among SMAD4/TP53, SMAD4/KRAS, SMAD4/CDKN2A, SMAD4/CDKN2B, CDKN2A/CDKN2B, BRCA2/AR, BRCA2/KMT2D, and ARID2/TERT — none reached adjusted significance.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — Most commonly mutated gene (58%); trend toward enrichment in Chilean tumors (76%, NS after adjustment).
  • SMAD4 — Mutated in 31% of tumors; independently associated with worse overall survival (HR 2.01; multivariable Cox).
  • ARID1A — Mutated in 25% of all tumors; entirely absent in Japanese cohort.
  • PIK3CA — Mutated in 12% of cohort; entirely absent in Japanese cohort; trend toward worse OS (P-adj = .09).
  • ATM — Mutated in 19% of cohort; trend toward mutual exclusivity with TP53.
  • CDKN2A — Mutated in 15%; trend toward worse OS (P-adj = .09).
  • TERT — Mutated in 12%; promoter region; co-occurrence trend with ARID2.
  • BRCA2 — Mutated in 10%; co-occurrence trends with AR and KMT2D.
  • KMT2D — Mutated in 10%.
  • ERBB3 — Mutated in 9% of cohort but absent in Chilean patients.
  • KRAS — Mutated in 9%; previously associated with anomalous pancreatobiliary duct junction (a Japanese-predominant risk factor).
  • NF1 — Mutated in 9%.
  • ARID2 — Mutated in 7% of cohort but absent in Chilean patients.
  • AR — Mutated in 7%.
  • CDKN2B — Mutated in 7%; co-occurrence trend with CDKN2A (chromosome 9 adjacency).
  • MDM2 — Mutated in 6%; same pathway as TP53 and ATM.

Clinical implications

  • SMAD4 as a converging prognostic biomarker. Although region-specific drivers (PIK3CA, ARID1A, ARID2, ERBB3) varied across cohorts, SMAD4 was mutated at comparable frequency (~one-third) in all three populations and predicted worse OS independently of stage, sex, and gallstone status. The authors propose SMAD4 mutation as a candidate convergent indicator of poor prognosis in GBCA across regions.
  • High frequency of actionable targets supports precision-oncology trial design. 80% of patients carried at least one mutation in a gene flagged as actionable by OncoKB or prior literature, providing rationale for prospective biomarker-driven trials in GBCA.
  • Regional differences argue for stratified clinical-trial enrollment. The absence of ARID1A and PIK3CA mutations in Japanese tumors, and of ERBB3 and ARID2 in Chilean tumors, suggests that single-arm trials enrolling internationally may obscure region-specific response signals.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small sample sizes per region (Japan n=11; Chile n=21) limit power for site-specific subgroup conclusions and may explain why several regional differences did not reach adjusted significance.
  • Retrospective design with potential selection bias; only 21% of cases were incidental, despite ~70% of GBCA being incidentally diagnosed in the general population (cases with adequate tumor for sequencing are over-represented).
  • Primary tumor-only sampling — 14 patients with metastatic-only tissue were excluded; the metastatic landscape may differ.
  • DNA sequencing alone may underestimate pathway activity. Authors note that expression assays might better capture compensatory pathway effects (e.g., TGF-β signaling cannot be fully resolved from somatic alterations alone).
  • National representativeness is uncertain. It is unclear whether the three single-institution cohorts reflect their broader national GBCA populations or center-specific referral biases.
  • Conflict with prior reports on ARID1A in Asian patients. This study found no ARID1A mutations in Japanese patients, contrasting with one Japanese report of inactivating ARID1A mutations (Sasaki et al., 2016) but consistent with other Asian-cohort reports that also failed to find ARID1A mutations.
