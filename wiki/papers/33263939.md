**PMID:** 33263939 · **DOI:** 10.1002/cjp2.190 · **Journal:** The Journal of Pathology: Clinical Research (2021)

## TL;DR

D'Alfonso et al. performed whole-exome sequencing (WES) on the hyperplastic epithelial component of three juvenile papillomatosis (JP) lesions of the breast, including one case (JuP3) with synchronously identified ductal carcinoma in situ ([DCIS](../cancer_types/DCIS.md)) and invasive ductal carcinoma of no special type (IDC-NST). They found clonal somatic [PIK3CA](../genes/PIK3CA.md) hotspot mutations in 2 of 3 JP cases, and demonstrated that the JP, DCIS, and IDC-NST components of JuP3 were clonally related — sharing a clonal *[PIK3CA](../genes/PIK3CA.md)* E542K hotspot mutation. The associated carcinomas additionally acquired 1q gains/16q losses (the hallmark of ER-positive breast cancer) and showed a shift from an aging-related mutational signature in JP toward an APOBEC-dominant signature in DCIS and IDC-NST, supporting the hypothesis that JP can constitute a substrate from which DCIS and invasive breast carcinomas develop.

## Cohort & data

- **n = 3 JP lesions** (JuP1, JuP2, JuP3) from MSKCC and Weill Cornell pathology archives, including 1 case (JuP3) with coexisting [DCIS](../cancer_types/DCIS.md) and invasive [IDC-NST](../cancer_types/IDC.md). All patients were young women (ages 31–43); JuP2 and JuP3 had a family history of [breast carcinoma](../cancer_types/BRCA.md).
- **Assay:** Whole-exome sequencing of microdissected hyperplastic epithelial components (and DCIS/IDC-NST of JuP3) matched against benign non-hyperplastic breast parenchyma. DNA extracted via Qiagen DNAeasy from 10–15 8-µm FFPE sections under stereomicroscopy. Sequencing performed at the MSKCC Integrated Genomics Operation.
- **Bioinformatics:** Reads aligned with [BWA](../methods/bwa.md) v0.7.15 to GRCh37; [GATK](../methods/gatk.md) v3.1.1 for local realignment, dedup, and BQSR; [MuTect](../methods/mutect.md) v1.0 for SNVs; [Strelka](../methods/strelka.md) v2.0.15, [VarScan2](../methods/varscan.md) v2.3.7, Scalpel v0.5.3, and Lancet v1.0.0 for indels. Manual review via [IGV](../methods/integrative-genomics-viewer.md). Cancer cell fraction (CCF) inferred via [ABSOLUTE](../methods/absolute.md) v1.0.6. Copy-number alterations and LOH from [FACETS](../methods/facets.md). Mutational signatures inferred via [SigMA](../methods/sigma-mutational-signatures.md). [Phylogenetic tree](../methods/phylogenetic-tree-reconstruction.md) of JuP3 built with Treeomics.
- **Dataset:** [brca_jup_msk_2020](../datasets/brca_jup_msk_2020.md) — WES data deposited on cBioPortal (`brca_jup_msk_2020`).

## Key findings

- **JP harbors recurrent clonal *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations.** Somatic clonal hotspot mutations were identified in 2 of 3 cases: JuP2 (*PIK3CA* H1047R) and JuP3 (*PIK3CA* E542K). JuP1 had a germline *PIK3CA* I391M variant (C2 domain) of uncertain significance (population frequency 6%).
- **Low mutation burden in JP.** Benign JP showed a median of 9 total mutations (range 6–25) and 6 nonsynonymous mutations (range 3–18), with a paucity of copy-number alterations.
- **JP, DCIS, and IDC-NST are clonally related in JuP3.** All three components shared the clonal *PIK3CA* E542K hotspot mutation plus *[CES1](../genes/CES1.md)* (S12A) and *[SMG1](../genes/SMG1.md)* (R420Q) missense mutations.
- **DCIS and IDC-NST share 29 carcinoma-private mutations** absent from JP, including a truncating mutation in *[ARID1A](../genes/ARID1A.md)*, missense mutations in *[KMT2C](../genes/KMT2C.md)* and *[PIK3CB](../genes/PIK3CB.md)*, and an E14K hotspot mutation in *[NUP93](../genes/NUP93.md)*.
- **Subclonal-to-clonal selection during DCIS→IDC-NST progression.** A *[PIK3C2A](../genes/PIK3C2A.md)* missense mutation that was subclonal in DCIS became dominant in IDC-NST.
- **Late acquisitions:** a truncating *[STAG2](../genes/STAG2.md)* mutation was private to DCIS; an *[FGF12](../genes/FGF12.md)* missense mutation was private to IDC-NST.
- **Concurrent 1q gain / 16q loss** — the hallmark CNA of ER-positive breast cancer — was present in the synchronous DCIS and IDC-NST but absent from the matched JP.
- **Mutational signature shift from aging to APOBEC.** JP of JuP3 was dominated by aging-related Signature 5 (exposure 76%) with Signature 2 (APOBEC) at 20%. In the same case the DCIS shifted to APOBEC Signature 2 at 55% and the IDC-NST to 64%. In JuP1, Signature 18 (cryptic origin) was dominant followed by Signature 5; JuP2 was dominated by aging-related Signatures 1 and 5.

## Genes & alterations

- [PIK3CA](../genes/PIK3CA.md) — clonal hotspot mutations: H1047R (JuP2), E542K (JuP3, shared by JP/DCIS/IDC-NST). Germline I391M variant in JuP1 (significance uncertain).
- [AKT1](../genes/AKT1.md) — context only; the prior Guillet et al. study reported [AKT1](../genes/AKT1.md) hotspot mutations in 2/10 JP cases, but no AKT1 mutations were identified in this 3-case cohort. AKT1 activating mosaic mutations also underlie Proteus syndrome, a disorder occasionally associated with JP.
- [ARID1A](../genes/ARID1A.md) — truncating mutation, private to DCIS+IDC-NST of JuP3.
- [KMT2C](../genes/KMT2C.md) — missense mutation, private to DCIS+IDC-NST of JuP3.
- [PIK3CB](../genes/PIK3CB.md) — missense mutation, private to DCIS+IDC-NST of JuP3.
- [PIK3C2A](../genes/PIK3C2A.md) — missense mutation, subclonal in DCIS, dominant in IDC-NST (clonal sweep).
- [NUP93](../genes/NUP93.md) — E14K hotspot mutation, private to DCIS+IDC-NST of JuP3 (nucleoporin implicated in cell migration).
- [STAG2](../genes/STAG2.md) — truncating mutation, private to DCIS.
- [FGF12](../genes/FGF12.md) — missense mutation, private to IDC-NST.
- [CES1](../genes/CES1.md) — S12A missense, shared by JP/DCIS/IDC-NST of JuP3.
- [SMG1](../genes/SMG1.md) — R420Q missense, shared by JP/DCIS/IDC-NST of JuP3.

## Clinical implications

- JP harbors *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations and can be clonally related to coexisting DCIS and invasive breast carcinoma, supporting a model in which JP — though histologically benign — may serve as a non-obligate substrate for invasive breast cancer development in some patients. This argues for long-term clinical follow-up of JP patients, consistent with the authors' prior 121-case follow-up series (Cheng et al. 2018).
- The IDC-NST in JuP3 was ER-positive/HER2-negative with the typical 1q gain/16q loss CNA profile, fitting the canonical molecular subtype of luminal breast cancer.
- The paper does not directly recommend any therapy. PI3K-pathway alterations being shared from JP to invasive carcinoma may be relevant to PI3K-pathway inhibitor strategies in the rare cases of JP-associated invasive disease, but the authors make no explicit treatment claim.

## Limitations & open questions

- **Very small cohort (n=3 JP cases, only 1 with coexisting DCIS/IDC-NST)**, reflecting the rarity of JP. The frequency with which JP gives rise to invasive carcinoma cannot be estimated from this study.
- Only the hyperplastic epithelial component of JP was sequenced; whether the recurrent *PIK3CA* mutations would also be present in other JP histologic components (e.g. cysts, apocrine metaplasia) is unknown.
- The significance of the JuP1 germline *PIK3CA* I391M C2-domain variant (6% population frequency) is uncertain.
- Whether JP shows similar genetic alterations to — or is clonally related to — JP-associated special-type breast carcinomas driven by other mechanisms (e.g. invasive lobular carcinoma with *[CDH1](../genes/CDH1.md)* loss; secretory carcinoma with *ETV6-NTRK3* fusion) was not addressed.
- The rare reports of JP in patients with Cowden syndrome (germline *[PTEN](../genes/PTEN.md)*) and Proteus syndrome (mosaic *AKT1*) hint at a broader PI3K-pathway etiology that remains uncharacterized.
