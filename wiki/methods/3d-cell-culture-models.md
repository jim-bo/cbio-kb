# 3D Cell Culture Models

## Overview

Three-dimensional (3D) cell culture models are an umbrella category of in vitro systems that grow cancer cells in configurations mimicking the spatial architecture and microenvironmental complexity of solid tumors. Unlike monolayer (2D) cultures, 3D models develop oxygen and nutrient gradients, hypoxic cores, cell–cell and cell–matrix interactions, and emergent drug-resistance phenotypes more representative of patient disease. Four principal families are recognized: (1) scaffold-free suspension systems (spheroids, tumorspheres, tumoroids — including patient-derived organoids); (2) scaffold-based systems (hydrogels such as Matrigel/collagen and electrospun/porous polymer scaffolds); (3) 3D bioprinting using decellularized extracellular matrix or synthetic bioinks; and (4) microfluidic platforms (droplet-based chips and organ-on-a-chip devices). IC50 values in 3D spheroids are typically 3–5-fold higher than in paired monolayer cultures, consistent with clinical drug-resistance patterns.

## Used by

- Methodological review of 3D cancer culture systems surveying spheroids, hydrogels, bioprinting, and microfluidics across breast, glioblastoma, osteosarcoma, ovarian, pancreatic, Ewing sarcoma, head-and-neck, and other cancer types; argues these models better recapitulate drug resistance and tumor heterogeneity than monolayers [PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md)
- 65 patient-derived rectal cancer tumoroid cultures established from 41 patients (77% success rate from 84 attempts, including minute 2.8 mm outpatient biopsy forceps samples); tumoroids recapitulated histopathology, mutational profiles, and ex vivo chemoradiation responses of parental tumors [PMID:31591597](../papers/31591597.md).

## Notes

- **Matrigel batch variability.** Matrigel contains ≈1851 proteins with as low as 53% similarity between batches/manufacturers, confounding mechanistic interpretation; PubMed publications using Matrigel have been declining since 2013 as defined-composition alternatives gain traction.
- **Size mismatch.** Cancer spheroids rarely exceed 1000 µm diameter (vs. clinical tumor sizes of several centimeters), limiting direct extrapolation to patient disease. Colon tumors average ≈4 cm; osteosarcomas ≈9.14 cm mean length in patients ≤14 years old.
- **Nomenclature inconsistency.** The terms "spheroid", "tumorsphere", "tumoroid", and "organoid" are used interchangeably in the literature; some authors reserve "tumoroid" for patient-tumor-derived 3D cultures.
- **High-throughput formats.** Droplet-based microfluidic chips can generate >1000 spheroids/min at 16–20 Hz, making 3D models compatible with drug-screening workflows. Anchored droplet chips produce IC50 values concordant with standard 96-well plates for combination chemotherapy.
- **Immune co-culture.** T cell + tumor spheroid co-cultures in droplets demonstrate cooperative cytotoxic T-lymphocyte accumulation at the spheroid surface, enabling immune-tumor interaction studies not possible in monolayers.
