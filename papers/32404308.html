The genomic landscape of intrinsic and acquired resistance to cyclin-dependent kinase 4/6 inhibitors in patients with hormone receptor positive metastatic breast cancer

TL;DR

Wander et al. performed whole-exome sequencing on 59 metastatic biopsies from 58 patients with HR+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer to map the genomic landscape of intrinsic and acquired resistance to CDK4/6 inhibitors (CDK4/6i). Eight categories of alterations — biallelic RB1 disruption, activating mutation/amplification of AKT1, activating KRAS/HRAS/NRAS mutations, FGFR2 alterations, ERBB2 mutations, AURKA amplification, CCNE2 amplification, and loss of ER expression — accounted for 27 of 41 (65.9%) resistant tumors versus 3 of 18 (16.7%) sensitive tumors. AKT1, RAS, and AURKA had not previously been demonstrated as mechanisms of CDK4/6i resistance in patients. In vitro overexpression / CRISPR knockout in T47D and MCF7 cells confirmed each alteration could confer resistance to palbociclib or abemaciclib, and cell lines cultured to resistance independently acquired RB1 loss, KRAS mutation, AURKA up-regulation, or CCNE2 up-regulation. A single patient with HR+/HER2- MBC who progressed on first-line palbociclib/letrozole derived ~11 months of disease control on the selective AURKA inhibitor LY3295668 in a phase I trial (NCT03092934).

Cohort & data

  • Patients: 58 patients with HR+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer (BRCA) treated with a CDK4/6 inhibitor +/- anti-estrogen, enrolled on DF/HCC tissue protocol 05-246.
  • Biopsies: 59 metastatic biopsies — 18 sensitive (baseline, subsequent clinical benefit), 28 intrinsic resistance (progression on first interval restaging or SD <6 months), 13 acquired resistance (biopsy at/after progression after initial benefit).
  • Treatment: 49/58 patients (84.5%) received palbociclib; 55/58 (94.8%) combined an aromatase inhibitor or fulvestrant with the CDK4/6i. Mean treatment duration 316 days (range 43–1052).
  • Assay: whole-exome sequencing of tumor + matched germline (PBMC) DNA, aligned to hg19; somatic SNV calling with MuTect, structural variants/long deletions with SvABA, cancer-cell fractions with ABSOLUTE, clonal reconstruction with PyClone, ER status by immunohistochemistry.
  • Dataset: brca_dfci_2020 on cBioPortal.

Key findings

  • Eight resistance categories cover 65.9% of resistant tumors. 27/41 resistant biopsies harbored at least one of biallelic RB1 loss, AKT1, RAS-family, FGFR2, ERBB2, AURKA, CCNE2, or ER-loss alterations, versus 3/18 (16.7%) sensitive biopsies PMID:32404308.
  • AURKA amplification is the most enriched novel mediator. AURKA amplification occurred in 11/41 (26.8%) resistant biopsies (7 intrinsic, 4 acquired) and 0/18 sensitive (p=0.0081, Welch test) PMID:32404308.
  • Biallelic RB1 disruption is exclusive to resistant samples but rare. 4/41 (9.8%) resistant biopsies; each combined single-copy loss with a point mutation, splice-site, or frameshift on the second allele. In one patient, two post-progression biopsies showed distinct second-allele lesions — convergent evolution of RB1 loss PMID:32404308.
  • AKT1 alterations in 5/41 (12.2%) resistant vs 1/18 (5.6%) sensitive; included a pathogenic W80R point mutation acquired post-progression and an acquired AKT1 amplification on a baseline L52H VUS background PMID:32404308.
  • RAS-pathway activation in 4/41 (9.8%) resistant, 0/18 sensitive: KRAS G12D, KRAS Q61L, HRAS K117N, focal NRAS amplification PMID:32404308.
  • CCNE2 amplification in 6/41 (14.6%) resistant vs 1/18 (5.6%) sensitive — the first reported association of CCNE2 (as opposed to CCNE1) with CDK4/6i resistance PMID:32404308.
  • ERBB2 alterations in 5/41 (12.2%) resistant (two were VUS — L377M and P1074L); FGFR2 alterations in 3/41 (7.3%, all intrinsic) PMID:32404308.
  • ER loss by IHC in 3/41 (7.3%) resistant, 0/18 sensitive, all in patients exposed to CDK4/6i + anti-estrogen PMID:32404308.
  • Co-occurrence is common: 12/41 (29.3%) resistant biopsies carried two or more of the eight drivers (e.g., concurrent ERBB2 + AURKA in samples 349_T1 and 366_T2; ER loss + AKT1 + RAS in 331_T1) PMID:32404308.
  • Background enrichments: TP53 mutated in 24/41 (58.5%) resistant vs 0/18 sensitive; MYC amplification 8/41 (19.5%) vs 1/18 (5.6%); CCND1 11/41 (26.8%) vs 3/18 (16.7%); ESR1 mutation 14/41 (34.1%) vs 3/18 (16.7%, all on fulvestrant). PIK3CA mutations were equally frequent in both arms (44.4% sensitive vs 43.9% resistant) — not a resistance marker PMID:32404308.
  • In vitro validation: in T47D cells, lentiviral overexpression of AKT1, KRAS G12D, AURKA, or CCNE2, or CRISPR knockout of RB1, was each sufficient to confer resistance to palbociclib or abemaciclib. Similar results in MCF7 except AURKA, indicating cell-context dependence PMID:32404308.
  • TP53 loss alone is insufficient. CRISPR knockout of TP53 in MCF7 (TP53 WT) did not confer CDK4/6i resistance, and T47D (TP53 mutant) is baseline CDK4/6i sensitive PMID:32404308.
  • Independent emergence under selection: cells cultured to resistance (361-AR-1, T47D-AR-1, 361-PR-1, MCF7-PR-1) spontaneously acquired KRAS G12V (ERK activation), RB1 down-regulation + AURKA up, AURKA + CCNE2 up, or CCNE2 up respectively PMID:32404308.
  • Synthetic-lethal vulnerabilities in resistant derivatives: KRAS/ERK-activated 361-AR-1 cells became sensitive to the ERK inhibitor LY3214996; AURKA-high or RB1-low cells (T47D-AR-1, 361-PR-1) to the selective AURKA inhibitor LY3295668; CCNE2-amplified MCF7-PR-1 cells to the CHEK1 inhibitor prexasertib PMID:32404308.
  • Clinical proof-of-concept: A patient with HR+/HER2-, RB1-protein-high MBC who had progressed after >3 years on first-line palbociclib + letrozole achieved ~11 months of stable disease on LY3295668 on phase I trial NCT03092934 PMID:32404308.

Genes & alterations

  • RB1 — biallelic disruption (single-copy loss + second-allele point/splice/frameshift event) in 4/41 (9.8%) resistant biopsies; exclusive to resistance; convergent evolution in one patient. CRISPR RB1 knockout confers CDK4/6i resistance in T47D/MCF7 cells.
  • AKT1 — activating mutations (e.g., W80R) and amplifications in 5/41 (12.2%) resistant; lentiviral overexpression confers CDK4/6i and fulvestrant resistance. First demonstration of AKT1 as a CDK4/6i resistance driver in patients.
  • KRAS — G12D (canonical activating), Q61L pathogenic mutation, plus a spontaneously acquired KRAS G12V in 361-AR-1 cells. KRAS G12D overexpression confers resistance; ERK inhibition (LY3214996) restores sensitivity.
  • HRAS — K117N activating mutation in one resistant biopsy.
  • NRAS — focal high-level amplification in one resistant biopsy.
  • AURKA — amplification (mostly low–moderate magnitude) in 11/41 (26.8%) resistant, 0/18 sensitive (p=0.0081). Spontaneous AURKA up-regulation in T47D-AR-1 and 361-PR-1 cells; conferred sensitivity to selective AURKA inhibitor LY3295668. First in-patient evidence for AURKA in CDK4/6i resistance.
  • CCNE2 — amplification in 6/41 (14.6%) resistant vs 1/18 (5.6%) sensitive; overexpression confers resistance; CCNE2-amplified cells are sensitized to the CHEK1 inhibitor prexasertib. First report linking CCNE2 (vs CCNE1) to CDK4/6i resistance.
  • ERBB2 — activating mutations and amplifications in 5/41 (12.2%) resistant biopsies; two VUS variants (L377M, P1074L) included. Consistent with prior work from the same group.
  • FGFR2 — mutations/amplifications in 3/41 (7.3%) resistant biopsies, all intrinsic resistance.
  • TP53 — mutated in 24/41 (58.5%) resistant vs 0/18 sensitive; enriched but not sufficient in vitro (CRISPR knockout in MCF7 does not produce CDK4/6i resistance).
  • ESR1 — mutations in 14/41 (34.1%) resistant vs 3/18 (16.7%) sensitive; sensitive-tumor ESR1 mutants occurred exclusively in fulvestrant-treated patients. Consistent with endocrine but not CDK4/6i resistance.
  • PIK3CA — mutated at similar frequencies in sensitive (8/18, 44.4%) and resistant (18/41, 43.9%) — not a resistance marker.
  • CCND1 — alterations in 11/41 (26.8%) resistant vs 3/18 (16.7%) sensitive; no consistent association with response.
  • FGFR1 — copy-number gains in 4/18 (22.2%) sensitive and 4/41 (9.8%) resistant — not enriched in resistance.
  • MYC — amplifications in 8/41 (19.5%) resistant vs 1/18 (5.6%) sensitive.
  • IGF1R — amplification in 4/41 (9.8%) resistant vs 1/18 (5.6%) sensitive; flagged as candidate driver for future study.
  • NF1 — alterations in 4/41 (9.8%) resistant vs 1/18 (5.6%) sensitive; potentially funnels into MAPK-pathway resistance.
  • CCNE1, CDK6, FAT1, PTEN — discussed as prior-literature resistance candidates; not enriched in this cohort (centromere-proximal CCNE1 copy calls noted as technically difficult by WES).

Clinical implications

  • Therapeutic nodes. The authors argue that 65.9% of resistant tumors could be addressed by targeting four pathway nodes: AURKA (covers RB1 loss + AURKA amplification), MAPK (covers RAS, ERBB2, FGFR2, NF1), AKT/MTOR (covers AKT1), and CCNE/CHEK1 (covers CCNE2).
  • AURKA inhibition (LY3295668, also called LY3295668) achieved ~11 months of disease control in a single HR+/HER2-, RB1-protein-high patient on phase I trial NCT03092934 after progression on first-line palbociclib + letrozole; supports a phase I study of LY3295668 in CDK4/6i-progressed HR+ MBC (NCT03955939). alisertib + fulvestrant is in randomized phase II (NCT02860000).
  • CHEK1 inhibition with prexasertib is rationalized for CCNE2-amplified post-CDK4/6i tumors.
  • ERK / MAPK inhibition (ERK inhibitors, MEK inhibitors, SHP2 inhibitors) is proposed as a unifying strategy for the RAS / ERBB2 / FGFR2 / NF1 subset.
  • No actionable role for PIK3CA status as a CDK4/6i resistance biomarker in this cohort.
  • Precision over empiric. The heterogeneity argues against a single non-selective post-CDK4/6i regimen and supports genomically guided trial design at the point of progression.

Limitations & open questions

  • Sample size. 41 resistant and 18 sensitive biopsies are too small to power formal tests for rare drivers (e.g., FAT1 loss reported elsewhere) or to compare intrinsic vs acquired subgroups for proportional differences.
  • 34.1% of resistant tumors (14/41) lack any of the eight defined mechanisms — non-genomic resistance via epigenetic, miRNA, or post-translational events remains unmeasured.
  • Bulk exome sequencing cannot resolve whether co-occurring drivers (29.3% of resistant biopsies have ≥2) sit in the same subclone or in parallel clones; the authors flag single-cell RNA-seq as a follow-up.
  • CCNE1 copy-number calls are unreliable by WES because of centromere proximity — true CCNE1 amplification rates may be under-counted.
  • Matched pre/post pairs available for only 7 acquired-resistance patients — limits inference about which alterations are truly acquired vs pre-existing minor clones.
  • TP53 enrichment in resistant tumors is unexplained — possibly reflects heavier pre-treatment, permissiveness for co-drivers, or cooperative interactions; CRISPR knockout alone does not phenocopy resistance.
  • Functional validation used T47D and MCF7 only — context dependence was already apparent (AURKA overexpression failed to drive resistance in MCF7), so generalizability to all HR+ tumors is uncertain.
  • VUS calls in ERBB2 (L377M, P1074L) are not formally demonstrated to drive resistance.
  • Single-patient clinical example for AURKA inhibition — the LY3295668 case is hypothesis-generating, not a trial result.
