Preoperative clinical and tumor genomic features associated with pathologic lymph node metastasis in clinical stage I and II lung adenocarcinoma

TL;DR

In 426 treatment-naive patients with clinical N2-negative stage I/II lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) sequenced with MSK-IMPACT, the authors built a multivariable logistic regression model combining preoperative clinical features (solid CT morphology, tumor SUVmax, clinical stage) with tumor genomic features (SMARCA4 and SMAD4 alterations) to predict pathologic lymph node (LN) metastasis. Both gene alterations were independently associated with pN-positive disease, alongside higher fraction of genome altered (FGA), copy number amplifications, whole-genome doubling, and enrichment of APOBEC mutational signatures. The dataset is released as the cBioPortal study luad_msk_npjpo_2021.

Cohort & data

  • N = 426 treatment-naive LUAD patients who underwent complete anatomic resection (lobectomy, segmentectomy, or pneumonectomy) with LN dissection at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center between 2010 and 2018.
  • Inclusion required clinical N2-negative (cN0 or cN1) disease and successful MSK-IMPACT sequencing on the primary tumor. Exclusions: induction therapy, wedge resection, R1/R2 residual disease, low-quality NGS.
  • Composition: 60% (255) male; 74% (316) ever-smokers; 78% (334) clinical stage I, 22% (92) clinical stage II; 85% (362) cN0 and 15% (64) cN1.
  • Pathologic outcomes: pN0 80% (n=341), pN1 11% (n=48), pN2 9% (n=37); pN1 and pN2 were grouped as pN-positive.
  • Sequencing performed across MSK-IMPACT 341-, 410-, and 468-gene panels (0.98, 1.06, 1.22 Mb coding region respectively). Copy number and WGD called from FACETS (facets); focal copy number from GISTIC 2.0; mutational signatures computed against COSMIC SBS reference for samples with >=13.8 Mut/Mb.
  • Dataset published as luad_msk_npjpo_2021 on cBioPortal.

Key findings

  • Clinical false-negative rate of preoperative LN staging was high. Of 362 cN0 patients, 15% (n=54) had pathologic LN metastasis at surgery; of 64 cN1 patients, 48% (31/64) had confirmed pN+ disease. False-negative rate for preoperative clinical staging was 64% PMID:34290393.
  • Three genes were altered at significantly higher frequency in pN-positive tumors (Fisher’s exact, alterations in >=2% of cohort): STK11 22% vs. 12% (p=0.024), SMARCA4 8% vs. 1.8% (p=0.006), SMAD4 7% vs. 1.5% (p=0.011). RBM10 was significantly enriched in pN-negative tumors (17% vs. 8%; p=0.044).
  • Most frequently altered genes overall: KRAS (35% pN-neg / 34% pN-pos), TP53 (34% / 45%), EGFR (33% / 31%).
  • No significant difference in targetable LUAD alterations (n=9 genes) between pN groups (Supplementary Fig. 2).
  • TMB did not differ between groups (median 4.9 vs. 6.1 mut/Mb; p=0.058), but fraction of genome altered (FGA) was higher in pN-positive tumors (median 0.343 vs. 0.269; p=0.037).
  • Copy number amplifications were significantly higher in pN-positive tumors (median 0.152 vs. 0.085; p=0.001); deletions did not differ (p=0.299). Arm-level changes on 2p and 2q were enriched in pN+ tumors (FDR p=0.122 for both, threshold FDR p<0.2).
  • Gene-level CNAs (in >=1% of cohort): CDK4 amplifications were more frequent in pN-negative tumors (8% vs. 1.2%; p=0.029); CDKN2A alterations were more frequent in pN-positive tumors (9% vs. 4%; p=0.039). MDM2 was the most frequently CNA-altered gene in pN-negative tumors (10%).
  • Whole-genome doubling (WGD) was more frequent in pN-positive tumors (22% vs. 13%; p=0.028).
  • APOBEC mutational signatures (SBS2 and SBS13) were enriched in pN-positive tumors: SBS2 27.3% vs. 12.8% (p=0.03); SBS13 31.8% vs. 14.9% (p=0.02). Smoking signature (SBS4) did not differ.
  • Multivariable model (Fig. 4) independently associated with pN metastasis: solid vs. nonsolid CT morphology (OR 4.25, 95% CI 1.81-9.93, p=0.001); tumor SUVmax (OR 1.09, 95% CI 1.03-1.15, p=0.002); clinical stage II vs. I (OR 3.31, 95% CI 1.87-5.88, p<0.001); SMARCA4 alteration (OR 3.67, 95% CI 1.02-13.16, p=0.046); SMAD4 alteration (OR 5.01, 95% CI 1.29-19.45, p=0.02).
  • STK11 enrichment in pN+ disease did not survive the final multivariable model, although it was significant in stage-adjusted univariable analysis (p=0.026) and entered model selection.
  • Smoking subgroup analysis: STK11/SMARCA4/SMAD4/RBM10 enrichment was driven by ever-smokers; no significant pN+ vs. pN- difference among never-smokers (likely small-sample / lower TMB; Supplementary Fig. 1).

Genes & alterations

  • SMAD4 — alterations independently associated with pathologic LN metastasis (multivariable OR 5.01, p=0.02); 7% in pN+ vs. 1.5% in pN- (p=0.011). Authors note prior literature linking reduced SMAD4 expression in NSCLC to increased DNA damage, reduced DNA repair, and topoisomerase inhibitor sensitivity PMID:34290393.
  • SMARCA4 — alterations independently associated with pN metastasis (multivariable OR 3.67, p=0.046); 8% in pN+ vs. 1.8% in pN- (p=0.006). SWI/SNF subunit; loss linked to poor oncologic outcomes and to co-occurrence with KRAS/STK11/KEAP1 in prior work.
  • STK11 — enriched in pN+ tumors (22% vs. 12%, p=0.024) and entered the multivariable selection but did not retain independence in the final model.
  • RBM10 — significantly more altered in pN-negative tumors (17% vs. 8%, p=0.044).
  • KRAS, TP53, EGFR — most prevalent overall; no significant pN-status difference for KRAS or EGFR; TP53 nominally higher in pN+ (45% vs. 34%) but not flagged significant in the >=2% Fisher analysis.
  • CDKN2A — copy number alterations enriched in pN-positive tumors (9% vs. 4%, p=0.039).
  • CDK4 — amplifications enriched in pN-negative tumors (8% vs. 1.2%, p=0.029).
  • MDM2 — most frequently copy-number altered gene in pN-negative tumors (10%).
  • MGA — altered 2.1% pN- vs. 6% pN+ (asterisked as significant in OncoPrint).
  • Other genes assessed at >=2% frequency without significant pN association: TERT, MET, NF1, BRAF, KEAP1, PIK3CA, ERBB2, NKX2-1, ARID2, SETD2, RB1, ATM, PTPRT, CTNNB1, PTEN, MED12, ARID1A, APC.

Clinical implications

  • Risk-stratification before surgery. The authors propose that combining preoperative clinical features (CT morphology, SUVmax, clinical stage) with targetable preoperative-biopsy genomic data (SMAD4 and SMARCA4 alteration status) could identify clinical stage I/II LUAD patients at high risk of occult LN metastasis who might benefit from neoadjuvant therapy rather than upfront resection PMID:34290393.
  • APOBEC enrichment as immunotherapy biomarker. APOBEC signature enrichment in pN+ tumors is discussed as a potential predictor of immunotherapy response in NSCLC.
  • No actionable target shift. Frequency of canonical targetable LUAD drivers did not differ by pN status, so the model is for surgical-planning risk stratification rather than redirecting first-line targeted therapy.

Limitations & open questions

  • NGS performed on single-region sampling of the primary tumor; intratumoral heterogeneity in LUAD may cause this to underrepresent clonal complexity.
  • Genomic analyses were on surgical specimens, not preoperative biopsies. The model’s clinical utility presumes biopsy NGS is feasible, which the authors argue is now realistic at 80-90% success rates but is not demonstrated here.
  • Modest absolute frequency of SMARCA4 and SMAD4 alterations (8% and 7% in pN+) limits population impact, especially in never-smokers where these genes are rarely altered.
  • External validation required — the model has not been tested in an independent cohort.
  • Never-smoker subgroup is underpowered to test the same gene-level associations; small sample (n=110 never-smokers total, of whom only 19 were pN+) plus lower TMB obscures any signal.
  • pN1 and pN2 patients were pooled as “pN-positive”; the model does not stratify the magnitude of nodal disease.
