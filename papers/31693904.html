Genomic Profiling of Childhood Tumor Patient-Derived Xenograft Models to Enable Rational Clinical Trial Design

Authors

Rokita JL

Rathi KS

Cardenas MF

Upton KA

Jespinosa-Cotton M

Cole KA

Maris JM

Doi

10.1016/j.celrep.2019.09.071

PMID: 31693904 · DOI: 10.1016/j.celrep.2019.09.071 · Journal: Cell Reports (2019)

TL;DR

Rokita and colleagues from the Pediatric Preclinical Testing Consortium (PPTC) report a comprehensive genomic characterization of 261 patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models spanning 37 unique pediatric cancer molecular subtypes. They performed whole-exome sequencing (n=240), RNA-seq (n=244), SNP microarrays (n=252), and STR profiling (n=261), and show that PDX models faithfully recapitulate the mutational, copy-number, and fusion landscapes of their histologies of origin — including relapsed disease. They also derive RNA-expression classifiers for TP53 and NF1 pathway inactivation as candidate response biomarkers. Data are deposited in pptc_2019 and queryable via PedcBioPortal as a public resource for pediatric oncology drug development.

Cohort & data

  • 261 PDX models from 37 unique pediatric cancer molecular subtypes (29 unique histologies), drawn from the Pediatric Preclinical Testing Consortium (PPTC) — successor to the Pediatric Preclinical Testing Program (PPTP). 26 full models and 3 RNA samples were excluded during QC for contamination or misidentification.
  • Major histology groups (cancer types linked where canonical OncoTree codes exist):
    • B-cell precursor BLL (BCP-ALL), Ph-like / Ph+ BLL, MLL-rearranged BLL.
    • T-cell / ETP TLL (T-ALL and ETP-ALL).
    • NBL (neuroblastoma).
    • OS (osteosarcoma).
    • ES (Ewing sarcoma).
    • WT (Wilms tumor, n=13).
    • RMS — ERMS (Fusion−) and ARMS (Fusion+, PAX3-FOXO1).
    • MBL (medulloblastoma, n=20) spanning SHH, WNT, group 3, group 4.
    • ATRT and extracranial MRT (rhabdoid).
    • EPMT (ependymomas: ST-EPN-RELA, ST-EPN-YAP, PF-EPN-A/B).
    • HGGNOS / glioblastoma / PXA / MYCN-subtype HGG.
    • DIFG / DMG including histone H3-wild-type DIPG (n=2).
    • ASPS (alveolar soft part sarcoma) and rare histologies (n=7).
    • PPB (pleuropulmonary blastoma).
    • ALAL (ambiguous/mixed-lineage leukemia).
  • Assays / methods: patient-derived xenograft generation (subcutaneous flank for solid; intracranial for CNS; intravenous in NOD/SCID or NSG for leukemias); whole-exome sequencing on the HGSC VCRome 2.1 capture (240 models); RNA-seq (strand-specific poly-A+, 244 models); Illumina HumanOmniExpress-24 SNP arrays for segmentation and ethnicity inference (252 models); STR profiling for identity verification (all 261 models).
  • Fusion calling combined four algorithms: deFuse, FusionCatcher, STAR-Fusion, and SOAPfuse, yielding 925 unique high-confidence fusions and 92 known oncogenic driver fusions.
  • Bioinformatic methods: hybrid hg19-mm10 competitive mapping for mouse-read subtraction; MutSigCV for SMG filtering; GSEA on Hallmark and TissGDB/TiGER gene sets; UCSC TumorMap for expression-cluster visualization; previously trained machine-learning classifiers for TP53 inactivation and NF1 inactivation (Knijnenburg et al. 2018; Way et al. 2017, 2018).
  • Common germline filtering used a TCGA panel of 809 normal samples; rare germline variants (<0.005 MAF in ExAC / 1000 Genomes / NHLBI ESP) were retained in the “somatic” MAF, so reported TMBs are likely inflated relative to matched-normal cohorts.
  • Predicted ancestry inferred from SNP array genotypes: 71% European, 11.5% South Asian/Hispanic, 9.1% African, 5.5% mixed/unknown, 2.4% East Asian.

Key findings

  • PDX models faithfully recapitulate driver landscapes.
    • BCP-ALL (BLL): 45–48% have canonical homozygous focal deletions of CDKN2A / CDKN2B on 9p; RAS-pathway enrichment (KRAS 30%, NRAS 18%); JAK-STAT enrichment (JAK2/JAK3 altered in 15%); KMT2D altered in 15%; fusion transcripts in 78% (25/33), commonly involving ETV6 (27%, 88% of those partnered with RUNX1), PAX5 (18%), and CRLF2 (6%).
    • ETP-ALL / T-ALL (TLL): CDKN2A/B focal deletions in 72–76%; NOTCH1 mutations in 68%; JAK1 or JAK2 lesions in 24%; STAT5B lesions in 4%; oncogenic fusions in ~48% (commonly TRBC2, TRBC1, ABL1, IGH, LMAN2, LMO1, LMO2, ETV6).
    • Ph-like / Ph+ ALL: BCR-ABL1 fusion confirmed in all three Ph+ models (ALL-04, ALL-55, ALL-56). 8/19 Ph-like models (42%) carry a canonical CRLF2 fusion (7 partnered with P2RY8, 1 with IGHM); JAK2 rearrangements in 55% (12/22); PAX5 rearrangements in 23% (5/22); CDKN2A/B deletions in 45% (10/22).
    • MLL-ALL: all models carry a canonical KMT2A fusion and have relatively silent genomes; majority derived from patients <1 year of age.
    • Neuroblastoma (NBL): MYCN amplification in 66% (most frequent alteration across the cohort), largely mutually exclusive with 11q deletion; 1p deletion in 77%; 17q gain in 97%; ALK hotspot mutations in 37%; additional alterations in TP53 (11%), PTPN11 (9%), NF1 (9%), BRAF (3%), CIC (3%), KRAS (3%).
    • Osteosarcoma (OS): TP53 altered in 82% of models; classifier-predicted TP53 pathway inactivation in 32/33 RNA-seq models (all except OS-55-SBX); global copy-number complexity consistent with chromothripsis (97% of OS models with high breakpoint density, 33/34).
    • Ewing sarcoma (ES): canonical EWSR1-FLI1 fusion in all RNA-seq-profiled models; CHLA-258 additionally harbored an FLI1-RP11-9L18.2 fusion; TP53 mutations in 7/10 (70%); homozygous CDKN2A/B loss (60%) mutually exclusive with STAG2 mutations (20%).
    • Wilms tumor (WT, n=13): WT1 hemizygous deletion in 61%; 11p15.5 LOH (imprinting region) in 69% (9/13); AMER1 (WTX) hemizygous deletions in 15% (2 models); CTNNB1 mutations in 15% (KT-6 and NCH-WT-6-S13-1506), mutually exclusive with WTX alterations; KT-9 (bilateral disease) harbored a TP53-FXR2 fusion plus partial homozygous TP53 deletion.
    • Rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS): all Fusion+ models (ARMS) carried PAX3-FOXO1; median patient age was 16y for Fusion+ vs 5y for Fusion− (ERMS); RAS-pathway mutations (NRAS, HRAS, KRAS, NF1) observed in 3/6 ERMS models. MYCN and CDK4 focal amplifications also observed; MYOD1 overexpressed in all but IRS-68.
    • Rhabdoid (ATRT / MRT): all carry inactivating alterations of SMARCB1 and/or SMARCA4; hedgehog, TNFα, and p53 signaling enriched.
  • Brain-tumor sub-classification. 20 medulloblastoma models span SHH (n=7), WNT (n=2), group 3 (n=7), group 4 (n=3); ATRT models include SHH (n=3) and MYC (n=3) subgroups; ETMR models (ICb-S1129MB, ICb-1343ENB, IBs-2373PNET) carry C19MC amplification, LIN28A overexpression, and/or TTYH1 fusions; CNS Ewing-like models (ICb-9850PNET and IC-22909PNET-rIII) carry CIC-DUX4 fusions; NCH-MN-1 (originally a rhabdoid meningioma suspected of being ATRT) was reclassified as high-grade glioma based on BRAF V600E plus CDKN2A/CDKN2B deletion. IC-1621GBM (Lynch / DNA mismatch-repair deficiency syndrome) carried 124 somatic mutations/Mb plus mutations in PMS1 (Q316), MSH2 (G721), MSH5, and POLE.
  • Relapse mutational landscape. Median TMB across all PDX models = 2.66 somatic Mut/Mb. PDXs derived from relapse tissue had significantly higher TMB than diagnosis-derived (3.08 vs 2.57 Mut/Mb, Wilcoxon p = 2.2e−5); the effect was significant in NBL (p = 0.016) and T-ALL (p = 0.0081), trended in BCP-ALL (p = 0.054), and was absent in OS (p = 0.42). In paired diagnosis-relapse PDXs, the relapse PDX had higher TMB (median 98.0 vs 27.5 mutations; Wilcoxon p = 0.0083).
  • Chromothripsis / structural complexity. 25% (64/252) of models had high breakpoint density (HBD) on one or more chromosomes. 97% (33/34) of osteosarcomas had HBD; 30% (10/33) on ≤4 chromosomes (localized chromothripsis) and 70% on ≥5 chromosomes (global rearrangement). Neuroblastoma HBD concentrated on chromosomes 2, 5, 16, 17, 19 — consistent with prior reports linking chromosome 2/5/17 chromothripsis to MYCN amplification, TERT rearrangement, and 17q gain respectively.
  • Expression classifiers for TP53 / NF1 pathway inactivation. Pre-trained TCGA PanCanAtlas classifiers (Knijnenburg 2018; Way 2017/2018) achieved AUROC = 0.89 for TP53 and AUROC = 0.77 for NF1, but only 0.55 for the RAS classifier (suggesting pediatric-specific RAS biology). TP53 classifier scores were significantly higher in TP53-altered (mean 0.790) vs unaltered (mean 0.419) models (Wilcoxon p < 2.2e−16). Models with alterations in MDM2 and RB1 also scored high — phenocopying TP53 loss. Across non-OS models, fusions in TP53 pathway genes produced the highest classifier scores, followed by SNVs and CNVs (Kruskal-Wallis p = 9.8e−11). Copy-number burden (but not TMB) correlated with TP53 classifier score (R = 0.51, p = 1.8e−17), supporting a causal link between TP53 inactivation and chromosomal instability.
  • Fusion catalog. 50,796 unique fusions called across the cohort were filtered to 925 high-confidence and 92 known oncogenic driver fusions. Five diagnosis-relapse pairs revealed four fusions present at diagnosis but lost at relapse (PAX5-RP11-465M18.1, IGH-MYC, CIC-DUX4, TP53-TNR), suggesting these were acquired after an alternative initiating event that was retained.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — altered in 82% of osteosarcomas, 70% of Ewing sarcomas, 11% of neuroblastomas; loss/inactivation is a recurrent osteosarcoma hallmark with TP53 intron-1 fusion breakpoints, including TP53-FXR2 (Wilms KT-9) and TP53-TNR (diagnostic-only). Inactivation classifier (AUROC 0.89) distinguishes “TP53-pathway-inactive” tumors even among TP53-wild-type models.
  • NF1 — altered in 9% of neuroblastoma models (nonsense/frameshift); inactivation classifier (AUROC 0.77) flags additional pathway-inactive models.
  • MYCN — focal amplification in 66% of neuroblastoma models (the cohort’s most frequent alteration); also amplified in MYCN-subtype HGG (IC-2664PNET) and in ARMS.
  • ALK — hotspot mutations in 37% of neuroblastomas; also flagged as a high-expression immunotherapy target.
  • CDKN2A / CDKN2B — homozygous 9p deletions in 45–48% of BCP-ALL, 72–76% of T-ALL/ETP-ALL, 45% of Ph/Ph-like ALL, 60% of Ewing sarcoma; concurrent with BRAF V600E in NCH-MN-1.
  • SMARCB1 / SMARCA4 — inactivating focal deletions, frameshifts, or nonsense mutations in all ATRT and extracranial MRT models; also three astrocytic tumors carried SMARCB1 hemizygous deletions (consistent with 6.7–7.5% reported in pediatric HGG cohorts).
  • EWSR1-FLI1 — canonical fusion in all RNA-seq-profiled Ewing sarcoma models.
  • PAX3-FOXO1 — fusion in all Fusion+ (ARMS) models.
  • ASPSCR1-TFE3 — canonical fusion detected in both ASPS models.
  • BCR-ABL1 — fusion in all three Ph+ ALL models.
  • KMT2A — fusions in all MLL-ALL models.
  • CIC-DUX4 — fusion in CNS Ewing-like model (ICb-9850PNET).
  • CTNNB1 — mutations in 15% of Wilms models (mutually exclusive with AMER1/WTX); also p.D32G in NCH-HEP1 hepatic model.
  • KRAS, NRAS, HRAS — RAS pathway mutations enriched in BCP-ALL and in Fusion− RMS (e.g. Rh-12 NF1 T2335fs; NCH-ERMS-1 NRAS Q61K; Rh-36 HRAS Q61K).
  • NOTCH1 — mutated in 68% of T-ALL/ETP-ALL.
  • WT1 — hemizygous deletions in 61% of Wilms models (often with 11p13 LOH).
  • H3-3A / H3-3B — both DIPG models (IBs-P1215DIPG, IBs-W0128DIPG) express histone H3.3 at high levels (FPKM > 50) but lack canonical H3.1/H3.3 mutations; IBs-W0128DIPG carries predicted-damaging RNA-derived missense variants in NRAS (G13R), CIC (C102Y), and KMT2C (C988F).
  • GPC2, ALK — both dramatically overexpressed in neuroblastoma models and in additional pediatric histotypes, flagged as antibody-drug-conjugate and T-cell-engager targets for basket-trial design.
  • CD274 (PD-L1) — intrinsic overexpression in ASPS xenografts, flagged as a checkpoint-inhibitor target.
  • AKR1C3 — RNA-seq identified TLL (T-ALL) as a target histology for an AKR1C3-activated prodrug.
  • MDM2, RB1 — alterations phenocopy TP53 inactivation per expression classifier.
  • MSH2, MSH5, PMS1, POLE — co-occurring mutations in IC-1621GBM (Lynch / constitutional MMR-deficiency); ultrahypermutated at 124 Mut/Mb.

Clinical implications

  • Resource for rational preclinical trial design. The authors explicitly frame the PPTC PDX dataset as a queryable resource (via PedcBioPortal) for selecting models that match a candidate-drug’s biomarker. Worked example: querying for ATM loss, ARID1A mutation, defective homologous recombination, or ATRX mutation/ALT identifies PDXs suitable for testing ATR inhibitors.
  • Biomarker-driven basket-trial design. RNA-seq-defined high expression of immunotherapy targets (GPC2 and ALK in neuroblastoma and other pediatric histotypes; CD274/PD-L1 in ASPS; AKR1C3-activated prodrug in T-ALL) supports preclinical basket design across histologies.
  • TP53 / NF1 pathway-activity scores as candidate response biomarkers. Authors propose using the expression-based TP53 inactivation classifier and NF1 inactivation classifier — rather than gene-level mutation status alone — to nominate tumors for pathway-targeted therapy. The classifiers identify functionally inactive cases driven by MDM2, RB1, or other phenocopies, and may be future biomarkers for response to TP53-pathway-targeting and NF1-loss-directed agents.
  • Relapse-enriched PDXs match the early-phase pediatric trial population. 27% of models were derived from relapsed disease and 6% from post-neoadjuvant resections, so the cohort more faithfully recapitulates the post-chemoradiotherapy phase-1 patient population than diagnosis-only sequencing studies.

Limitations & open questions

  • No paired normal samples. Rare germline variants could not be removed, so reported TMB values are likely inflated; trends across histology and phase of therapy should remain valid, but absolute Mut/Mb cannot be directly compared with matched-normal cohorts.
  • WES misses pathogenic non-coding lesions (e.g., the mechanism of TP53 inactivation in OS-34-SJ, OS-43-TPMX, and OS-51-CHLX remains unresolved and may require whole-genome sequencing).
  • No DNA methylation profiling — particularly limiting for pediatric brain tumors where methylation-based classification is now standard.
  • Histology gaps. No models for acute myelogenous leukemia, juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia, lymphomas, retinoblastoma, melanoma, thyroid malignancies, or histone-mutant midline gliomas. Several rare-histology subtypes have low n.
  • RAS-pathway expression classifier underperforms (AUROC 0.55), likely reflecting biology differences between pediatric and adult tumors; the TP53 inactivation classifier and NF1 inactivation classifier classifiers also require prospective validation in PDX response studies before clinical translation.
  • PDX cohort is biased toward highly aggressive disease, reflecting the populations enrolled in pediatric phase-1 trials but not the broader pediatric oncology disease spectrum.
  • Selection / passage effects. A few alterations (e.g. CDK4 amplification in Rh-30) are not retained between paired diagnosis and relapse PDXs, complicating direct genotype-phenotype mapping at the model level.
