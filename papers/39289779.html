Real-world experience with circulating tumor DNA in cerebrospinal fluid from patients with central nervous system tumors

PMID: 39289779 · DOI: 10.1186/s40478-024-01846-4 · Journal: Acta Neuropathologica Communications (2024)

TL;DR

This study reports on the clinical implementation of prospective CSF circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) sequencing using MSK-IMPACT at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center. The authors profiled 1,007 CSF samples from 711 patients with over 90 distinct CNS tumor types (primary and metastatic) collected between November 2018 and November 2022. Genetic alterations were detected in 53% of CSF samples from patients with clinically documented CNS tumors (489/922), while none of 85 samples from patients without CNS tumors had detectable ctDNA (100% specificity). ctDNA positivity was associated with a three-fold increased risk of death (HR 3.23, p < 0.001), and the distribution of clinically actionable alterations was consistent with tumor-type specific patterns seen in the AACR GENIE cohort PMID:39289779.

Cohort & data

  • Sample size: 1,007 CSF samples from 711 patients (adult and pediatric); 150/711 patients (21.1%) had multiple CSF samples (median 2, range 2-12) PMID:39289779.
  • Cancer types: Over 90 distinct tumor types including lung cancer (n=188), breast cancer (n=150), gliomas (n=148), metastatic melanoma, GI cancers, embryonal tumors, and others PMID:39289779.
  • Negative controls: 85/1,007 CSF samples from patients without clinically documented CNS involvement PMID:39289779.
  • Dataset: csf_msk_2024 PMID:39289779.
  • Assays: IMPACT468 (n=274 samples) and IMPACT505 (n=733 samples); a subset compared with ACCESS129 plasma ctDNA (n=31 patients) PMID:39289779.
  • Collection period: November 2018 to November 2022; median follow-up 240 days (IQR 112-483 days) PMID:39289779.

Key findings

  • ctDNA detection rate: 53.0% (489/922) of CSF samples from patients with clinically documented CNS tumors harbored at least one somatic genetic alteration PMID:39289779.
  • Specificity: 100% (0/85 positive among patients without CNS tumors) PMID:39289779.
  • Total alterations detected: 7,110 somatic alterations across 489 ctDNA-positive samples (3,944 non-synonymous mutations, 2,980 copy number alterations, 186 structural rearrangements) PMID:39289779.
  • Variant allele frequencies: Median VAF 38.7% (IQR 23.2-51.1%) across ctDNA-positive samples, reflecting the high tumor purity of CSF cfDNA PMID:39289779.
  • Clinical actionability: 50.7% (248/489) of ctDNA-positive samples carried a level 1 OncoKB actionable alteration. Lung carcinomas had the highest level 1 actionability PMID:39289779.
  • Survival association: ctDNA positivity was associated with a three-fold increased risk of death (HR 3.23, 95% CI 2.58-4.05, p < 0.001). Median OS was 854 days shorter in ctDNA-positive patients (235 days vs. 1,089 days) PMID:39289779.
  • Leptomeningeal disease detection (lung cancer): In NSCLC patients, leptomeningeal involvement was a strong predictor of ctDNA positivity (OR 20.17, 95% CI 9.65-42.16, p < 0.0001). CSF ctDNA had greater sensitivity than positive cytology for leptomeningeal disease (85.4% vs. 61.7%) and greater negative predictive value (80% vs. 66%) PMID:39289779.
  • CSF vs. tumor tissue: Among 56 tumor/CSF pairs (55 patients), 43% of alterations were shared, 27% private to CSF, 29% private to tumor. CSF VAFs were significantly higher than tumor tissue (median 32% vs. 24%, p < 0.01) PMID:39289779.
  • CSF vs. plasma: Among 31 patients, 54% of alterations were shared between CSF and plasma. CSF VAFs were markedly higher (median 36.4% vs. 2.3%, p < 0.01), demonstrating the superiority of CSF over plasma for CNS tumor monitoring PMID:39289779.
  • Pre-analytic factors: Volumes below 2 mL critically impacted ctDNA positivity (as low as 8.3% for primary CNS tumors). Optimal volume was 5 mL or above. Delays beyond 7 days reduced ctDNA proportion but did not eliminate detection PMID:39289779.
  • Mutational signatures: In samples with TMB >= 13.8 mutations/Mb (n=35), signatures for UV (n=3), APOBEC (n=7), smoking (n=8), and temozolomide (n=2) were identified in melanoma, breast, lung, and glioma samples, respectively PMID:39289779.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53: Most frequently altered gene across all tumor types (n=242, 49% of 489 ctDNA-positive samples) PMID:39289779.
  • EGFR: Mutations and high-level amplification in lung cancer; acquired resistance mutations detected in CSF including p.T790M, p.C797S, p.L792H, p.L718Q, p.L718V, p.G724S PMID:39289779.
  • MET: Amplification in lung cancer; exon 14 skipping mutations detected; resistance mutation p.Y1230N emerged on crizotinib PMID:39289779.
  • ERBB2: Mutations and amplification in lung cancer and breast cancer PMID:39289779.
  • KRAS: Mutations in lung cancer; off-target resistance alteration in EGFR-mutant patients PMID:39289779.
  • BRAF: p.V600E in metastatic melanoma; fusions as off-target resistance in EGFR-mutant lung cancer; BRAF::KIAA1549 fusions in gliomas PMID:39289779.
  • PIK3CA: p.E545K common in breast cancers; off-target resistance alteration in EGFR-mutant lung cancer PMID:39289779.
  • ALK: EML4::ALK fusions in lung carcinomas; resistance mutations p.G1202R and p.G1269A detected upon targeted therapy progression PMID:39289779.
  • EML4: Fusion partner in EML4::ALK rearrangements in lung carcinomas PMID:39289779.
  • RET: Rearrangements with diverse gene partners in lung carcinomas PMID:39289779.
  • ROS1: Rearrangements with diverse gene partners in lung carcinomas PMID:39289779.
  • IDH1: p.R132H in IDH-mutant gliomas PMID:39289779.
  • STK11: Mutations in lung cancer PMID:39289779.
  • KEAP1: Mutations in lung cancer PMID:39289779.
  • H3-3A: p.K28M histone mutation supporting diagnosis of diffuse midline glioma in CSF ctDNA PMID:39289779.
  • KIAA1549: BRAF::KIAA1549 fusions detected in gliomas PMID:39289779.

Clinical implications

  • CSF ctDNA sequencing via MSK-IMPACT is feasible and clinically informative in a routine hospital setting, providing actionable cancer genotype information in a large fraction of patients with CNS tumors PMID:39289779.
  • CSF ctDNA had greater sensitivity than cytology for leptomeningeal disease detection in lung cancer patients (85.4% vs. 61.7%), supporting its use as a complementary or alternative diagnostic modality PMID:39289779.
  • Serial CSF ctDNA profiling identified clonal evolution and emergence of resistance mechanisms (EGFR gatekeeper mutations, ALK resistance mutations, MET resistance mutations), directly informing treatment changes PMID:39289779.
  • ctDNA detection in CSF was associated with significantly shortened overall survival (HR 3.23), suggesting prognostic utility of CSF ctDNA status PMID:39289779.
  • CSF liquid biopsy outperformed plasma for CNS cancer monitoring, with significantly higher VAFs (median 36.4% vs. 2.3%) due to blood-brain barrier limiting tumor DNA shedding into peripheral blood PMID:39289779.
  • Mutational signatures in CSF ctDNA aided in determining or confirming the primary tumor site (e.g., UV signatures for melanoma, smoking signatures for lung adenocarcinoma) PMID:39289779.

Limitations & open questions

  • Sensitivity ceiling: Only 53% of CSF samples from patients with known CNS disease were ctDNA-positive; the 47% false-negative rate limits clinical utility as a standalone diagnostic test PMID:39289779.
  • Volume dependence: Samples below 2 mL had ctDNA positivity rates as low as 8.3%, creating a practical barrier when CSF volume is limited PMID:39289779.
  • Heterogeneous cohort: The study encompasses over 90 tumor types with variable ctDNA shedding; disease-specific conclusions are limited for rarer subtypes PMID:39289779.
  • Lack of treatment outcome data: While the study describes actionable alterations and resistance mutations, it does not systematically report treatment decisions or outcomes driven by CSF ctDNA findings PMID:39289779.
  • Single-institution study: All samples from MSK; generalizability to settings without equivalent pre-analytic infrastructure and sequencing capabilities is uncertain PMID:39289779.
  • No longitudinal resistance analysis at scale: Clonal evolution and resistance monitoring examples are illustrative case studies rather than systematic longitudinal analyses PMID:39289779.
  • CSF vs. plasma comparison limited: Only 31 patients had contemporaneous CSF and plasma samples, and comparison was restricted to the overlapping genomic regions of MSK-IMPACT and MSK-ACCESS PMID:39289779.
