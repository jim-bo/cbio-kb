Proteogenomic Characterization of Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma

Authors

Cao L

Huang C

Cui Zhou DC

Hu Y

Lih TM

Savage SR

Krug K

Clark DJ

Schnaubelt M

Ding L

Doi

10.1016/j.cell.2021.08.023

PMID: 34534465 · DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2021.08.023 · Journal: Cell (2021)

TL;DR

The CPTAC consortium performed integrated proteogenomic characterization of 140 treatment-naive pancreatic cancers (135 PDACs + 5 adenosquamous), 67 paired normal adjacent tissues (NATs), and 9 macrodissected normal pancreatic ductal tissues using WES, WGS, RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, DNA methylation, TMT proteomics, phosphoproteomics, and glycoproteomics. KRAS alterations were detected in 97% of tumors. After filtering to 105 tumors with sufficient neoplastic cellularity (KRAS VAF ≥ 0.075), the authors mapped genomic alterations to functional protein/phospho/glyco effects, identified candidate early-detection biomarkers and glycoprotein/kinase therapeutic targets, characterized an “immune-cold” subtype driven by endothelial remodeling and glycolysis, and defined two proteogenomic subtypes (C1 classical, C2 basal-like) with stronger prognostic separation than the Moffitt RNA-only subtypes (CoxPH HR 3.4 vs 2.3).

Cohort & data

  • 140 treatment-naive pancreatic tumors (135 PDAC + 5 pancreatic adenosquamous carcinomas; PAAD, PAASC), 67 paired NATs, and 9 normal macrodissected pancreatic ductal tissues; cohort = paad_cptac_2021.
  • Prospectively collected from multiple international source sites with controlled ischemia time; 75% of resected tumors from pancreatic head; 59% Stage I–II, 9 Stage IV; 42% alive at data freeze. Risk-factor prevalence: smoking 37%, chronic pancreatitis 22%, obesity 11%, type II diabetes 28%.
  • 8 omics layers on the same tissue portion: whole-exome-seq, whole-genome-seq, rna-seq, mirna-seq, dna-methylation-array, tmt-proteomics, tmt-phosphoproteomics, and N/O-linked glycoproteomics (via lc-ms-ms-proteomics).
  • Quantification yields: 28,057 genes (RNA), 2,416 miRNAs, 850,000 CpG sites, 11,662 proteins (8,781/sample), 51,469 phosphosites (25,764/sample), 34,024 glycopeptides (30,660 N-linked + 3,364 O-linked).
  • Subtyping/deconvolution used xcell, DNA-methylation deconvolution, nmf-clustering, and gsea.

Key findings

  • KRAS landscape. KRAS alterations in 97% of 140 tumors (96% hotspot mutations + 1 amplification); G12D, G12V, and G12R were the dominant hotspots. TP53 altered in 83%, CDKN2A in 48% (CNV-driven; more focal deletions than intragenic mutations), SMAD4 in 29% of the 105 high-cellularity tumors.
  • Additional SMGs (>5% of tumors): ARID1A, RNF43, GNAS, KMT2C, KMT2D, TGFBR2, RBM10.
  • Cellularity gating. 105/140 samples passed neoplastic-purity QC (KRAS VAF ≥ 0.075, equivalent to ≥15% neoplastic cellularity, or significant mutation burden + CNVs); 4 KRAS-WT tumors were retained based on other SMG alterations. KRAS VAF correlated with DNA-methylation deconvolution at Spearman r = 0.81.
  • RNA-protein correlation median 0.35 across tumors (0.36 tumor-only, 0.26 NAT-only), consistent with prior CPTAC tissue types.
  • Genomic-to-proteomic trans-effects. TP53 mutations had the largest trans-effect footprint, with elevated phosphorylation of DNA-damage-repair proteins (MSH6, TP53, TP53BP1) and the proliferation marker MKI67-S1376. SMAD4 mutations down-regulated SERPINE1 RNA/protein and up-regulated MAPK3 + E2F4 phosphorylation. SMAD4 deletions raised phospho-HDAC1 (S406/S410/S421), phospho-SMARCA4 S613, and phospho-CREBBP S274; CDKN2A deletions raised phospho-NPM1 S70/S214. RBM10 mutations showed a cis-effect increasing cognate mRNA/protein/phospho levels.
  • Epigenetics. 86 epigenetically silenced genes identified (22 also reported by TCGA); two (ZNF544, THNSL2) silenced in >10% of tumors and associated with patient survival. Two methylation clusters (M1, M2) identified.
  • Early-detection candidates. 27 proteins remained > 2-fold up-regulated in PDAC vs NAT after stromal/immune adjustment; 21 also > 2-fold up vs normal ductal tissue and similarly elevated in early-stage tumors. 12 are secreted (serum/pancreatic-juice candidates). Examples include THBS2, LAMC2, LOXL2 (associated with shorter overall survival), HK2, COL12A1, C19orf33, TSPAN1, MDK, SDR16C5, ANKRD22; the authors are first to report elevated GSDMB and LECT2 in PDAC. 14/21 validated by orthogonal DIA mass spectrometry.
  • Glycoproteomics. 75 N-linked glycoproteins upregulated > 2-fold in tumors (48/75 DIA-validated); 18 were newly identified. Mucin-type O-linked glycoproteins MUC1, MUC3A, MUC5AC, MUC5B, MUC13, MUC16 were significantly up-regulated, including in early-stage tumors. CEACAM5 and CEACAM6 were upregulated in tumors with KRAS G12D, G12V, and Q61H — but not G12R — mutations.
  • Glycosylation enzyme dysregulation. ST6GAL1, ST3GAL1, FUT3, FUT11, B4GALT1, B4GALT4, B3GALT5, ST6GALNAC1 were elevated in PDAC vs NAT; FUT3/FUT11/B4GALT1/B4GALT4 validated by DIA. ST6GAL1/ST3GAL1/B4GALT1 elevation was protein-specific (not seen at the transcript level), supporting sialyl/fucosyltransferase inhibition as a therapeutic hypothesis.
  • Kinase-substrate signaling. Five kinase-substrate axes with FDA-approved or investigational inhibitors were identified across 41 tumor/NAT pairs: CDK7–MCM2, AKT1–FLNA, PAK1–BAD (S134), PAK2–MAPK6 (S189), SRC–STAT3. PAK1 elevated in >70% and PAK2 in ~90% of tumors; concordant up-regulation of MET with KRAS, RAC1, PAK1, PAK2 was observed. 19 kinases stratified by KRAS hotspot, including 7 FDA-approved drug targets.
  • Immune subtyping (all 140 samples). xCell-based deconvolution into 4 clusters (A–D). Cluster D = “immune hot” (high CD8+ T-cell infiltration, cytotoxic enzymes, immune checkpoint molecules; only ~6% of tumors, with one case explained by lymph-node contamination). Clusters B and C are “immune-cold” tumors (cluster A enriched with non-neoplastic acinar/islet cells). Immune-hot tumors were also enriched for endothelial cells; immune-cold tumors had reduced endothelial-adhesion proteins, elevated VEGF and hypoxia pathway activity, increased glycolysis (GAPDH, PGAM1, ENO1 phosphorylation), and elevated phosphorylation of cell-junction proteins.
  • Proteogenomic subtyping (105 high-purity tumors). NMF on CNV + mRNA + protein + phosphosite + glycosite revealed two clusters: C1 (proteogenomic classical; pancreas beta-cell, bile-acid/fatty-acid metabolism, KRAS-signaling suppression) and C2 (proteogenomic basal-like; EMT, DNA repair, glycolysis, hypoxia, apoptosis, ROS, proliferation/signaling). Subtypes overlap Moffitt classical/basal-like but reclassify 22/97 tumors. Proteogenomic-dichotomized subtypes had stronger prognostic separation than Moffitt RNA-only (CoxPH HR 3.4 vs 2.3). C1 enriched for SMAD4 mutations; C2 enriched for higher MTOR/MAP3K2/MAP4K4/MET expression and TGF-β signaling. DSigDB GSEA associated chemotherapeutic-drug signatures (docetaxel, vinblastine, cabazitaxel) with C1 and kinase-inhibitor signatures (PP-242/mTOR, CP466722/ATM-ATR, sunitinib/PDGFR-VEGFR) with C2.
  • Survival associations. CD8+ T-cell infiltration was favorable; elevated VEGF and hypoxia pathway signaling both associated with decreased survival. C1 correlated with longer survival and tumor-free status; C2 correlated with tumor necrosis and Stage IV status (persisted after excluding Stage IV tumors). 392 proteins and 258 phosphosites carried significant prognostic value (CoxPH adj. p < 0.15).

Genes & alterations

  • KRAS — 97% prevalence in this PDAC cohort; G12D/G12V/G12R hotspot dominance; G12C present in <1% of PDACs (relevant to adagrasib/MRTX849 and sotorasib targeting). Drives AKT1 and PAK1/PAK2 signaling axes.
  • TP53 — altered in 83% of high-purity tumors; missense mutations show cis-effect on TP53 protein and TP53-S315 phosphosite; both missense and truncating alterations show trans-effects on MKI67-S1376, MSH6-S219, TP53BP1-S321.
  • CDKN2A — altered in 48% (CNV-driven focal deletions > intragenic mutations); deletions raise phospho-NPM1 S70/S214.
  • SMAD4 — altered in 29% (more frequent in C1 subtype); downregulates SERPINE1 mRNA/protein; deletions raise phospho-HDAC1 (S406/S410/S421), phospho-SMARCA4 S613, phospho-CREBBP S274.
  • ARID1A, RNF43, GNAS, KMT2C, KMT2D, TGFBR2, RBM10 — each altered in ≥5% of tumors.
  • GATA6 — focal amplification identified among arm-level/focal CNVs (9p, 11q, 18q, 22q amps; CDKN2A del).
  • PAK1 / PAK2 — elevated in >70% and ~90% of tumors respectively; downstream effectors of KRAS via RAC1; combined PAK1/2 + MAPK-ERK/PI3K-AKT-mTOR inhibition proposed.
  • MET — concordantly upregulated with KRAS/RAC1/PAK1/PAK2 at RNA and protein levels.
  • CEACAM5, CEACAM6 — upregulated specifically in KRAS G12D/G12V/Q61H tumors (not G12R); CEACAM6 overexpression linked to gemcitabine resistance and low cytolytic T-cell activity.
  • BRCA1, BRCA2 — referenced as germline-mutation context for PARP-inhibitor therapy in PDAC.

Clinical implications

  • Early detection biomarkers. 12 secreted proteins (subset of the 21 PDAC-vs-normal up-regulated set) are candidate serum/pancreatic-juice early-detection markers, including THBS2, LAMC2, LOXL2, GSDMB, LECT2. 14/21 validated by DIA mass spectrometry. Mucin glycoproteins (MUC1, MUC5AC, MUC13) and LGALS3BP enriched even in early-stage tumors.
  • KRAS hotspot-stratified therapy. Anti-CEACAM5/6 monoclonal antibodies (e.g., preclinical MN-15, MN-3) coupled with first-line chemotherapy proposed for KRAS G12D/G12V/Q61H tumors based on selective CEACAM5/6 upregulation in these hotspots.
  • KRAS-downstream targeting. adagrasib (MRTX849) and analogous KRAS-G12C inhibitors apply to <1% of PDACs; for the broader cohort the authors propose combined PAK1/PAK2 inhibition with MAPK-ERK and PI3K-AKT-mTOR inhibitors as a strategy to target KRAS-driven signaling.
  • Glycosylation-enzyme inhibition. Sialyl-/fucosyltransferase inhibition (targeting ST6GAL1, ST3GAL1, FUT3, FUT11, B4GALT1) proposed as therapeutic strategy; FUT3 upregulation in C1 subtype suggests subtype-tailored glycan-capping blockade. Authors caution that systemic sialylation blockade (e.g., 3F-NeuAc) caused liver/kidney toxicity in mouse models.
  • Immune-cold biology. Antiangiogenic therapy (sorafenib, NGR-TNF) and HIF-1 inhibition proposed to normalize endothelial cells and promote immune infiltration in immune-cold PDACs. Immune checkpoint blockade remains effective only for MSI-H tumors (<2% of PDACs).
  • Subtype-directed treatment. sunitinib (PDGFR/VEGFR) and PP-242/mTOR-axis inhibitors enriched in C2 (basal-like) drug signatures; chemotherapeutics (docetaxel, vinblastine, cabazitaxel) enriched in C1 (classical) drug signatures.
  • Prognosis. Proteogenomic subtyping refines Moffitt classical/basal-like assignments and yields stronger prognostic separation (HR 3.4 vs 2.3). LOXL2 protein abundance associated with shorter OS; an APOD N-linked glycosite, two PIGR phosphosites, and an ERRFI1 phosphosite carry prognostic signal independent of protein abundance.

Limitations & open questions

  • All tissues are treatment-naive and surgically resected (CPTAC protocol), limiting extrapolation to metastatic disease treated with systemic therapy; adjuvant regimens were non-standardized across institutions.
  • Findings are correlative — causal effects cannot be inferred without cell-line, PDX, or clinical-trial validation; the tumors profiled were not used to generate PDX models.
  • Bulk-tissue measurements; heterogeneity in cellularity and microenvironment partially addressed by KRAS-VAF/methylation/RNA-based purity filtering and by including 9 normal ductal macrodissections, but LCM or single-cell follow-up would be beneficial.
  • One immune-hot case (C3N-00303) was driven by inadvertent lymph-node inclusion, underscoring the necessity of histologic review.
  • Therapeutic hypotheses for sialyl-/fucosyltransferase inhibition face on-target toxicity concerns based on prior mouse work; selective inhibitors not yet developed.
  • Clinical-trial data linking proteogenomic subtypes to treatment outcomes remain limited because most trials generate only transcriptomic data.
