Characterization of hepatocellular adenoma and carcinoma using microRNA profiling and targeted gene sequencing

Authors

Jian Zheng

Eran Sadot

Joana A. Vigidal

David S. Klimstra

Vinod P. Balachandran

T. Peter Kingham

Peter J. Allen

Michael I. D’Angelica

Ronald P. DeMatteo

William R. Jarnagin

Andrea Ventura

Doi

10.1371/journal.pone.0200776

PMID: 30052636 · DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0200776 · Journal: PLOS ONE (2018)

TL;DR

Zheng and colleagues at Memorial Sloan Kettering performed a pilot study of 11 hepatocellular adenomas (HCA) and 10 hepatocellular carcinomas (HCC) from patients without underlying viral hepatitis or cirrhosis — explicitly excluding the “carcinogenic field effect” — using small RNA sequencing and the MSK-IMPACT 341-gene targeted DNA panel (hcc_msk_venturaa_2018). They show that HCA miRNA profiles cluster between normal liver and HCC, that six miRNAs (miR-200a, miR-429, miR-490-3p down; miR-452, miR-766, miR-1180 up) are similarly deregulated in both HCA and HCC vs. adjacent liver, and that members of the chromosome 19 miRNA cluster (C19MC: miR-515-5p, -517a, -518b, -520c-3p) are 3–4 fold higher in HCC than in HCA or normal liver. On the DNA side, HNF1A was the most frequently mutated gene in HCA (3/9 IMPACT-sequenced), while TERT (7/10), TP53 (3/10), CTNNB1 (2/10) and APC (2/10) dominated the HCC cohort.

Cohort & data

  • 21 patients total: 11 HCA (LIAD) + 10 HCC (HCC) resected at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center 1999–2013.
  • Exclusion criteria: no viral hepatitis, no prior liver-directed therapy, no cirrhosis; no HCC fibrolamellar variant.
  • HCA subtype distribution (immunohistochemistry + mutation-based): inflammatory I-HCA n=6 (54%), steatotic H-HCA n=4 (37%), β-catenin B-HCA n=1 (9%).
  • HCA cohort: median age 40 (24–56), 91% female, 64% history of oral contraceptive use.
  • HCC cohort: median age 73 (63–81), 40% female; median AFP 15 ng/ml (1.9–92,881).
  • Dataset: cBioPortal hcc_msk_venturaa_2018.
  • Assays: small RNA-seq on Illumina HiSeq (TruSeq Small RNA kit, ~11.5M reads/sample, BWA mapping to miRBase) — mirna-seq; targeted DNA-seq via MSK-IMPACT 341-gene panel (IMPACT341) on 9 HCA + 10 HCC matched tumor/normal (HCA1, HCA9 lacked IMPACT); IHC for β-catenin, LFABP, SAA, CRP, glutamine synthetase — immunohistochemistry. Reads aligned with BWA-MEM to hg19/b37 + GATK realignment.

Key findings

  • HCA cluster between normal liver and HCC in miRNA space. Unsupervised multidimensional scaling places all three HCA subtypes intermediate between adjacent normal liver and HCC (S2 Fig).
  • 86 miRNAs are significantly dysregulated in HCC vs. adjacent normal liver (unpaired, FDR-adjusted p ≤ 0.05; S2 Table); only 10 miRNAs differ between HCA and adjacent normal (S3 Table), and 57 miRNAs differ between HCC and HCA (S4 Table).
  • Six “adenoma-carcinoma-sequence” miRNAs are concordantly deregulated in HCA and HCC vs. normal liver (Table 3): miR-200a (log fold-change −2.46 HCC / −2.82 HCA), miR-429 (−2.24 / −3.03), miR-490-3p (−3.98 / −2.83) down; miR-452 (+2.08 / +2.25), miR-766 (+1.36 / +1.07), miR-1180 (+1.59 / +1.52) up. All adjusted p ≤ 0.05.
  • C19MC discriminates HCC from HCA. miR-515-5p, miR-517a, miR-518b, miR-520c-3p are 3–4 fold higher in HCC than in HCA or normal liver (Fig 3A–D) — proposed as a malignant-transformation marker.
  • HCA mutational landscape (IMPACT, n=9): 7 distinct mutations identified. Most frequent gene = HNF1A (3/9, all H-HCA, all LFABP-negative). Single tumors carried mutations in CTNNB1 (the lone B-HCA), GNAS, FAT1, HGF, PIK3CA, and KMT2D (reported as MLL2; KMT2D is the HUGO symbol).
  • HCC mutational landscape (IMPACT, n=10): 41 distinct mutations. Top genes: TERT promoter 7/10, TP53 3/10, CTNNB1 2/10, APC 2/10.
  • No TERT mutations in any HCA. Both HCC patients with CTNNB1 mutations also had TERT promoter mutations — consistent with the model that CTNNB1 is an early event and TERT a later event in the HCA→HCC sequence.
  • None of 11 HCA patients progressed to HCC during follow-up; the authors note follow-up time may be insufficient.

Genes & alterations

  • HNF1A — biallelic-style inactivating mutations in 3/9 IMPACT-sequenced HCA; defines the steatotic H-HCA subtype with corresponding LFABP loss by IHC.
  • CTNNB1 — mutation in 1/9 HCA (the B-HCA with aberrant nuclear β-catenin) and 2/10 HCC; both HCC CTNNB1-mutant cases co-carried TERT promoter mutations.
  • TERT — promoter mutation in 7/10 HCC; absent in HCA. Authors interpret this as TERT being a later step in HCA-to-HCC transformation.
  • TP53 — mutation in 3/10 HCC; not detected in HCA. Frames as a late-stage HCC event consistent with prior literature.
  • APC — mutation in 2/10 HCC.
  • GNAS, FAT1, HGF, PIK3CA, KMT2D (reported as MLL2) — each mutated in a single inflammatory HCA (Table 2).
  • C19MC locus (chr19q13.41) — not covered by the IMPACT341 panel, so copy-number drivers of C19MC overexpression in HCC could not be assessed in this cohort (explicitly noted as a limitation).

Clinical implications

  • The six concordantly deregulated miRNAs (miR-200a, miR-429, miR-490-3p, miR-452, miR-766, miR-1180) and the C19MC cluster are proposed as candidate surrogate biomarkers to predict HCA→HCC transformation, but the authors explicitly call this a hypothesis-generating pilot — no clinical actionability is claimed.
  • The pattern of CTNNB1 without TERT (HCA) vs. CTNNB1 co-occurring with TERT (HCC) supports prior models in which CTNNB1 is the early oncogenic hit and TERT promoter activation marks malignant transformation, with potential utility for risk-stratifying B-HCA patients.
  • No drug-targeting claims are made.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small sample size (n=11 HCA, n=10 HCC) and only 1 B-HCA — the subtype most prone to malignant transformation. All three HCA subtypes had to be pooled for statistical power; per-subtype miRNA analysis was performed but underpowered.
  • No HCA in the cohort progressed to HCC during the available follow-up, so the “adenoma-carcinoma sequence” interpretation is inferred from cross-sectional similarity, not longitudinal observation.
  • IMPACT341 does not cover chr19q13.41, so the mechanism (amplification vs. hypomethylation) of C19MC overexpression in HCC cannot be distinguished here.
  • HCC cohort excludes the dominant viral/cirrhotic etiology; findings may not generalize to hepatitis-B/C- or cirrhosis-associated HCC.
  • miR-490-3p direction is consistent with one prior report but at odds with another — discrepancy unresolved.
