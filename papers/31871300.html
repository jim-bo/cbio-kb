TRK Fusions are Enriched in Cancers with Uncommon Histologies and the Absence of Canonical Driver Mutations

PMID: 31871300 · DOI: 10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-19-3165 · Journal: Clinical Cancer Res. (2020)

TL;DR

Rosen et al. mined a center-wide prospective sequencing program at Memorial Sloan Kettering covering more than 26,000 patients and identified 76 confirmed TRK fusion-positive cancers (0.28% overall prevalence) spanning 48 unique rearrangements and 17 tumor types. Compared with TRK-negative tumors, TRK fusion-positive cancers were markedly depleted of concurrent oncogenic drivers (1.5% vs 31.4% MAPK-pathway co-alteration; p<0.001) and had lower tumor mutation burden (median 1.8 vs 3.5, p<0.001) — the lone exception being colorectal cancers, where TRK fusions co-occurred with MSI-H (6/7 cases). Among 51 patients who developed advanced disease, non–TRK-inhibitor first-line therapy yielded a median PFS of 9.1 months and the best ORR with chemotherapy across all lines was 62.5%; the only checkpoint-inhibitor response was in an MSI-H colorectal patient. The authors argue the “clean” genomic background of most TRK fusion-positive tumors may help explain the tumor-agnostic efficacy of TRK inhibitors.

Cohort & data

  • 76 confirmed TRK fusion-positive patients identified at MSK between April 7, 2015 and August 15, 2018, drawn from a denominator of 26,312 prospectively sequenced patients (ntrk_msk_2019).
  • Tumor types represented (n>=4): salivary carcinoma including mammary analogue secretory carcinoma (12, 15.8%), thyroid (10, 13.2%), sarcoma NOS (9, 11.8%), colon (8, 10.5%), lung (6, 7.9%), melanoma (5, 6.6%), glioblastoma (4, 5.3%), pancreatic (4, 5.3%); plus uterine sarcoma, infantile fibrosarcoma, breast, biliary, appendiceal, and others.
  • Assays: DNA-based MSK-IMPACT (versions 1/310, 2/410, 3/468 genes) plus RNA-based MSK-Fusion anchored multiplex PCR (ArcherDx FusionPlex). MSK-IMPACT v3 added intronic tiling of NTRK1 introns 3, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12 and NTRK2 intron 15, plus ETV6 introns 4–5. ETV6 break-apart FISH was accepted as inferred TRK evidence in secretory carcinoma cases.
  • Confirmation: pan-TRK immunohistochemistry in select cases; manual curation for in-frame predictions; RT-PCR confirmation in the discordant melanoma case. Survival analyses used Kaplan-Meier methods.

Key findings

  • Overall TRK fusion prevalence was 0.28% (74/26,312) with the highest rates in salivary cancers (5.29%, 95% CI 2.76–9.05; driven by ETV6-NTRK3 in mammary analogue secretory carcinomas, 11/12 salivary cases) and thyroid (2.22%, 10/451); 0 of >1,561 prostate cancers were TRK fusion-positive (PMID:31871300).
  • 48 unique fusion rearrangements involving 17 cancer types were identified; the canonical kinase domain was preserved in every fusion. The most common upstream partner was ETV6, followed by EML4, both observed exclusively with NTRK3 (PMID:31871300).
  • TRK fusions were detected by both DNA and RNA in 34 cases, DNA alone in 19, RNA alone in 21, and FISH alone in 2. Among 46 patients tested by both DNA and RNA, 26% (12/46) of true fusions were missed by DNA — all involving NTRK2/3 introns too large for MSK-IMPACT to tile (PMID:31871300).
  • TRK fusion-positive tumors were profoundly depleted of canonical MAPK-pathway driver alterations: 1.5% (1/65) vs 31.4% in TRK-negative cases (p<0.001). The one co-altered case was a melanoma with concurrent GON4L-NTRK1 fusion and NRAS Q61R; the patient had progressive disease on larotrectinib in the pivotal program, and a cell line derived from this tumor was insensitive to larotrectinib and LOXO-195 but responsive to trametinib, with absent pan-TRK protein expression (PMID:31871300).
  • Excluding MSI-H colorectal cases, median TMB was 1.8 (TRK+) vs 3.5 (TRK−) mut/Mb (p<0.001); the difference persisted when restricted to oncogenic/hotspot calls (median 1 vs 2, p<0.001) (PMID:31871300).
  • TRK fusions were the dominant exception in colorectal cancer: 6/7 (86%) of TRK fusion-positive colorectal tumors with MSI data were MSI-H, consistent with kinase-fusion enrichment in mismatch-repair-deficient/hypermethylated MLH1, BRAF/KRAS-wild-type CRC. No non-CRC TRK fusion-positive case was MSI-H (PMID:31871300).
  • Longitudinal profiling in 17 patients with multiple sequenced samples showed the TRK fusion present at all timepoints in 82% (14/17) — consistent with clonality. The three discordant cases included two breast cancers (fusion in metastasis but not primary) and one glioblastoma with a TPM3-NTRK1 fusion that was lost upon progression on a brain-penetrant TRK inhibitor with concurrent acquisition of focal EGFR amplification (19.6-fold) (PMID:31871300).
  • Co-altered cancer genes were dominated by TERT promoter, TP53, and NOTCH1/NOTCH2 mutations (PMID:31871300).
  • Outcomes in the 51 patients who progressed to advanced disease: median PFS on first-line therapy excluding TRK inhibitors = 9.1 months (95% CI 4.8–13.1); first-line ORR across all non-TRK classes 46.7% (7/15; 95% CI 21.3–73.4); best ORR with chemotherapy across all lines 62.5% (15/24; 95% CI 40.6–81.2); ORR on TRK inhibitors 64.7% (23/34; 95% CI 46.5–80.3) and 67.6% by best response (PMID:31871300).
  • Immunotherapy was largely ineffective: among 12 patients receiving checkpoint inhibitors (including 2 MSI-H colorectal), only 1 response (the MSI-H CRC patient, a CR lasting 3.5 years) was observed; overall ORR 11.1% (1/9 evaluable) (PMID:31871300).
  • Median RFS in 39 patients treated with curative intent was 3.5 years (95% CI 2.4–4.9); median OS from diagnosis across all 76 patients was 19.8 years (95% CI 9.1–NR), noting that 68% of the cohort received a TRK inhibitor at some point (PMID:31871300).
  • The MSK cancer-type distribution was broadly similar to the pivotal larotrectinib development program; infantile fibrosarcoma was enriched in the trial cohort, while colorectal and pancreatic cancers were over-represented in the MSK real-world cohort, and 4 GIST cases were present in the larotrectinib dataset but absent from MSK (PMID:31871300).

Genes & alterations

  • NTRK1 / NTRK2 / NTRK3 — recurrent in-frame kinase-domain-preserving fusions; NTRK1 fusions predominantly intrachromosomal (not interstitial deletions), NTRK2 and NTRK3 fusions predominantly interchromosomal; 48 unique events across the cohort (PMID:31871300).
  • ETV6 — the most common upstream partner, exclusively paired with NTRK3; near-pathognomonic ETV6-NTRK3 in mammary analogue secretory carcinomas (11/12 salivary cases) and infantile fibrosarcoma (PMID:31871300).
  • EML4 — second most common upstream partner, exclusively with NTRK3 (PMID:31871300).
  • LMNA — upstream partner of NTRK1 in a colorectal cancer cell line used as a control for functional studies (PMID:31871300).
  • GON4L-NTRK1 + NRAS Q61R — co-occurring in one melanoma; PD on larotrectinib; cell line insensitive to larotrectinib/LOXO-195 but sensitive to trametinib; pan-TRK IHC negative, suggesting transcript not translated (PMID:31871300).
  • TPM3-NTRK1 — present in a glioblastoma at primary resection; lost at progression on a brain-penetrant TRK inhibitor, with new focal EGFR amplification (19.6×) (PMID:31871300).
  • TERT promoter, TP53, NOTCH1/NOTCH2 — most common co-mutations in TRK fusion-positive cohort (PMID:31871300).
  • MLH1, BRAF, KRAS — TRK fusions in CRC enriched in tumors with hypermethylated MLH1 and wild-type BRAF/KRAS (MSI-H context; 6/7 cases in this cohort) (PMID:31871300).

Clinical implications

  • Treatment: TRK fusion-positive cancers respond to alternative standards of care; chemotherapy ORR 62.5% across all lines and median PFS 9.1 months on first-line non-TRK-inhibitor therapy support these as legitimate options, though TRK inhibitors remain the preferred targeted therapy (PMID:31871300).
  • Biomarker: in the absence of MSI-H, TRK fusion-positive tumors show limited benefit from checkpoint inhibitors (1 response in 12 patients) — outside MSI-H CRC, immunotherapy is unlikely to be effective and the authors recommend caution given the small numbers (PMID:31871300).
  • Diagnostic: DNA-only assays miss ~26% of true TRK fusions (driven by NTRK2/3 intron size); reflex RNA fusion testing is warranted in driver-negative cancers and in tumor types with known fusion enrichment (lung, thyroid, biliary, sarcoma, secretory salivary/breast). ETV6 break-apart FISH is sufficient inferred evidence in classic secretory carcinoma morphology (PMID:31871300).
  • Resistance / non-response: rare cases with concurrent canonical drivers (e.g., NRAS Q61R) may lack TRK oncogenic dependence and not benefit from TRK inhibition — pan-TRK IHC and orthogonal drug-sensitivity testing in cell lines can help triage such cases. Loss of the TRK fusion at progression on TRK inhibition (with new EGFR amplification) represents an off-target/clonal escape mechanism distinct from on-target kinase domain mutations (PMID:31871300).
  • The MSK cohort’s tumor-type distribution broadly mirrors the larotrectinib development program, supporting the generalizability of the pivotal efficacy data to post-approval use (PMID:31871300).

Limitations & open questions

  • Tumor-type heterogeneity precluded formal comparison with a matched TRK-negative control and complicates interpretation of pooled RFS/PFS/OS estimates (PMID:31871300).
  • 68% of patients received a TRK inhibitor at some point, so reported OS already reflects the benefit of this targeted therapy and cannot speak to the natural history of untreated TRK fusion-positive cancer (PMID:31871300).
  • TMB and TP53 mutation frequency comparisons may be confounded by underlying cancer-type imbalance between TRK fusion-positive and negative cohorts (PMID:31871300).
  • Checkpoint-inhibitor analysis is underpowered (n=12; only 9 evaluable); larger multi-institutional efforts (e.g., AACR Project GENIE) are needed to firm up the immunotherapy non-response signal in TRK fusion-positive non-MSI-H cancers (PMID:31871300).
  • Even RNA-based assays that pre-specify upstream fusion partners may miss novel partners, suggesting universal-adapter / fusion-agnostic chemistry plus orthogonal pan-TRK IHC for confirmation (PMID:31871300).
  • No prospective comparison of brain-penetrant vs first-generation TRK inhibitors in CNS tumors; the single glioblastoma example with TRK loss at progression hints at distinct CNS resistance biology requiring dedicated study (PMID:31871300).
