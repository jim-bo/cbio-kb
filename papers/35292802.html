TL;DR

MAPPYACTS (NCT02613962) is a European prospective precision-medicine trial that performed on-purpose tumor sampling plus WES and RNA-seq in 787 pediatric and young-adult patients with recurrent/refractory malignancies enrolled across France, Italy, Ireland, and Spain (Feb 2016–Jul 2020). Among 624 patients with successful tumor sequencing, 69% (432/624) had at least one “potentially actionable” alteration; 10% (44/432) carried an alteration deemed “ready for routine use.” Of 356 patients with ≥12 months of follow-up, 107 (30%) received ≥1 matched targeted therapy — 56% within phase I/II trials, mostly the AcSé-ESMART platform (NCT02813135). Overall ORR for matched therapy was 17%, rising to 38% for ready-for-routine-use alterations. In an ancillary cfDNA arm, plasma WES captured 76% of actionable alterations seen in matched extracerebral solid-tumor tissue. Data are deposited at EGA under accession EGAS00001005935 and the cBioPortal study is pancan_mappyacts_2022.

Cohort & data

  • Enrollment: 791 patients consented, 787 included, 774 underwent ≥1 procedure (833 procedures total) — median age 11.6 years (range 0.5–38.5), 59% male, median time since initial diagnosis 1.8 years, median first relapse (range 1–10) — PMID:35292802.
  • Tumor distribution (n=774): sarcomas 290 (37%), CNS tumors 216 (28%), other solid tumors 181 (23%), leukemia 54 (7%), lymphoma 33 (4%) — covering RMS (alveolar ARMS / embryonal ERMS), osteosarcoma OS, Ewing sarcoma ES, DSRCT, MPNST, synovial sarcoma SS, rhabdoid tumor MRT, neuroblastoma NBL, Wilms tumor, hepatoblastoma LIHB, high-grade glioma HGGNOS, low-grade glioma LGGNOS, medulloblastoma MBL, ependymoma EPM, ATRT, ALCL, B-ALL BLLNOS, T-ALL TLL, and AML.
  • Sample acquisition: biopsy in 55% of patients; on-purpose for MAPPYACTS in 62% of solid-tumor/lymphoma procedures; 53% from metastatic sites. 67 procedure-related AEs across 55 procedures (6% of on-purpose, 9% of therapeutic/diagnostic); grade 3/4 in 19/833 cases.
  • Sequencing pipeline: paired tumor/germline WES (Agilent SureSelect V5 50 Mb, Clinical Research Exome 54 Mb, SureSelect XT All Exon CRE v1/v2, or Twist Human Core Exome; 100× mean depth, hg19/GRCh37) plus RNA-seq (TruSeq Stranded mRNA, fusion calling via in-house metacaller); panel sequencing in 3 cases of low cellularity. SNVs/indels called with GATK3 + samtools + Varscan; CNAs and structural variants from in-house pipelines; ANNOVAR annotation; dbNSFP30a functional prediction.
  • Sample yield: 695/833 samples (84%) had sequencing performed (138 excluded mostly for <30% tumor cellularity); 632 samples / 624 patients (91%) successfully sequenced — 547 WES+RNA-seq, 81 WES only, 1 RNA-seq only, 2 RNA-seq + panel, 1 panel only.
  • cfDNA ancillary arm: plasma WES at 100× attempted in 234 extracerebral solid-tumor patients (of first 500 enrolled); 225 analyzable, 128/190 (67%) with paired-tumor WES success — see cfdna-wes.
  • Tumor board: weekly molecular tumor board (MTB) → clinical molecular tumor board (CMTB) with treating physician using an ESCAT-adapted pediatric actionability scale (“ready for routine use” / “investigational” / “hypothetical” / “resistance”).
  • Data sharing: EGA accession EGAS00001005935; cBioPortal study pancan_mappyacts_2022.

Key findings

  • Actionable yield: 1,144 “potentially actionable” findings across 436/632 samples (69%) in 432/624 patients (69%) — 533 SNVs (484 somatic, 49 germline), 527 focal CNAs (212 amplifications/high-level gains, 315 deletions), 59 targetable gene fusions, 25 elevated TMB.
  • Ready-for-routine-use alterations (10% of actionable patients, 4% of all detected alterations; n=44 patients): gene fusions in ALK (n=10), BRAF (n=14 — combining fusions and BRAF V600E mutations), NTRK1/2/3 (n=9), ROS1 (n=2), COL1A1/PDGFB (n=1), and RET (n=1); pathogenic mutations in BRAF (n=4), NF1 (n=2), and IDH1 (n=1); mutation/deletion in PTCH1 (n=4) in selected tumor types. 19/44 (42%) had been previously unknown to the patient’s clinical workup.
  • Treatment recommendations (median 2/patient, range 1–7): most-recommended drug classes were WEE1 inhibitors (n=150), mTOR (n=123), CDK4/CDK6 (n=105), MEK (n=95), PARP (n=64), BET (n=59), EZH2 (n=38), FGFR (n=31), and PD1/PD-L1 (n=31).
  • Evidence-level distribution of 1,144 actionable events: “ready for routine use” 4%, “investigational” 80%, “hypothetical” 16%.
  • Recurrent investigational alterations: MYCN/MYC amplification, fusion, or activating mutation (n=43); RAS family (KRAS/NRAS/HRAS) n=42; FGFR1/FGFR2/FGFR3/FGFR4 n=27; PIK3CA/PIK3R1 n=23; ALK n=15; deletions/deleterious mutations in TP53 n=215, CDKN2A/CDKN2B n=157, NF1 n=33, SMARCA4/SMARCB1 n=27, PTEN n=25, RB1 n=11.
  • Resistance mutations: 10 patients with putative resistance mutations in NTRK1/NTRK2/NTRK3 (n=3), SMO (n=3), ALK (n=3), NRAS (n=1) — 9/10 had previously received the specific targeted agent.
  • Diagnostic impact: initial diagnosis revised in 12/624 patients (1.9%); 8 of those revisions driven by specific gene fusion detection. Genetic counseling recommended for 51/674 patients (7.6%) with a potentially relevant germline finding.
  • Follow-up & treatment delivery: 356/432 patients with ≥12 months follow-up; 107 (30%) received 122 matched targeted therapies (range 1–4 per patient). 14/122 (11%) therapies were “ready for routine use,” 97 (80%) “investigational,” 11 (9%) “hypothetical.” 64/122 (52%) given as single agent, 45 (37%) combined with chemotherapy, 13 (11%) with another targeted therapy. 68/122 (56%) administered within phase I/II trials; 49/68 (72%) within AcSé-ESMART.
  • Reasons for not receiving matched therapy (n=249 patients): death prior to CMTB (n=24, 10%) or within 3 months after (n=44, 18%), other non-matched therapy (n=92, 37%), effective ongoing therapy (n=35, 14%).
  • Response outcomes (n=109 evaluable patients on matched therapy): ORR 17% (18 PR; 95% CI 10–25%), SD 25% (n=27), DCR 41% (95% CI 32–51%). Median treatment duration for the 45 patients with disease control was 129 days (range 58–697). ORR by evidence tier: ready-for-routine-use 38% (5/13; 95% CI 18–65%, all single-agent); investigational 14% (12/86); hypothetical 10% (1/10). By combination: matched-targeted + chemotherapy ORR 18% (8/44), single-agent matched 13% (5/39), targeted-targeted combinations 0% (0/13). When patients who had received their high-evidence match prior to MAPPYACTS inclusion are included (12 evaluable, 7 PR + 2 CR), global ready-for-routine-use ORR was 56% (95% CI 37–73%).
  • Tumor mutational burden: median 0.6 mut/Mb (range 0–195.3) across the first 451 enrolled solid-tumor patients (452 WES samples). 7 patients with TMB >10 mut/Mb (3 high-grade glioma — one >100, 3 neuroblastoma, 1 medulloblastoma); 3 of those linked to probable constitutional mismatch repair deficiency involving MSH2, MLH1, and MSH6; 5 tumors showed microsatellite instability.
  • cfDNA / ctDNA findings: cfDNA quantity differed significantly across cancer types (ANOVA P=1.4e−03), highest in neuroblastoma vs. other tumors (Wilcoxon P=3e−04). ctDNA fraction by Facets also highest in neuroblastoma — median 35% (range 0–95%) vs. 16% (0–86%) in other diseases (Wilcoxon P=1.4e−08). cfDNA WES succeeded in 128/190 (67%) of patients with successful paired tumor WES; success was higher in metastatic vs. localized disease (66% vs. 47%, χ² P=0.04). 71/94 (76%) potentially actionable SNVs in tumor were also detected in plasma; 14 patients had 35 additional actionable SNVs detected by cfDNA WES that were not seen in tumor (median 1 per patient, range 1–13), including an ALK p.Arg1275Gln seen only in plasma. Across all SNVs in successful pairs: 57% shared, 31% tumor-only, 11% plasma-only.
  • Salvage cfDNA in failed tumor WES: cfDNA WES succeeded in 11/35 patients (34%) where tumor WES was not done or failed, identifying 12 SNVs in actionable genes in 5 patients.
  • Germline / cancer predisposition: 7%–12% of pediatric patients harbored germline cancer-predisposition findings consistent with prior reports (matching Zhang et al. 2015 N Engl J Med predisposition gene list).
  • PDX resource: 9 participating laboratories established 131 patient-derived xenografts over the 4-year trial; shared with the IMI2 ITCC-P4 project.

Genes & alterations

  • ALK — fusions (n=10) ready-for-routine-use across ALCL (ALK–NPM1/TFG/TRAF1) and other tumors; activating mutations (n=15) investigational in NBL; a p.Arg1275Gln seen only in plasma by cfDNA WES; 3 resistance mutations after prior ALK inhibitor exposure.
  • BRAF — V600E mutations (n=4 in non-midline HGG) and KIAA1549–BRAF fusions (n=10 in low-grade glioma) ready-for-routine-use; targeted by dabrafenib.
  • NTRK1/NTRK2/NTRK3 — fusions (n=9) ready-for-routine-use across multiple solid tumors (e.g., NTRK1-LMNA, NTRK3-ETV6, NTRK2-TLE4, NTRK3-SPECC1L); 3 resistance mutations after prior TRK inhibitor.
  • ROS1 — ROS1-GOPC fusions (n=2) ready-for-routine-use in non-RMS soft-tissue sarcoma and low-grade glioma.
  • RET — RET-CCDC6 fusion (n=1) ready-for-routine-use in carcinoma.
  • COL1A1 / PDGFB — COL1A1–PDGFB fusion (n=1) ready-for-routine-use, targetable by imatinib.
  • IDH1 — R132L mutation (n=1) ready-for-routine-use in AML, targetable by ivosidenib.
  • PTCH1 / SMO — PTCH1 mutation/deletion (n=4) ready-for-routine-use in selected tumor types (SHH-pathway medulloblastoma); SMO investigational; 3 SMO resistance mutations after prior hedgehog inhibitor.
  • NF1 — pathogenic mutations (n=2) ready-for-routine-use; broader investigational role in MPNST and low-grade glioma (n=33 deleterious events).
  • TP53 — most frequent driver — 215 deleterious mutations/deletions; recommended for WEE1 inhibitor matching based on ovarian-cancer data (Leijen et al. 2016) and explored in AcSé-ESMART arms in combination with chemotherapy. Three CNS subgroups (HGG, MBL, EPN) carried TP53 mutations as a top recurrent lesion.
  • CDKN2A / CDKN2B — 157 deletions/inactivating events across sarcomas, CNS tumors, and leukemia; matched to CDK4/6 inhibitors.
  • CDK4 / CDK6 — amplifications particularly in RMS and other sarcomas — 105 CDK4/6 inhibitor recommendations.
  • MYCN / MYC — amplifications/fusions/activating mutations (n=43) recurrent in neuroblastoma (with 11q deletion and ALK) and medulloblastoma; matched to BET inhibitors.
  • RAS family — KRAS, NRAS, HRAS — 42 events; matched to MEK inhibitors (n=95 recommendations) such as selumetinib. Authors deliberately did not classify HRAS/KRAS mutations as ready-for-routine-use given limited clinical activity of MEK inhibitors in pediatric mutant tumors — differing from the INFORM registry classification.
  • FGFR1/FGFR2/FGFR3/FGFR4 — 27 events across HGG, rhabdomyosarcoma, and other sarcomas.
  • PIK3CA / PIK3R1 / PTEN / MTOR — 23 PIK3CA/PIK3R1 events plus 25 PTEN losses; matched to mTOR inhibitors (vistusertib, everolimus) explored in AcSé-ESMART arms E/F.
  • SMARCA4 / SMARCB1 — 27 inactivating events recurrent in rhabdoid tumor (MRT/ATRT) and other CNS tumors; matched to EZH2 inhibitors.
  • EZH2, BRD4, MDM2 — investigational matches; 38/59/(low frequency) recommendations respectively.
  • ZFTA (formerly C11orf95) — supratentorial ependymoma driver gene fusion partner — 6% of CNS-tumor oncomap alterations.
  • EWSR1 — Ewing sarcoma-defining fusions; covered in the sarcoma oncomap.
  • EGFR / ERBB2 / ERBB3 / ERBB4 — 5% of CNS-tumor oncomap alterations; investigational ERBB-inhibitor matches.
  • Germline / DNA repair — BRCA1, BRCA2, ATM, MSH2, MLH1, MSH6 — 49 germline SNVs across cohort; CMMRD identified in 3 hypermutated patients. Heterozygous chr11q deletions encompassing ATM, CHK1, MRE11A, H2AFX considered actionable for PARP inhibitors.
  • Other recurrent (CNS oncomap): ATRX, ARID1A, CTNNB1, TSC1/TSC2, MN1, KDR, KIT, PDGFRA.

Clinical implications

  • Feasibility: comprehensive paired tumor + germline WES + RNA-seq is feasible at relapse in a multicenter pediatric setting: 91% success on WES, 90% on RNA-seq, manageable procedure safety profile (19/833 procedures with grade 3–4 AEs).
  • Actionability ceiling at relapse: only ~10% of patients with actionable alterations have a high-evidence (“ready for routine use”) match, but those matches yield meaningful clinical benefit — 38% single-agent ORR (and 56% global ORR including pre-MAPPYACTS matches). Authors advocate moving high-evidence alteration testing — ALK/BRAF/NTRK/ROS1/RET fusions, IDH1 mutations, PTCH1 alterations — into front-line pediatric diagnostics.
  • Half of high-evidence alterations were missed by prior routine diagnostics, underscoring the need for comprehensive profiling beyond targeted panels.
  • Investigational/hypothetical-tier matches still beat historical chemotherapy in pediatric phase I/II trials (14% vs. ~4% historical ORR), supporting structured platform trials such as AcSé-ESMART for low-evidence matches.
  • Targeted-targeted combinations underperformed (0/13 ORR) vs. matched-single-agent (13%) or matched + chemotherapy (18%), suggesting careful prioritization of combinations.
  • Liquid biopsy / cfDNA WES is clinically informative in extracerebral pediatric solid tumors — recovering 76% of tumor actionable SNVs and capturing additional plasma-only actionable events including a clinically relevant ALK Arg1275Gln mutation. Particularly useful in neuroblastoma (high ctDNA fraction) and when re-biopsy is not feasible (e.g., bone-marrow-only relapses). Deserves prospective validation, addressed in planned MAPPYACTS 2 and integrated into France’s PFMG2025.
  • Germline findings in 7–12% justify integrated genetic counseling pathways for relapsed pediatric patients.
  • Resistance profiling at progression (10 cases here) supports re-biopsy and re-sequencing at targeted-therapy failure to inform second-generation drug choice.
  • TP53 + WEE1 inhibition rationale carried into AcSé-ESMART platform combination arms, alongside CDK, PI3K/mTOR, homologous-recombination, and immune-checkpoint targeting arms.

Limitations & open questions

  • Evolving evidence levels: classifications were made at the time of each CMTB and will shift with new data; the trial’s “investigational” and “hypothetical” tiers carry inherent subjectivity (96% of matches fell into these tiers).
  • Treatment-recommendation lag: most patients began first-line salvage chemotherapy while awaiting molecular results, so matched treatment was typically administered at a later relapse — temporal tumor evolution may have altered the actionable landscape.
  • cfDNA WES caveats: 38% sequencing failure rate (often in low-burden disease); restricted largely to SNV detection (CNA threshold 20% vs. SNV 5%); different capture kits between tumor and plasma complicate CNA calling; results were not used to drive treatment decisions in this iteration.
  • Disagreement with INFORM on ALK and HRAS/KRAS classification — MAPPYACTS withheld these from “ready for routine use” given limited MEK and ALK inhibitor activity in pediatric mutant settings; harmonization across European programs remains an open question.
  • Tumor heterogeneity between spatial sites and primary vs. metastatic is widely observed but not systematically resolved; paired primary–relapse sequencing comparisons remain to be done.
  • No unique drivers in most cancers — multiple oncogenic events per hallmark argue for combination strategies and innovative trial designs that current pediatric platforms are not yet built for.
  • Predisposition-gene list dependency: germline yield (7–12%) varies with the predefined gene panel.
  • Functional gaps: variants of unknown significance, post-translational and immunologic context remain underexplored.
  • Generalizability: all patients received salvage tumor sampling at recurrence, so findings may differ from at-diagnosis cohorts (e.g., INFORM, Zero Childhood Cancer, NCI-COG Pediatric MATCH).
  • MAPPYACTS 2 is positioned to address liquid-biopsy validation, health-economic outcomes, and psychosocial impact alongside the French PFMG2025 program.
