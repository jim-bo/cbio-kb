Glioma progression is shaped by genetic evolution and microenvironment interactions

Kevin C. Johnson

PMID: 35649412 · DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2022.04.038 · Journal: Cell (2022)

TL;DR

Varn and colleagues (the GLASS Consortium) profile temporally separated tumor pairs from 304 adult patients with diffuse glioma — 168 with longitudinal RNA-seq, 256 with longitudinal DNA-seq, and 115 with both — to dissect how genetic evolution and microenvironment interactions shape recurrence in IDH1/IDH2-wild-type and IDH-mutant disease. Combining bulk and single-cell transcriptomics, CIBERSORTx deconvolution against an 11-patient single-cell reference (55,284 cells), histology mapping via the Ivy Glioblastoma Atlas Project, and multiplex immunofluorescence, the authors identify three convergent recurrence phenotypes — neuronal, mesenchymal, and proliferative — that link acquired somatic alterations (notably CDKN2A deletion, CCND2 amplification, and temozolomide-induced hypermutation) to cell-state shifts and stromal interactions, including an OSM/OSMR-mediated myeloid–neoplastic axis driving mesenchymal transition.

Cohort & data

  • GLASS expansion cohort (difg_glass; supersedes the 222-patient difg_glass_2019 release): 381 patients enrolled across 37 hospitals; 304 retained after QC.
    • 168 patients with ≥2 timepoints of RNA-seq (128 IDH-wild-type, 31 IDH-mutant 1p/19q-intact, 9 IDH-mutant 1p/19q-codeleted).
    • 256 patients with ≥2 timepoints of DNA-seq.
    • 115 patients with overlapping DNA + RNA at ≥2 timepoints.
  • Cancer types: adult diffuse glioma — glioblastoma (IDHwt), astrocytoma (IDHmut-noncodel), and oligodendroglioma (IDHmut-codel); collectively spans low-grade glioma through grade 4 GBM.
  • Reference datasets: TCGA GBM and TCGA LGG (687 glioma transcriptomes for myeloid-subtype analyses), the Ivy Glioblastoma Atlas Project (Ivy GAP) anatomic feature reference, and pan-glioma single-cell reference from 11 patients (Johnson et al., 2021).
  • Newly generated data added in this release: DNA-seq from 109 samples (53 patients) and RNA-seq from 392 samples (206 patients) across Henry Ford Health System, Seoul National University, Case Western Reserve University, Chinese University of Hong Kong, Luxembourg Institute of Health, and MD Anderson cohorts.
  • Assays / methods: whole-exome and whole-genome sequencing (b37 alignment, BWA-MEM + GATK, MuTect2 for somatic calls, TITAN for purity/ploidy/CNA); bulk RNA-seq (STAR + kallisto); single-cell RNA-seq (including SMART-seq2 reference); CIBERSORTx deconvolution; HLA typing via OptiType and HLA loss-of-heterozygosity via LOHHLA; neoantigen calling with pVACseq; whole-slide multiplex immunofluorescence and H&E histo-cytometry.
  • Data availability: Synapse syn23589349 (https://www.synapse.org/glass); digitized H&E images on the Emory Digital Slide Archive; code at https://github.com/fsvarn/GLASSx.

Key findings

  • Three convergent recurrence phenotypes. Integrating cell-state, histology, and somatic-alteration changes, the authors define neuronal, mesenchymal, and proliferative recurrence phenotypes. IDH-wild-type tumors can exhibit all three (often simultaneously); a subset of IDH-mutant tumors exhibits the proliferative phenotype (PMID:35649412, Figure 7).
  • Subtype switching is common in IDHwt and rare in IDHmut. Among IDHwt cases the dominant bulk transcriptional subtype switched in 49% of patients (most commonly classical → mesenchymal), whereas 78% of IDHmut tumors remained proneural at both timepoints.
  • Recurrent IDHwt tumors are more invasive. IDHwt recurrences showed significantly higher oligodendrocyte content (P = 5e-6, paired t-test) and lower differentiated-like neoplastic cells (P = 4e-3) — changes preserved after adjusting for extent of surgical resection. Leading-edge histological content was significantly higher at recurrence (P = 4e-5).
  • Proliferative phenotype links genetics to cell state. Within IDHmut tumors, acquired CDKN2A deletions and CCND2 amplifications associated with increases in proliferating stem-like neoplastic cells (P = 3e-3, paired t-test, n = 6); multiplex IF confirmed significantly more SOX2+/Ki67+ cells in a recurrent IDHmut tumor with acquired CDKN2A deletion (P < 1e-5, Fisher’s exact test). IDHwt tumors typically already harbor CDKN2A deletions at initial presentation, so this association was not observed in that subtype.
  • Treatment-induced hypermutation drives proliferating stem-like increases in both subtypes. Hypermutation (defined as >10 mutations/Mb at recurrence, frequently following alkylator/temozolomide exposure) was associated with increased proliferating stem-like cells in both IDHwt (n = 13) and IDHmut (n = 7) tumors (P < 0.05, paired t-test), with multiplex IF confirming the SOX2+/Ki67+ increase. In IDHmut tumors hypermutation occurred largely independently of CDKN2A/CCND2 events.
  • Proliferating stem-like burden predicts worse outcome in IDHmut. Increases in proliferating stem-like neoplastic cells in IDHmut tumors were significantly associated with reduced overall survival (P = 0.02, log-rank), and the association persisted after adjusting for age, grade, and 1p/19q codeletion status (P = 0.02, Wald test).
  • Microenvironment changes track specific driver events in IDHwt. Non-hypermutated IDHwt recurrences that acquired NF1 mutations (n = 3) all underwent mesenchymal transition with significant granulocyte increases (P = 0.03). Loss of EGFR amplification (n = 11) and loss of PDGFRA amplification (n = 4) at recurrence both associated with increased non-neoplastic cell content (P < 0.05, paired t-test) and a transition to the mesenchymal subtype (P = 0.05, Fisher’s exact test).
  • Neuronal signaling phenotype affects ~66% of IDHwt recurrences. Of 7,511 genes inferred in stem-like neoplastic cells, 10.0% were differentially expressed at IDHwt recurrence (FDR < 0.05, Wilcoxon signed-rank). The up-regulated stem-like recurrence signature was enriched in neuronal-signaling GO terms; the signature correlated with leading-edge histological content at both timepoints and was significantly higher in single neoplastic cells from the invasive rim than from the tumor core. Multiplex IF showed SNAP25+ neoplastic cells (SOX2+) co-localizing with NeuN+ neurons in infiltrating-tumor regions, while cellular-tumor regions had neither.
  • Distinct mesenchymal myeloid program. Myeloid cells in mesenchymal IDHwt tumors expressed an immunosuppressive signature shared with the classical subtype (including the immune-checkpoint genes PDCD1LG2 and IDO1) plus an additional 300-gene mesenchymal-specific program, of which 186 genes (FDR < 0.05, fold-change > 1.5) were enriched for chemokine signaling / lymphocyte chemotaxis. Single-myeloid-cell expression of this signature correlated tightly with bulk mesenchymal subtype score (R = 0.89, P = 3e-3); IDHwt tumors undergoing mesenchymal transition longitudinally up-regulated the signature (P = 8e-8, Wilcoxon signed-rank).
  • OSM–OSMR ligand–receptor axis as mesenchymal driver. From 1,894 literature-supported ligand–receptor pairs, 105 had both members co-rising with mesenchymal score over time; 49 also replicated in single-cell data. Myeloid OSM paired with neoplastic OSMR emerged as one of the strongest mesenchymal correlates; multiplex IF showed OSM+ CD14+ myeloid cells co-localizing with CD44+/SOX2+ mesenchymal neoplastic cells near blood vessels in mesenchymal — but not classical — IDHwt glioma.
  • HLA loss-of-heterozygosity is a passenger event in glioma. HLA LOH was observed in 19% of GLASS patients at ≥1 timepoint, and acquired more often at recurrence in IDHmut-noncodel tumors (P = 0.04, Fisher’s exact). Simulation tests found no positive selection in either IDHwt or IDHmut recurrences (P > 0.05). Acquired HLA LOH in IDHmut-noncodel tumors instead tracked with significantly larger increases in genome-wide SCNA burden (P = 0.02, Wilcoxon rank-sum), supporting a passenger interpretation contrasting with selection-driven HLA LOH seen in other cancer types.

Genes & alterations

  • IDH1 / IDH2 — the foundational stratification axis for the cohort (IDHwt vs. IDHmut-noncodel vs. IDHmut-codel) (PMID:35649412).
  • CDKN2A — acquired focal deletion at recurrence in IDHmut glioma drives expansion of proliferating stem-like neoplastic cells (P = 3e-3, n = 6); already prevalent at initial diagnosis in IDHwt tumors and therefore not enriched at recurrence there.
  • CCND2 — acquired amplification at recurrence in IDHmut tumors, co-acting with CDKN2A loss in the proliferative phenotype.
  • EGFR — loss of EGFR amplification at recurrence (n = 11) associates with increased non-neoplastic cell content and mesenchymal subtype switching in IDHwt tumors.
  • PDGFRA — loss of PDGFRA amplification at recurrence (n = 4) similarly associates with increased non-neoplastic cells and mesenchymal transition.
  • NF1 — acquired mutation at recurrence (n = 3) co-occurs with mesenchymal transition and significant granulocyte increase (P = 0.03).
  • OSM / OSMR — myeloid-expressed OSM × differentiated-like-neoplastic OSMR ligand–receptor pair is a top correlate of mesenchymal transition; spatial co-localization confirmed by multiplex IF.
  • SOX2, OLIG2, CD44 — neoplastic-cell-state markers used to define stem-like (SOX2+, OLIG2+), proliferating stem-like (SOX2+/Ki67+), and mesenchymal-like (CD44+) populations in deconvolution and IF.
  • SNAP25 — neuronal marker; component of the stem-like recurrence signature, detected by IF on neoplastic cells in infiltrating-tumor regions of recurrent glioma.
  • PDCD1LG2 (PD-L2) and IDO1 — immune-checkpoint genes up-regulated by myeloid cells in classical and mesenchymal IDHwt tumors, contributing to the immunosuppressive phenotype.

Clinical implications

  • Three recurrence phenotypes as therapeutic anchors. The authors propose neuronal, mesenchymal, and proliferative recurrence phenotypes as a framework for guiding clinical decision-making in recurrent diffuse glioma — each anchored to distinct cellular, genetic, and histological features.
  • Cell cycle therapy in IDHmut recurrence. Acquired CDKN2A loss / CCND2 amplification at IDHmut recurrence drives a proliferating stem-like expansion with worse overall survival, identifying a candidate subset for cell-cycle-directed therapies at recurrence.
  • Hypermutation as predictor of proliferative phenotype. Temozolomide-induced hypermutation at recurrence is a shared route to the proliferative phenotype across IDHwt and IDHmut tumors, motivating monitoring of mutation burden as a longitudinal biomarker.
  • OSM/OSMR as a targetable mesenchymal-transition axis. Spatially convergent OSM+ myeloid and OSMR+ mesenchymal-like neoplastic cells in recurrent IDHwt glioma nominate this ligand–receptor pair for therapeutic interruption of mesenchymal transition.
  • Neuron-glioma interactions. Recurrent IDHwt invasion into normal brain parenchyma is accompanied by neuronal-signaling up-regulation in stem-like neoplastic cells at the invasive rim, consistent with prior reports that neuron-to-glioma synapses promote tumor invasion and growth — supporting neuronal-circuitry-directed strategies as a candidate intervention.
  • HLA LOH should not be over-interpreted in glioma. Because HLA LOH appears to be a passenger of genome-wide SCNA burden rather than a T-cell-selected escape event in glioma, its use as an immunotherapy biomarker should be calibrated differently than in other cancer types.

Limitations & open questions

  • The GLASS cohort is biased toward younger patients with favorable enough prognosis to undergo repeat surgery — findings may not transfer to patients with severe early-progressive disease.
  • Bulk RNA-seq deconvolution cannot resolve rare cell populations and is constrained by the input signature matrix; the matrices used here lacked neurons, astrocytes, and recurrence-specific histological features, limiting direct assessment of their contributions to resistance.
  • Several driver-alteration → microenvironment associations rest on small subgroups (e.g., NF1-acquisition n = 3; PDGFRA-amplification loss n = 4); these warrant replication in larger longitudinal cohorts.
  • Directionality of the OSM–OSMR axis (myeloid-driven mesenchymal induction vs. mesenchymal-recruited myeloid response) is not resolved here.
  • Whether the neuronal-signaling stem-like signature has prognostic value remains unclear — no association with overall survival was observed (P > 0.05, Wald test) despite plausible mechanistic links to invasion.
  • The cohort spans 37 hospitals with heterogeneous DNA/RNA-seq platforms (whole-exome, whole-genome, exon capture variants; Illumina HiSeq 2000 through NovaSeq 6000); batch effects on rare-event detection cannot be fully excluded.
