Differences in Prostate Cancer Genomes by Self-Reported Race: Contributions of Genetic Ancestry, Modifiable Cancer Risk Factors, and Clinical Factors

TL;DR

In a single-institution Memorial Sloan Kettering cohort of 2,069 men with prostate cancer who received clinical-grade MSK-IMPACT tumor–normal sequencing (1,841 self-reported White, 165 Black, 63 Asian), Stopsack et al. show that somatic genomic differences by self-reported race largely persist after adjusting for age, PSA, disease extent, stage, and sample type. Tumors from Black men carried more AR alterations and chromosome 8q gains and fewer PTEN and TP53 alterations than those from White men; tumors from Asian men were enriched for class-1 forkhead-domain FOXA1 mutations and ZFHX3 alterations. Genetic ancestry (inferred from 5,072 1KGP SNPs and supervised ADMIXTURE) tracked self-reported race, but area-level household income was independently associated with chr8q gain, arguing that ancestry differences cannot be interpreted as purely germline-driven.

Cohort & data

  • Cohort: 2,069 men with histologically confirmed prostate adenocarcinoma sequenced at MSK (1,841 White, 165 Black, 63 Asian by self-report).
  • Dataset: prad_msk_stopsack_2021 — clinical and genomic data available on cBioPortal at https://www.cbioportal.org/study/summary?id=prad_msk_stopsack_2021.
  • Assay: FDA-cleared MSK-IMPACT targeted hybridization-capture panel, 341 to 468 genes depending on version (IMPACT341, IMPACT410, IMPACT468); captures SNVs, indels, CNAs, and rearrangements. ZFHX3 was only on later panel versions, so only 1,924 samples (93%) were profiled for it.
  • Germline: 1,781 men (86%) consented to germline analysis, with no difference in consent by race.
  • Oncogenicity annotation: OncoKB v2.8 (Sep 17, 2020).
  • Ancestry inference: 5,072 bi-allelic autosomal SNPs from 1000 Genomes Project within MSK-IMPACT468 baits; supervised ADMIXTURE v1.3 to estimate AFR, EUR, EAS, NAM, SAS proportions; arm-level CNAs called with ASCETS.

Key findings

  • Clinical baseline differences (PMID:34667026, Table 1). Black men were diagnosed 3.0 years younger (95% CI, 0.6 to 5.4) than White men, had higher median PSA at diagnosis (17 vs. 9 ng/mL; difference 8.2 ng/mL, 95% CI 0.1 to 16.3), and lived in zip codes with average household income 36% lower (95% CI 30 to 42%). Current smoking was twice as prevalent in Black men (13%) as in White men (6%; difference 7 percentage points, 95% CI 2 to 12). Asian men had a more advanced stage at sample procurement (32% castration-resistant vs. 19% White; difference 13 pp, 95% CI 1 to 24).
  • AR alterations (Fig. 2). Observed in 18% of Black, 19% of Asian, and 12% of White tumors. After adjusting for clinical features, the Asian–White difference attenuated fully (0 pp, 95% CI −5 to 6) whereas the Black–White difference persisted (5 pp, 95% CI 1 to 8).
  • FOXA1 alterations. 27% of Asian-men tumors vs. 13% White (adjusted difference 15 pp, 95% CI 6 to 26), driven by class-1 forkhead-domain mutations (21% Asian vs. 8% White; Supplementary Fig. 3A).
  • ZFHX3 alterations. 17% Asian vs. 7% White (adjusted difference 8 pp, 95% CI 1 to 17).
  • TP53 alterations. Less common in Black men (19%) than White (30%; adjusted difference −10 pp, 95% CI −17 to −3), driven by fewer missense mutations (11% Black vs. 18% White).
  • PTEN alterations. Less common in Black (14%) and Asian men than in White men (20%; adjusted Black–White difference −7 pp, 95% CI −12 to −1), particularly fewer mutations (8% Black vs. 3% White — note the paper reports the directionality as “fewer”, values per Supplementary Fig. 3C; adjusted difference −4 pp, 95% CI −7 to 0).
  • No difference in DNA-repair alterations (ERCC5, MRE11, TP53BP1, POLE, RAD21, MSH2, MSH6, BRCA1, BRCA2, ATR, ATM) by race, contrasting with the prior Project GENIE-based analysis by Mahal et al. (NEJM 2020). MSI prevalence and TMB also did not differ by race (Supplementary Table 1).
  • Chr8q gain (Fig. 4). 49% of Black, 47% of Asian, and 37% of White tumors had chr8q gains. After clinical-feature adjustment the Asian–White difference attenuated (5 pp, 95% CI −6 to 17) but the Black–White difference persisted (11 pp, 95% CI 4 to 18). 100% AFR ancestry vs. 0% AFR was associated with +13 pp chr8q gain (95% CI 4 to 23).
  • Chr8q gain is independently prognostic. Among White men, chr8q gain HR for death = 1.61 (95% CI 1.33 to 1.96); among Black men, HR = 2.00 (95% CI 1.00 to 4.01), each adjusted for disease extent at sequencing.
  • Income is independently associated with chr8q gain. Each $50,000/yr increase in area-level median household income reduced chr8q-gain prevalence by 2 pp (95% CI −4 to 0) after adjusting for self-reported race and inferred ancestry. Adjusting for income reduced the Black–White chr8q-gain difference from 13 to 10 pp (95% CI 0 to 20).
  • Germline contribution is not the driver. Of the genes with race/ancestry-associated prevalence differences, none had more than a single germline mutation among the 1,781 germline-consented patients (Supplementary Table 2).
  • Overall survival. Among Black men with follow-up (n=156), HR vs. White = 1.15 (95% CI 0.85 to 1.57); not significantly different but cannot exclude an effect.

Genes & alterations

  • AR — alterations enriched in tumors from Black and Asian men; only the Black-vs-White difference persists after clinical adjustment (adjusted difference +5 pp, 95% CI 1 to 8).
  • BRAF — modestly higher in Black men, consistent with prior reports; targeted-therapy relevance noted by authors.
  • FOXA1 — class-1 forkhead-domain mutations enriched in Asian men (21% vs. 8% White); same structural class previously described as early-occurring activating events.
  • ZFHX3 — mutations and CNAs enriched in Asian men (17% vs. 7% White); profiled in 93% of samples due to later panel inclusion.
  • TP53 — fewer alterations in Black men (19% vs. 30% White), specifically fewer missense mutations; R273 hotspot prevalence is only 2% in White men.
  • PTEN — fewer alterations in Black and Asian men, particularly fewer mutations; relevant to PI3K-inhibitor strategies in prostate cancer.
  • MYC — copy-number gains enriched in Black men; co-located on chromosome arm 8q whose gain is the dominant CNA difference.
  • TMPRSS2 / ERG — TMPRSS2:ERG fusions intentionally excluded from race comparisons due to suboptimal panel sensitivity for fusions; known to be more common in European-ancestry men.
  • DNA-repair genes (ERCC5, MRE11, TP53BP1, POLE, RAD21, MSH2, MSH6, BRCA1, BRCA2, ATR, ATM) — no significant prevalence difference by race, contradicting one prior Project GENIE-based report.

Clinical implications

  • The persistence of AR and BRAF alteration differences in Black men, even after controlling for healthcare-access proxies, supports the case that race-aware reporting of tumor genomics may inform pre-test expectations and treatment selection.
  • Lower PTEN alteration rates in non-White men may translate into smaller absolute benefit from PI3K-pathway inhibitors (e.g., ipatasertib), which had reached phase 3 in mCRPC at the time of publication.
  • Higher chr8q-gain prevalence is associated with worse survival in both Black and White men; therapeutic targets on chr8q beyond MYC may be especially relevant for Black patients.
  • Authors explicitly argue against attributing tumor-genomic racial differences solely to germline genetics: at most one germline mutation per race-associated gene was observed across 1,781 consented patients, and area-level income independently predicted chr8q gain.

Limitations & open questions

  • Single-institution hospital-based cohort with sequenced patients only — denominator of men at risk is unknown, precluding causal attribution.
  • Black men (n=165) and Asian men (n=63) are underrepresented; the study cannot resolve Hispanic ethnicity, EAS vs. SAS, or within-AFR substructure (Northern/Eastern/Western African ancestry collapsed).
  • TMPRSS2:ERG fusions and other rearrangements excluded due to panel sensitivity limitations; relevant given known ancestry differences in fusion prevalence.
  • ZFHX3 only on later MSK-IMPACT versions, leaving 7% of samples unprofiled.
  • Some patients overlap with the prior Mahal et al. Project GENIE analysis, so findings are not a fully independent replication of prevalence-difference estimates.
  • Asian-men cohort (n=58 with follow-up, 15 deaths) was too small for survival analysis.
  • Area-level (zip-code) household income is a crude, ecological proxy for socioeconomic status; cannot pinpoint which specific modifiable factors (education, pollution, food insecurity, violence) drive the chr8q association.
