MAPK pathway genetic alterations are associated with prolonged overall survival in low-grade serous ovarian carcinoma

PMID: 35443055 · DOI: 10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-21-4183 · Journal: Clinical Cancer Research (2022)

TL;DR

Manning-Geist and colleagues performed MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing (341–505 cancer genes) on tumors from 119 patients with centrally pathology-reviewed low-grade serous ovarian carcinoma (LGSOC) treated at MSK between 2015 and 2021. Sixty percent of tumors harbored a somatic MAPK pathway alteration, dominated by KRAS hotspot mutations (33%), NRAS hotspots (11%), BRAF alterations (11%), and EIF1AX (10%). On multivariate analysis, both MAPK pathway alteration (HR 2.5 for absence, p=0.019) and platinum sensitivity (p=0.005) were independently associated with improved overall survival. Among 79 patients with germline testing, only 7 (9%) carried a pathogenic germline mutation and none had BRCA1/2 mutations despite 13% of the cohort identifying as Ashkenazi Jewish — sharply distinguishing the germline landscape of LGSC from HGSC.

Cohort & data

  • n = 119 patients with centrally reviewed LGSC (ovarian, primary peritoneal LGSC, and serous borderline tumors with invasive implants), drawn from 2,063 epithelial ovarian carcinomas sequenced on the MSK-IMPACT panel between 04/2015 and 02/2021.
  • Cancer type: LGSOC (low-grade serous ovarian cancer).
  • Dataset: lgsoc_mapk_msk_2022.
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT panel at four versions — IMPACT341 (n=8), IMPACT410 (n=23), IMPACT468 (n=83), and IMPACT505 (n=5); median sequencing depth 588x (range 94–1,231x).
  • Tumor samples: primary (n=81) and recurrent (n=38); matched normal blood for all.
  • Germline: 79/119 (66%) underwent germline genetic testing (custom panels of 76–90 genes at MSK plus outside labs); LOH inferred via FACETS.
  • Clinical: 110 (92%) had advanced-stage (FIGO III/IV) disease; median age at diagnosis 48 years (range 20–79); median OS follow-up 62.7 months.
  • 16 patients (13%) self-identified as Ashkenazi Jewish.

Key findings

  • MAPK pathway alteration prevalence: 71/119 (60%) of LGSCs carried a somatic MAPK pathway alteration, with BRAF (n=13), KRAS (n=39), HRAS (n=1), NRAS (n=13), NF1 (n=2), NF2 (n=1), MAP3K1 (n=1), and ERBB2 (n=5) hits (PMID:35443055).
  • KRAS hotspots dominate: 33% of cases (G12V n=19; G12D n=14; G12C n=3; G12R n=2; G12A n=1).
  • NRAS hotspots: 11% (Q61R n=9; Q61K n=4). BRAF: 11% (V600E n=8; D594N n=2; N581I n=1; BRAF–MKRN1 fusion n=1; BRAF–AGAP3 fusion n=1).
  • EIF1AX alterations in 10% (n=12) and CDKN2A homozygous deletions in 8% (n=9).
  • Mutually exclusive RAS/RAF: KRAS vs NRAS p=0.004 (q=0.46); KRAS vs BRAF p=0.034 (q=1.0); BRAF vs NRAS p=0.204 (q=1.0); only one tumor had co-existing BRAF + KRAS.
  • No driver alterations in 18% (n=21) of LGSCs despite adequate tumor purity (median 30% vs 40% in altered cases; p=0.12).
  • Low TMB: median 1.8 mutations/Mb (range 0–17.8); median 1 non-synonymous somatic mutation (range 0–16). All 102 evaluable cases were microsatellite stable (median MSIsensor score 0.12). Median fraction of genome altered 0.16.
  • KRAS association with age and platinum sensitivity: KRAS-mutant tumors were enriched in patients diagnosed at >50 years (p=0.03) and in platinum-sensitive disease (p=0.04). NRAS and BRAF showed no significant association with either variable.
  • Overall survival (univariate): MAPK pathway alteration (p=0.005) and platinum sensitivity (p=0.001) were associated with improved OS; age (p>0.99) and residual disease (p=0.29) were not.
  • Overall survival (multivariate, n=90 landmark cohort): Both MAPK pathway alteration (HR 2.5 [1.2–5.2] for absence vs presence, p=0.019) and platinum sensitivity (HR 0.4 [0.2–0.7] for sensitive vs resistant, p=0.005) remained independently associated with OS.
  • Crude mortality: 14/53 (26%) MAPK-altered patients died versus 18/37 (49%) MAPK-wildtype; 12/24 (50%) platinum-resistant died versus 20/66 (30%) platinum-sensitive.
  • Median OS: 339 months (MAPK-altered) vs 125 months (MAPK-wildtype); 234 months (platinum-sensitive) vs 45 months (platinum-resistant).
  • PFS: Only residual disease after initial cytoreduction remained significantly associated with PFS on multivariate analysis (p=0.04).
  • Sequential tumor sampling (n=8): In 2 of 5 patients whose first tested tumor lacked driver alterations, subsequent recurrences harbored drivers (one CDKN2A/B deletion, one BRAF G469V), supporting a role for repeat sequencing across the disease course.
  • Germline: 7/79 tested (9%) carried a pathogenic germline mutation: RB1 (n=1), BAP1 (n=1), MUTYH (n=2; both c.1187G>A p.Gly396Asp), CHEK2 (n=1), APC (n=1), FANCA (n=1). No germline BRCA1/2 mutations were identified.
  • Biallelic germline driver: LOH at the germline locus was only observed in 1 of the 2 MUTYH carriers; the BAP1 carrier had a somatic frameshift BAP1 but retained BAP1 IHC, suggesting cis configuration on one allele rather than biallelic loss.
  • MEK inhibitor experience: 4 patients received MEK inhibitor for recurrence (3 MAPK-altered); none progressed on MEK inhibitor, but all discontinued for toxicity at a median 6.7 months (range 0.7–22.6).

Genes & alterations

  • KRAS — hotspot mutations in 33% (39/119); enriched in age >50 (p=0.03) and platinum-sensitive disease (p=0.04). G12V is the most frequent variant (n=19).
  • NRAS — Q61R/Q61K hotspots in 11% (13/119); not significantly associated with age or platinum sensitivity but with a numerical trend toward platinum sensitivity (90% vs 71%, p=0.28).
  • BRAF — 11% (13/119): V600E (n=8), D594N (n=2), N581I (n=1), and two fusions (BRAF–MKRN1, BRAF–AGAP3).
  • HRAS, NF1, NF2, MAP3K1 — singletons/rare events contributing to the broader MAPK pathway-altered group.
  • ERBB2 — 5 cases including 2 amplifications and 1 Y772_A775dup hotspot; included in MAPK-altered group.
  • EIF1AX — 10% (12/119); recurrent in LGSC but not part of the MAPK pathway gene set used for survival analysis.
  • CDKN2A — homozygous deletion in 8% (9/119).
  • USP9X — discussed as a candidate driver reported in up to 27% of LGSCs in prior whole-exome work; not on MSK-IMPACT and so may explain some of the 18% no-alteration cases.
  • Negative results: no detected alterations in MAP2K1, MAP2K2, MAP2K4, MAP3K13, MAP3K14, MAPK1, MAPK3, MAPKAP1, DUSP4, RRAS, RRAS2, or RAF1 in this cohort.
  • TP53 — characterized in the introduction as rare in LGSC (consistent with WHO classification); not a recurrent driver in this cohort.
  • Germline pathogenic carriers: RB1, BAP1, MUTYH p.Gly396Asp ×2, CHEK2, APC, FANCA.
  • BRCA1 / BRCA2 — explicitly no pathogenic germline mutations identified, contrasting with reported 3.5–5.7% prevalence in prior LGSC literature and the 20% rate in HGSC.

Clinical implications

  • Prognostic biomarker: MAPK pathway alteration is an independent favorable prognostic factor for OS in LGSC (multivariate HR 2.5 for absence vs presence, p=0.019), and platinum sensitivity remains independently favorable (HR 0.4, p=0.005). The authors recommend that future LGSC trials stratify or report MAPK alteration distribution.
  • Predictive context: The result is biologically coherent with the MILO/ENGOT-ov11 phase III trial showing a PFS advantage for binimetinib in KRAS-mutated vs KRAS-wildtype recurrent LGSC. The current paper extends that signal from PFS-on-MEKi to OS in a real-world cohort treated heterogeneously.
  • Germline testing yield: Pathogenic germline mutation rate was 9% across an 88-gene panel — higher than the 6% reported by Norquist et al. (likely due to broader panels) but well below HGSC’s 20%. The complete absence of germline BRCA1/2 mutations argues against routine HBOC framing of LGSC and supports separate germline counseling pathways.
  • MEK inhibitor tolerability remains the limiting factor: 4/4 patients on MEKi for recurrence discontinued due to toxicity (median 6.7 months) despite no progression events while on drug.
  • Sequential sequencing: Re-biopsy and re-sequencing at recurrence may unmask actionable MAPK pathway drivers absent at first sampling (2/5 cases without initial drivers acquired one — CDKN2A/B deletion or BRAF G469V).
  • Maintenance therapy: Bevacizumab and endocrine maintenance regimens were used heterogeneously; no maintenance regimen reached statistical significance for PFS in this cohort (p=0.11 in NED group; p=0.17 in evidence-of-disease group).

Limitations & open questions

  • Single-center, retrospective design with referral bias toward advanced-stage disease (92% FIGO III/IV); sensitivity analyses restricted to patients first seen at diagnosis (n=60) lost power to detect OS associations.
  • Panel coverage gaps: MSK-IMPACT does not cover USP9X, which a prior whole-exome study reported altered in up to 27% of LGSCs — a plausible explanation for some of the 18% of tumors lacking detectable drivers. WES studies of these “negative” cases are needed.
  • Germline ascertainment bias: Only 66% of patients underwent germline testing, and 28 cases relied on outside-lab reports (Myriad, Ambry, Invitae, etc.) with heterogeneous gene panels. The absence of BRCA1/2 carriers might partly reflect ascertainment, although prior LGSC literature also reports low BRCA1/2 prevalence (3.5–5.7%).
  • Tumor sample heterogeneity: Both primary (n=81) and recurrent (n=38) tumors were sequenced; molecular alterations may evolve over time as demonstrated in 8 paired-sample analyses.
  • MEK inhibitor cohort is tiny (n=4) and uninterpretable for efficacy; toxicity-driven discontinuation remains the dominant limitation.
  • Open question: What drives the 18% of LGSCs without any detectable alteration on MSK-IMPACT, and do they belong to a distinct clinical subtype? Multi-institutional WES studies are warranted.
  • Open question: Is MAPK pathway alteration the cause of platinum sensitivity, a marker of indolent biology, or both? The independent association of both with OS on multivariate analysis suggests partially distinct biology.
