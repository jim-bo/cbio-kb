TL;DR

This is the capstone Perspective of the TCGA PanCancer Atlas, synthesizing analyses across 11,000 tumors from 33 cancer types into three themes: (1) interactions between somatic drivers and germline pathogenic variants, (2) links between the tumor genome and the methylome/transcriptome/proteome, and (3) interactions between cancer cells and the tumor microenvironment. Using a consensus driver list of 299 genes, >3,200 oncogenic mutations, and >800 pathogenic/likely-pathogenic germline variants in 99 predisposition genes (~8% of cases), Ding et al. characterize the joint germline–somatic landscape, somatic–somatic co-occurrence and mutual-exclusivity networks, cis/trans transcriptional effects of driver mutation type, and driver-immune-subtype associations across six immune subtypes (C1–C6).

Cohort & data

  • 11,000 tumors across 33 cancer types from the TCGA PanCancer Atlas; 9,389 samples used for joint germline/somatic analyses (PMID:29625049).
  • Germline calling on dbGaP-deposited paired tumor/normal data aligned to GRCh37-lite with bwa v0.5.9; germline SNVs via varscan v2.3.8 and GATK HaplotypeCaller; indels via Varscan, GATK, and pindel (≥2-of-3 consensus, or Pindel-unique ≥30× coverage / ≥20% VAF). Germline analysis identified ~1.5 billion calls, refined to 1,461 manually-reviewed pathogenic/likely-pathogenic variants (PMID:29625049).
  • Somatic calling via the mc3 PASS MAF (syn7824274), produced by 7 callers: mutect, varscan, indelocator, pindel, somatic-sniper, radia, and muse. 22,485,627 putative variants reduced to 2,907,335 high-confidence calls; ~3.6 million somatic calls reported across the cohort (PMID:29625049).
  • Tumors and dataset bundles correspond to the TCGA PanCancer Atlas study set including gbm_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, brca_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, coadread_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, ov_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, luad_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, lusc_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, ucec_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, stad_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, and others.
  • Assays: TCGA whole-exome-seq for somatic/germline variants, rna-seq (RSEM via rsem) for expression, dna-methylation-array for methylation, rppa for proteomics, and CIBERSORT-derived immune fractions (cibersortx, 22 cell types aggregated to 9). MSI quantified via msisensor (threshold score >4).

Key findings

  • Predisposition genes are dominated by genome-integrity pathways. Genome-integrity genes account for 63% (490/769) of germline pathogenic variants but only 14% (8,850/75,825) of somatic driver events (Fisher’s exact p = 7e-151) (PMID:29625049).
  • OV is the cancer type most skewed toward genome-integrity disruption in both germline and somatic compartments, driven by BRCA1/BRCA2 germline variants and high-frequency TP53 mutation. LUSC has 4% germline vs 89% somatic genome-integrity disruption (PMID:29625049).
  • Earlier age of onset with germline BRCA1/2. Mean age at diagnosis was 54.4 ± 13.0 years for germline-BRCA1/2 carriers vs 62.3 ± 13.4 years when the BRCA1/2 mutation is somatic (P = 2.07e-10, 95% CI -10.27 to -5.57). Significant per-tumor FDRs: OV 9.12e-6, LUSC 9.23e-3, BRCA 1.15e-2 (t-test) (PMID:29625049).
  • BRCA1/2 has 854 somatic mutations (571 samples) and 153 germline mutations (152 samples) when considered jointly across the pan-cancer cohort (PMID:29625049).
  • MMR/MSI: 250/1,464 samples with non-synonymous somatic MMR mutations and 18/60 samples with germline pathogenic MMR variants reach MSIsensor score >4. 16/18 germline-MMR MSI-high samples also carry somatic MMR mutations, a candidate checkpoint-blockade-responsive population (PMID:29625049).
  • MSI-high tumors over-express immune effectors (GZMA, PRF1, GZMK, GZMH) in COADREAD, STAD, and UCEC (Kolmogorov–Smirnov P < 0.01) (PMID:29625049).
  • TP53 is the largest co-occurrence/exclusivity hub of the somatic driver network (Mantel–Haenszel FDR < 0.1): co-occurring with IDH1, ATRX, PPP2R1A, RB1, CDKN2A; mutually exclusive with PIK3CA, HRAS, CTNNB1, ARID1A, FGFR3 (PMID:29625049).
  • Ras-pathway exclusivity is preserved across virtually all 33 tumor types: BRAF mutually exclusive with KRAS/NRAS/HRAS; homologous pairs IDH1/IDH2 and GNAQ/GNA11 show exclusivity (the latter dominating uveal melanoma); PIK3CA–PIK3R1 show exclusivity within the PI3K complex (PMID:29625049).
  • Context-dependent driver interactions: TP53 and KRAS are mutually exclusive in COAD, READ, and LUAD but significantly co-occur in PAAD, demonstrating that pan-cancer relationships do not always hold tissue-by-tissue (PMID:29625049).
  • Cis transcriptional effect of mutation type: Missense mutations tend to up-regulate driver expression; nonsense/frameshift mutations down-regulate it (consistent with nonsense-mediated decay). Tumor suppressors ATRX, BRCA1, NF1, RB1 show reduced expression in mutants; oncogenes EGFR and KIT show elevated expression in missense mutants (FDR < 0.1). GATA3 is the notable exception in BRCA: frameshift/nonsense alleles increase mRNA (FDR = 4.54e-18 Welch’s t-test), consistent with reported gain-of-function (PMID:29625049).
  • Multiplatform integration: Of 40 driver genes associated with multiplatform iCluster combinations, TP53, KRAS, and PIK3CA are each enriched in ≥10 multiplatform clusters, with ARID1A, BRAF, CTNNB1, KMT2D, PTEN, and APC enriched in ≥4 (PMID:29625049).
  • LGG/GBM split by methylation cluster, not by mRNA cluster: IDH1-driven LGGs fall into methylation cluster 1 (330/351 samples are IDH1-mutant), while EGFR-driven LGG and GBM fall into methylation cluster 16, despite both groups occupying the same mRNA and RPPA clusters (PMID:29625049).
  • Six pan-cancer immune subtypes (Wound Healing C1 = 2,416; IFN-γ Dominant C2 = 2,591; Inflammatory C3 = 2,397; Lymphocyte Depleted C4 = 1,157; Immunologically Quiet C5 = 385; TGF-β Dominant C6 = 180), derived by mclust on 5 representative signatures and BIC selection (160-signature ssGSEA panel) (PMID:29625049).
  • Driver mutations sculpt the immune infiltrate: Within C3, BRAF-mutant tumors have higher CD8 T-cell fraction than NRAS-mutant tumors (ANOVA P < 2e-5), and a CD8–CD274 (PD-L1)–PDCD1 (PD-1) signaling loop is recovered, suggesting BRAF + PD-L1 co-targeting may be synergistic. Within C5, ATRX- or TP53-mutant tumors have more macrophages and fewer CD8 T-cells (ANOVA P < 2e-8); IDH1-driven gliomas show lower STAT1 and reduced CXCL10 (PMID:29625049).
  • Neoantigen–infiltrate correlations differ by immune subtype: CD8 fraction rises with neoantigen load in C2 (FDR < 1e-15); CD4 and neutrophil fractions rise with neoantigen load in C3 (FDR < 1e-25); macrophage fraction rises with neoantigen load in C5 (FDR < 5e-3) (PMID:29625049).
  • Splicing & fusions as drivers: The Splicing AWG identified 1,964 mostly missense/synonymous mutations creating novel splice junctions across 8,656 tumors; the Spliceosome Pathway AWG flagged 119 of >400 surveyed splicing-factor genes as likely drivers; fusion drivers range from disease-defining (e.g., SS18/SSX1 or SSX2 in synovial sarcoma) to small subsets (e.g., ALK/ROS1 fusions in LUAD) (PMID:29625049).

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — Largest hub of somatic–somatic interactions; co-occurs with IDH1, ATRX, PPP2R1A, RB1, CDKN2A; mutually exclusive with PIK3CA, HRAS, CTNNB1, ARID1A, FGFR3. Drives one of two mutually-exclusive UCEC networks (with PPP2R1A). Mutual exclusivity with KRAS in COAD/READ/LUAD but co-occurrence in PAAD (PMID:29625049).
  • BRCA1 / BRCA2 — 854 somatic mutations (571 samples) + 153 germline mutations (152 samples). Germline carriers have ~8 years earlier onset than somatic-only carriers. In BRCA, somatic BRCA1/2 mutants down-regulate antigen-processing and leukocyte-cytotoxicity programs; germline BRCA1/2 mutants down-regulate mitochondrial respiratory-chain and metabolic programs (PMID:29625049).
  • IDH1 — Defines methylation cluster 1 in LGG (330/351 samples mutant); IDH1-driven gliomas exhibit lower STAT1, decreasing CXCL10 and immune infiltrate (PMID:29625049).
  • EGFR — Drives methylation cluster 16 in LGG and GBM; missense mutations are associated with elevated EGFR mRNA in the cis-expression analysis (PMID:29625049).
  • KRAS / NRAS / HRAS / BRAF — Strong mutual exclusivity within Ras signaling across nearly all 33 tumor types; KRAS/NRAS/HRAS and BRAF V600 are among the most frequently predicted neoantigens. BRAF-mutant tumors in C3 have higher CD8 T-cell fractions than NRAS-mutant tumors (PMID:29625049).
  • PIK3CA — Among the three most multiplatform-cluster-enriched drivers (with TP53 and KRAS); drives BRCA mRNA/RPPA clusters 23/C6 but spans methylation clusters 6 and 24 (PMID:29625049).
  • GNA11 / GNAQ — Activating mutations in uveal melanoma; drive calcium-signaling deregulation in UVM (PMID:29625049).
  • GATA3 — In BRCA, frameshift/nonsense alleles paradoxically increase mRNA (FDR = 4.54e-18) and are associated with downregulated microtubule/cytoskeleton programs not seen in missense mutants, consistent with reported gain-of-function (PMID:29625049).
  • CDH1 — In BRCA, nonsense/frameshift mutants upregulate leukocyte-migration genes; missense mutants do not (PMID:29625049).
  • CASP8 — Missense mutants over-express CASP8 (FDR < 0.1) (PMID:29625049).
  • APC — APC- and KRAS-driven COAD/READ tumors share mRNA cluster 15 and RPPA C8 but partition into methylation clusters 10 vs 11 (PMID:29625049).
  • ARID1A, PTEN, CTNNB1, PPP2R1A, CTCF — Partition UCEC into TP53-PPP2R1A vs CTNNB1-PTEN-CTCF mutually-exclusive networks; ARID1A/PTEN/CTNNB1/PIK3CA-enriched cluster has low TP53, consistent with copy-number-low vs copy-number-high endometrial subtypes (PMID:29625049).
  • DDR predisposition genes CHEK2, ATM, BRIP1, PALB2, PMS2 — Among the top germline-variant-bearing genes in the core DDR pathway (23 of 36 DDR-affecting predisposition genes belong to Core DDR) (PMID:29625049).
  • MMR genes MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 — Somatic non-synonymous mutations in 250/1,464 samples reach MSI-high; germline variants in MMR (18/60) also reach MSI-high, with 16/18 carrying both germline and somatic hits (PMID:29625049).
  • POLE — Hypermutated UCEC subtype context (PMID:29625049).
  • CD274 (PD-L1), PDCD1 (PD-1) — Form a CD8–PD-L1–PD-1 signaling loop in the C3 immune subtype, where BRAF-driven tumors are enriched (PMID:29625049).
  • SS18 / SSX1 / SSX2 — SS18–SSX1/SSX2 fusion is disease-defining in synovial sarcoma (referenced from the Fusion AWG) (PMID:29625049).
  • ALK, ROS1 — Fusion drivers in small subsets of LUAD (referenced from the Fusion AWG) (PMID:29625049).

Clinical implications

  • Germline testing rationale: ~8% of TCGA cases carry pathogenic germline variants in 99 predisposition genes, dominated by genome-integrity (PARP/HRD) pathways; germline BRCA1/2 carriers present nearly a decade earlier than somatic-only carriers, supporting age-stratified germline screening in OV, BRCA, and LUSC (PMID:29625049).
  • Checkpoint-blockade biomarker stack: 16 of 18 germline-MMR carriers also carry somatic MMR mutations and are MSI-high, defining a population with elevated neoantigen load expected to respond to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. MSI-high tumors in COADREAD/STAD/UCEC over-express GZMA/PRF1/GZMK/GZMH (PMID:29625049).
  • Combination therapy hypothesis: In the C3 immune subtype, BRAF-mutant tumors enrich CD8 T-cells and assemble a CD8–PD-L1–PD-1 loop, suggesting synergy between BRAF inhibition and PD-L1 blockade (PMID:29625049).
  • Functional redundancy as a resistance mechanism: TP53/PIK3CA and KRAS/BRAF mutual exclusivity in cell-death and MAPK processes (permutation P < 1e-5) implies that single-driver loss-of-function programs can be re-achieved through alternative drivers in heterogeneous tumors — relevant to acquired resistance and metastatic seeding (PMID:29625049).
  • Subtype-specific pathway therapy: Calcium-signaling deregulation in UVM (GNA11/GNAQ) and Notch deregulation in BLCA highlight tumor-type-anchored druggable axes (PMID:29625049).
  • Bioinformatic care plans: The Perspective anticipates that multi-omic, network-aware tools will be required to navigate the combinatorial space of drug + immunotherapy + signature-targeted regimens (PMID:29625049).

Limitations & open questions

  • Authors highlight: TCGA samples are overwhelmingly primary tumors; metastatic-driver biology is largely unaddressed and is a stated goal of the Cancer Moonshot / Human Tumor Atlas Network (HTAN) (PMID:29625049).
  • Authors highlight: Oncogenic mutations such as BRAF V600E are observed in phenotypically normal tissues, raising the question of which somatic events are clonally tolerated vs. truly transformative (PMID:29625049).
  • Methodological caveats acknowledged in STAR Methods: Mutation-burden dichotomization at 500 mutations for co-occurrence/exclusivity testing is heuristic; OV and LAML samples retained wga filter flags to preserve sample size, introducing artifact risk; cell-of-origin clustering used private communications with the Cell-of-Origin AWG, complicating reproducibility (PMID:29625049).
  • Open question for cross-paper synthesis: Many findings are reported as fractions or odds ratios at FDR < 0.1, leaving room for stricter follow-up (e.g., effect-size meta-analysis against later MSK-IMPACT panel cohorts).
  • Coverage gap: This Perspective references but does not deeply re-analyze the dedicated AWG manuscripts (Aneuploidy, Fusions, Splicing, Driver, Germline, MC3, Cell of Origin, PanImmune, Imaging, DDR, Spliceosome) — those are the primary sources for the underlying numbers.
