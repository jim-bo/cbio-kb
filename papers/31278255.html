Upper tract urothelial carcinoma has a luminal-papillary T-cell depleted contexture and activated FGFR3 signaling

TL;DR

Robinson, Vlachostergios and colleagues performed integrated whole-exome and RNA sequencing of 37 high-grade upper tract urothelial carcinoma (UTUC) tumors from Weill Cornell Medicine (WCM, n=22) and Baylor College of Medicine / MD Anderson Cancer Center (BCM-MDA, n=15), compared against the TCGA muscle-invasive bladder cancer cohort. They show that the great majority of high-grade sporadic UTUCs are luminal-papillary by UNC/MDACC/TCGA classifiers, are T-cell depleted at the immune-contexture level, harbor FGFR3 mutations in ~30% of cases, and despite reduced MMR protein expression are not microsatellite-unstable and carry a lower tumor mutational burden than urothelial carcinoma of the bladder (UCB). They further propose FGFR3 as a cell-intrinsic driver of T-cell exclusion via attenuated IFNG signaling and provide rationale for combining FGFR3 inhibitors (e.g. erdafitinib) with immune checkpoint blockade in UTUC.

Cohort & data

  • Patients: 37 high-grade UTUC tumor–normal pairs profiled by whole-exome sequencing (WCM UTUC n=22; BCM-MDA UTUC n=15). RNA-seq meta-dataset: 32 UTUC tumors (WCM n=17; BCM-MDA n=15). 64.8% of patients were former/current smokers.
  • Cancer type: high-grade upper tract urothelial carcinoma (UTUC), nephroureterectomy or endoscopic biopsy specimens; “conventional” urothelial histology only.
  • Comparator cohort: TCGA muscle-invasive bladder cancer (blca_tcga_pub_2017, n=124 WES / n=128 RNA-seq); see PMID:28988769 for the source TCGA UCB study.
  • Dataset of record: utuc_cornell_baylor_mdacc_2019 on cBioPortal (https://www.cbioportal.org/study?id=utuc_cornell_baylor_mdacc_2019).
  • Assays / methods: whole-exome sequencing (Illumina HiSeq 2500, 2×100 bp; ~85× mean coverage for BCM-MDA samples), RNA-seq aligned with STAR, consensus SNV calling combining MuTect / Strelka / VarScan, variant annotation by Oncotator, MSIsensor for microsatellite instability, Bayesian NMF for mutational signature discovery, consensus hierarchical clustering for subtype calling (UNC BASE47, MDACC, TCGA classifiers), GSEA, and immunohistochemistry (MLH1, PMS2, MSH2, MSH6 on a Leica Bond III stainer with HALO H-score quantification).
  • Functional validation: Re-analysis of a published FGFR3 shRNA / doxycycline-inducible knockdown microarray dataset in the RT-112 UCB line; pharmacologic FGFR3 inhibition with erdafitinib at 1 nM and 5 nM in RT-112, RT-4, and SW780 UCB cell lines, with RT-qPCR readout of BST2.

Key findings

  • FGFR3 mutation enrichment in UTUC. FGFR3 mutations were detected in 11/37 (29.7%) UTUC tumors versus 17/124 (13.7%) in the TCGA UCB comparison (Wilcoxon test, P=0.04).
  • No significant difference in other commonly mutated bladder-cancer drivers. Mutation prevalence in chromatin modifiers (KMT2D, ARID1A, KDM6A), RTK / PI3K pathway (PIK3CA, HRAS), transcription factors (RXRA, KLF5, ELF3), and cell-cycle genes (TP53, RB1, CDKN1A, CDKN2A) did not differ significantly between UTUC and TCGA UCB.
  • Dominant mutational signatures in UTUC are APOBEC (COSMIC 2, 13), defective MMR (COSMIC 6, C>T at CpG), and an ERCC2-like signature (COSMIC 5). Identified by hierarchical clustering of cosine similarity against the 30 COSMIC signatures.
  • UTUC is luminal-papillary. Across three independent classifiers, the high-grade UTUC tumors clustered overwhelmingly with luminal lineages:
    • UNC BASE47: 27/32 (84.3%) UTUC luminal vs 59/128 (46.1%) UCB luminal.
    • MDACC classifier: 22/32 (68.7%) UTUC luminal vs 47/128 (36.7%) UCB luminal.
    • TCGA classifier: 20/32 (62.5%) UTUC luminal-papillary vs 35/128 (27.3%) UCB luminal-papillary; the majority of remaining UTUC tumors (8/12, 67%) carried other luminal labels.
    • Unsupervised NMF on the top-25%-variable genes (rank k=3) confirmed UTUCs partition with the luminal/CIS-low component.
  • UTUC is T-cell depleted. A 170-immune-gene classifier built from the top variable genes containing CD8A split tumors into T-cell-inflamed and T-cell-depleted clusters. 28/32 (87.5%) UTUC tumors were T-cell depleted versus a roughly even split in UCB (57/128 inflamed, 71/128 depleted); Fisher’s exact P=9×10⁻⁵ (UTUC vs UCB) / P=0.0009 reported in the text. T-cell-depleted tumors showed concordant downregulation of CD8A, CCL2, CCL3, CCL4, CCL5, CXCL9, CXCL10, and IFNG response genes.
  • FGFR3 is an outlier-overexpressed gene in UTUC. Outlier analysis on 74 cancer-related genes flagged FGFR3 high in 14/32 (43.7%) UTUC tumors. Nine activating missense mutations were identified. FGFR3 mRNA was significantly higher in the T-cell-depleted cluster (Wilcoxon P=1.3×10⁻⁶); PPARG showed the same pattern (P=1.1×10⁻⁵).
  • FGFR3 cell-autonomously suppresses IFNG response. In RT-112 UCB cells, doxycycline-induced shRNA knockdown of FGFR3 upregulated IFNG-response genes BST2 (P<0.001), MX2 (P=0.003), IRF9 (P=0.005), and GBP2 (P=0.038). Pharmacologic inhibition with erdafitinib at 1 nM and 5 nM significantly upregulated BST2 in RT-112, RT-4, and SW780 cells (all three lines harbor activating FGFR3 fusions: RT-112 and RT-4 carry FGFR3–TACC3, SW780 carries FGFR3–BAIAP2L1); no statistically significant difference between 1 nM and 5 nM doses in any line.
  • Somatic MMR transcript and protein loss without MSI or TMB elevation. 35 canonical DDR genes were differentially expressed between WCM UTUC and TCGA UCB; the MMR transcripts MLH1, MSH2, and MSH6 were significantly down in UTUC. Matched IHC on 16 WCM UTUC vs 14 stage-matched WCM UCB showed significantly lower H-scores for MLH1, PMS2, MSH2, and MSH6 (t-test P<0.05; with one MMR-protein composite reported at P=1.9×10⁻⁵). No germline MMR mutations (in MLH1, PMS2, MSH2, MSH6) were detected on germline WES of the UTUC cohorts.
  • UTUC is not microsatellite-unstable. Mean MSIsensor scores in WCM UTUC and TCGA UCB were both below the MSI-H threshold of 3.5 and indistinguishable from each other.
  • Sporadic UTUC has lower TMB than UCB. Mean total mutational burden was 2.91 mutations/Mb in UTUC (WCM+BCM-MDA) vs 5.46 mutations/Mb in TCGA UCB (Mann–Whitney P=1.9×10⁻⁵).

Genes & alterations

  • FGFR3 — activating missense mutations in 11/37 (29.7%) UTUC tumors, significantly higher than 13.7% in TCGA UCB (P=0.04); outlier-overexpressed in 14/32 UTUC tumors and proposed as a cell-intrinsic driver of the T-cell-depleted phenotype via suppression of IFNG response genes. Recurrent activating FGFR3–TACC3 and FGFR3–BAIAP2L1 fusions are present in the cell-line models used for functional validation.
  • TP53, KMT2D, ARID1A, PIK3CA, KDM6A, RB1, ERCC2, CDKN1A, STAG2, FBXW7, RXRA, KLF5, ELF3, TSC1, RHOB, HRAS, CDKN2A, KMT2C, ATM — recurrently mutated in the UTUC cohort at frequencies not significantly different from TCGA UCB (Fig. 1a–b).
  • MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 — significantly reduced transcript (for MLH1/MSH2/MSH6) and protein (all four by IHC) expression in UTUC vs UCB in the absence of germline or somatic mutations; insufficient to cause MSI or elevate TMB.
  • CD8A, CCL2, CCL3, CCL4, CCL5, CXCL9, CXCL10, IFNG — concordantly downregulated in the T-cell-depleted UTUC cluster.
  • BST2, MX2, IRF9, GBP2 — IFNG-response genes upregulated upon shRNA knockdown or pharmacologic inhibition of FGFR3; proposed mechanistic link to T-cell exclusion in luminal-papillary UTUC.
  • PPARG — significantly higher mRNA expression in the T-cell-depleted cluster (P=1.1×10⁻⁵), consistent with a luminal/PPARG-driven phenotype.

Clinical implications

  • Rationale for FGFR3 inhibition in UTUC. The combination of (i) enrichment of FGFR3 activating mutations, (ii) outlier FGFR3 overexpression in luminal-papillary UTUC, and (iii) reversal of IFNG-response gene suppression by FGFR3 inhibition supports clinical evaluation of FGFR3 inhibitors in UTUC. The authors invoke the FDA accelerated approval of erdafitinib in metastatic urothelial carcinoma harboring actionable FGFR alterations (phase 2 response rate 32.2% in 87 patients) as direct precedent.
  • Rationale for combining FGFR3 inhibitors with immune checkpoint blockade. Because FGFR3 signaling cell-autonomously dampens IFNG response and is enriched in T-cell-depleted UTUC, the authors propose combining FGFR3 inhibitors with PD-1/PD-L1 blockade as a targeted strategy to remodel the immune contexture of UTUC, potentially extendable to other FGFR3-activated tumors.
  • Mechanistic explanation for blunted ICI response in luminal-papillary UC. The luminal-papillary, T-cell-depleted, low-TMB profile of sporadic UTUC is consistent with the lower response rates of luminal-papillary UCB to atezolizumab (Rosenberg et al., IMvigor210; cluster I had the lowest response) and nivolumab (Sharma et al., CheckMate 275; cluster I tumors with low IFNG signature were less responsive).
  • MMR/MSI implications. Despite reduced MMR transcript and protein expression, sporadic (non-Lynch) UTUC is not hypermutated or microsatellite-unstable — arguing against MSI/TMB-based ICI biomarkers as primary stratifiers in sporadic UTUC and aligning with the earlier observation that incomplete MMR loss can preserve microsatellite stability.

Limitations & open questions

  • Sample size. 37 UTUC tumors across three institutions remains small given the rarity of UTUC; the authors explicitly call for confirmation in larger cohorts.
  • Primary-only sampling. Stability of luminal-papillary subtype calls and immune contexture across matched primary and metastatic UTUC is not addressed and is flagged as future work.
  • High-grade only. The cohort is restricted to high-grade UTUC; low-grade UTUC may differ in subtype distribution (prior unsupervised clustering of mixed high+low grade UTUC produced four molecular subtypes — different framing than the UCB-anchored “continuum” used here).
  • FGFR3 mechanism in vivo. The IFNG-suppressing effect of FGFR3 is demonstrated in three UCB cell lines (RT-112, RT-4, SW780) under shRNA or short-duration erdafitinib exposure; in vivo evidence in UTUC models and direct evidence in patient tumors treated with FGFR inhibitors is not provided.
  • Drug-class generalization. Whether other pan-FGFR inhibitors such as BGJ398 (infigratinib) phenocopy the IFNG-response upregulation seen with erdafitinib is not tested here, though the authors note encouraging early clinical signals for both agents.
