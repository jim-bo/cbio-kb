Prospective Genotyping of Hepatocellular Carcinoma: Clinical Implications of Next-Generation Sequencing for Matching Patients to Targeted and Immune Therapies

TL;DR

Harding et al. prospectively sequenced matched tumor/normal DNA from 127 patients with advanced hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) at MSKCC using the MSK-IMPACT targeted NGS assay (341/410/468 genes) and correlated genotype with response to contemporary systemic therapy. They confirmed the canonical HCC genomic landscape (TERT 55.6%, CTNNB1 35.7%, TP53 32.5%; WNT and TP53 alterations mutually exclusive). Two clinically actionable findings emerged: oncogenic PI3K-mTOR pathway alterations were associated with shorter PFS/OS on sorafenib (n=81), and activating WNT/β-catenin alterations were associated with innate resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors (n=31, 0% vs 53% disease control). 24% of tumors harbored potentially actionable alterations, and 6% of systemically-treated patients were matched to genome-driven therapy.

Cohort & data

  • N = 127 unique HCC patients (134 specimens), enrolled August 2014 – August 2017 at MSKCC under prospective genotyping protocol NCT01775072.
  • Cancer type: conventional HCC (fibrolamellar and biphenotypic HCC-cholangiocarcinoma excluded).
  • Dataset: hcc_mskimpact_2018 — all data available via cBioPortal at study?id=hcc_mskimpact_2018 and variant calls in cBioPortal DataHub.
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT hybridization-capture targeted NGS sequenced on Illumina HiSeq 2500. Panel expanded during study: IMPACT341 (21 cases), IMPACT410 (70 cases), IMPACT468 (36 cases). Standard input 250 ng DNA (min 50 ng); mean coverage 690×, median tumor purity 60%.
  • TMB calculated per sample; MSI status via MSIsensor (MSI-H threshold ≥10).
  • Variant filtering for oncogenicity via OncoKB levels 1–4.
  • Cohort demographics: 79% male, 76% non-Asian; etiology HBV 17%, HCV 35%, nonviral 49%; BCLC stage A 29%, B 40%, C 30%; 61% not eligible for curative resection or transplantation; 16% local recurrence/extrahepatic specimens.
  • Treatment subsets: sorafenib n=81 (75 first-line, 6 second-line); immune checkpoint inhibitor n=31 (1 anti-CTLA-4 mono, 25 anti-PD-1/PD-L1 mono, 5 anti-PD-1 + anti-CTLA-4/KIR/LAG-3 combos).
  • Reference comparator: TCGA HCC cohort (N = 196).

Key findings

  • Mutation landscape (134 specimens): alterations in 127 (95%). Most frequent genes (≥10%): TERT 55.6%, CTNNB1 35.7%, TP53 32.5%, ARID1A 12.7%, TSC2 10.6%.
  • WNT pathway alterations 45% overall; additional WNT-pathway members: AXIN1 6.3%, AXIN2 0.05% (sic), APC 5.2%.
  • CNAs: CCND1/FGF19 amplification 7%, MYC 6.1%, MET 1.5%, VEGFA <1%; HLA-B loss 2.7%.
  • Mutual exclusivity: TP53 mutations vs. WNT pathway alterations P = 0.003 — defines at least two major genomic subsets.
  • Etiology: virally mediated HCC enriched for TP53 mutations vs. nonviral (45% vs. 21%, P = 0.001); numerically less WNT pathway alteration (41% vs. 60%, P = 0.7).
  • TMB: median 4.08; variance low (SD = 2.71, second only to germ cell tumors among solid tumors); no MSI-H or hypermutators observed; only 1/27 immunotherapy-evaluable patients had TMB > 10.
  • Sorafenib (n = 81; 79 evaluable): median PFS 4.8 mo, median OS 16.4 mo overall.
    • Oncogenic PI3K-mTOR pathway alterations (n = 12 of 79) → lower DCR (8.3% vs. 40.2%, P = 0.05), shorter median PFS (1.9 vs. 5.3 mo; HR 3.8, 95% CI 2.0–7.5, P < 0.0001) and OS (10.4 vs. 17.9 mo; HR 2.5, 95% CI 1.21–5.31, P = 0.01).
    • WNT, MAPK, TP53, cell-cycle, and chromatin-remodeling pathway alterations had no effect on sorafenib outcomes.
    • VEGFA amplification rare (3.9%) and anecdotally not associated with extreme sorafenib response.
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors (n = 31; 27 evaluable): ORR 11.1% (1 CR, 2 PR, 10 SD, 14 PD); median PFS 5.4 mo, median OS 12.9 mo.
    • Activating WNT/β-catenin alterations (10 patients) → 0/10 clinical benefit vs. 9/17 (53%) for non-WNT patients (P = 0.009).
    • Shorter median PFS in WNT-activated tumors (2.0 vs. 7.4 mo; HR 9.2, 95% CI 2.9–28.8, P < 0.0001) and numerically shorter median OS (9.1 vs. 15.2 mo; HR 2.6, 95% CI 0.76–8.7, P = 0.11).
    • TMB did not stratify ICI response: median TMB 3.8 (responders) vs. 3.9 (non-responders), P = 0.98; PFS for TMB <3.8 vs. ≥3.8 was 3.1 vs. 3.0 months (P = 0.21).
  • Actionability: 24% of patients harbored ≥1 potentially actionable alteration via OncoKB. No level 1/2A alterations were found. Actionable hits included TSC1 <1% / TSC2 7% truncating/inactivating, PTEN truncating/homdel 3.9%, FGF19 amplifications 6.3%, MET amplifications 1.5%, and oncogenic missense in HRAS, NRAS, PIK3CA, IDH1 (all <1%).
  • Matched therapy: 5/87 (5.7%) systemically-treated patients matched to genome-directed therapy. 4/10 TSC1/2-altered patients received everolimus off-label or on-trial; 1 patient with VEGFA + MET co-amplification received anti-VEGFR2 + anti-MET mAbs on trial. Of these 5, 4 had progressed on first-line sorafenib within 1.5–2.5 mo; all 4 achieved at least stable disease on matched therapy at first scan; 3/4 had ≥5 mo clinical benefit.

Genes & alterations

  • TERT — promoter/coding alterations in 55.6% (most frequently altered gene).
  • CTNNB1 — activating mutations in 35.7%; defines WNT-activated subset; predicts innate resistance to immune checkpoint blockade.
  • TP53 — mutated in 32.5%; mutually exclusive with WNT pathway alterations (P = 0.003); enriched in virally mediated HCC (45% vs. 21%, P = 0.001).
  • ARID1A — mutated in 12.7%.
  • TSC2 — inactivating/truncating in ~10.6% (7% by actionable-call); together with TSC1 these mTOR negative regulators are the rationale for matched everolimus. One TSC2 E95* patient progressed on sorafenib then had disease stabilization with minor tumor shrinkage on everolimus (Fig 4C).
  • TSC1 — truncating/inactivating <1%.
  • AXIN1 — inactivating in 6.3%; AXIN1-inactivated tumors grouped with CTNNB1-activated tumors as “WNT-activated” for ICI resistance analysis.
  • AXIN2 — reported at 0.05% (as published).
  • APC — alterations 5.2%.
  • CCND1 / FGF19 — coamplification on 11q13 amplicon in 7% (FGF19 amplification 6.3% by actionable-call); rationale for FGFR4 inhibitors in HCC.
  • MYC — amplification 6.1%.
  • MET — amplification 1.5%; one patient with concurrent 2.1× MET and 3.7× VEGFA amplification received anti-VEGFR2 + anti-MET mAbs.
  • VEGFA — amplification <1% in overall cohort, 3.9% of sorafenib-evaluable; proposed biomarker for extreme sorafenib response but not confirmed here.
  • HLA-B — loss 2.7%.
  • PTEN — truncating/homozygous deletion 3.9%; contributes to actionable PI3K-mTOR pathway alterations.
  • HRAS, NRAS, PIK3CA — oncogenic missense each <1%.
  • IDH1 — missense <1%; possibly defines a cholangiocarcinoma-like HCC subset susceptible to selective IDH1 inhibitors such as ivosidenib.
  • MTOR — discussed as part of mTOR pathway/actionability framework (with TSC1/TSC2 alterations driving everolimus response per prior renal-cell evidence).

Clinical implications

  • Predictive biomarker — sorafenib: activating PI3K-mTOR pathway alterations identify a subset (~15% of evaluable sorafenib patients) with markedly worse outcomes (PFS HR 3.8, OS HR 2.5). The authors propose mTOR inhibition as a rational salvage strategy in this group, citing umbrella trials NCI-MATCH (NCT02465060) and ASCO TAPUR (NCT02693535).
  • Predictive biomarker — immune checkpoint blockade: activating WNT/β-catenin pathway alterations (CTNNB1 gain-of-function or AXIN1 loss) are associated with innate resistance to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 ± anti-CTLA-4 therapy in HCC. All 10 WNT-altered ICI-treated patients had progressive disease at first scan vs. 53% disease control in non-WNT patients. Authors caution data are preliminary but, if validated, would inform patient selection and trial design.
  • Negative biomarker — TMB / MSI for ICI: TMB and MSI status are unlikely to be major determinants of ICI response in advanced HCC in this cohort (no MSI-H, TMB-median 3.8 nondiscriminating between responders and non-responders).
  • Genome-matched targeted therapy: 24% of HCCs have ≥1 OncoKB-actionable alteration; 6% of systemically-treated patients were matched to therapy in this study. Reported matched options: everolimus for TSC1/2-altered HCC; FGFR4 inhibitors for FGF19-amplified HCC (in development); MET inhibitors / cabozantinib for MET-amplified HCC; selective IDH1 inhibitors for IDH1-mutant HCC.
  • Other HCC standard agents discussed: regorafenib (second-line TKI), lenvatinib (first-line TKI non-inferior to sorafenib), nivolumab (CheckMate 040), pembrolizumab, tivantinib (failed MET-high HCC trial METIV-HCC).

Limitations & open questions

  • Targeted panel coverage: MSK-IMPACT cannot detect alterations in genes outside the 341/410/468 design, epigenetic silencing, or HBV integration events.
  • Tumor heterogeneity: single-site biopsy cannot fully characterize clonal architecture in multisite metastatic HCC; authors propose ctDNA or single-cell follow-on studies.
  • Small sample size: confounding by HCC etiology, AFP, extrahepatic spread, vascular invasion not fully controlled — particularly for the n = 31 ICI subset and the n = 12 PI3K-mTOR-altered sorafenib group.
  • Selection bias: favorable OS (16.4 mo) vs. published sorafenib (12.2 mo, ref 5) likely reflects MSKCC referral bias, access to novel therapies post-progression, and high proportion of preserved hepatic function (Child-Pugh A 96%).
  • Open question — WNT predictive value: whether the WNT-resistance signal is causal vs. confounded; needs validation in larger retrospective cohorts and prospective ICI studies. HLA-I heterogeneity (Chowell et al.) cited as another possible innate-resistance axis worth integrating.
  • Open question — mTOR inhibitor efficacy in HCC: matched everolimus produced cytostatic but modest benefit in TSC1/2-altered HCC; durability and the right drug/combination remain unresolved.
  • No level 1/2A alterations: underscores the gap between actionability annotation and FDA-validated biomarkers in HCC.
