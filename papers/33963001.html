Intrahepatic Cholangiocarcinoma with Lymph Node Metastasis: Treatment-Related Outcomes and the Role of Tumor Genomics in Patient Selection

TL;DR

Single-institution retrospective MSKCC study of 573 patients with liver-limited intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (IHC) treated 2000–2018 with curative-intent resection (N=237), hepatic arterial infusion chemotherapy (HAIC; N=196), or systemic chemotherapy alone (SYS; N=140). For node-positive (N1) patients, resection and HAIC produced statistically indistinguishable overall survival (19.7 vs 18.1 months, P=0.560), and both outperformed SYS (11.2 months, P=0.024). MSK-IMPACT sequencing of 219 patients showed that a composite “high-risk” alteration set — TP53 mutation, KRAS mutation, or CDKN2A/B deletion — stratified N1 patients into a poor-prognosis subgroup (median OS 12.1 vs 30.9 months, P=0.002), while IDH1/2 mutations and FGFR2 fusions had no impact on survival in the N1 setting.

Cohort & data

  • 573 patients with liver-limited IHCH treated 2000–2018 at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, stratified by initial treatment: resection (N=237, 41.4%), HAIC with floxuridine via hepatic arterial infusion pump (N=196, 34.2%), or systemic chemotherapy alone (N=140, 24.4%).
  • N1 (lymph-node-positive) breakdown: 38/237 resected (16.0%), 56/196 HAIC (28.6%), and 57/140 SYS patients with suspicious locoregional adenopathy on imaging (40.7%).
  • Genomic profiling on 219/433 resection+HAIC patients (50.6%) via MSK-IMPACT — IMPACT341 (N=26), IMPACT410 (N=93), and IMPACT468 (N=100); see msk-impact-panel.
  • Sequencing tissue source: primary tumors (N=195, 91.5%), local recurrence (N=6, 2.8%), extrahepatic recurrence (N=4, 1.9%), lymph-node metastases (N=14, 6.6%).
  • Sequencing data publicly available via cBioPortal study ihch_mskcc_2020.
  • Oncogenic-driver calls based on OncoKB.

Key findings

  • N0 disease: resection produced longest median OS (59.9 months, 95% CI 47.2–74.3), followed by HAIC (24.9 months, 95% CI 20.3–29.6), then SYS (13.7 months, 95% CI 8.7–19.4) (P<0.001) (PMID:33963001).
  • N1 disease: no survival difference between resection (median OS 19.7 months, 95% CI 12.1–27.2) and HAIC (18.1 months, 95% CI 14.1–26.6), P=0.560; both better than SYS (11.2 months, 95% CI 8.9–15.9), P=0.024.
  • N1 patients had ~half the median OS of N0 patients (19.7 vs 50.5 months), confirming LNM as a dominant prognostic factor.
  • Multivariable Cox model: HAIC vs resection was worse for N0 (HR 2.09, 95% CI 1.6–2.8) but equivalent for N1 (HR 0.81, 95% CI 0.5–1.3); the treatment × nodal-status interaction was significant (P<0.001).
  • HAIC cohort had heavier disease burden than resected: multifocal disease 74.0% vs 22.4%, median tumor size 8.6 vs 5.6 cm, poorly differentiated 41.7% vs 26.2% (all P<0.001).
  • Among IDH1/2-mutant patients, 10/49 (20.4%) received ivosidenib or vorasidenib on clinical trial (8 HAIC, 2 resection).
  • Eleven genes were altered in ≥5% of profiled patients; the five most prevalent: IDH1 (18.7%), ARID1A (15.5%), FGFR2 (15.5%), TP53 (14.6%), BAP1 (13.2%).

Genes & alterations

  • IDH1 / IDH2 mutations: present in IHC at high prevalence (IDH1 18.7% across the profiled cohort). In N1 patients, IDH1/2 mutation had no significant survival effect — IDH1/2mut HR 0.73 (95% CI 0.3–1.6, P=0.429); median OS 27.7 vs 18.1 months (P=0.427).
  • FGFR2 fusions: prevalence 15.5% in the profiled cohort overall; in N1 patients (N=4 FGFR2fus vs N=48 FGFR2wt), no survival difference (HR 0.64, 95% CI 0.15–2.7, P=0.545; median OS 31.4 vs 21.5 months, P=0.542).
  • TP53 and KRAS mutations: components of the composite “high-risk” alteration set associated with worse OS in N1 disease.
  • CDKN2A deletion (HR 3.3, 95% CI 1.1–9.5, P=0.030) and CDKN2B deletion (HR 6.0, 95% CI 2.5–14.6, P<0.001) — and combined CDKN2A/B deletion (HR 5.0, 95% CI 2.1–11.6, P<0.001) — each independently predicted worse OS in N1 patients. Median OS for CDKN2A/B-deleted N1 patients was ~7 months vs ~27 months for wild-type.
  • High-risk composite (any TP53 mut, KRAS mut, or CDKN2A/B deletion) in N1 disease: median OS 12.1 months (95% CI 5.7–21.5) vs 30.9 months (95% CI 18.1–47.0) for wild-type, P=0.002.
  • ARID1A (15.5%) and BAP1 (13.2%): among the top five most prevalent altered genes but not part of the high-risk classifier in this analysis.

Clinical implications

  • For lymph-node-positive IHC, the authors argue that HAIC is a viable alternative to resection: equivalent OS with lower morbidity (major hepatectomy carries ~50% morbidity and up to 5% 90-day mortality; HAIP placement has lower morbidity and admits a minimally invasive approach).
  • Both locoregional approaches (resection and HAIC) outperform systemic chemotherapy alone in N1 disease, suggesting that hepatic disease control remains clinically meaningful even when LNM is present.
  • The authors recommend pretreatment MSK-IMPACT (or comparable NGS) profiling for all IHC patients with N1 disease: presence of TP53 mutation, KRAS mutation, or CDKN2A/B deletion identifies a uniformly poor-prognosis subgroup (median OS ≤1 year) regardless of treatment modality, and these patients are unlikely to benefit from any liver-directed therapy.
  • Conversely, N1 patients with IDH1/2 mutations or FGFR2 fusions should not be excluded from locoregional therapy on the basis of those alterations alone — they did not portend worse outcomes in this series, despite being the dominant targetable lesions.
  • No survival difference was observed between N0 and NX patients (39.2 vs 37.1 months), supporting selective rather than routine lymphadenectomy when contemporary imaging plus intraoperative assessment is reliable.

Limitations & open questions

  • Retrospective, single-institution design with non-randomized treatment assignment; resection and HAIC cohorts differ substantially in disease burden, which the multivariable models attempt to correct for but cannot eliminate.
  • The SYS cohort’s “node-positive” classification rests on imaging-based locoregional adenopathy rather than pathologic confirmation, which the authors acknowledge over-estimates true pathologic LNM and may inflate apparent SYS underperformance.
  • Genomic-survival analyses pooled resection and HAIC patients (N1 N=52 total), limiting the ability to assess treatment-by-genotype interaction and producing small per-alteration subgroups (e.g., FGFR2fus N=4 in N1 disease).
  • Heterogeneity in adjuvant chemotherapy receipt across the resection cohort (30.8% overall; 76.3% of N1 resected) reflects evolving practice during 2000–2018; pre-BILCAP/PRODIGE-12 patients largely received none.
  • A high proportion of SYS patients did not receive gemcitabine-plus-platinum first-line (the current standard), which may further depress SYS survival in this comparison.
  • MSK-IMPACT gene-panel content expanded over the study period (341 → 410 → 468 genes), so some alterations were not assessable in earlier-enrolled patients; only genes altered in ≥5% of patients were included in statistical analyses.
  • The high-risk classifier (TP53/KRAS/CDKN2A/B) is hypothesis-generating and requires prospective validation; the underlying biology of why CDKN2A/B loss in particular confers such poor prognosis in node-positive IHC is not addressed.
  • Whether HAIC remains noninferior to resection in N1 disease in a prospective randomized setting is an open question the authors flag for future study.
