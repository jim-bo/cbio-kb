Alterations in homologous recombination repair genes in prostate cancer brain metastases

Authors

Antonio Rodriguez-Calero

John Gallon

Dilara Akhoundova

Sina Maletti

Alison Ferguson

Joanna Cyrta

Charlotte K. Y. Ng

Silke Gillessen

Salvatore Piscuoglio

Mark A. Rubin

Doi

10.1038/s41467-022-30003-5

PMID: 35504881 · DOI: 10.1038/s41467-022-30003-5 · Journal: Nature Communications (2022)

TL;DR

Rodriguez-Calero et al. profiled prostate cancer brain metastases (PCBM) from 51 Swiss patients by multi-region whole-exome sequencing and targeted RNA-seq (168 samples: 105 metastases + 63 primaries, with non-synchronous matched primaries in 20/51). Compared with mCRPC samples from the SU2C/PCF CRPC500 cohort and primary PCa from prad_tcga, PCBM showed significantly elevated mutation burden, enrichment of the COSMIC SBS3 homologous-recombination-deficiency (HRD) signature, and a high prevalence of pathogenic alterations in the 15 PROfound homologous recombination repair (HRR) genes — 10/51 (19.6%) patients would have met PROfound inclusion criteria and 5/51 (9.8%) carried pathogenic BRCA1/BRCA2 alterations. The authors argue PCBM patients, who were excluded from the PROfound trial, may benefit from PARP inhibitors.

Cohort & data

  • 51 patients with prostate adenocarcinoma brain metastases (PCBM); average age at PCBM diagnosis 71 years.
  • 168 tumor regions sequenced: 105 from PCBM (41% parenchymal, 35% dural, 24% mixed/unclear), 63 from matched primary PCa available in 20/51 patients.
  • Histology: pure acinar adenocarcinoma in 48/51 (94%) PCBM and 20/20 (100%) primaries; remaining 3/51 had neuroendocrine differentiation (focal NE in 2; small-cell NE areas in 1; intermediate NE/acinar features in 2 patients).
  • Clinical exposure: 42/51 (82%) received androgen deprivation or orchiectomy; 11/42 (26%) received next-generation ARSi (abiraterone and/or enzalutamide).
  • Comparator cohorts: TCGA primary PCa (n=495), CRPC500 non-brain mCRPC (prad_su2c_2019, n=411 metastases).
  • Cohort dataset: prostate_pcbm_swiss_2019.
  • Assays: whole-exome sequencing via Agilent SureSelectXT V7 on Illumina NovaSeq6000 (2×100 bp; ~258× target); targeted RNA-seq using a custom Ion AmpliSeq prostate-specific RNA panel for fusion detection; IHC for ERG, p53, PTEN, plus Chromogranin-A, Synaptophysin, PSA, CK5/6, p63 in NE-suspected cases.

Key findings

  • Elevated mutation burden in PCBM. Mean coding alteration rate 4.43 mut/Mb across 168 samples (range 0.28–50.45); mean 2.98 SNVs/Mb, 0.49 deletions/Mb, 0.15 insertions/Mb. PCBM had significantly more SNVs (q=6.75×10⁻⁶), insertions (q=3.06×10⁻⁵) and deletions (q=9.30×10⁻³) than matched primaries (Wilcoxon).
  • PCBM > non-brain metastases. PCBM enriched for somatic alterations versus CRPC500: SNVs q=2.38×10⁻¹⁹, insertions q=2.82×10⁻¹⁵, deletions q=8.44×10⁻¹⁴ (Wilcoxon). Differences persisted under stringent VAF/read thresholds and downsampling to match TCGA/CRPC500 depth.
  • HRD signature enrichment. SBS3 (HRD COSMIC signature) was high in 4 patients (>10% of mutations: P7, P9, P19, P30). By SigMA, HRD signature was significantly more represented in PCBM vs CRPC500 (q=0.041) and in PCBM primaries vs TCGA (q=0.0003, also significant vs high-grade TCGA subset q=0.0034).
  • PROfound criteria fulfilment. 10/51 (19.6%) patients met PROfound inclusion criteria with pathogenic alterations in one of 15 HRR genes (BRCA1, BRCA2, ATM, BRIP1, BARD1, CDK12, CHEK1, CHEK2, FANCL, PALB2, PPP2R2A, RAD51B, RAD51C, RAD51D, RAD54L). 8/51 (15.7%) showed biallelic loss; 5/51 (9.8%) had pathogenic BRCA1/BRCA2 specifically. All 51 patients (100%) had at least some alteration (mutation or CNA loss/deletion) in ≥1 PROfound gene.
  • HRR alteration prevalence. Defining HRR alteration as SigMA-detected HRD signature and/or LOH/deletion/mutation in a PROfound gene, PCBM had 100% vs CRPC500 87.26% affected (P=0.0025, Fisher’s exact); trend toward higher HRR alteration in PCBM primaries vs high-grade TCGA (P=0.052).
  • Most-mutated genes in PCBM metastases. TP53 13/51 (25%), APC 8/51 (16%), SPOP 8/51 (16%); AR coding alterations 7/51 (14%) and AR amplifications 32/51 (63%); PTEN loss/deletion 33/51 (64.71%); TMPRSS2–ERG fusions in 13/51 (25%), correlated with ERG IHC overexpression (Pearson r=0.70, P<2.2×10⁻¹⁶).
  • Hypermutators. Four patients (P39, P58, P48, P55) exceeded ~15 mut/Mb. P39 and P48 carried MSH2 missense/frameshift respectively and were MSI-H by clinical assay (high SBS44 signature). P58 had a POLD1 frameshift plus RFC4 missense; P55 had RNMT and POL3RC missense without MMR/HRR alterations but high SBS10b.
  • Aging signatures. SBS1 in 19/51 (37%) and SBS5 in 22/51 (43%) — consistent with median age 71. SBS16 (strand bias of DNA damage / nucleotide excision repair) in 19/51 (37%).
  • Clonal evolution. In 18/20 matched-primary cases, subclonal primary clones expanded to clonal level (CCF>0.9) in metastases. Phylogenetic trees recovered in 14/20 patients. Detailed examples: patient P4 (dural; truncal mutations in ZFHX3, TP53, POU2F); P8 (parenchymal; truncal CDK12, EPHA2, KMT2C); patient P5 metastases carried an ASXL2 variant in expanding clones.
  • Site-specific pathway enrichment. Metastatic-clonal genes were enriched for calcium signaling (including EP300 paralog CREBBP) and FAT10 signaling (TCF7L2, CTNNB1). Parenchymal metastases additionally enriched for calcium signaling (q=0.013) and EMT regulation in development (q=0.020); dural metastases enriched for aldosterone signaling (q=0.047). Trend toward EZH2-regulated targets in dural and mir17HG/SIRT1-regulated targets in parenchymal metastases (both q=0.07).

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — mutated in 13/51 (25%) PCBM metastases; truncal in patient P4.
  • APC — mutated in 8/51 (16%) of PCBM.
  • SPOP — mutated in 8/51 (16%) of PCBM.
  • AR — coding alterations 7/51 (14%); amplifications 32/51 (63%) — consistent with high prior ADT/ARSi exposure.
  • PTEN — loss/deletion in 33/51 (64.71%).
  • ERG / TMPRSS2 — TMPRSS2–ERG fusions detected by targeted RNA-seq in 13/51 (25%), highly correlated with ERG IHC (r=0.70, P<2.2×10⁻¹⁶).
  • BRCA1, BRCA2 — pathogenic alterations meeting PROfound criteria in 5/51 (9.8%).
  • ATM, CDK12, BRIP1, BARD1, CHEK1, CHEK2, FANCL, PALB2, PPP2R2A, RAD51B, RAD51C, RAD51D, RAD54L — together comprise the 15 PROfound HRR genes; 10/51 (19.6%) patients qualified by alteration in any; 8/51 (15.7%) had biallelic loss in at least one.
  • CDK12 — truncal in patient P8 (parenchymal metastases).
  • MSH2 — missense (P39) and frameshift (P48) variants in two MSI-H hypermutator patients with SBS44 enrichment.
  • POLD1, RFC4 — patient P58 hypermutator (POLD1 frameshift + RFC4 missense).
  • RNMT — missense in patient P55 (hypermutator, high SBS10b, also POL3RC missense).
  • EPHA2, KMT2C, ZFHX3 — truncal in matched primary/metastasis lineage of patients P4/P8 illustrating clonal evolution.
  • ASXL2 — variant in expanding metastatic clones of patient P5.
  • EP300 — paralog CREBBP altered among calcium-signaling enriched metastatic-clonal genes.
  • EZH2 — trend toward dural-metastasis enrichment of EZH2-regulated targets (q=0.07).
  • TCF7L2, CTNNB1 — in FAT10 signaling enrichment among metastatic-clonal genes.
  • SIRT1 — implicated together with mir17HG in dsDNA break repair link to parenchymal HRD enrichment.
  • RB1 — cited as known mCRPC outcome driver in the background; not the primary focus.

Clinical implications

  • Biomarker for brain metastasis risk. HRD signature (SBS3) was significantly higher in PCBM primaries than in TCGA primaries (q=0.0003), even after restricting to high-grade TCGA (q=0.0034). The authors propose the HRD signature may serve as a risk-stratification biomarker for progression of metastatic PCa to brain.
  • PARP inhibitor opportunity. 10/51 (19.6%) PCBM patients would have met PROfound inclusion criteria; 5/51 (9.8%) had pathogenic BRCA1/BRCA2. Because PROfound excluded known brain-metastasis patients, this cohort suggests a sizable population that could benefit from olaparib but was not eligible. The authors note that PARPi including olaparib and niraparib have shown activity against brain metastases in HRD breast cancer and in murine models, supporting CNS-permeable PARPi efficacy.
  • Treatment line context. 11/42 (26%) of ADT-treated patients had received abiraterone and/or enzalutamide — matching PROfound’s post-ARSi setting.
  • MMR testing. Two MSI-H hypermutator PCBM patients with MSH2 alterations were identified, supporting MMR/MSI testing in the PCBM setting (potential ICI eligibility, not explicitly trialed here).

Limitations & open questions

  • No matched normal in 24/51 patients. Mutations in unmatched cases were called against a pooled normal panel; the authors note conservative filters and report that enrichment results held when restricted to matched-normal cases.
  • Limited site-comparison power. The dural vs parenchymal pathway enrichment was based on small subsets (9 dural, 10 parenchymal) and is described as exploratory.
  • Retrospective/archival material. All samples are FFPE; biopsies vs autopsy material vary across patients, contributing tumor purity/depth variability.
  • No direct PARPi efficacy data in PCBM. The clinical implication is by analogy to the PROfound trial; the paper does not present prospective treatment outcomes on this cohort.
  • Mechanism of brain tropism unclear. Calcium signaling, FAT10, EMT, EZH2, and mir17HG/SIRT1 are flagged but not functionally validated; epigenetic and microenvironmental factors were not assessed.
  • POU2F annotation. The paper writes “POU2F” as a truncal mutation for patient P4 without specifying the family member (POU2F1/POU2F2/POU2F3); we did not include POU2F in the validated gene list.
