TL;DR

Using prospective MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing of paired blood and tumor from 24,146 solid-tumor cancer patients, Bolton et al. show that the fitness landscape of clonal hematopoiesis (CH) is shaped in a gene-specific way by environmental and therapeutic exposures: smoking selects for ASXL1 mutations, while cancer therapy (especially platinum agents, topoisomerase II inhibitors, and external beam radiation) selects for clones with DNA damage response (DDR) mutations in TP53, PPM1D, and CHEK2. Sequential blood sampling in 525 patients provided direct in-human evidence that DDR clones outcompete other CH clones under therapy but not in its absence. In 19/35 paired CH→tMN cases the leukemia-defining mutation was already present at the CH timepoint, and CH was a strong predictor of subsequent therapy-related myeloid neoplasm (tMN) risk (HR=6.9 for CH VAF>2%) in a 9,437-patient pooled analysis. Mutation calls are released as msk_ch_2020 on cBioPortal. PMID:33106634

Cohort & data

  • Cross-sectional cohort: 24,146 cancer patients with matched tumor + blood MSK-IMPACT sequencing on protocol NCT01775072, prior to July 1, 2018 — 56 primary tumor types represented. CH defined as a blood-only variant at VAF ≥2% with ≥10 supporting reads. Dataset released as msk_ch_2020 on cBioPortal. PMID:33106634
  • Treatment-annotated subcohort: 10,138 patients with complete cancer-care histories at MSK; 5,978 (59%) exposed to cancer therapy prior to blood draw, 4,160 (41%) treatment-naive. PMID:33106634
  • Serial sampling cohort: 525 patients with solid tumors and ≥2 blood draws (median interval 23 months, range 6–53). 389 (74%) had CH at first draw; 590/620 (95%) of CH mutations were present at both time points. None developed secondary hematologic malignancies during follow-up. PMID:33106634
  • CH→tMN cohort: 35 paired pre-tMN and tMN samples (median interval 24 months, range 5–90). PMID:33106634
  • Pooled tMN risk cohort: 9,437 cancer patients exposed to therapy (MSK + Gillis MOF n=68 + Takahashi MDA n=67 + Gibson DFC n=401), of whom 75 developed tMN. AML/MDS risk reference panel: Abelson PMC (n=969) + Young WSU (n=103) for healthy individuals. PMID:33106634
  • Assays: MSK-IMPACT — 341-gene (IMPACT341), 410-gene (IMPACT410), and 468-gene (IMPACT468) versions. Serial-sampling repeat CH testing used a comparable 163-gene myeloid capture panel (median depth 665×, range 111–1987×). Median blood coverage on MSK-IMPACT 497×; tumor 790×. Reference genome hg19. PMID:33106634
  • Calling stack: BWA 0.7.5a → ABRA 0.92 → GATK 3.3-0 base-quality recalibration; SNVs by MuTect + VarDict (retained if called by both); indels by Somatic Indel Detector + VarDict; annotated with VEP v86 and OncoKB. Filters: gnomAD frequency ≤0.005, blood/tumor VAF ratio ≥2× (≥1.5× for lymph-node biopsies), homopolymer/strand-bias filters. PMID:33106634
  • Selection inference: dNdScv limited to IMPACT-410/468 with q<0.1 and ≥25 variants per gene. Absolute-risk modeling used the iCARE R package combined with NCCN AML/MDS rates and SEER mortality. PMID:33106634

Key findings

  • Prevalence of CH. 7,216/24,146 patients (30%) carried at least one CH mutation (VAF ≥2%); 11,076 unique CH mutations. Median VAF 5.0% (range 2–78%). 69% of CH carriers had one mutation, 31% had two or more. 52% of CH calls (n=5,810) met CH-PD (putative-driver) criteria; 91% of CH-PD (n=5,301) were recurrent in myeloid neoplasms (CH-myeloid-PD). PMID:33106634
  • Mutational landscape. Most-frequent CH genes were DNMT3A, TET2, and ASXL1. 48% of all CH mutations fell in myeloid driver genes, even though those genes make up only 20% of the MSK-IMPACT content — indicating a strong fitness advantage in HSPCs for myeloid drivers. Median VAF was higher for myeloid drivers (0.047 vs 0.038, P significant) and for CH-PD (0.050 vs 0.038). DNMT3A R882 hotspot mutations carried higher VAFs than other DNMT3A mutations even after adjusting for total mutation count. PMID:33106634
  • Clinical determinants of CH. Age (OR=1.9 per category, p<10⁻⁶), prior cancer therapy (OR=1.3, p=1×10⁻⁶), and smoking (OR=1.1, p=5×10⁻³) were independently associated with CH. CH was less common in Asian than Caucasian patients (OR=0.7, p=1×10⁻³). PMID:33106634
  • Gene × exposure specificity.
    • Aging (DNMT3A weaker than other CH mutations): spliceosome SRSF2 (OR=3.6, q=7×10⁻⁶) and SF3B1 (OR=5.0, q<10⁻⁶) showed the strongest age effects.
    • Smoking: ASXL1 (OR=2.5, q=1×10⁻⁴; current-smoker OR=3.1, former-smoker OR=2.4).
    • DDR genes × therapy: TP53 (OR=2.8, q=2×10⁻⁴), PPM1D (OR=4.3, q<10⁻⁶), CHEK2 (OR=4.5, q=6×10⁻⁶).
    • Epigenetic-modifier and spliceosome CH was not enriched by therapy exposure. PMID:33106634
  • Tumor-type heterogeneity. PPM1D mutations were found in 13% of ovarian and 7% of endometrial cancer patients (vs <5% in other subgroups). Among untreated patients, PPM1D CH was seen in 8% of ovarian and 0% of endometrial cancer patients — confirming therapy/exposure context drives most tumor-type heterogeneity. PMID:33106634
  • Therapy-class associations. Strongest CH-PD associations: external-beam radiation (OR=1.4, p<10⁻⁶), cytotoxic chemotherapy (OR=1.2, p=2×10⁻³), radionuclide therapy (OR=1.6, p=0.01). Within cytotoxics: topoisomerase II inhibitors (OR=1.3, p=0.01) and platinum agents (OR=1.2, p=0.02). Carboplatin was strongly associated (OR=1.4, p=0.001) while cisplatin (OR=1.1, p=0.10) and oxaliplatin (OR=0.98, p=0.88) were not. Targeted therapy and immunotherapy showed no significant CH association. PMID:33106634
  • Gene-specific therapy associations. PPM1D was most strongly linked to platinum (OR=3.2, q<10⁻⁶) and radionuclide therapy (OR=6.2, q=7×10⁻⁶) plus topoisomerase II inhibitors (OR=2.0, q=0.002), taxanes (OR=1.8, q=0.003), topoisomerase I inhibitors (OR=1.7, q=0.002), and external-beam radiation (OR=1.8, q=0.04). TP53 was associated with platinum (OR=2.1, q=0.03), radiation (OR=1.8, q=0.04), and taxanes (OR=1.9, q=0.05). CHEK2 was associated with platinum (OR=2.4, q=0.02) and topoisomerase II inhibitors (OR=2.2, q=0.02). PMID:33106634
  • Dose-response. Trend tests showed CH-PD risk rose with cumulative platinum exposure (p-trend=0.04), with the strongest effects for carboplatin (p-trend=3×10⁻⁵) and cisplatin (p-trend=0.04). PMID:33106634
  • Longitudinal clonal dynamics. Of 590 CH mutations seen at both timepoints, 62% (n=367) were stable, 28% (n=164) grew, and 10% (n=59) shrank. Under cytotoxic chemo or external-beam radiation, DDR CH mutations in TP53, PPM1D, and CHEK2 grew fastest. Patients with multiple CH mutations had faster growth (p=0.03). New CH mutations appeared in 4% (n=13) of patients receiving interval cytotoxic/radiation therapy vs 1% (n=2) of those who did not (p=0.06). PMID:33106634
  • In-patient competition. Among 34 patients each carrying one DDR-CH and one non-DDR-CH mutation, DDR clones outgrew non-DDR clones under cytotoxic or radiation therapy, but were outcompeted by non-DDR clones (e.g. DNMT3A) in untreated patients — providing direct, gene-controlled evidence of treatment-driven selection. PMID:33106634
  • CH→tMN transitions. In 19/32 (59%) paired CH→tMN cases, ≥1 leukemia-defining mutation was present at the CH timepoint with ≥1 supporting read; 13 of these (41%) harbored two or more. In all such cases the CH mutation was present at tMN diagnosis. 91% of transformations also acquired additional events — chromosomal aneuploidies or mutations in late-progression genes such as FLT3, KRAS, NRAS. 14/35 (40%) tMN cases carried TP53 mutations; 10/14 of these were detectable at the CH stage. At transformation, 12/13 TP53-mutant tMN cases had isolated chromosomal aneuploidies or complex karyotype. PMID:33106634
  • tMN risk modeling (n=9,437; 75 events). CH VAF >2% increased tMN risk with HR=6.9 (p<10⁻⁶), with effect rising with mutation number and clone size. Strongest gene-specific HRs were for TP53 and spliceosome genes (SRSF2, U2AF1, SF3B1). Effect sizes for tMN in cancer patients were similar to those for AML risk in healthy individuals, suggesting CH-mediated risk is largely independent of cancer/therapy context (multiplicative model). PMID:33106634
  • Absolute-risk illustration. In a synthetic iCARE model of women aged 50–75 with breast cancer in the U.S., 96% had 10-year absolute MN risk <1%; for the top 1% of risk (by CH and blood-count features), adjuvant chemotherapy increased absolute MN risk by ≈9%, plausibly exceeding the survival benefit of chemo in some early-stage BRCA patients. PMID:33106634

Genes & alterations

  • DNMT3A — most frequent CH gene; weaker age effect than other CH mutations; R882 hotspot has higher VAFs than non-hotspot variants even after adjustment. Outcompetes DDR clones in untreated patients. PMID:33106634
  • TET2 — second most common CH gene; an epigenetic modifier whose CH was not strongly modulated by therapy exposure. PMID:33106634
  • ASXL1 — significantly enriched in current/former smokers (OR=2.5, q=1×10⁻⁴), with stronger signal in current smokers (OR=3.1) than former (OR=2.4). PMID:33106634
  • TP53 — DDR; therapy-associated CH (OR=2.8, q=2×10⁻⁴). Detected at CH stage in 10/14 tMN cases that harbored TP53. Co-occurred with complex karyotype/aneuploidy in 12/13 evaluable TP53-mutant tMNs. Strong predictor of tMN risk. PMID:33106634
  • PPM1D — DDR; strongest therapy effect (OR=4.3, q<10⁻⁶); highest enrichment in ovarian (13%) and endometrial (7%) cancers, almost entirely explained by therapy exposure. Truncating C-terminal variants are the canonical CH form (PMID:31768066 corroborates from cfDNA). PMID:33106634
  • CHEK2 — DDR; therapy-associated CH (OR=4.5, q=6×10⁻⁶); platinum (OR=2.4) and topoisomerase II inhibitor (OR=2.2) class effects. PMID:33106634
  • SRSF2, SF3B1, U2AF1 — spliceosome CH; strongly age-associated; not enriched by therapy; collectively among the strongest tMN risk predictors. SRSF2 and PPM1D were not on the IMPACT-341 panel and so were under-ascertained in earlier sub-cohorts. PMID:33106634
  • FLT3, KRAS, NRAS — late-progression drivers acquired between CH and tMN diagnosis in many transformations. PMID:33106634
  • ATM — included in CH-myeloid-PD criteria when truncating; partially under-ascertained in the serial-sampling panel (missing from the 163-gene myeloid panel). PMID:33106634
  • RUNX1, BCOR, STAG2, CALR, TERT, KMT2D, IDH1, IDH2, JAK2 — additional CH-myeloid-PD genes whose truncating, hotspot, or in-frame variants were classed as putative drivers per the paper’s annotation rules. PMID:33106634

Clinical implications

  • Biomarker. CH at VAF >2% identifies cancer patients at materially elevated risk of subsequent tMN (HR=6.9), with risk scaling by mutation count, clone size, gene context, and peripheral-blood indices (RDW, hemoglobin). Pre-treatment screening for CH is presented as a feasible, scalable risk-stratification tool because MSK-IMPACT-style panels are already in clinical use. PMID:33106634
  • Therapy choice. Carboplatin, topoisomerase II inhibitors, and external-beam radiation drive the strongest gene-specific (DDR) clonal selection. The authors raise the possibility of substituting or deferring such agents in patients with high-risk CH when the absolute survival benefit is marginal — illustrated by a synthetic iCARE model in early-stage BRCA. PMID:33106634
  • Mechanistic prediction. DDR-CH clones (particularly TP53) have lower competitive fitness than DNMT3A-class clones in the absence of therapy, but become dominant under genotoxic stress — implying that the same patient’s CH risk profile depends on prospective treatment plan, not just baseline genotype. PMID:33106634
  • No evidence for targeted-therapy or immunotherapy selection of CH at this resolution, suggesting non-cytotoxic regimens may be CH-safer where clinically interchangeable. PMID:33106634

Limitations & open questions

  • The MSK-IMPACT panel surveys cancer-driver genes; it misses CH drivers such as SRCAP, BRCC3, ZNF318, and (in earlier panel versions) PPM1D and SRSF2. ATM was absent from the 163-gene serial-sampling panel, restricting sub-cohort analyses. PMID:33106634
  • VAF cutoff of 2% means small CH clones — known to be relevant in healthy aging cohorts — were excluded. Error-corrected sequencing is needed to extend findings to sub-2% VAF. PMID:33106634
  • The serial cohort did not include patients who went on to develop hematologic malignancy, so growth-rate estimates are a lower bound for tMN-bound clones. Sampling did not bracket therapy temporally (no pre-/intra-/post-therapy triplets), so kinetics of clone growth during versus after therapy could not be resolved. PMID:33106634
  • The pooled tMN risk cohort (n=9,437) restricted to therapy-exposed patients, so gene-by-treatment interactions on tMN risk could not be formally estimated; the absolute-risk model assumes multiplicative effects. PMID:33106634
  • TP53-driven tMN is one mechanism — the authors flag that processes leading to MLL fusions, complex karyotype, and other tMN-associated alterations may follow distinct selection rules not captured by this design. PMID:33106634
  • The synthetic breast-cancer risk model is exploratory, depends on external HRs (NCCN/SEER) and assumes the MSK untreated solid-tumor population is representative of the general untreated breast-cancer population. PMID:33106634
  • Bulk sequencing cannot determine whether multiple CH mutations within a patient sit on the same clone — single-cell sequencing is needed to confirm the competitive interpretation of multi-mutation patients. PMID:33106634
