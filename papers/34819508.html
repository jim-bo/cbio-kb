Geometric network analysis provides prognostic information in patients with high grade serous carcinoma of the ovary treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors

Authors

Rena Elkin

Jung Hun Oh

Ying L. Liu

Pier Selenica

Britta Weigelt

Jorge S. Reis-Filho

Dmitriy Zamarin

Joseph O. Deasy

Larry Norton

Arnold J. Levine

Allen R. Tannenbaum

Doi

10.1038/s41525-021-00259-9

PMID: 34819508 · DOI: 10.1038/s41525-021-00259-9 · Journal: npj Genomic Medicine (2021)

TL;DR

Elkin et al. apply a novel geometric measure — Ollivier–Ricci (OR) network curvature — to copy-number alteration (CNA) profiles of 45 high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma (HGSOC) patients treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) at Memorial Sloan Kettering. CNAs were extracted from targeted (MSK-IMPACT) and whole-exome sequencing, mapped onto a fixed protein–protein interaction (PPI) topology from HPRD, and a patient-specific “total curvature” κG was computed. Stratifying patients at the 25th percentile of κG separated high- vs low-risk groups for overall survival (OS) with log-rank p = 0.00047 — almost two orders of magnitude better than TMB (p = 0.032), LST (p = 0.43), or FGA (p = 0.20). Curvature was not prognostic in an HGSOC cohort that did not receive ICI, suggesting it is an ICI-specific biomarker. Top curvature-ranked genes recapitulated known HGSOC biology, with TP53 dominating the positive-Δκ list.

Cohort & data

  • n = 45 patients with recurrent HGSOC treated with ICIs, drawn as a subset from the 69-patient series in PMID:32616680 (Zamarin et al., reanalyzed here). PMID:34819508
  • Cancer type: high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma (HGSOC); broader bucket ovarian cancer (OV).
  • Sample composition: 32 metastases, 13 primary (adnexal) tumors; 32 deaths at analysis. Mean age 58 y; mean TMB 5.9 mut/Mb.
  • Dataset: hgsoc_msk_2021 — publicly available at cBioPortal.
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT targeted panel sequencing for both tumor and normal samples; CNA segments called by FACETS and mapped to GRCh37 genes. Whole-exome data also contributed to CNA calls.
  • Network topology: HPRD-derived PPI restricted to the largest connected component intersecting the data — 3,489 genes and 9,710 edges (mean degree 5.57).
  • Validation cohort: METABRIC breast cancer (n = 1903), Supplementary Fig. 14.
  • ICI regimen breakdown: 25 PD-1/PD-L1 monotherapy, 15 PD-1/PD-L1 + CTLA-4 combo, 4 PD-1/PD-L1 + other, 1 CTLA-4 monotherapy. Specific agents included nivolumab, ipilimumab, and avelumab (drawn from the parent Zamarin cohort and cited combination-ICI studies with olaparib and durvalumab).

Key findings

  • Total curvature κG stratifies OS after ICI: low-curvature (n = 12) vs high-curvature (n = 33) Kaplan–Meier comparison yielded log-rank p = 0.00047. Median OS was 7.4 mo (low) vs 20.3 mo (high curvature) — Table 2. PMID:34819508
  • Curvature beats standard genomic biomarkers: in the same cohort, TMB p = 0.03153, LST p = 0.42865, FGA p = 0.19568. Curvature outperformed TMB by roughly two orders of magnitude in log-rank p-value.
  • Curvature is ICI-specific, not prognostic at baseline: when the curvature pipeline was applied to MSK-IMPACT data from HGSOC patients who did not receive ICI, total curvature was not significantly associated with OS (Supplementary Fig. 8). The authors interpret this as evidence the biomarker is predictive of ICI response rather than generally prognostic.
  • Top curvature-ranked genes recapitulate HGSOC biology: TP53 had the largest positive Δκrisk (0.209) across every sub-analysis (risk, OS, primary vs metastasis, topology reference), reflecting its functional centrality. Other top positive-Δκ genes included ATXN1, EP300, SMAD2, PIK3R1, SRC, SMAD4, RB1, ESR1, and AKT1; top negative-Δκ genes included CREBBP, SHC1, PTK2, AR, MYC, and MDM2 (Table 1).
  • Total curvature correlates with FGA but not TMB: Pearson r p-values were TMB p = 0.9674, FGA p = 0.0059, LST p = 0.0867. FGA alone is not prognostic for OS in this cohort despite the correlation, supporting that curvature captures network-level information beyond CNA burden.
  • Cohort-level clinical features that differ by curvature group: age at ICI start (p = 0.023), OS (p = 0.007), sample type (all 13 primaries in high-curvature group, p = 0.010), FGA (p = 0.005), LST (p = 0.024). TMB, platinum status, BRCA1/2 mutation status, and ICI target distribution did not differ between groups.
  • Generalizability: the curvature method was validated on the METABRIC breast cancer cohort (n = 1,903) in Supplementary Fig. 14.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — loss-of-function mutations and CNAs are the defining HGSOC alteration; TP53 ranked #1 by positive Δκrisk (0.209) and topped every sub-analysis, validating the methodology against known HGSOC biology.
  • KRAS, MYC, CCNE1, AKT1 — cited as canonical HGSOC oncogenes commonly overexpressed via CNA-driven aneuploidy; together they drive G1→S progression and centrosome dysregulation downstream of TP53 loss. MYC and AKT1 appear in the curvature-ranked gene lists.
  • MDM2 — appears in the negative Δκrisk list; mechanistically linked to AKT-driven p53 degradation in the paper’s pathway model (Fig. 3).
  • BRCA1 / BRCA2 — captured as clinical covariates (10/45 patients mutated); no association with curvature group (p = 1.000). BRCA1 also appears in the top-50 positive Δκrisk list (rank 43, Δκ = 0.003).
  • EP300, SMAD2, PIK3R1, RB1, ESR1, ATXN1, EGFR — high-positive Δκrisk genes flagged as putative network drivers of ICI-modulated robustness.
  • CREBBP — top-ranked negative-Δκrisk gene (−0.064), the largest decrease in curvature between low- and high-risk groups.

The paper studies copy-number alteration (CNA) signal at the gene level, not point mutations; alterations are integer copy numbers per gene (range [0, 38]) mapped from FACETS segments to GRCh37 gene coordinates.

Clinical implications

  • Total network curvature κG, derived from gene-level CNAs over a fixed PPI topology, is a candidate predictive biomarker for OS in HGSOC patients treated with ICIs — distinct from prognostic biomarkers because it had no significant association with OS in HGSOC patients who did not receive ICI.
  • Curvature outperformed TMB, FGA, and LST in this cohort. The authors note that traditional HGSOC ICI biomarkers — TMB (low in HGSOC), PD-L1 IHC, TILs — have been inconsistent or unhelpful in HGSOC and propose curvature as a complement.
  • The method requires only DNA copy-number data plus a PPI scaffold, making it applicable to any MSK-IMPACT-style targeted CNA assay; it does not depend on RNA, IHC, or immune-cell quantification.
  • The authors explicitly position curvature as a tool to be combined with FGA in larger validation cohorts.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small cohort: n = 45 ICI-treated patients, with a 12/33 low/high split — adequate for a proof-of-concept Kaplan–Meier signal but underpowered for multivariate modeling. The authors call for larger datasets to validate.
  • Single time point: only one CNA snapshot per patient. The “change in curvature” is computed by comparing risk groups (or topology references), not longitudinal samples.
  • Cutoff selection: the 25th-percentile split was chosen by inspection of the sorted curvature curve; a maximally-selected log-rank cutoff was reported as comparable (Supplementary Fig. 7) but the curve-shape choice is admitted to be partly arbitrary.
  • PFS not significant: no significant PFS difference in this cohort; only OS differs by curvature group. The authors cite a broader pattern of ICI affecting OS without PFS in HGSOC.
  • No PD-L1 / TIL comparison: insufficient tissue prevented head-to-head comparison with established biomarkers in the same patients.
  • PPI scaffold dependence: the curvature pipeline assumes HPRD-derived interactions are an adequate proxy for functional connectivity; alternative interactomes were not evaluated here.
  • No mechanistic validation in patients: the top-ranked genes were validated only against known HGSOC biology and a referenced mouse-model study; functional perturbation experiments in patient material were not performed.
