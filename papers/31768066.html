High-intensity sequencing reveals the sources of plasma circulating cell-free DNA variants

Authors

Pedram Razavi

Bob T. Li

David N. Brown

Byoungsok Jung

Earl Hubbell

Ronglai Shen

Wassim Abida

David B. Solit

Alexander M. Aravanis

Jorge S. Reis-Filho

Doi

10.1038/s41591-019-0652-7

PMID: 31768066 · DOI: 10.1038/s41591-019-0652-7 · Journal: Nature Medicine (2019)

TL;DR

Razavi et al. prospectively profiled plasma cell-free DNA (cfDNA) and matched white-blood-cell (WBC) DNA from 124 patients with metastatic breast cancer, non-small cell lung cancer, or castration-resistant prostate cancer, plus 47 non-cancer controls, using a high-intensity targeted assay covering 508 genes / ~2 Mb at >60,000X raw depth. With matched tumor biopsies sequenced by MSK-IMPACT, they showed that the assay detects 84% of patients’ tumor-derived mutations de novo, but that the majority of “somatic” cfDNA variants — 81.6% in controls and 53.2% in cancer patients — are actually clonal hematopoiesis (CH) events identifiable only with matched WBC sequencing. The work establishes that joint cfDNA + WBC sequencing is necessary to avoid mis-calling CH variants (notably in TP53, DNMT3A, TET2, and PPM1D) as tumor-derived.

Cohort & data

  • 161 enrolled metastatic cancer patients (53 MBC, 53 NSCLC, 55 CRPC); 124 evaluable in the concordance subset (39 MBC, 41 NSCLC, 44 CRPC). Enrollment Sept 24, 2015 – Aug 1, 2016 at MSKCC (protocol 12-245, NCT01775072) PMID:31768066.
  • 50 non-cancer control whole-blood samples from San Diego Blood Bank; 47 evaluable after three cfDNA-sequencing failures PMID:31768066.
  • Plasma cfDNA and buffy-coat WBC gDNA sequenced with the GRAIL targeted DNA assay (grail_cfdna_508): 508 genes spanning ~2 Mb, UMIs, >60,000X raw / ~4,400X median collapsed target coverage; assay error rate 1×10⁻⁵ to 3×10⁻⁵; 95% LoD ≈0.36% VAF at 30 ng input PMID:31768066.
  • Matched tumor biopsies sequenced by MSK-IMPACT (410-gene FDA-authorized CLIA assay, mean >900X coverage); comparisons restricted to the 410-gene panel intersection PMID:31768066.
  • Dataset deposited as cfdna_msk_2019 in cBioPortal.

Key findings

  • De novo tumor-mutation detection. At least one tumor-derived (biopsy-matched) mutation was detected in cfDNA in 104/124 patients (84%, 95% CI 76–90%). Per-cancer detection: MBC 95% (95% CI 83–99%), NSCLC 76% (95% CI 60–80%), CRPC 82% (95% CI 67–92%); MBC > NSCLC, p=0.0258 PMID:31768066.
  • Per-mutation recovery. 530/740 (72%, 95% CI 66–75%) of MSK-IMPACT tumor-biopsy mutations were re-detected de novo in cfDNA, with comparable rates across MBC (73%), NSCLC (71%), and CRPC (71%) PMID:31768066.
  • Clonality matters. Detection in cfDNA correlated strongly with cancer cell fraction in the biopsy; clonal mutations were detected far more often than subclonal ones (p=5.33e-21) PMID:31768066.
  • Disease burden drives ctDNA fraction. ctDNA fraction varied significantly by tumor type (p=0.0046) and correlated with imaging-based disease burden: MBC (n=34, p=1.03e-4) and NSCLC (n=29, p=0.042) by volumetric CT, CRPC (n=39, p=0.023) by automated bone scan index PMID:31768066.
  • Most cfDNA “somatic” variants are CH. 81.6% (297/364) of cfDNA somatic mutations in controls and 53.2% (918/1727) in non-hypermutated cancer patients had supporting reads in matched WBCs (WBC-matched). 89.5% of cancer patients and 83% of controls had evidence of CH in cfDNA; 99.1% of cancer-patient WBCs and 93.6% of control WBCs harbored at least one CH mutation PMID:31768066.
  • CH genes recur. WBC-matched cfDNA variants preferentially affected canonical CH genes — DNMT3A, TET2, PPM1D, TP53; in 41.6% of cancer patients the highest-VAF mutation was in one of 15 canonical CH genes (DNMT3A, TET2, ASXL1, PPM1D, TP53, JAK2, RUNX1, SF3B1, SRSF2, IDH1, IDH2, U2AF1, CBL, ATM, CHEK2) PMID:31768066.
  • Therapy-related CH in PPM1D. PPM1D mutations — particularly truncating variants enriched in the C-terminal domain — were significantly more common in cancer patients than controls (age-adjusted p=0.0115) and significantly more common in patients with prior chemotherapy and/or radiation than in untreated patients (age- and smoking-adjusted p=0.0008) PMID:31768066.
  • Age dependence. Number of WBC-matched variants strongly correlated with age (smoking-adjusted p=3.86e-27); biopsy-matched / biopsy-subthreshold counts did not. VUSo (variants of unknown source) were only weakly age-associated (p=0.141) PMID:31768066.
  • Tumor heterogeneity in hypermutators. 10 cfDNA samples were called hypermutated (cfDNA TMB > median + 2×IQR); four matched a biopsy TMB call >13.8 mut/Mb, six were detected only by cfDNA. The 10 hypermutators accounted for 75% of biopsy-subthreshold + VUSo variants across the cohort and 77.7% (994/1280) of cancer-patient VUSo. In hypermutated samples, 41.2% of tumor-derived non-WBC-matched variants were subclonal vs 15.3% in non-hypermutated samples (p=1.7e-13) PMID:31768066.
  • Mutational signatures recoverable from cfDNA. All five hypermutated MBCs and one of three hypermutated CRPCs exhibited APOBEC signatures (COSMIC sigs 2 & 13); hypermutated NSCLCs combined smoking signature with APOBEC, HRD, and MMR contributions PMID:31768066.
  • MSI detection. Using a depth-adjusted MSIsensor, one CRPC patient was identified as MSI-H from cfDNA and exhibited a dominant MMR mutational signature; the patient received an anti-PD-L1 inhibitor and showed rapid sustained tumor regression by RECIST v1.1 and PSA PMID:31768066.
  • CNV detection. Concordance of cfDNA-called CNVs with biopsy was good only when ctDNA fraction ≥10%. Among five patients with actionable amplifications (4 ERBB2-amplified MBCs, 1 MET-amplified NSCLC), three ERBB2 amplifications were detected de novo; the two missed cases had ctDNA fractions of 1.3% and 1.9% PMID:31768066.
  • VUSo are largely tumor-derived in hypermutators. Cancer-patient VUSo affected late-evolution / subclonal cancer genes — ESR1, RB1, NF1 in MBC; EGFR T790M in NSCLC; AR mutations in CRPC — and orthogonal ddPCR validation showed complete agreement PMID:31768066.

Genes & alterations

  • DNMT3A, TET2 — most frequent CH drivers; the genes whose mutations most often had the highest VAF in cfDNA in both cancer patients and controls PMID:31768066.
  • PPM1D — therapy-related CH gene; truncating C-terminal mutations significantly enriched in cancer patients vs controls (age-adjusted p=0.0115) and in chemotherapy/radiation-exposed patients (age- and smoking-adjusted p=0.0008) PMID:31768066.
  • TP53 — recurrent in WBC-matched cfDNA variants; can be mis-interpreted as tumor-derived without matched WBC sequencing PMID:31768066.
  • ASXL1, JAK2, RUNX1, SF3B1, SRSF2, IDH1, IDH2, U2AF1, CBL, ATM, CHEK2 — full list of 15 canonical CH genes used in this paper to classify WBC-matched variants PMID:31768066.
  • ESR1, RB1, NF1 — late/subclonal mutations in MBC detected in cfDNA as VUSo not captured by single biopsy PMID:31768066.
  • EGFR T790M — example of subclonal NSCLC resistance mutation detected as VUSo PMID:31768066.
  • AR — recurrent VUSo in CRPC, consistent with subclonal androgen-receptor evolution PMID:31768066.
  • ERBB2 — actionable amplification detected de novo in 3 of 4 ERBB2-amplified MBC cases by cfDNA CNV calling PMID:31768066.
  • MET — actionable amplification in one NSCLC case; cfDNA CNV detection limited by low ctDNA fraction PMID:31768066.

Clinical implications

  • Joint cfDNA + WBC sequencing is required for accurate variant interpretation. Without matched WBC, CH variants in cancer genes (e.g., TP53) can be misattributed to the tumor, distorting tumor mutational burden, mutational-signature, and actionable-mutation calls PMID:31768066.
  • cfDNA-based TMB / signature / MSI inference is feasible. With sufficient genomic footprint and depth, the assay supports de novo measurement of TMB, MSI, and mutational signatures, including in patients without contemporaneous tissue PMID:31768066.
  • MSI-H detection from cfDNA can guide immunotherapy. A CRPC patient identified as MSI-H solely from cfDNA had a rapid, sustained response to anti-PD-L1 therapy PMID:31768066.
  • Therapy history is a confounder. Prior chemotherapy/radiation enriches for PPM1D-driven CH, so interpretation of plasma PPM1D variants in heavily pre-treated patients must consider therapy-related CH PMID:31768066.
  • Hypermutators benefit most from broad-footprint cfDNA. A single biopsy systematically under-samples subclones in hypermutator tumors; cfDNA can complement biopsy by recovering resistance-associated subclonal mutations (e.g., EGFR T790M, ESR1, AR) PMID:31768066.
  • CNV detection requires adequate ctDNA fraction. De novo cfDNA CNV calling for actionable amplifications (e.g., ERBB2, MET) was only reliable when ctDNA fraction ≥10%; low-ctDNA cases need orthogonal tissue confirmation PMID:31768066.

Limitations & open questions

  • Colorectal carcinoma — a major indication for cfDNA testing — was not included PMID:31768066.
  • Tumor sequencing relied on a single FFPE biopsy per patient; multiregional sampling was not feasible, so tumor heterogeneity may be under-captured even after the cfDNA add-on PMID:31768066.
  • Non-cancer controls came from a different source (San Diego Blood Bank) and were processed in different batches than cancer samples, introducing potential batch effects PMID:31768066.
  • Per-subtype cohorts (~40 each for MBC, NSCLC, CRPC) are small relative to the clinical and genomic heterogeneity of these diseases PMID:31768066.
  • The hierarchical Bayesian noise model was trained on <50 healthy donors; longitudinal expansion would improve calibration PMID:31768066.
  • A residual subset of VUSo may still originate from CH not detectable in matched WBC, other forms of somatic mosaicism, occult cancers, or benign neoplasms — these cannot be ruled out even with this assay design PMID:31768066.
  • The high per-sample cost of >60,000X cfDNA sequencing is currently a barrier to broad clinical adoption PMID:31768066.
  • Open question: how the prevalence and clinical impact of therapy-related PPM1D CH evolve under successive lines of cytotoxic therapy.
