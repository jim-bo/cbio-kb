Tumor mutational load predicts survival after immunotherapy across multiple cancer types

Authors

Robert M. Samstein

Chung-Han Lee

Alexander N. Shoushtari

Matthew D. Hellmann

Ronglai Shen

Yelena Y. Janjigian

David A. Barron

Ahmet Zehir

Michael F. Berger

Nadeem Riaz

David B. Solit

Timothy A. Chan

Doi

10.1038/s41588-018-0312-8

PMID: 30643254 · DOI: 10.1038/s41588-018-0312-8 · Journal: Nature Genetics (2019)

TL;DR

Samstein et al. analyzed clinical and targeted-NGS genomic data (msk-impact-panel) for 1,662 advanced cancer patients treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) and 5,371 non-ICI–treated patients sequenced at MSKCC, in order to test whether tumor mutational burden (TMB) predicts overall survival (OS) after ICI across cancer types. Higher somatic TMB (top 20% within each histology) was associated with better OS (HR 0.52; p = 1.6 × 10⁻⁶), an effect present in most histologies but not in glioma. The TMB cutpoint defining “high” varied markedly across cancer types, arguing against a single universal threshold. The cohort is the cBioPortal study tmb_mskcc_2018.

Cohort & data

  • N = 1,662 ICI-treated patients with advanced cancer profiled by MSK-IMPACT, plus N = 5,371 non-ICI–treated patients as a control for general prognostic effects of TMB.
  • Histologies represented include NSCLC (n = 350), melanoma (MEL / SKCM; n = 321), renal cell carcinoma (RCC; n = 151), bladder cancer (BLCA; n = 214), head & neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC; n = 138), esophagogastric cancer (EGC), colorectal cancer (COAD), and glioma (GB / DIFG).
  • 94% of non-glioma tumors were stage IV / metastatic; 10 patients had locally recurrent disease, and a subset of melanoma patients had regionally advanced unresectable (stage III) disease.
  • ICI regimens: 146 received anti-CTLA4 (ipilimumab, tremelimumab); 1,447 received anti–PD-1/PD-L1 (nivolumab, pembrolizumab, atezolizumab, avelumab, durvalumab); 189 received both.
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT targeted NGS using 341-, 410-, or 468-gene versions (IMPACT341, IMPACT410, IMPACT468), with matched germline normal sequencing to identify true somatic mutations.
  • TMB definition: non-synonymous somatic mutations normalized by exonic megabases sequenced. TMB-high defined within each histology (top 20th percentile, with sensitivity at 10–50%).
  • Outcome: OS from first ICI dose to death or last follow-up; median follow-up 19 months (range 0–80), 830 patients (50%) alive and censored at last follow-up.
  • Data availability: the cBioPortal study tmb_mskcc_2018 (http://www.cbioportal.org/study?id=tmb_mskcc_2018).

Key findings

  • Pan-cancer survival benefit with high TMB. Across the entire ICI cohort, the top-20%-by-histology TMB group had univariate HR 0.52 (95% CI 0.42–0.64) for OS compared to the bottom 80% (two-sided log-rank). The 10–20% decile group had HR 0.76 (95% CI 0.62–0.94) [Fig. 1A].
  • Cutpoint-robust. The trend of decreasing HR with increasing TMB cutoff was observed across cutpoints ranging from the top 10–50% [Fig. 1B; Supp. Figs. 3–4].
  • Multivariable confirmation. Cox regression (cox-proportional-hazards) adjusting for cancer type, age, ICI drug class, and year of ICI start showed TMB was significantly associated with OS as a continuous variable (HR = 0.985 per unit, p = 3.4 × 10⁻⁷) and as a binary top-20%-of-each-histology variable (HR 0.61, 95% CI 0.508–0.733, p = 1.3 × 10⁻⁷) [Table 1].
  • Effect not driven by NSCLC or melanoma alone. Removing these two histologies preserved the significant association of higher TMB with longer OS [Supp. Table 2].
  • Histology-stratified analysis. Selecting the top 20% TMB within each cancer type gave HR < 1 in nearly all histologies, with GB the clearest exception [Fig. 2].
  • No prognostic benefit absent ICI. In the 5,371-patient non-ICI cohort, higher TMB was not associated with better OS (HR 1.12, p = 0.11), consistent across histologies — arguing the OS benefit is ICI-specific rather than a general prognostic effect of mutation load.
  • Histology-specific cutpoints. The TMB value defining the top 20% varied widely across cancer types (e.g., 52.2 mut/Mb for COAD, reflecting MSI-high enrichment), undermining the idea of a single universal TMB-high threshold [Fig. 2].
  • PFS and response concordance. Similar associations between higher TMB and clinical benefit / progression-free survival were observed in histologies where response data was available — NSCLC, melanoma, esophagogastric, head & neck, and renal cell cancer [Supp. Figs. 7–8].
  • Cohort enrichment ruled out. Recomputing top-20% TMB across both ICI- and non-ICI–treated patients did not materially change cutpoints or the OS associations [Supp. Figs. 10–11], arguing clinicians were not systematically triaging high-TMB patients to ICI.

Genes & alterations

This study does not focus on specific gene alterations as predictors. The biomarker analyzed is aggregate non-synonymous somatic tumor mutational burden across the MSK-IMPACT 341/410/468-gene panels; mutations in driver oncogenes were not excluded from the TMB count. No per-gene survival associations are reported in the main text.

Clinical implications

  • TMB is a pan-cancer predictive biomarker for ICI, supporting its use as a candidate selection biomarker across diverse histologies treated with anti-CTLA4 or anti-PD-1/PD-L1 agents.
  • No universal numerical cutoff — TMB-high thresholds vary by histology, so a single pan-cancer cutpoint is unlikely to be clinically appropriate. The authors explicitly note that the top-20% thresholds shown “may not represent the ideal values for use clinically.”
  • Targeted panels are sufficient. Sampling ~3% of the coding exome via MSK-IMPACT provides enough information for TMB to retain predictive value for OS after ICI, supporting use of clinical-grade targeted NGS rather than WES for this biomarker.
  • Mismatch-repair / MSI alignment. The findings are consistent with the FDA’s tissue-agnostic approval of pembrolizumab for MSI-high / dMMR tumors; high-TMB COAD in this cohort likely reflects MSI-high biology.
  • Glioma is the outlier. No OS benefit was observed in glioma, possibly because TMB in glioma often reflects post-temozolomide subclonal expansion of less immunogenic mutations or CNS-specific immune biology.

Limitations & open questions

  • Heterogeneous, retrospective cohort. Patients varied in line of therapy, prior treatment, combination regimens, and timing of MSK-IMPACT testing relative to ICI start; non-ICI prior/concurrent treatments were not recorded for the analysis.
  • No response data for some histologies. PFS / clinical-benefit analyses were limited to NSCLC, melanoma, esophagogastric, head & neck, and renal cell carcinoma; for other histologies only OS is reported.
  • Top-20% cutoff is methodological, not clinical. The authors caution that the dichotomization was for analytic convenience and does not define a clinically actionable threshold.
  • Platform portability. TMB measured by targeted panels correlates with WES, but absolute cutpoints are not directly transferable across platforms.
  • Mechanistic factors unaddressed. Other determinants of ICI response (clonality, neoantigen quality, HLA genotype, immune-cell infiltration, expression of checkpoint molecules) were not jointly modeled here.
  • Glioma signal. Whether the lack of TMB–OS benefit in GB reflects temozolomide-induced subclonal mutations, CNS-specific immunology, or insufficient sample size remains open.
