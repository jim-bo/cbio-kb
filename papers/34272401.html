The landscape of driver mutations in cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma

Authors

Darwin Chang

A. Hunter Shain

Doi

10.1038/s41525-021-00226-4

PMID: 34272401 · DOI: 10.1038/s41525-021-00226-4 · Journal: npj Genomic Medicine (2021)

TL;DR

Chang and Shain performed a meta-analysis of publicly available whole-exome/whole-genome sequencing data from 10 prior studies of cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (CSCC), reprocessing 105 tumors and retaining 88 after quality control. Using four cancer-gene discovery tools (MutSig, LOFsigrank, dN/dS, OncodriveFML) plus cancer hotspots and focal-CNA analysis, they nominate 30 driver genes that operate in the NOTCH, p53, cell-cycle, SWI/SNF chromatin-remodeling, Hippo, stress-response, and MAPK/PI3K pathways. They highlight four genes mutated in >10% of tumors that prior CSCC studies had missed — EP300, PBRM1, USP28, and CHUK — and deposit their reanalyzed mutation calls in cBioPortal as study cscc_ucsf_2021.

Cohort & data

  • 105 tumors aggregated from 10 published whole-exome / whole-genome sequencing studies of CSCC with publicly available raw sequencing data (Durinck 2011, Wang 2011, South 2014, Zheng 2014, Cammareri 2016, Chitsazzadeh 2016, Yilmaz 2017, Cho 2018, Inman 2018, Ji 2020). After QC, 88 tumors retained for downstream analyses; 17 removed for low neoplastic cell content, low reference coverage, sample mismatch, or tumor contamination of reference.
  • Cancer type: cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (CSCC).
  • Dataset: reanalyzed mutation calls deposited at cscc_ucsf_2021 on cBioPortal.
  • Reference genome: hg19. Reads aligned with BWA-MEM v0.7.13; deduplicated with GATK v4.1.2.0 and Picard v4.1.2.0; somatic SNVs called with MuTect2 v4.1.2.0 and annotated with Funcotator v4.1.2.0; indels called with Pindel v0.2.5; copy-number alterations called with CNVkit in reference mode using per-study panels of normals.
  • Tumor cellularity inferred per sample (range 12% – 99%); mean sequencing coverage 12.4× – 498×; the Footprints software quantified per-sample “call-able” footprint (the basepair count with sufficient coverage to call somatic mutations). Mean call-able coverage was 91.2% of target bases (range 52.3% – ~100%).
  • Five clinical/etiologic subtypes were resolved by mutational-signature analysis: xeroderma pigmentosum (XP), sporadic, immunosuppressed without azathioprine, immunosuppressed on azathioprine, and recessive dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa (RDEB).

Key findings

  • 30 cancer genes nominated in CSCC across the meta-analysis (Fig. 4b). 12 genes were called by the discovery tools (MutSig, LOFsigrank, dN/dS, OncodriveFML), of which 7 were called by ≥2 tools. Additional candidates came from cancerhotspots.org overlap and from focal copy-number analysis.
  • NOTCH-pathway loss-of-function mutations were present in 79.5% of tumors (NOTCH1 55.4%, NOTCH2 36.1%). p53-pathway loss-of-function in 71.1% (TP53 66.3%, USP28 12.0%, MDM2 2.4%). The authors call these “defining features” of CSCC.
  • Cell-cycle-checkpoint (Rb pathway) mutations in 38.6%, primarily CDKN2A (34.9%, including focal homozygous deletions).
  • SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex mutations in 38.6% (ARID2 27.7%, PBRM1 12%, EZH2 2.4%).
  • Hippo-pathway mutations in 37.3% (FAT1 30.1%, AJUBA 7.2%, YAP1 1.2%).
  • Stress-response genes (grouped CASP8, CHUK, NFE2L2) mutated in 32.5% of tumors collectively.
  • MAPK / PI3K activating mutations in 31.3% (HRAS 9.6%, PIK3CA 6.0%, PTEN 6.0%, plus RAP1B, RAC1, KRAS, RRAS2, GNA11, GNAS, ERBB3, MTOR).
  • Four newly nominated genes mutated in >10% of tumors but not previously implicated in CSCC by other studies: EP300, PBRM1, USP28, CHUK.
  • Focal copy-number alterations affected: homozygous deletions of CDKN2A and PTEN; heterozygous deletion of AJUBA (combined with a point mutation on the other allele in the same tumor); focal amplifications of CCND1, MDM2, YAP1, and RAP1B (the RAP1B-amplified tumor also carried an activating point mutation on the amplified allele).
  • TERT was included in the final 30-gene list despite the exome-focused study being unable to interrogate the locus, because prior studies report frequent TERT-promoter mutations in CSCC.
  • Subtype-specific selection: CASP8 mutations were significantly enriched in CSCC from RDEB patients (chronic blistering / inflammation), consistent with CASP8’s role in inflammatory-cytokine-induced apoptosis. After multiple-testing correction, no other gene/pathway/subtype enrichment reached significance.
  • Mutational-signature subtypes: XP tumors (n=2) had high TMB and high C>T at CC dipyrimidines but lacked canonical “signature 7” — attributed to differing trinucleotide contexts in mutant-XPC tumors. Azathioprine-treated immunosuppressed tumors carried high signature 32 burdens. RDEB tumors had relatively low TMB primarily from APOBEC mutagenesis.
  • Genes “noted but not nominated” curated as likely false positives / requiring more evidence: COL11A1 (borderline q-value, unusual gene structure with many small exons, low expression in keratinocytes), DIS3, H3C3 (HIST1H3C), KDR, MAP2K4 (OncoKB classifies their hotspots as unlikely to be oncogenic), and KNSTRN S24F (RNA-seq suggests the mutation lies in the promoter, not coding, mirroring TERT/melanoma promoter-vs-coding annotation errors).
  • KMT2D — implicated in >1 other CSCC study but not nominated here because the majority of observed KMT2D mutations were silent or predicted-benign missense.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — mutated in 66.3% of tumors; defining p53-pathway lesion in CSCC.
  • NOTCH1 (55.4%) and NOTCH2 (36.1%) — NOTCH-pathway loss-of-function, ubiquitous across CSCC.
  • CDKN2A — 34.9%, including focal homozygous deletions.
  • FAT1 — 30.1%, Hippo-pathway tumor suppressor.
  • ARID2 — 27.7%; PBRM1 — 12% — both SWI/SNF chromatin-remodeling subunits.
  • CASP8 — 22.9%, selectively enriched in RDEB CSCC.
  • EP300 — 21.7%; loss-of-function mutations including missense mutations clustered in the histone acetyltransferase domain; some have been functionally validated to abolish HAT activity. Highlighted as a novel CSCC driver in this study.
  • CREBBP — 15.7%; paralog of EP300.
  • USP28 — 12.0%; high frequency of truncating mutations. Encodes a deubiquitinase that stabilizes DNA-repair proteins and is required for Chk2–p53–PUMA-mediated apoptosis. Novel CSCC driver in this study.
  • CHUK — 10.8%; encodes IKKα. High truncating-mutation frequency with biallelic alterations in most mutated tumors. Knockout mice and humans with defective CHUK fail to differentiate keratinocytes; a Sleeping-Beauty transposon screen previously implicated CHUK in keratinocyte tumorigenesis on a Pten-sensitized background. Novel CSCC driver in this study.
  • HRAS — 9.6%; canonical hotspot codons (G12D, G13D, G13R, Q61L, Q61K observed).
  • AJUBA — 7.2%; one tumor had a focal heterozygous deletion plus a point mutation on the other allele.
  • PIK3CA (6.0%) and PTEN (6.0%) — PI3K pathway; PTEN focally deleted in some tumors.
  • CCND1 — 6.0%, focal amplifications.
  • RAP1B — 2.4%; the amplified tumor also carried an activating point mutation analogous to known Ras-activating residues.
  • MDM2 — 2.4%, focal amplification.
  • EZH2 — 2.4%, hotspot Y646N observed.
  • RAC1 — 2.4%, P29S/P29L hotspots.
  • YAP1 — 1.2%, focal amplification.
  • NFE2L2 — 1.2%.
  • KRAS, RRAS2, GNA11, GNAS, ERBB3, MTOR — 1.2% each, hotspot mutations.
  • TERT — included in the 30-gene set on the basis of prior literature reporting frequent promoter mutations; not directly interrogated here.
  • XPC — germline loss in two xeroderma-pigmentosum patients; modifies the trinucleotide context of UV-induced mutations.
  • COL7A1 — germline cause of RDEB; not a somatic CSCC driver, but the RDEB context drives the CASP8 enrichment.
  • “Possible false positive” / additional-evidence-needed: COL11A1, DIS3, H3C3, KDR, MAP2K4, KNSTRN, EGFR P551L (a single hotspot call), and SPOP (single hotspot call).
  • Nominated in prior CSCC studies but not by this meta-analysis: KMT2D — flagged by the authors as a candidate that may yet prove to be under selection but for which the present data did not support nomination.

Clinical implications

  • The authors frame CSCC management context: current standard of care is immune-checkpoint blockade, to which roughly half of patients do not respond and responses are not always durable; immunosuppressed patients (especially organ-transplant recipients) carry ~100-fold elevated CSCC risk and are typically ineligible for immunotherapy. The driver-gene catalog is presented as a source of “points of therapeutic intervention,” but the paper does not test specific drugs or report response/survival outcomes.
  • Mechanistic implications: the high prevalence of NOTCH and p53 pathway loss-of-function suggests these pathways are essential constraints whose inactivation is near-obligate in CSCC. The novel EP300 / PBRM1 / USP28 / CHUK nominations point at chromatin-remodeling, DNA-damage response, and NFκB signaling as additional vulnerabilities.
  • Subtype-specific biology: CASP8 enrichment in RDEB-CSCC is interpreted as a selective response to chronic inflammation in blistered skin — a biology-of-context insight rather than a directly actionable biomarker.
  • Sample-quality message for downstream cBioPortal users of cscc_ucsf_2021: the authors document per-tumor cellularity and call-able-footprint estimates and recommend accounting for these technical variables when comparing mutation burdens or driver-gene frequencies across tumors.

Limitations & open questions

  • Exome-only for most studies. Non-coding driver mutations (notably the TERT promoter, included on prior-literature grounds without direct evidence here) and structural variants / viral integrations could not be systematically assessed.
  • Power floor ~15% mutation frequency. Despite being the largest CSCC cohort to date (88 QC-passed tumors), the discovery tools could only detect cancer genes mutated in ≥15% of tumors; rarer drivers were rescued only through hotspot or focal-CNA overlap. The authors explicitly state CSCC has not yet reached driver-discovery saturation.
  • Curation judgment calls. The “likely false positive” / “additional evidence needed” classifications for COL11A1, DIS3, H3C3, KDR, MAP2K4, and KNSTRN S24F rest on gene structure, expression, OncoKB hotspot classification, or annotation correctness — useful filters but not formal tests.
  • No functional validation in this study. EP300, PBRM1, USP28, and CHUK as CSCC drivers are nominated on statistical/mutational grounds; functional/mechanistic confirmation is left to future work.
  • Heterogeneous source studies. 10 source cohorts with different sequencing depths, capture kits, neoplastic-cell content, and sample-collection methods. The authors mitigate this with the Footprints call-able-footprint framework but cannot fully eliminate batch effects.
  • No germline-cancer-risk analysis beyond the XP / RDEB contextual annotations.
