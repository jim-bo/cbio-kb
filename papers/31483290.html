TL;DR

Ho, Ochoa, and colleagues performed an integrated genomic analysis of 1,045 adenoid cystic carcinomas (177 primary, 868 recurrent/metastatic [R/M]) — by far the largest ACYC cohort assembled to date — combining WES, WGS, and targeted NGS data from Sanger/MDACC, MSKCC (MSK-IMPACT, n=94), and Foundation Medicine (n=730). R/M tumors were significantly enriched for activating NOTCH1 mutations (26.3% vs. 8.5% in primary, OR 3.86, P<0.0001), other Notch-pathway genes, and chromatin-remodeling genes (KDM6A, KMT2C, ARID1B, ARID1A, BCOR, CREBBP). TERT promoter mutations (13.1% of R/M cases) were mutually exclusive with both NOTCH1 mutations (q = 3.3×10⁻⁴) and MYB/MYBL1 fusions (q = 5.6×10⁻³), defining four mutually exclusive molecular subgroups (MYB⁺NOTCH1⁺, MYB⁺/other, MYBᵂᵀNOTCH1⁺, MYBᵂᵀTERT⁺) with distinct survival. The data are deposited as the cBioPortal study acc_2019.

Cohort & data

  • Total cases: 1,045 ACCs — 177 primary, 868 recurrent/metastatic (R/M) PMID:31483290.
  • Cancer types: Adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACYC); 89.8% salivary head/neck origin, 6.8% lung, 3.4% breast.
  • Dataset: acc_2019 on cBioPortal (study_id: acc_2019).
  • Assays / platforms:
    • Whole-exome sequencing and whole-genome sequencing (n=193 combined across cohorts; mean TMB 0.34 mut/Mb).
    • Targeted NGS on n=852, including MSK-IMPACT (94 MSKCC R/M cases; 410 or 468 genes; median exon coverage >×600) and FoundationOne (730 R/M cases; median exon coverage >×500).
    • Multiregion validation sequencing via a custom Ion AmpliSeq panel (165 amplicons; mean coverage ×5,406) on one heavily sampled salivary ACC (targeted deep amplicon seq).
    • FISH for MYB and MYBL1 rearrangements; clonality of MYB-NFIB confirmed with a 3-color BAC probe mix.
    • Clonality and ITH inferred with FACETS and PyClone.
    • Microsatellite status by MSIsensor and orthogonal MiMSI.
    • Variant annotation by OncoKB.
  • Germline: 90 R/M MSKCC cases analyzed against a 76-gene cancer-predisposition panel; pathogenic variants scored per ACMG.
  • Clinical follow-up: 84 MSKCC R/M ACC patients with median follow-up 8.1 years.

Key findings

  • R/M enrichment of Notch pathway alterations:
    • NOTCH1 26.3% (R/M) vs. 8.5% (primary), OR 3.86, P<0.0001; 18.3% of R/M had activating hot-spot mutations (HD or PEST domain) PMID:31483290.
    • NOTCH2 4.6% vs. 2.3% (OR 2.09, P=0.17); NOTCH3 5.7% vs. 2.3% (OR 2.63, P=0.067); NOTCH4 3.6% vs. 0.6% (OR 6.59, P=0.067).
    • In aggregate, 34.1% of R/M cases harbored a mutation in NOTCH1–4 and 39.6% in the broader Notch pathway (including SPEN 6.7% and FBXW7 3.8%).
  • R/M enrichment of chromatin-remodeling alterations (all R/M vs. primary, BH FDR<0.10):
    • KDM6A 15.2% vs. 3.4% (OR 5.12, P=0.0001).
    • KMT2C / MLL3 14.3% vs. 4.0% (OR 4.06, P=0.0005).
    • ARID1B 14.1% vs. 4.0% (OR 4.00, P=0.0006).
    • ARID1A 13.7% vs. 2.3% (OR 6.87, P=0.0002).
    • BCOR 13.3% vs. 1.7% (OR 8.92, P=0.0002).
    • KMT2D / MLL2 12.8% vs. 4.5% (OR 3.10, P=0.0027).
    • CREBBP 11.1% vs. 4.5% (OR 2.63, P=0.011).
  • Other R/M-enriched genes: ATM 6.8% vs. 1.7% (OR 4.22, P=0.016); LRP1B 6.8% vs. 1.1% (OR 6.43, P=0.010).
  • TERT promoter: Mutated in 13.1% of R/M cases. Mutually exclusive with NOTCH1 mutation (OR 0.62, q=3.3×10⁻⁴) and with MYB/MYBL1 fusion (OR 3.71 in fusion-negative cases, q=5.6×10⁻³; OR 4.60 in FISH-confirmed subset).
  • Co-occurrence with NOTCH1: KDM6A (OR 1.20, q=3.2×10⁻¹⁰), ARID1A (OR 1.15, q=3.1×10⁻⁵), CREBBP (OR 1.35, q=3.2×10⁻¹⁰), EP300 — suggesting cooperativity between NOTCH1 and chromatin-remodeling alterations.
  • Four molecular subgroups (defined by mutually exclusive MYB / NOTCH1 / TERT status, accounting for 53.2% (305/573) of R/M cases with data): MYB⁺NOTCH1⁺, MYB⁺/other, MYBᵂᵀTERT⁺, MYBᵂᵀNOTCH1⁺ — significantly different OS (P<0.0001). MYBᵂᵀNOTCH1⁺ and MYB⁺NOTCH1⁺ subgroups had the worst prognosis.
  • NOTCH1-mutant survival: Median OS 55.1 vs. 204.5 months (NOTCH1-mutant vs. WT, P=1.10×10⁻⁴). Among NOTCH1-mutant cases, activating mutations conferred worse OS than non-activating (median 31.1 vs. 73.8 months, P=0.042).
  • KDM6A-mutant survival: Median OS 48.5 vs. 169.3 months (KDM6A-mutant vs. WT, P=1.32×10⁻³).
  • MYB and TERT alone not prognostic: MYB-NFIB (P=0.87) and TERT promoter (P=0.12) status did not significantly affect OS.
  • MYB/MYBL1 rearrangements: 22.2% (129/581) of R/M tumors with available data; primarily detected via MYB intron-14 capture or FISH, so MYBᵂᵀ may include cryptic fusions.
  • Triple-negative subgroup: 60.1% (160/266) of MYBᵂᵀNOTCH1ᵂᵀTERTᵂᵀ cases nonetheless harbored at least one chromatin-remodeling alteration.
  • Sequencing depth not a confounder: Downsampling MSK-IMPACT BAMs to ×100 changed mean VAF by 0.011; only 1/101 enriched mutations was lost.
  • Intratumor genetic heterogeneity (ITH):
    • 34.5% of 58 bulk-sequenced cases had ≥2 subclonal populations (SCPs), mean 1.5 SCPs/tumor; 6.9% had ≥3 SCPs — distribution similar to HR⁺ breast cancer.
    • Multiregion WES + ultra-deep targeted sequencing (mean ×5,406 primary, ×1,556 metastases) of one salivary ACC with 6 primary subregions + 8 lung metastases identified 36 unique mutations, only 7/36 present in all regions; 70% of metastatic mutations were detected in only 1–3 of 6 primary subregions and 4 mutations not detected in any primary subregion.
    • NMF on PyClone CCFs defined 3 clusters; the most divergent cluster (metastases 6D, 2B, 4A) carried subclonal SF3B1, XDH, LTF, and TMEM2 mutations — genes previously implicated in metastasis.
    • In 2 breast ACCs transforming to high-grade triple-negative disease, MYB-NFIB and (in one case) NOTCH1 were clonal, while distinct subclonal mutations emerged in high-grade regions.
  • Germline: 33/90 (36.7%) of MSKCC R/M cases carried ≥1 pathogenic germline variant; 38 pathogenic variants total (mean 0.42 per sample). 22.2% involved DNA-repair pathways; 4/90 (4.4%) had splicing-region variants in BRCA1 (c.5152+20T>A, c.5406+8T>C, c.5332+3A>G) or BRCA2 (c.9257–16T>C), all monoallelic on FACETS. 15/90 (16.7%) carried MLH1 or MSH6 germline variants, but all cases were microsatellite-stable on MSIsensor.
  • Tyrosine kinase landscape: 40.3% (346/868) of R/M tumors harbored mutations in genes potentially targetable with available kinase inhibitors, although only 10.6% of alterations had OncoKB-supported clinical evidence.

Genes & alterations

  • NOTCH1 — Activating hot-spot mutations in HD and PEST domains (18.3% of R/M cases); 26.3% of R/M had any NOTCH1 alteration vs. 8.5% of primaries (OR 3.86, P<0.0001). Strongly associated with poor OS (median 55.1 vs. 204.5 months, P=1.10×10⁻⁴). Mutually exclusive with TERT promoter mutations; co-occurs with chromatin-remodeling alterations. Authors call activating NOTCH1 mutations “targetable with Notch-directed therapies such as γ secretase inhibitors” PMID:31483290.
  • NOTCH2, NOTCH3, NOTCH4 — Lower-prevalence Notch-family mutations also numerically enriched in R/M (NOTCH4 OR 6.59, P=0.067).
  • SPEN (6.7%), FBXW7 (3.8%) — Broader Notch-pathway activators mutated in R/M ACC.
  • KDM6A — Most-enriched chromatin-remodeling gene (15.2% R/M vs. 3.4% primary, OR 5.12). KDM6A-mutant OS 48.5 vs. 169.3 months (P=1.32×10⁻³).
  • KMT2C (MLL3) — 14.3% R/M vs. 4.0% primary, OR 4.06.
  • KMT2D (MLL2) — 12.8% R/M vs. 4.5% primary.
  • ARID1A, ARID1B — SWI/SNF complex; both significantly enriched in R/M (OR 6.87 and 4.00).
  • BCOR — Largest fold-change among chromatin genes (OR 8.92, P=0.0002).
  • CREBBP, EP300 — MYB transcriptional coactivators; co-occur with NOTCH1.
  • ATM — DNA damage repair gene enriched in R/M (OR 4.22, P=0.016).
  • LRP1B — Enriched in R/M (OR 6.43, P=0.010).
  • TERT — Promoter mutations in 13.1% of R/M cases, defining a distinct subgroup that is mutually exclusive with both NOTCH1 mutation and MYB/MYBL1 fusion. TERT promoter status alone not prognostic (P=0.12).
  • MYB – NFIB fusion — Canonical ACC driver; confirmed clonal by FISH across primary and metastatic regions in multiregion case. MYB intron-14 rearrangement present in 22.2% of R/M cases with data; not associated with prognosis.
  • MYBL1 — Alternative ACC fusion partner; rearrangements likely under-counted (NGS panels did not capture MYBL1).
  • PIK3CA — Hot-spot mutations in 8 MSK-IMPACT cases; 6 were enrolled on PI3K-inhibitor basket trials.
  • SF3B1, XDH, LTF, TMEM2 — Subclonal mutations identified in divergent metastatic cluster of the multiregion case, implicated in metastasis in other tumor types.
  • BRCA1, BRCA2 — Splicing-region germline variants in 4/90 R/M MSKCC cases; all monoallelic (no LOH).
  • MLH1, MSH6 — Germline variants in 15/90 (16.7%) of cases, but all tumors microsatellite-stable.

Clinical implications

  • Notch-directed therapy candidates: ~18% of R/M ACCs carry activating NOTCH1 hot-spot mutations and are anticipated to be candidates for γ-secretase inhibitors; authors reference ongoing trials NCT03691207, NCT03422679, NCT01695005 PMID:31483290.
  • PI3K-inhibitor activity in PIK3CA-mutant ACC: Of 6 MSK-IMPACT patients with PIK3CA hot-spot mutations enrolled on phase I basket trials of taselisib (β-sparing PI3K inhibitor, NCT01296555) or alpelisib (α-specific PI3K inhibitor, NCT01928459), 1/6 achieved partial response and 5/6 had stable disease after 2 months; 5/6 had any tumor-volume reduction (mean 18.8%, range 4.8%–30.6%); 4/6 had PR or SD lasting >6 months. No clear genomic differences distinguished responders.
  • Multikinase inhibitors: Authors cite prior phase II trials of axitinib (potential benefit in 4q12-amplified ACC) and lenvatinib (ORR 16%–27% in R/M ACC) as evidence that tyrosine-kinase inhibition may benefit subsets of ACC patients, while emphasizing the need for predictive biomarkers.
  • Prognostic biomarkers: Activating NOTCH1 mutations and KDM6A mutations each independently identify R/M ACC patients with markedly worse OS; MYB-NFIB and TERT promoter status do not.
  • Germline counseling: Nearly one-third of MSKCC R/M ACC patients carried at least one pathogenic germline variant — most would not have been detected by guideline-based testing. Authors caution that BRCA1/2 splice variants identified were all monoallelic and of unclear clinical relevance, and that wider validation is needed before recommending universal germline analysis in ACC.

Limitations & open questions

  • ACC is rare, so cases were aggregated from heterogeneous platforms (WES, WGS, multiple targeted NGS panels), limiting analyses such as tumor mutational burden and uniform germline testing.
  • Clinical data (stage, histology, type of metastasis, survival) were missing for many cases.
  • MYB/MYBL1 status was primarily inferred from MYB intron-14 capture or FISH; cryptic MYBL1 fusions and other MYB breakpoints likely went undetected, meaning “MYBᵂᵀ” likely overcounts true fusion-negative cases. MYB overexpression can occur independently of detectable fusion.
  • Germline analysis was limited to 90 anonymized MSKCC R/M cases; penetrance of these variants in salivary tissues is unknown, and ACC is otherwise considered noninheritable (only one potentially familial case reported).
  • Multiregion clonality analyses were performed on a small number of patients (1 salivary multiregion case + 2 published breast ACC cases); broader validation of branching-evolution patterns in ACC is needed.
  • Authors note that cooccurrence between NOTCH1 and chromatin-remodeling alterations could reflect treatment-induced selective pressure in R/M tumors, not solely biological synergy.
  • Activity of γ-secretase inhibitors in NOTCH1-mutant ACC remained under active clinical investigation at publication and was not directly assessed here.
