**PMID:** 32897884 · **DOI:** [10.1172/JCI139682](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI139682) · **Journal:** Journal of Clinical Investigation (2020)

## TL;DR

Taylor et al. assembled a 15-patient MSK cohort (1998–2019) of young men with primary mediastinal nonseminomatous germ cell tumors (PM [NSGCT](../cancer_types/NSGCT.md)) and concomitant hematologic malignancies, and performed targeted ([msk-impact-panel](../methods/msk-impact-panel.md)) and whole-exome sequencing on the paired solid tumors, blood/marrow malignancies, and fingernail-derived germline DNA. Phylogenetic reconstruction in 5 WES-profiled patients showed that the [GCT](../cancer_types/GCT.md) and the hematologic malignancy in each individual evolved from a shared ancestral clone, nearly always harboring an allelically imbalanced [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) and/or RAS-pathway ([KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md), [NRAS](../genes/NRAS.md), [RRAS2](../genes/RRAS2.md)) driver, plus frequent isochromosome 12p. The associated leukemias/sarcomas lacked the canonical de novo AML/MDS drivers ([FLT3](../genes/FLT3.md), [DNMT3A](../genes/DNMT3A.md), [TET2](../genes/TET2.md), [NPM1](../genes/NPM1.md)) and were not detectable in pre-transplant autologous stem cell products, arguing that they arise from a germ-cell-lineage precursor rather than from clonal hematopoiesis or from differentiated GCT components.

## Cohort & data

- **15 patients** with concurrent germ cell tumor and hematologic malignancy, identified at MSKCC over a 21-year period (1998–2019); all males aged 18–33 (median 25) with PM [NSGCT](../cancer_types/NSGCT.md) ([PMID:32897884](../papers/32897884.md)).
- **GCT histology**: teratoma component in 73% (predominant in 60%); yolk sac tumor component in 53% ([TT](../cancer_types/TT.md), [TYST](../cancer_types/TYST.md)).
- **Hematologic malignancies** ([MNM](../cancer_types/MNM.md)) included [AML](../cancer_types/AML.md) (53% of patients, nearly half [AMKL](../cancer_types/AMKL.md) M7), [MDS](../cancer_types/MDS.md), [CMML](../cancer_types/CMML.md), and histiocytic sarcoma ([HS](../cancer_types/HS.md)); one-third of patients developed multiple temporally distinct hematologic subtypes.
- **Sequencing**: targeted [MSK-IMPACT](../methods/msk-impact-panel.md) on 11 patients with available tissue; [whole-exome sequencing](../methods/whole-exome-seq.md) on a 5-patient subset, with fingernail DNA as patient-matched normal control.
- **Reference cohorts** for genomic comparison: 51 PM mediastinal GCTs without secondary malignancy (10 seminoma + 41 nonseminoma) — corresponds to [gct_msk_2016](../datasets/gct_msk_2016.md) lineage — and 200 de novo AMLs from the [TCGA AML cohort](../datasets/laml_tcga_pub.md) ([PMID:32897884](../papers/32897884.md)).
- **Allele-specific copy number and clonality**: [FACETS v0.5.6](../methods/facets.md); only clonal CNAs (>=80% cellular fraction) considered. Oncogenic-alteration annotation via [OncoKB](../methods/oncokb-annotation.md). [Phylogenetic trees](../methods/phylogenetic-tree-reconstruction.md) constructed manually by most-parsimonious sequence of mutations and CNAs.
- **Data deposition**: WES at dbGaP `phs2231.V1`; IMPACT mutation calls deposited via cBioPortal as study [gct_msk_2020](../datasets/gct_msk_2020.md).

## Key findings

- **Median overall survival of the dual-cancer cohort was 6.3 months (95% CI 4.6–25.2 months)**; only one patient was alive at the time of analysis ([PMID:32897884](../papers/32897884.md)).
- **Median time between GCT and blood-cancer diagnosis was 4.8 months**; 4 of 15 patients developed both malignancies concurrently, before any anticancer therapy — incompatible with a purely therapy-induced second neoplasm.
- **Isochromosome 12p [i(12p)]** was common in both mediastinal GCTs and their associated hematologic malignancies but was *never* observed in de novo [AML](../cancer_types/AML.md); in several patients i(12p) appeared as an isolated clone by conventional cytogenetics, marking it as an early event.
- **[TP53](../genes/TP53.md) mutations were shared between the GCT and the blood cancer in 91% (10/11) of paired patients**, versus 61% in mediastinal GCTs without hematologic malignancy and only 9% in de novo AML.
- **Activating mutations in [KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md) or [NRAS](../genes/NRAS.md)** were present in 63% of mediastinal GCTs with associated hematologic malignancies, 37% of mediastinal GCTs without, and only 13% of de novo AML.
- **Absence of canonical myeloid drivers**: the associated hematologic malignancies lacked mutations in [FLT3](../genes/FLT3.md), [DNMT3A](../genes/DNMT3A.md), [TET2](../genes/TET2.md), and [NPM1](../genes/NPM1.md) — distinguishing them molecularly from de novo myeloid neoplasms and from clonal hematopoiesis.
- **Shared ancestral precursor identified in every WES patient** (n=5). Examples:
  - 24-year-old PM NSGCT + [CMML](../cancer_types/CMML.md): shared i(12p), [KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md) G12S, and 8 additional truncal mutations, followed by branching divergent evolution.
  - 28-year-old PM NSGCT + AML: shared [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) R248L with copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity (CNLOH) defining the ancestral clone.
  - Patient with PM NSGCT and high-risk [MDS](../cancer_types/MDS.md): shared [KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md) G12D precursor with identical chromosomes 13 and 17 CNAs; each malignancy independently acquired distinct [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) hotspot mutations with CNLOH (mutually exclusive on aligned reads).
  - 19-year-old with successive GCT → histiocytic sarcoma → CMML → AML within 18 months: all 4 malignancies derived from a precursor bearing [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) Q144Rfs\*26 with CNLOH plus a [PIK3CD](../genes/PIK3CD.md) mutation; daughter precursors then split GCT+AML from histiocytic sarcoma+CMML, with the histiocytic sarcoma further acquiring [NRAS](../genes/NRAS.md) Q61H. The inferred clonal tree did not match the order of clinical diagnosis. Notably this patient's GCT lacked i(12p), so karyotype alone would have missed the clonal relationship.
- **Autologous stem cell products from 5 high-risk PM GCT patients showed no clonal hematopoiesis** by sequencing, even though one of them later developed a clonally related MDS and histiocytic sarcoma sharing a somatic [BCOR](../genes/BCOR.md) G943D mutation and an [RRAS2](../genes/RRAS2.md) activating mutation with CNLOH (with each subsequent disease acquiring its own distinct [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) mutation with independent LOH). This argues the precursor is not a hematopoietic stem cell circulating during mobilization.
- **2 of the 15 PM NSGCT patients had clinically diagnosed Klinefelter syndrome**; none of the 41 PM NSGCT comparators without hematologic malignancy were known to have it (germline confirmation not done in comparators).

## Genes & alterations

- **[TP53](../genes/TP53.md)** — Shared truncal mutations (including hotspots R248L, Q144Rfs\*26, Q144Rfs\*28) with CNLOH defined the GCT-blood-cancer ancestral clone in multiple patients; mutually exclusive secondary [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) hits accumulated independently in each lineage. Shared in 91% of GCT/hematologic-malignancy pairs vs 61% of GCT-only and 9% of de novo AML.
- **[KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md)** — Activating mutations (e.g., G12S, G12D) seen as truncal precursor alterations; [KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md) or [NRAS](../genes/NRAS.md) mutations together in 63% of GCT+hematologic-malignancy cases vs 13% of de novo AML.
- **[NRAS](../genes/NRAS.md)** — Q61H observed as a late, lineage-specific event in a histiocytic sarcoma; part of the activating RAS-pathway driver set together with KRAS.
- **[RRAS2](../genes/RRAS2.md)** — Activating mutation with CNLOH identified as a shared truncal alteration in a patient whose GCT, MDS, and post-transplant histiocytic sarcoma were all clonally related.
- **[BCOR](../genes/BCOR.md)** — G943D shared across the GCT, MDS, and post-allogeneic-transplant histiocytic sarcoma of one patient, supporting host (not donor) origin and shared precursor.
- **[PIK3CD](../genes/PIK3CD.md)** — Mutation present in the ancestral precursor of the 19-year-old with 4 clonally related malignancies (text also writes "PI3KCD" in one figure callout; the gene symbol is [PIK3CD](../genes/PIK3CD.md) per OncoKB/HUGO).
- **[PTEN](../genes/PTEN.md)** — Referenced in prior reports (Lu et al., 2016) describing concurrent GCT/AMKL with shared TP53 and [PTEN](../genes/PTEN.md) mutations; not a primary driver in this 15-patient cohort.
- **Copy-number / cytogenetic features** — Isochromosome 12p shared between GCT and hematologic malignancy in this cohort, *never* seen in de novo AML; complex karyotypes common in the blood malignancies; copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity at TP53 a recurrent mechanism of biallelic inactivation.

## Clinical implications

- **Hematologic malignancies arising in PM NSGCT patients should be recognized as a distinct clinical syndrome** — not therapy-related AML/MDS — and treated with that biology in mind: high TP53 alteration burden and absence of canonical AML drivers ([FLT3](../genes/FLT3.md), [DNMT3A](../genes/DNMT3A.md), [TET2](../genes/TET2.md), [NPM1](../genes/NPM1.md)) likely explains the documented refractoriness to standard MDS/AML therapy and contributes to the 6.3-month median survival.
- **The same TP53 alteration burden in the GCT precursor provides a mechanistic basis for [cisplatin](../drugs/cisplatin.md) resistance**, consistent with prior work linking p53 pathway alterations to cisplatin failure in advanced germ cell tumors. Treatments listed in this cohort included [cisplatin](../drugs/cisplatin.md), [etoposide](../drugs/etoposide.md), [ifosfamide](../drugs/ifosfamide.md) (VIP regimen), and [paclitaxel](../drugs/paclitaxel.md) plus high-dose [carboplatin](../drugs/carboplatin.md) and [etoposide](../drugs/etoposide.md) (TICE regimen), followed by autologous SCT.
- **Autologous stem cell transplant products were not contaminated with clonal hematopoiesis in any of the 5 PM GCT patients tested**, including one who later developed a clonally related MDS — so the relapse hematologic malignancy is not attributable to mobilized CHIP. This is useful biology when counseling about auto-SCT for relapsed mediastinal GCT.
- **Karyotype-only screening is insufficient** to identify clonally related hematologic malignancy in GCT patients: i(12p) was absent in at least one clonally related case.
- **Klinefelter syndrome should be evaluated** (including for mosaicism / subclinical disease) in PM NSGCT patients who develop hematologic malignancies, given that 2 of 15 in this cohort had known Klinefelter syndrome.

## Limitations & open questions

- **Small cohort (n=15 total; WES in 5)** — phylogenetic conclusions rest on a handful of patients given the rarity of the syndrome.
- **WES patient 2's post-allogeneic histiocytic sarcoma required filtering of donor-derived contamination** using preallogeneic variant allele frequencies; that sample was excluded from copy-number analysis.
- **Germline predisposition is not characterized** — the authors call for systematic germline studies to evaluate for predisposition (including occult Klinefelter mosaicism).
- **The basis for the specific association between PM NSGCTs and somatic-type hematologic malignancies remains unexplained**; no testicular or seminomatous GCT patient in this series developed a clonally related hematologic malignancy.
- **The proposed cell of origin — embryonal carcinoma cells reprogrammed from primordial germ cells — is a model**, not a directly demonstrated lineage; further single-cell or fate-mapping studies would be needed.
- **Comparator cohorts (51 PM mediastinal GCTs; 200 TCGA AMLs)** are MSKCC-internal and TCGA respectively; differences in age distribution, sequencing assay, and treatment era introduce confounding.
