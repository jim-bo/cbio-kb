Tracking Tumour Evolution in Glioma through Liquid Biopsies of Cerebrospinal Fluid

PMID: 30675060 · DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-0882-3 · Journal: Nature (2019)

TL;DR

Miller and colleagues sequenced cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) cell-free DNA from 85 adult glioma patients at Memorial Sloan Kettering using the msk-impact-panel assay and detected tumour-derived ctDNA in 42/85 (49.4%) patients. The CSF genomic landscape closely resembled matched tumour tissue, with 100% concordance for glioma-defining “truncal” alterations (IDH1, TP53, ATRX, TERT, 1p/19q codeletion) in non-hypermutated cases. CSF ctDNA positivity was associated with disease progression, higher tumour burden, and a four-fold increased risk of death (HR 4.16, 95% CI 2.15–8.05, p=0.000024) independent of IDH1 status and extent of resection. The work establishes CSF as a more informative liquid-biopsy reservoir than plasma for diffuse glioma and documents convergent/branched evolution in growth-factor-receptor pathways over time.

Cohort & data

  • n = 85 adult patients treated for glioma at MSKCC, January 2015 – April 2017; 113 CSF samples total (22 patients had ≥2 CSFs). Patient-level data deposited as glioma_msk_2018 on cBioPortal.
  • WHO grade composition: grade IV GBM 46/85 (54%), grade III glioma 26/85 (31%), grade II glioma 13/85 (15%); all subsumed under DIFG (diffuse glioma), with 39 LGG-grade tumours (II + III).
  • Prior treatment near-universal: surgery 85/85 (100%), radiation 84/85 (99%), ≥1 systemic chemotherapy 81/85 (95%).
  • All CSF and tumour DNA sequenced by msk-impact-panel (410 cancer-associated genes + 46 introns from 17 recurrently rearranged genes); tumours used IMPACT341, IMPACT410, or IMPACT468. Genetically matched normal available for 73/85 (86%).
  • Plasma cfDNA from a subset of patients was sequenced on a separate 129-gene duplex-UMI capture assay at >18,000× raw / 1,032× median unique coverage.
  • Copy-number called with facets; structural variants manually reviewed in integrative-genomics-viewer.
  • Benchmark cohort: 553 MSKCC glioma biopsies from 512 patients previously sequenced with MSK-IMPACT.

Key findings

  • Tumour-derived ctDNA detected in 42/85 (49.4%) glioma CSFs; 0/7 non-malignant control CSFs harboured oncogenic variants (PMID:30675060).
  • CSF ctDNA positivity correlated with: tumour progression (Fisher p=0.0005), tumour burden (SPD, Wilcoxon p=1.7×10⁻⁶), and tumour spread toward ventricular/subarachnoid spaces (Fisher p=0.02). Median tumour SPD 1,553 mm² (ctDNA+) vs 373 mm² (ctDNA−).
  • In a multivariable Cox model adjusting for extent of resection, tumour burden, and IDH status, CSF ctDNA positivity carried HR 4.16 (95% CI 2.15–8.05, p=2.43×10⁻⁵) for death from time of CSF collection.
  • Median OS from CSF collection: 3.15 months (ctDNA+) vs 11.91 months (ctDNA−) overall (log-rank p=7.87×10⁻⁶); 2.04 vs 9.89 months in IDH-WT GBM (p=6.24×10⁻⁵).
  • 35/42 (83%) of ctDNA-positive CSFs had no malignant cells by standard cytopathology — molecular detection outperformed cytology.
  • Median tumour mutation burden in CSF ctDNA was 4.90 mut/Mb (vs 4.46/Mb in the MSK-IMPACT tissue cohort, n=553; after excluding hypermutators, 3.92/Mb CSF vs 3.94/Mb tissue) — substantially above reported TCGA exome rates (LGG 0.7–0.8/Mb, GBM 2.2/Mb).
  • 5/42 (12%) CSFs displayed alkylator-induced hypermutation (G:C→A:T signature); all five had received temozolomide.
  • Plasma was an inferior reservoir: among 19 CSF-ctDNA-positive patients, 16/19 (84%) had no detectable mutations in plasma; among the three with positive plasma, average VAF was 0.58% (plasma) vs 23.96% (CSF) (Wilcoxon p=5.82×10⁻¹¹). All three had disseminated CNS disease. The data argue tumour DNA is shed directly into CSF rather than transiting through plasma.
  • In 30 non-hypermutated tumour/CSF pairs, median shared-mutation rate was 81.7% (range 0–100%); in 6 hypermutated pairs, median was 19.6% (range 3–49%). Clonal tumour mutations were shared with CSF even under hypermutation; subclonal mutations were not.
  • Truncal alterations were 100% concordant: in 10/10 non-hypermutated LGG and 20/20 non-hypermutated GBM tumour/CSF pairs, the LGG-defining combinations (IDH1 ± TP53/ATRX ± 1p/19q ± TERT promoter) matched the original tumour.
  • Genomic profiles of contemporaneous CSF replicates collected from different anatomical CNS locations (LP vs VP-shunt placement, within 3 weeks) were highly concordant (n=6 patients); 5 tumour/CSF pairs collected within 3 weeks were near-identical.

Genes & alterations

  • IDH1, IDH2 — catalytic-domain hotspot mutations (esp. IDH1 R132) are truncal LGG-defining events; persisted through evolution and were detected in CSF with 100% concordance to tumour.
  • TERT — promoter mutations; truncal in the IDH-mutant 1p/19q-codeleted subtype; among most commonly observed CSF alterations.
  • TP53 — protein-coding mutations; common in IDH-mutant non-codeleted gliomas alongside ATRX; among the most frequent CSF alterations.
  • ATRX — loss-of-function alterations; mutually exclusive with TERT promoter mutations; truncal in IDH-mutant non-codeleted LGGs.
  • EGFR — amplifications, in-frame EGFRvIII deletion, and missense (e.g. G719C in patient #25) were common in CSF; later sample of patient #25 showed loss of the original EGFR amplification with acquired PDGFRA amplification + mutation (convergent evolution in growth-factor-receptor signalling).
  • CDKN2A / CDKN2B — homozygous deletions among the most frequent CSF alterations.
  • PDGFRA — amplifications/mutations acquired during evolution (patients #25 and #28), illustrating convergent evolution within the receptor tyrosine kinase axis.
  • MET, KIT, CDK4, MYC — patient #28 (GBM, IDH-WT) acquired amplifications across multiple receptor tyrosine kinases and cell-cycle regulators across serial tumour and CSF samples; CDK4 amplification was stable across all four biospecimens, while PDGFRA/KIT amplification appeared in tumour #3 but was lost in the later CSF sample, which retained MET and gained MYC amplification.
  • PIK3CA — activating E545K mutation in patient #28’s initial tumour was replaced by other pathway alterations in later samples (branched evolution).
  • MSH6 — mismatch-repair gene implicated as a mechanism of alkylator-induced hypermutation per cited prior work; mentioned as context for the 5/42 hypermutator phenotype.

Clinical implications

  • Prognostic biomarker. CSF ctDNA positivity is an independent prognostic factor for shorter overall survival in adult diffuse glioma after CSF collection (HR 4.16) and may serve as an early indicator of disease progression.
  • Alternative to repeat craniotomy. Lumbar puncture is safer than craniotomy for genotyping recurrent disease; CSF ctDNA captures the contemporary tumour genome with high fidelity when collection is temporally proximate to the lesion.
  • Resistance monitoring. The convergent-evolution patterns observed within growth-factor-receptor pathways (e.g. EGFR → PDGFRA switching) argue that serial CSF profiling could identify acquired resistance mechanisms and guide genotype-directed therapy.
  • Hypermutation surveillance. Patients exposed to temozolomide (and other alkylators) can develop a hypermutator phenotype detectable in CSF; this has implications for immunotherapy candidacy and for interpreting the dominance of subclonal vs clonal variants.
  • Plasma is not interchangeable. For diffuse glioma, plasma cfDNA is a far less sensitive reservoir than CSF and is informative only in the setting of disseminated CNS disease.

Limitations & open questions

  • Authors caveat that shedding of tumour DNA into CSF is not a universal property of diffuse glioma even in previously treated patients — half the cohort was ctDNA-negative. The earliest time at which CSF becomes informative is unknown.
  • Cohort is from a single institution (MSKCC) with prior pretreatment and lumbar punctures driven by neurological symptoms, biasing toward more advanced disease — generalisability to newly diagnosed or asymptomatic glioma is unproven.
  • The MSK-IMPACT panel enriches for frequently mutated cancer genes, which inflates apparent TMB relative to whole-exome estimates. Cross-platform mutation-rate comparisons (panel vs TCGA exome) require this caveat.
  • Concordance with tumour decays with the CSF-tumour collection interval: pairs >1,000 days apart shared only ~29% of mutations vs 79% within 3 weeks — limiting retrospective archival-tissue comparisons.
  • 12/85 patients lacked a genetically matched normal, requiring variant filtering against a pool plus 1000 Genomes — increases false-negative risk for low-frequency somatic variants.
  • Plasma comparison was performed only in CSF-ctDNA-positive patients (n=19); whether plasma might be informative in CSF-negative patients is not tested.
