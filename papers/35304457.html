TL;DR

Rosen et al. characterize molecular determinants of response and resistance to the selective RET inhibitor selpercatinib in 72 patients with RET fusion- or mutation-positive lung and thyroid cancers enrolled on the registrational phase 1/2 LIBRETTO-001 trial (NCT03157128) at Memorial Sloan Kettering. Pre-treatment genomic profile did not predict initial response (except for rare RAS-mediated primary resistance), but acquired resistance was driven by MAPK pathway reactivation through two main routes: on-target secondary RET solvent-front mutations or MET amplifications, and off-target selection of RET-wild-type subclones harboring alternative mitogenic drivers (KRAS, NRAS, BRAF, FGFR1). Multiple distinct resistance mechanisms frequently co-existed in the same patient, indicating polyclonal resistance is common in RET-driven cancers.

Cohort & data

  • 72 patients with RET activating fusion or mutation treated with selpercatinib on LIBRETTO-001 (NCT03157128) at Memorial Sloan Kettering; 70 included in correlative genomic analyses after two were lost to follow-up.
  • Cancer types: lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) predominated among fusion-positive cancers (81%), followed by papillary thyroid (THPA, 12%); also large-cell neuroendocrine lung (LCNEC), lung adenosquamous (LUAS), high-grade neuroendocrine rectal carcinoma (HGNEC), poorly differentiated thyroid, and non-Langerhans cell histiocytosis. All RET-mutant cancers were medullary thyroid carcinoma (THME).
  • Dataset: mixed_selpercatinib_2020.
  • Assays: tumor tissue MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing (up to 468 genes, n=51 pre-treatment + 9 post-progression matched pairs); whole-exome sequencing on 44 pre-treatment tumors; pre-treatment plasma cfDNA via MSK-ACCESS (n=68); longitudinal cfDNA on 23 patients with 18 progressing patients sequenced post-progression.
  • Data freeze: December 2019. Enrollment between May 2017 and December 2019. Response assessed by RECIST 1.1.

Key findings

  • Selpercatinib was active independent of tissue of origin, alteration class (fusion vs mutation), or prior multikinase-inhibitor exposure; investigator-assessed RECIST 1.1 ORR was 67% in RET fusion-positive and 58% in RET-mutant cancers, with 82% of responders ongoing at 1 year.
  • 19 of 72 patients received selpercatinib as first-line; of 53 previously treated, 29 (55%) had prior multikinase inhibitor (MKI) therapy (vandetanib, cabozantinib, or RXDX-105). Prior MKI exposure did not alter outcomes (HR=1.2, 95% CI 0.6–2.6, P=0.6).
  • Pre-treatment tumor mutational burden and subclonal heterogeneity were not associated with depth or durability of response (P>0.4 for all comparisons).
  • Concurrent TP53 mutations (n=8, all in fusion-positive cancers) were associated with shorter median PFS on selpercatinib (HR=3.5, 95% CI 1.3–9.7, P=0.016).
  • Pre-treatment plasma profiling detected the qualifying RET alteration in 95% (18/19) of RET-mutant cases and 74% (36/49) of fusion-positive cases. Patients with no or low pre-treatment cfDNA shedding had lower baseline disease burden (P=0.02) and superior PFS on selpercatinib (HR=3.7, 95% CI 1.1–12.4, P=0.013).
  • In 23 longitudinally followed patients, the qualifying RET mutant allele frequency in plasma decreased by a median of 82.5% upon selpercatinib initiation, supporting cfDNA as a surrogate for target engagement.
  • Four MTC patients previously treated with MKIs harbored coincident secondary RET mutations (V804M ×2, G601E, K666E, D898Y) in baseline plasma at low frequencies (0.65–7%); all decreased with selpercatinib, indicating these prior-therapy-acquired alleles remained selpercatinib-sensitive.
  • Pre-existing PI3K pathway alterations (PTEN loss-of-function in 2 tumor-tissue cases, additional PTEN/PIK3CA mutations in 9 plasma cases) did not preclude clinical benefit (CBR 91%).
  • Primary resistance: 2 patients had pre-existing KRAS G12D and G12V mutations detected only in baseline plasma (not tissue) at low allele frequencies; these KRAS-mutant subclones grew out while the RET fusion-positive clone responded, indicating inter-tumoral heterogeneity of mitogenic drivers.
  • Acquired resistance was identified in 11/18 evaluable progressing patients (61%). Mechanisms:
    • On-target (n=3): secondary RET mutations including solvent-front G810C, G810S, and compound cis mutants (e.g. M918T/V804M/Y806C triple mutant); 293T expression experiments confirmed the triple cis mutant abrogated selpercatinib inhibition of pRET and pERK.
    • Off-target / bypass (n=7): acquired KRAS (G12A/R/V, G13D, A59del), NRAS (G13D, Q61R), BRAF activating mutations, or MET / FGFR1 amplifications.
  • One patient with subclonal pre-existing MET amplification responded to combination selpercatinib + crizotinib at progression, but an emerging BRAF D594N (kinase-dead, RAS- and BRAF-dimer–dependent) clone was detected during combination therapy, reinforcing MAPK dependence of RET-altered tumors.
  • Polyclonal resistance was common: two patients had co-occurring RET solvent-front mutations and KRAS G12A/G12R mutations emerging at distinct timepoints in plasma, indicating distinct resistant subclones.
  • No evidence of lineage/histology change as a mechanism of acquired resistance was found in three evaluable fusion-positive lung cancers with post-progression biopsies.

Genes & alterations

  • RET — activating fusions (KIF5B-RET most common, plus CCDC6-RET, NCOA4-RET, TAF3-RET, ERC1-RET, RBPMS-RET, CLIP1-RET, EML4-RET, KIF13A-RET, KIAA1468-RET, RUFY3-RET) and kinase-domain mutations (germline and somatic; e.g. M918T) define trial-qualifying drivers. Acquired solvent-front G810C/G810S, gatekeeper V804M, Y806C and compound cis mutants (M918T/V804M/Y806C) drive on-target resistance.
  • KIF5B, CCDC6, NCOA4 — recurrent RET fusion partners retaining full kinase domain.
  • KRAS — G12D/G12V pre-existing primary resistance (subclonal in plasma); acquired G12A/G12R/G12V, G13D, A59del at progression in fusion-positive lung cancers.
  • NRAS — acquired G13D, Q61R at progression.
  • BRAF — acquired activating mutations including kinase-dead D594N (RAS- and BRAF-dimer–dependent) emerging during selpercatinib + crizotinib combination therapy.
  • MET — focal amplifications mediating bypass resistance; one patient with subclonal pre-treatment MET amplification re-responded to selpercatinib + crizotinib.
  • FGFR1 — focal amplification mediating MAPK reactivation in an oligo-progressing site.
  • TP53 — concurrent mutations in 8 fusion-positive cases linked to shorter PFS (HR=3.5).
  • PTEN, PIK3CA — PI3K pathway lesions present at baseline did not preclude response; PI3K activating mutations decreased on therapy alongside RET, inconsistent with a primary driver of resistance.
  • MAP2K1 C121S — observed among acquired MAPK-activating alterations.
  • ALK, ROS1, EGFR — referenced as analogous oncogene-addicted systems where compound on-target mutations and bypass signaling drive resistance to selective inhibitors.

Clinical implications

  • Selpercatinib is broadly active in RET-driven cancers across tissue of origin and alteration class; pre-treatment co-mutation profiling has limited predictive value, except TP53 co-mutations (shorter PFS) and rare RAS-mediated primary resistance.
  • Plasma cfDNA (MSK-ACCESS) is informative for both pre-treatment risk stratification (low shedders have better PFS) and longitudinal target engagement (median 82.5% decrease in qualifying RET mutant allele frequency on therapy).
  • At selpercatinib progression, prospective molecular characterization (tissue + plasma) is warranted because polyclonal resistance is common: ≥1 patient often harbors both on-target RET mutations and off-target MAPK reactivation through distinct subclones.
  • Sequential single-agent next-generation RET TKI strategy (analogous to ALK) may be insufficient because of MAPK-pathway-driven bypass; combination therapy targeting MET, RAS-MAPK effectors, or solvent-front–competent next-generation RET inhibitors will likely be required.
  • Selpercatinib + crizotinib can re-induce response in MET-amplified bypass resistance, but new MAPK-activating clones (e.g. BRAF D594N) can emerge under combination pressure.
  • PI3K pathway co-alterations do not preclude selpercatinib benefit; clinical benefit rate in PI3K-co-altered patients was 91%.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small cohort (72 patients; 18 evaluable progressing patients with paired pre/post-progression material) limits statistical resolution of co-mutation effects beyond TP53.
  • A genetic mechanism of resistance was identified in 11/18 (61%) of progressing patients with sequencing material — the remaining 7 (39%) lacked an identified discrete genetic driver, leaving non-genetic mechanisms (e.g. transcriptional adaptation, epigenetic) unexplored.
  • Lineage plasticity was excluded only in three evaluable fusion-positive lung cancers with post-progression biopsies; broader sampling needed.
  • The functional significance of low-allele-frequency RAS mutations in baseline plasma (sub-clonal mitogenic drivers in a RET-naive context) requires prospective validation.
  • Whether next-generation selpercatinib-successor RET inhibitors active against solvent-front G810 mutations can overcome compound cis mutants (M918T/V804M/Y806C) is not addressed clinically.
  • Optimal combination strategies (RET + MEK, RET + MET, RET + pan-RAS) at the time of monotherapy progression remain to be tested in trials.
