PTEN Loss-of-Function Alterations Are Associated With Intrinsic Resistance to BRAF Inhibitors in Metastatic Melanoma

Authors

Donavan T. Cheng

Alexander N. Shoushtari

PMID: 32913971 · DOI: 10.1200/PO.16.00054 · Journal: JCO Precision Oncology (2017)

TL;DR

Catalanotti et al. profiled pretreatment tumors from 66 patients with metastatic BRAF V600E/K-mutant melanoma (55 treated with BRAF-inhibitor monotherapy, 11 with a BRAFi + MEKi combination) using a >250-gene exon-capture sequencing panel (PMID:32913971). Loss-of-function PTEN alterations were significantly enriched in poor responders to BRAFi monotherapy and were associated with shorter PFS (HR 3.46, 95% CI 1.79–6.71; P < .001) and OS (HR 3.10, 95% CI 1.59–6.05; P < .001). Excellent responders were enriched for elevated BRAF-mutant allele fraction, suggesting that selective amplification of the mutant allele or loss of the wild-type allele sensitizes tumors to BRAFi. The authors propose co-targeting BRAF and the PI3K/AKT pathway in patients with concurrent BRAF and PTEN alterations.

Cohort & data

  • Cohort: 66 patients with stage IV or unresectable stage III BRAF V600E/K-mutant metastatic melanoma (SKCM), collected at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center and Vanderbilt-Ingram Cancer Center before BRAFi-based therapy (PMID:32913971).
  • Treatment split: 55 patients received BRAFi monotherapy (49 vemurafenib, 5 dabrafenib, 1 vemurafenib + cobimetinib) and 11 received a BRAFi + MEKi combination (3 vemurafenib + cobimetinib; 8 dabrafenib + trametinib). One BRAFi-monotherapy patient received cobimetinib later for chronic myelomonocytic leukemia.
  • Dataset: skcm_vanderbilt_mskcc_2015; data deposited at the MSK cBioPortal for Cancer Genomics. Comparator: 151 BRAF V600E/K-mutant tumors from the TCGA SKCM cohort (skcm_tcga_pub_2015).
  • Assay / panel: custom capture-based DNA sequencing across all coding exons and selected introns of >250 cancer-associated genes (msk-impact-panel precursor, per Wagle et al. 2012 and Cheng et al. 2015), mean coverage 622×.
  • Variant calling: MuTect for SNVs; GATK Somatic Indel Detector for indels; FACETS for tumor purity, ploidy, and allele-specific copy number.
  • Response endpoints: RECIST 1.1 plus a composite “response grade” (excellent: >50% shrinkage for >7 months or any shrinkage for >12 months; poor: tumor growth, new lesions, or <50% shrinkage for <4 months; intermediate: neither).
  • Follow-up: median 14.6 months (cutoff March 2016).

Key findings

  • BRAFi monotherapy response (RECIST): 30/55 (55%) objective responses, 5/55 (9%) complete responses, 25/55 (45%) partial responses, 11/55 (20%) stable disease, 10/55 (18%) primary progressive disease, 4/55 (7%) not evaluable (PMID:32913971).
  • Response-grade distribution (BRAFi monotherapy): excellent 17/55 (31%), poor 19/55 (35%), intermediate 15/55 (27%), not evaluable 4/55 (7%). RECIST and response-grade classifications were discordant in 8/66 patients (PR by RECIST but poor responders by grade due to short duration).
  • PTEN alterations enriched in poor responders: 11 PTEN alterations in poor responders vs. 3 in intermediate and 2 in excellent responders (P = .005). Of the 11 PTEN alterations in the poor group, 10 were likely inactivating: deep deletions (n = 5), truncating mutations (n = 4), and one C2-domain missense (P204S); a P38S variant in the phosphatase domain was of unknown significance.
  • Survival impact of PTEN alteration: patients with PTEN alterations had shorter PFS (HR 3.46, 95% CI 1.79–6.71; P < .001) and shorter OS (HR 3.10, 95% CI 1.59–6.05; P < .001). The association persisted after controlling for stage and ECOG performance status (PFS HR 3.30, 95% CI 1.70–6.43, P < .001; OS HR 3.37, 95% CI 1.70–6.70, P < .001).
  • PTEN alterations are not exclusive to poor responders: 2 excellent and 3 intermediate responders also harbored PTEN deep deletions or recurrent missense variants (R15S, G132C, Y177H), indicating PTEN loss does not preclude BRAFi response.
  • Elevated BRAF-mutant allele fraction marks excellent responders: significantly enriched in excellent responders (P < .001) versus poor responders (P = .02), with FACETS confirming allelic imbalance in two excellent responders; consistent with selective amplification of mutant BRAF or loss of the wild-type allele as a sensitizing event.
  • CDKN2A does NOT predict response: 21/51 (41%) BRAFi-monotherapy patients had CDKN2A alterations (19 putative loss-of-function), but neither mutation nor homozygous deletion correlated with response grade — contradicting an earlier report (Nathanson et al. 2013, Clin Cancer Res 19:4868).
  • Cohort vs. TCGA comparison: RB1 (P = .0036) and MDM2 (P = .045) alterations were enriched in the MSK/Vanderbilt cohort relative to TCGA, likely reflecting the more aggressive clinical profile.
  • MAPK-pathway escape mutations rare in pretreatment tumors: 0/66 RAS mutations; 1 patient had an NF1 splice variant (X2441_splice) and was a poor responder; 2 MAP2K1 mutations (P124S in an intermediate responder on BRAFi monotherapy; Q56P at 0.04 allele frequency in an intermediate responder on BRAFi + MEKi, indicating a subclonal population).
  • PI3K-pathway hits beyond PTEN: 4 PIK3CA missense mutations (3 hotspots: V344G, E545K, H1047R), present in both excellent and poor responders; one AKT3 E17K hotspot in a poor responder treated with BRAFi + MEKi; no hotspot AKT mutations in BRAFi-monotherapy patients.
  • Other notable alterations: BRAF amplification in 3 tumors (all intermediate responders) — BRAFi response is not precluded by BRAF amplification, contrasting with its known role in acquired resistance; MITF amplifications (n = 3) seen exclusively in poor/intermediate responders (not statistically significant); 4 KDR mutations (1 hotspot R1032Q, 3 VUS), all in poor responders, with significantly shorter PFS and OS in KDR-mutant patients; TP53 + MDM2 alterations in 13/51 (25%) of BRAFi-monotherapy patients did not correlate with response grade or survival.
  • Co-mutation landscape: PTPRT, PTPRD, and KMT2C were among the most commonly co-altered genes with BRAF, but most variants were missense VUS, consistent with passenger events given the high mutation burden of melanoma.

Genes & alterations

  • BRAF — V600E/K confirmed in all 66 tumors; amplification in 3 (all intermediate responders); elevated mutant-allele fraction (more than 2× the median variant fraction) in 9 patients and significantly enriched in excellent responders (P < .001 vs. poor).
  • PTEN — loss-of-function alterations (deep deletions, truncations, C2-domain P204S, plus VUS) are the headline biomarker of intrinsic BRAFi resistance: enriched in poor responders (P = .005) and associated with shorter PFS (HR 3.46) and OS (HR 3.10).
  • CDKN2A — altered in 21/51 (41%) BRAFi-monotherapy patients (19 LOF), but no association with response grade, contradicting prior reports.
  • PIK3CA — 4 missense (3 hotspots: V344G, E545K, H1047R); present in excellent and poor responders alike; one excellent responder carried the activating H1047R.
  • AKT1, AKT2 — no hotspot mutations observed in 51 BRAFi-monotherapy patients.
  • AKT3 — one E17K hotspot in a poor responder on BRAFi + MEKi.
  • TSC1, TSC2, MTOR — rare non-recurrent VUS in both excellent and poor responders.
  • NRAS — 0 RAS mutations across all 66 pretreatment tumors.
  • NF1 — one splice variant (X2441_splice) in a poor responder, consistent with preclinical models of NF1-loss BRAFi resistance.
  • MAP2K1 (MEK1) — P124S (BRAFi monotherapy intermediate responder); Q56P at 0.04 allele frequency (subclonal) in BRAFi + MEKi intermediate responder; both previously linked to context-dependent BRAFi/MEKi resistance.
  • TP53, MDM2, MDM4 — 25% of BRAFi-monotherapy patients harbored functional TP53 or MDM2 events (TP53 in 8; MDM2 amplification in 4 and mutation in 1) but no association with response grade or survival.
  • RB1, CCND1 — alterations or amplifications in 11% of BRAFi-monotherapy patients; no association with response.
  • MITF — 3 amplifications, exclusively in poor/intermediate responders (not statistically significant).
  • KDR (VEGFR2) — 4 mutations (1 hotspot R1032Q, 3 VUS), all in poor responders, with significantly shorter PFS and OS.
  • PTPRT, PTPRD, KMT2C — frequently co-mutated with BRAF, but most variants were VUS likely representing passenger events given the high tumor mutational burden of melanoma.

Clinical implications

  • Pretreatment biomarker: the authors argue that PTEN loss-of-function alterations identified by capture-based sequencing of pretreatment tumors mark patients with BRAF-mutant melanoma who are unlikely to derive durable benefit from vemurafenib/dabrafenib monotherapy (PMID:32913971).
  • Combination strategy: the results provide a rationale for combining BRAFi (+/- MEKi) with PI3K/AKT-pathway inhibitors in patients with concurrent BRAF and PTEN alterations; the authors further suggest such combinations might extend response depth/duration even in PTEN wild-type patients because complete responses are uncommon.
  • BRAF-mutant allele fraction as a candidate predictive marker: elevated mutant-allele fraction (selective gain of mutant BRAF or loss of the wild-type allele) tracks with excellent response; the authors call for broader whole-exome/whole-genome sequencing of BRAF-mutant tumors to test this prospectively.
  • BRAF amplification does not preclude response: in contrast to acquired resistance, pretreatment BRAF amplification (n = 3) did not predict primary BRAFi failure.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small cohort, low statistical power for rare events: 66 patients is too few to define associations for genes that are infrequently altered in melanoma.
  • Standard of care has shifted: since accrual, BRAFi + MEKi combinations are now standard, and PTEN’s predictive value in the combination setting is not well characterized here (only 11 patients received the combination, with no formal response-grade analysis powered against PTEN status).
  • Targeted panel misses non-coding and epigenetic events: the >250-gene capture panel cannot detect PTEN expression changes from epigenetic silencing, structural variants outside captured intervals, or alterations in genes not on the panel.
  • Pretreatment sampling limitations: variability between sample collection and BRAFi initiation; single-site sampling cannot capture intratumoral or lesion-to-lesion heterogeneity. The authors are exploring cell-free DNA / plasma profiling as a follow-up modality.
  • PTEN-alteration heterogeneity in responders: because two excellent and three intermediate responders also harbored PTEN deep deletions or recurrent missense variants, PTEN loss is not a deterministic predictor — additional cooperating events must contribute to intrinsic resistance.
  • Open question — allelic imbalance as a predictive marker: does selective gain of mutant BRAF / loss of wild-type BRAF prospectively identify patients who will derive complete and durable responses? Whole-genome sequencing or zygosity-resolved assays are needed to validate.
