Comprehensive molecular profiling of intra- and extrahepatic cholangiocarcinomas: potential targets for intervention

TL;DR

Lowery et al. prospectively profiled tumors from 195 patients with cholangiocarcinoma (158 intrahepatic, 37 extrahepatic) at Memorial Sloan Kettering using the MSK-IMPACT targeted NGS assay covering 341 or 410 cancer-associated genes. The most commonly altered genes in intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma were IDH1 (30%), ARID1A (23%), BAP1 (20%), TP53 (20%), and FGFR2 gene fusions (14%), while KRAS, SMAD4, TP53, and STK11 were enriched in extrahepatic disease. CDKN2A/CDKN2B and ERBB2 alterations were associated with shorter overall survival and shorter time to progression on first-line chemotherapy. Potentially actionable alterations were identified in 47% of patients, and 16% of patients with advanced disease received biomarker-matched therapy.

Cohort & data

  • n = 195 patients with cholangiocarcinoma; 91% sequencing success rate (195/214 attempted).
  • Anatomic split: 158 (81%) intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (IHCH) and 37 (19%) extrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (EHCH); overall cholangiocarcinoma (CHOL) cohort.
  • Dataset: chol_msk_2018 — prospectively consented under protocol NCT01775072 at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center over a two-year period starting July 2014.
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT targeted DNA sequencing — 341-gene panel (IMPACT341, n=20) or 410-gene panel (IMPACT410, n=318 samples); matched germline DNA from blood used for somatic calling. Median sample coverage 759X. Standard DNA input 250 ng (50 ng minimum).
  • Sample types: 141 (72%) primary tumor biopsy/resection; 54 (27%) biopsy of metastatic site. Archival FFPE tissue only.
  • Demographics: 51.8% male; median age 62 (range 24–86); 89% Caucasian; 12% (n=24) with hepatitis B.
  • Outcome cohort: 158 patients (81%) received first-line chemotherapy for advanced disease (127 patients / 80% on gemcitabine/platinum). Median time to progression on first-line chemotherapy was 8.8 months. Survival analyzed in the 178 stage IV patients (either at diagnosis or after surgical recurrence).
  • Actionability classification: OncoKB levels 1–4.

Key findings

  • Mutation frequencies (whole cohort, n=195): IDH1 25%, TP53 24%, ARID1A 21%, BAP1 15%, KRAS 13%, PBRM1 12%, SMAD4 9%, ATM 8% PMID:29848569.
  • Focal copy-number alterations: CDKN2A deletions 8%, MDM2 amplifications 4%, ERBB2 amplifications 4%, MCL1 amplifications 4%.
  • Structural variants: 38 structural alterations in 35 patients (18%); majority were in-frame fusions predicted to activate FGFR2. Most frequent FGFR2 fusion partners were BICC1 and KIAA1217. No NTRK1/NTRK3 or ROS1 fusions were observed.
  • IHC vs EHC enrichment (Fisher’s exact, q-values reported):
    • Enriched in IHC: IDH1 46/158 vs 2/37 (OR 7.14, p=0.001, q=0.011); BAP1 30 vs 0 (q=0.013); FGFR2 20 vs 0 (q=0.087). BAP1 mutations and FGFR2 fusions were observed exclusively in IHC.
    • Enriched in EHC: KRAS 11 vs 14 (OR 0.125, q<0.001); SMAD4 8 vs 11 (q=0.001); TP53 28 vs 18 (q=0.007); STK11 1 vs 4 (q=0.031).
  • Mutual exclusivity (OR < 0.5): IDH1:TP53 (p=0.001), IDH1:SMAD4 (p=0.004), TP53:BAP1 (p=0.004), BAP1:KRAS (p=0.017), IDH1:FGFR2 (p=0.030), IDH1:KRAS (p=0.046), TP53:FGFR2 (p=0.049), PBRM1:KRAS (p=0.049).
  • Co-occurrence tendencies: TP53:CDKN2A, SMAD4:KRAS, and TP53:CDKN2B.
  • MSI: A single tumor (0.5%) was MSI-H (MSIsensor score 35.1) with 48 somatic mutations and loss of MLH1 and MSH6 protein on IHC. Patient had a history of choledochal cyst; no Lynch-suggestive family history; did not receive immunotherapy.
  • Primary vs metastatic: No genetic alterations significantly differed in frequency between 141 primary and 54 metastatic samples.
  • Chemo PFS associations (158 first-line chemo patients): Shorter time to progression for CDKN2A/CDKN2B alterations (n=15, p=0.002), ERBB2 (n=8, p=0.028), and MDM2 (n=7, p=0.026). No significant PFS difference for IDH1, FGFR2, BAP1, ATM, ARID1A, or TP53.
  • Overall survival (178 stage IV patients): Shorter OS for CDKN2A/CDKN2B (n=18, p=0.0015), ERBB2 (n=9, p=0.0015), and KRAS (n=21, p=0.026).
  • Actionability: 93/195 patients (47.6%) had at least one alteration classified as OncoKB level 3B or higher. No patients had a level 1 or 2A alteration (no standard-of-care targeted agents exist for cholangiocarcinoma). Sixteen patients (8%) had a level 2B alteration (ERBB2 amplification ×6; TSC1/TSC2 likely pathogenic ×3; BRCA1/BRCA2 ×2; BRAF V600E ×1; MET amplification ×1). 77 patients (39%) had a level 3 alteration as their highest, predominantly IDH1 mutations (43 patients) and FGFR2 fusions (17 patients), with additional PIK3CA (n=6), NRAS (n=4), and ERBB2 hotspot mutations (n=2).

Genes & alterations

  • IDH1 — most common alteration in IHC (30%); mutually exclusive with TP53, SMAD4, FGFR2, and KRAS. Drove 43 patients’ OncoKB level 3 classification; rationale for ivosidenib (AG-120) clinical trial enrollment.
  • FGFR2 — gene fusions in 14% of IHC, observed exclusively in intrahepatic cases. Multiple fusion partners with wide breakpoint variation; most frequent partners were BICC1 and KIAA1217. Targeted by infigratinib (BGJ398) and other pan-FGFR inhibitors.
  • CDKN2A / CDKN2B — focal deletions in 8% overall; associated with significantly shorter OS (p=0.0015) and shorter PFS on first-line chemo (p=0.002). Negative prognostic biomarker.
  • ERBB2 — amplification in 4% (HER2 directed therapy rationale); shorter OS (p=0.0015) and PFS (p=0.028). Six patients had ERBB2 amplification as a level 2B alteration; two additional patients had ERBB2 hotspot mutations.
  • KRAS — enriched in EHC (38% vs 7% in IHC); associated with shorter OS in advanced disease (p=0.026); mutually exclusive with IDH1, BAP1, and PBRM1.
  • BAP1 — mutated only in IHC (30/158); mutually exclusive with TP53, KRAS, and SMAD4.
  • TP53 — enriched in EHC; mutually exclusive with IDH1, BAP1, and FGFR2; co-occurs with CDKN2A and CDKN2B.
  • SMAD4 — enriched in EHC; co-occurs with KRAS; mutually exclusive with IDH1 and BAP1.
  • MDM2 — amplification 4%; shorter PFS on first-line chemo (p=0.026).
  • ARID1A, PBRM1, ATM — common but not significantly prognostic.
  • BRAF V600E (n=1), MET amplification (n=1), TSC1/TSC2 (n=3), BRCA1/BRCA2 (n=2) — rare level 2B actionable findings.
  • PIK3CA (n=6), NRAS (n=4) — low-frequency level 3 actionable hotspot mutations.
  • MLH1 / MSH6 — loss in the single MSI-H tumor; rationale for pembrolizumab eligibility post-FDA approval, though this patient died before approval.

Clinical implications

  • Prognostic biomarkers: Alterations in CDKN2A/CDKN2B and ERBB2 are negative prognostic markers in advanced cholangiocarcinoma — significantly shorter overall survival (p=0.0015 for both) and shorter time to progression on first-line chemotherapy.
  • Biomarker-directed therapy: 25 patients (16% of those with advanced disease) received matched therapy based on profiling — 13 ivosidenib/IDH1 inhibitor, 6 FGFR inhibitors (e.g., infigratinib), 2 HER2-directed therapy, 1 EZH2 inhibitor, 1 ERK inhibitor, and 1 sorafenib. Sixteen of 25 (64%) had evidence of response or clinical benefit.
  • Trial eligibility: Demonstrates feasibility of prospective NGS for enrolling cholangiocarcinoma patients onto biomarker-selected trials (e.g., the phase III ClarIDHy trial of AG-120/ivosidenib in IDH1-mutant cholangiocarcinoma).
  • Anatomic stratification supported by molecular signatures: IHC and EHC are biologically distinct — FGFR2 fusions and BAP1 mutations are exclusive to IHC; KRAS, SMAD4, STK11, and TP53 are enriched in EHC.
  • MSI screening: MMR deficiency in advanced cholangiocarcinoma is rare (0.5% in this cohort), lower than prior retrospective reports, but MSIsensor on clinical NGS can identify the rare patient potentially eligible for pembrolizumab under the tissue-agnostic indication.
  • First-line chemotherapy: Gemcitabine/platinum remains standard (80% of first-line regimens here); alternative regimens used included FOLFOX, capecitabine, and gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel.

Limitations & open questions

  • Archival FFPE tissue was used in all cases; molecular state at the time of treatment may have diverged from the sequenced sample (though no primary vs metastatic differences were found).
  • Targeted NGS (341/410 genes) cannot detect actionable alterations outside the panel — whole exome/genome or transcriptome sequencing might find additional gene signatures, but the authors note this would not be feasible at scale given cost, FFPE limitations, and turnaround time constraints in advanced disease.
  • Definitive prognostic conclusions are limited by clinical and molecular heterogeneity of the cohort.
  • Survival analysis was calculated from date of diagnosis with advanced/metastatic disease (some patients had prior surgical resection), which differs from prior studies (e.g., Javle et al.) and may impact comparability.
  • NTRK1/NTRK3 and ROS1 fusions, previously reported in cholangiocarcinoma, were not observed in this cohort.
  • Germline pathogenic variants were not analyzed despite paired germline sequencing being performed.
  • Validation of CDKN2A/B and ERBB2 prognostic signal in an independent prospective cohort is needed.
  • Open question: do the clinical phenotypes of common targetable alterations vary based on co-mutation patterns? The authors flag this as an important consideration for trial design.
