TL;DR

Prospective tumor and matched-normal sequencing of 197 tumors from 189 patients with advanced endometrial cancer at Memorial Sloan Kettering using the MSK-IMPACT panel (341 or 410 genes). The cohort was enriched for high-grade and metastatic disease relative to TCGA, yet histology- and grade-matched genomic profiles were broadly similar. Genomic MSIsensor and mutational-signature decomposition recovered microsatellite instability with high concordance to IHC and even rescued one MMR-proficient case with occult deficiency. Hierarchical clustering of FACETS-derived copy-number profiles uncovered a previously underappreciated heterozygous-loss cluster (Cluster C) with significantly worse PFS1 (median 9.6 vs. 17.0 and 17.4 months, p=0.006). 67% of patients carried at least one potentially actionable alteration; 27% of those were enrolled to matched trials, of whom 47% achieved clinical benefit.

Cohort & data

  • 189 patients / 197 tumors with advanced endometrial cancer profiled by MSK-IMPACT (n=70 on the 341-gene IMPACT341 panel; n=127 on the 410-gene IMPACT410 panel), average ~735× coverage PMID:30068706.
  • Cancer types: endometrioid carcinoma (FIGO grades 1–3; 75/189), serous carcinoma (46/189), carcinosarcoma (35/189), mixed (15), clear cell (13), undifferentiated/dedifferentiated (4), neuroendocrine (1) — all grouped under endometrial carcinoma (UCEC).
  • Disease state: 95% advanced disease (FIGO III–IV at diagnosis or at recurrence); 51% tumor samples from metastatic sites; 39% sequenced after chemotherapy and 11% after hormonal therapy.
  • Dataset: ucec_msk_2018 on cBioPortal; compared throughout to the primary-tumor ucec_tcga cohort.
  • Assays/analytical methods: FACETS v0.5.6 (allele-specific CN, cval=100); MSIsensor v0.2 (MSI scoring, MSI-H ≥10); mutational signature decomposition for hypermutated samples; OncoKB for actionability (annotated 22-Dec-2016); germline calling over 76 cancer-predisposition genes in the MSK-IMPACT panel; MMR IHC for MLH1/PMS2/MSH2/MSH6.

Key findings

  • Cohort vs TCGA outcomes: MSK cohort median PFS1 15.3 months vs. TCGA median not reached at 23-month follow-up. Restricted to the 19% of TCGA patients who recurred, the survival profiles were comparable (median PFS1 13.7 vs. 15.3 months, p=0.1) PMID:30068706.
  • Grade matters within endometrioid: high-grade (FIGO grade 3) endometrioid carcinoma had worse median PFS1 than low-grade (12.7 vs. 21.6 months, p=0.005).
  • Pathway alterations: PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway altered in 70% of patients; RTK/RAS/β-catenin pathway in 65% — consistent with prior endometrial profiling.
  • TP53 vs PTEN cohort skew: TP53 mutations more common in MSK cohort vs TCGA (63% vs. 36%, p=4×10⁻⁵); PTEN alterations more common in TCGA (56% vs. 22%, p=8×10⁻⁹) — attributable to histologic composition.
  • High-grade enrichment: TP53 mutations significantly enriched in high-grade endometrioid (29% vs. 3%, p=0.013); PIK3CA mutations enriched in high-grade tumors overall (p=0.035) and most prevalent in mixed histology.
  • Histology-specific amplifications: FGFR1 amplification enriched in carcinosarcoma (p=0.007); ERBB2 amplification enriched in serous tumors (p=0.018).
  • Mutational burden: median 5.7 mutations/Mb (range 0–419.8). 15.9% (30/189) had ≥10 SNVs enabling signature decomposition. Two POLE exonuclease-domain hotspot tumors were ultramutators (V411L at 419.8 mut/Mb; P286R at 152.8 mut/Mb).
  • MMR concordance: 24/189 (13%) tumors were MMR-D by IHC (vs. 39% in TCGA, p=5×10⁻¹¹); median TMB 31.6 mut/Mb in MMR-D (excluding the POLE-hotspot case) vs. 4.7 mut/Mb in MMR-proficient (p=1.9×10⁻¹⁴). 29/30 MMR-D/presumed-MMR-D tumors had high MSIsensor scores; the discordant case was a POLE-hotspot ultramutator that was MSS by MSIsensor.
  • IHC misses one occult MMR-D case: one MMR-proficient-by-IHC tumor showed high TMB and high MSIsensor, suggesting genomic methods can rescue cases standard IHC misses.
  • Novel copy-number cluster: hierarchical clustering of FACETS profiles produced three clusters. Cluster A: TP53-mutant, whole-genome doubled. Cluster B: PTEN-mutant, low-grade endometrioid, gains of 1q/8/10 (TCGA-like clusters 2–3). Cluster C: heterozygous losses across the genome, high-grade endometrioid and non-endometrioid — significantly worse PFS1 (median 9.6 vs. 17.4 and 17.0 months for A and B; p=0.006).
  • Germline findings: three pathogenic germline BRCA2 mutations (one serous, two carcinosarcomas; two carrying the c.5946delT/6174delT founder allele, one with c.5799_5802delCCAA frameshift); one Lynch-syndrome germline MLH1 splice mutation (c.1731+1G>T) with biallelic somatic CN-LOH and MSIsensor=19.19.
  • ESR1 de novo mutations: five endometrioid tumors carried ESR1 ligand-binding-domain hotspot mutations; only 2/5 were from samples obtained after endocrine therapy, suggesting de novo emergence in endometrial cancer (not previously reported in breast).
  • Primary vs metastatic: no global differences except PTEN mutations more common in primary than metastatic endometrioid carcinomas (67% [16/24] vs. 34% [10/29], p=0.03). In 4 paired primary–metastatic cases, MTOR L2427Q and PIK3R1 E468* hotspots showed heterogeneity.
  • Actionability and matching: 67% (127/189) carried ≥1 potentially actionable alteration. Most common: PIK3CA mutation (35%, 66/189), PTEN mutation (29%, 54/189), MSI-H (16%, 30/189), ERBB2 amplification (16%, 16/189). 27% (34/127) of actionable patients enrolled to matched trials; 47% (16/34) achieved clinical benefit. Trial inhibitor classes: PI3K (n=12), dual PI3K/mTOR (n=6), ERBB2 (n=6), AKT (n=4), mTOR (n=2), FGFR (n=2).
  • Hypermutation source attribution: of high-TMB samples, n=28 attributed to MSI, n=1 to POLE, n=1 to MSI+POLE — no other MSS high-TMB cases identified.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — mutation in 63% of MSK cohort vs. 36% of TCGA (p=4×10⁻⁵); enriched in high-grade endometrioid (29% vs. 3%, p=0.013); defines copy-number Cluster A together with WGD PMID:30068706.
  • PTEN — altered in 22% (MSK) vs. 56% (TCGA, p=8×10⁻⁹); more common in primary than metastatic endometrioid (67% vs. 34%, p=0.03); 29% somatic mutation rate cohort-wide; defines Cluster B alongside endometrioid histology.
  • PIK3CA — most common actionable alteration (35%, 66/189); enriched in high-grade (p=0.035) and mixed histology; n=12 patients matched to PI3K-pathway trials.
  • POLE — two exonuclease-domain hotspot mutations (V411L and P286R) drove ultramutator phenotypes (419.8 and 152.8 mut/Mb); seven additional non-hotspot POLE variants of uncertain significance lacked ultramutation; neither hotspot case received an ICI and neither recurred at 2–2.5 yrs.
  • ERBB2 — amplification in 16/189 (16% of actionable subset); enriched in serous carcinoma (p=0.018); one heavily pretreated serous patient achieved a 14-month ongoing complete response to ado-trastuzumab-emtansine (T-DM1).
  • FGFR1 — amplification enriched in carcinosarcoma (p=0.007).
  • FGFR2 / FGFR3 — FGFR2/3 fusions noted as therapeutically relevant; n=2 patients enrolled to FGFR-inhibitor trials.
  • ESR1 — ligand-binding-domain hotspot mutations in 5 endometrioid patients; 3/5 arose in apparent absence of endocrine therapy (de novo). One de novo case progressed on letrozole then had ongoing 18-month response on bevacizumab + fulvestrant.
  • MTOR — hotspot L2427Q showed primary-metastatic heterogeneity.
  • PIK3R1 — hotspot E468* showed primary-metastatic heterogeneity.
  • BRCA2 — three pathogenic germline truncating mutations (one serous, two carcinosarcomas); founder c.5946delT in two patients; c.5799_5802delCCAA frameshift in the third. None of the germline-BRCA1/2 patients were enrolled to PARP inhibitor trials.
  • BRCA1 — present in the actionable-alteration tally (n=1 patient at OncoKB level).
  • MLH1 — Lynch-associated germline splice mutation c.1731+1G>T with biallelic CN-LOH, MSIsensor=19.19; also assayed by MMR IHC.
  • MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 — components of the standard MMR IHC panel used to call MMR-D status.
  • BRAF, CDK4, KIT, MAP2K1 — each n=1 OncoKB-level-annotated actionable alterations contributing to the 67% actionable tally.
  • AKT1 — pathway target of n=4 patients enrolled to AKT-inhibitor trials.

Clinical implications

  • Genomic MSI calling complements IHC. Combining MSIsensor with mutational-signature decomposition recovered an MMR-D case that was scored MMR-proficient by IHC, suggesting prospective NGS can refine eligibility for pembrolizumab in MSI-high tumors (5/30 MSI-H patients had received ICIs prior to FDA approval; 2/5 had clinical benefit) PMID:30068706.
  • Matched targeted therapy is feasible but attrits sharply. Of 189 sequenced patients, 67% had actionable alterations, but only 27% of those (18% of the full cohort) reached a matched trial; 47% of matched patients (8.5% of all sequenced) achieved clinical benefit. Barriers: lack of matching trial, standard-of-care response, alternative unmatched immunotherapy options, clinical deterioration, patient choice.
  • ERBB2-amplified serous endometrial cancer responds to T-DM1. One heavily pretreated serous patient achieved an ongoing 14-month complete response to ado-trastuzumab-emtansine, supporting ERBB2-targeted therapy in this histology.
  • Prognostic copy-number Cluster C. Heterozygous-loss-dominant copy-number profile defines a subset with PFS1 ≈ 9.6 months, worse than TP53-mutant/WGD Cluster A — relevant for risk stratification and clinical-trial design.
  • Germline screening matters even off-histology. Three pathogenic BRCA2 carriers (not historically endometrial-associated) and a Lynch MLH1 carrier were identified via universal tumor/normal sequencing — supporting unbiased germline analysis irrespective of phenotype.
  • Hypermutated endometrial cancer is essentially MSI or POLE. No other MSS-high-TMB mechanism emerged, simplifying the mechanistic interpretation of TMB as an ICI biomarker in this disease.
  • Panel-size trade-off: authors note 30–50 genes likely covers the currently “actionable genome”, though large panels facilitate simultaneous MSI/germline assessment.

Limitations & open questions

  • Sample size and retrospective design. Cluster-C prognostic signal needs validation in an additional advanced-endometrial cohort. Re-analysis of TCGA Cluster 4 identified Cluster-A-like and Cluster-C-like subgroups, but heavy censoring (21 progression events / 81 patients) prevented robust outcome comparison.
  • Single-center bias. Trial matching rates are dependent on institutional study portfolio; results may not generalize to community practice.
  • MSI-indeterminate calls in low-purity samples. Two MMR-D-by-IHC tumors were MSI-indeterminate on MSIsensor due to low tumor purity (samples with purity <20% excluded from CN-focused analyses, retaining 154/189).
  • Modest matched-therapy yield. 18% of all sequenced patients enrolled to matched-targeted therapy and 8.5% of all sequenced patients benefited — substantial room to improve trial portfolio breadth.
  • De novo ESR1 mutations in endometrial cancer (no prior endocrine therapy exposure) are reported here for the first time; mechanism (selection by what? estrogen exposure from obesity?) is unresolved — patients’ BMIs were similar to acquired-mutation cases (median 22.9 vs 24.7).
  • No germline-BRCA1/2 patients enrolled to PARP-inhibitor trials, leaving the question of PARP-inhibitor benefit in germline-mutant advanced endometrial cancer open.
  • Anonymization tradeoff: irreversible anonymization required for germline analysis prevented further clinical description of germline-positive cases beyond histology and copy-number summaries.
