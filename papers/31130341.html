Genomic and Transcriptomic Profiling of Combined Hepatocellular and Intrahepatic Cholangiocarcinoma Reveals Distinct Molecular Subtypes

Lu Chen

Chong Zhang

Masashi Fujita

Ruoyan Li

PMID: 31130341 · DOI: 10.1016/j.ccell.2019.04.007 · Journal: Cancer Cell (2019)

TL;DR

Xue et al. performed a pan-Asia, multi-center integrative genomic and transcriptomic study of 133 combined hepatocellular and intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (cHCC-ICC) cases — a rare primary liver cancer (PLC) showing both hepatocellular and biliary differentiation — using WES (173 tumor samples, 121 cases), WGS (41 tumor samples, 37 cases), RNA-seq (97 tumor samples, 77 cases), and single-nucleus sequencing (SNS) of one mixed-type case. They classify cHCC-ICC by the Allen-Lisa criteria into separate, combined, and mixed subtypes, show that combined-type cHCC-ICC is molecularly ICC-like and mixed-type cHCC-ICC is HCC-like (Hoshida-S2), demonstrate that combined- and mixed-type tumors are monoclonal while separate-type tumors can be either mono- or multiclonal, and identify Nestin (NES) as a diagnostic and prognostic biomarker for cHCC-ICC.

Cohort & data

  • 133 cHCC-ICC cases from a pan-Asia multi-center study (8 hospitals across China, Singapore, and Japan); cohort published in cBioPortal as hccihch_pku_2019.
  • Pathologic subtypes (per Allen & Lisa 1949 criteria, 121 classified cases): 6 separate (Sep), 56 combined (Com), 59 mixed (Mix).
  • Etiology: 100 HBV+, 7 HCV+, 1 double-positive, 25 double-negative.
  • Assays: whole-exome-seq (173 tumors / 121 cases, mean depth 108× tumor / 101× normal), whole-genome-seq (41 tumors / 37 cases), rna-seq (97 tumors / 77 cases), single-cell-dna-seq on case Mix_19 (74 tumor + 11 normal nuclei).
  • Diagnostic validation: immunohistochemistry for Nestin on 128 cHCC-ICC, 99 newly recruited HCC, and 86 newly recruited ICC cases.
  • Tissue source: laser capture microdissection (LCM) used to separate HCC and ICC components in 3 separate-type and 41 combined-type cases.
  • Cancer types: MIXED (cHCC-ICC index group); reference cohorts of HCC and IHCH drawn from TCGA-LIHC (lihc_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018), RIKEN-LIHC (lihc_riken), and ICGC cholangiocarcinoma (chol_icgc_2017).

Key findings

  • Significantly mutated genes (q < 0.1, dNdScv): TP53, AXIN1, RB1, PTEN, ARID2, and BRD7. MutSigCV identified TP53 as the only significantly mutated gene at FDR < 0.1.
  • TP53 mutation rate 49.2% in cHCC-ICC, significantly higher than TCGA-HCC (31%, p < 0.001), ICGC-ICC (22%, p < 0.0001), China-HCC (35%, p < 0.05); not significantly different from China-ICC (38%, p = 0.207).
  • TERT promoter C228T mutation in 22.9% of cases — lower than TCGA-HCC (46%, p < 0.001) and absent in ICGC-ICC.
  • Epigenetic dysregulation: KMT2D, IDH1, BAP1, EZH2 mutated in 35.6% of cases (histone modification / DNA methylation). SWI/SNF complex (ARID1A, ARID2, PBRM1, BRD7) mutated in 19.5%.
  • Cellular energetics disruption: KEAP1, IDH1, APOB, ALB mutated in 21.2% of cases.
  • AXIN1 enrichment in mixed type: 20% in Mix vs 2% in Com (p < 0.001); AXIN1 expression in combined-type cHCC-ICC significantly higher than in mixed-type (p < 0.05, Student’s t-test).
  • Strikingly low CTNNB1 (6%) and KRAS (0%) mutation frequencies vs HCC and ICC reference cohorts respectively, identified as a unique feature of cHCC-ICC not explained by etiology or ethnicity.
  • HBV integrations (133 cases): TERT and KMT2B most recurrent. Promoter integrations in LANCL2, PLG, MDGA2, PTPRM correlated with elevated expression.
  • Noncoding & fusion: NEAT1 mutated in 29.7% of cases (most frequent intergenic ncRNA); PTMS-AP1G1 fusion in 11.7%; FGFR-related fusions in 6.5%.
  • Focal CNAs: amplifications/gains in MYC (73%), MET (24%), CCNE1 (24%), CDK6 (20%), TERT (19%); deletions/losses in TP53 (46%), CDKN2A (37%), RB1 (26%), AXIN1 (25%).
  • Mutational signatures (sigprofiler, mutational-signatures): three de novo signatures match COSMIC 22 (aristolochic acid, 63.5%), COSMIC 5 (clock-like), and COSMIC 24 (aflatoxin B1, 38.8%). TP53 R249S — an AFB1-induced HCC marker — accounted for 25.8% of TP53 mutations, all in HBV+ patients with signature 24.
  • Transcriptomic clusters (PLC, n = 367): P1 (n = 103, biliary/ICC-like, enriched for combined-type cHCC-ICC and KRAS/IDH1 mutations), P2 (n = 99, Hoshida-S2 HCC-like, enriched for mixed-type cHCC-ICC, TP53, high AFP), P3 (n = 65, Hoshida-S1, TGF-β-activated), P4 (n = 100, Hoshida-S3, CTNNB1-enriched, well-differentiated HCC).
  • Combined-type cHCC-ICCs are enriched in P1 (p < 0.001); mixed-type cHCC-ICCs are enriched in P2 (p < 0.001). EMT is the top GSEA-enriched pathway in P1; xenobiotic and bile acid metabolism in P2.
  • Survival: P1 and P2 patients have significantly poorer prognosis than P3 and P4 (p < 0.0001, log-rank).
  • Immune microenvironment: three immune clusters (High, Medium, Low) using CTLA4, PDCD1, CD274, etc. — High immune is enriched in mixed-type/P2; Medium in combined-type/P1.
  • Clonal origin (separate type): 4/6 separate-type cases (Sep_01/03/05/06) shared 46–67% of mutations → monoclonal origin with branched evolution. Sep_02 and Sep_04 shared no mutations → independent (multiclonal) origins. Sep_03 CCF analysis suggests an HCC-component subclone subsequently transdifferentiated into the ICC phenotype.
  • Clonal origin (combined and mixed): all combined-type H-I pairs (Com cases) are monoclonal; shared mutation fraction ranged 27–95% (median 74%). Mixed-type Mix_19 SNS (74 tumor nuclei) recapitulates one founding clone and two subclones consistent with pyclone bulk CCF. All mixed-type cases harbored clonal mutations consistent with monoclonal origin.
  • Nestin (NES) as biomarker: RNA expression in cHCC-ICC significantly higher than HCC (p < 0.01), not different from ICC (p = 0.07). 104/128 (81.3%) of cHCC-ICC cases IHC Nestin-positive vs HCC and ICC (p < 0.001 for both). Median OS Nestin-positive 18.7 mo vs Nestin-negative 46.6 mo across all PLC types (p < 0.0001, log-rank).

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — mutated 49.2% (significantly enriched vs HCC and ICC); R249S AFB1-signature hotspot in 25.8% of TP53-mutant cases (all HBV+). Focal deletion/loss in 46%. Trunk driver in phylogenetic trees.
  • AXIN1 — significantly enriched in mixed type (20% Mix vs 2% Com, p < 0.001). Loss/deletion in 25%.
  • RB1 — driver gene (q < 0.1); focal loss in 26%; trunk mutation in some separate-type cases.
  • PTEN, ARID2, BRD7 — significantly mutated drivers (dNdScv q < 0.1).
  • TERT — promoter C228T hotspot in 22.9% of cases; most recurrent HBV-integration site; gain/amplification in 19%.
  • KMT2D, IDH1, BAP1, EZH2 — histone modification / DNA methylation pathway, jointly mutated in 35.6%.
  • ARID1A, ARID2, PBRM1, BRD7 — SWI/SNF complex, jointly mutated in 19.5%.
  • KEAP1, APOB, ALB — cellular energetics group, jointly mutated (with IDH1) in 21.2%.
  • CTNNB1 — paradoxically low (6%) in cHCC-ICC vs HCC reference cohorts; enriched in P4 cluster.
  • KRAS — zero mutations in cHCC-ICC; enriched in P1 cluster transcriptionally.
  • KMT2B — second most recurrent HBV integration target.
  • NEAT1 — most frequently mutated intergenic noncoding RNA (29.7%).
  • MYC, MET, CCNE1, CDK6 — focal amplifications in 73%, 24%, 24%, 20% respectively.
  • CDKN2A — focal loss in 37%.
  • FGFR2 — FGFR-related fusion events in 6.5% of cases.
  • NES (Nestin) — proposed cHCC-ICC diagnostic and pan-PLC prognostic biomarker; positively stained in 81.3% of cHCC-ICC vs significantly lower rates in HCC/ICC.
  • EPCAM, KRT19, PRDM5 — biliary markers elevated in P1 (combined-type cHCC-ICC).
  • GPC3, APOE, ALB, TBX3, SALL4 — hepatocyte markers elevated in P2/P4 (HCC-like clusters).
  • RPS6KA3, SYNE1, ADGRV1 — recurrently mutated in cHCC-ICC; ECM / cell adhesion programs.

Clinical implications

  • Allen-Lisa pathologic subtyping has molecular and likely therapeutic weight. Combined-type cHCC-ICC is ICC-like and may benefit from ICC-directed therapies; mixed-type cHCC-ICC is HCC-like (Hoshida-S2) and may benefit from HCC-directed therapies.
  • Nestin (NES) IHC is proposed as a diagnostic biomarker for cHCC-ICC, outperforming EpCAM because Nestin is positive in both HCC and ICC components of combined-type tumors (EpCAM is only positive in the ICC component). It is also a pan-PLC prognostic marker (median OS 18.7 mo positive vs 46.6 mo negative, p < 0.0001).
  • Differential immune microenvironments between subtypes (mixed-type/P2 enriched for High immune cluster) suggest combined- and mixed-type cHCC-ICCs may respond differently to immune checkpoint therapies targeting PDCD1, CD274, CTLA4.
  • FGFR-related fusions in 6.5% of cHCC-ICCs may identify a subset targetable by FGFR inhibitors (this paper does not test the hypothesis directly).
  • Mutational-signature etiology — aristolochic acid (signature 22, 63.5%) and aflatoxin B1 (signature 24, 38.8%) — implicate preventable exposures in Chinese cHCC-ICC; TP53 R249S + signature 24 is a marker for AFB1-driven HBV+ disease.

Limitations & open questions

  • Subtype imbalance: only 6 separate-type cases — too few for genomic subtype comparison; separate type excluded from many quantitative comparisons.
  • Reference-cohort heterogeneity: comparisons to TCGA-HCC, ICGC-ICC, China-HCC, and China-ICC pool studies with differing assays, depths, and ethnic compositions; intermediate TERT-promoter mutation rate (22.9%) in cHCC-ICC explicitly flagged for validation in larger Chinese HCC/ICC cohorts.
  • Geographic / etiologic bias: 75% HBV+ pan-Asia cohort; generalizability to HCV-driven or non-viral cHCC-ICC in Western populations is untested.
  • Order of phenotypic transition vs metastasis in separate-type monoclonal cases (e.g., Sep_01, Sep_03) is unresolved — whether transdifferentiation precedes or follows dissemination is left as a future-studies item.
  • Mechanism of Nestin upregulation: the paper notes the TP53–NES repression axis from mouse models (Tschaharganeh et al., 2014) but does not directly demonstrate it in cHCC-ICC.
  • Therapeutic claims are inferential: the recommendation to apply ICC therapies to combined-type and HCC therapies to mixed-type cHCC-ICC is based on molecular similarity, not on prospective trial evidence.
  • Single-cell coverage is limited — SNS performed on one mixed-type case (Mix_19, 74 tumor nuclei); other mixed-type cases analyzed only by bulk CCF inference.
