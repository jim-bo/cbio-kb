TL;DR

The CPTAC consortium performed the first proteogenomic study of a prospectively collected human colon adenocarcinoma cohort: 110 patients with paired tumor and normal-adjacent tissue (NAT) profiled by whole-exome sequencing, copy-number array, RNA-Seq, miRNA-Seq, label-free shotgun proteomics, and TMT-based global and phosphoproteomics (coad_cptac_2019). Proteogenomic integration corrected genomic inferences (re-classifying SOX9 as oncogenic despite truncating mutations), revealed RB1 hyperphosphorylation as a colon-cancer driver suggesting CDK2 inhibition as a therapy, identified 88 putative HLA-binding neoantigens and three recurrent cancer/testis antigens (IGF2BP3, SPAG1, ATAD2), and tied increased glycolysis to decreased CD8 T-cell infiltration in MSI-H tumors — proposing glycolysis inhibition as a strategy to overcome checkpoint-blockade resistance in microsatellite-unstable disease.

Cohort & data

  • Patients: 110 prospectively collected colon cancer patients (65 female, 45 male; mean age 65, range 40–93). Inclusion criterion: newly diagnosed, untreated patients undergoing primary surgery for colon adenocarcinoma. Rectal cancers excluded (untreated rectal tumors are hard to obtain).
  • Cancer type: COAD — colon adenocarcinoma only (the study deliberately excluded rectal tumors).
  • Dataset: coad_cptac_2019 — CPTAC-2 colon cohort. Raw genomics in SRA BioProject PRJNA514017; raw proteomics in CPTAC Data Portal S045; processed matrices in LinkedOmics (http://linkedomics.org/cptac-colon/).
  • Platforms applied per omics:
    • 106 tumor/normal-blood pairs profiled by whole-exome sequencing (Illumina Nextera Rapid Capture Exome, HiSeq4000, ≥150× target coverage).
    • 105 tumor/normal pairs with sufficient off-target reads used for copy-number inference (CopywriteR + GISTIC2).
    • 106 tumors with RNA-Seq; also miRNA-Seq.
    • Label-free shotgun proteomics on 100 tumors plus 96 tumor/NAT pairs profiled by TMT global proteomics and TMT phosphoproteomics.
    • Selected Reaction Monitoring (SRM) for targeted validation of CD8A, SLC2A3, and PKM isoforms.
    • PCR-based MSI testing (Promega MSI Analysis System; 5 mononucleotide + 2 pentanucleotide markers; see msi-pcr-pentaplex) on 85 samples — 100% concordance with WXS-based MSI calls.
  • Cross-cohort comparison: Average mRNA profiles correlated with the TCGA colorectal cohort at Pearson’s r = 0.92; average label-free protein profiles at r = 0.96 — both higher than between different cancer types or between colon tumors and cell lines.

Key findings

  • Mutation landscape: WXS identified 64,010 somatic SNVs, 7,691 somatic INDELs, and 6,186 somatic microsatellite INDELs across 106 tumors — 56,592 unique protein-altering events. The MS INDEL distribution was bimodal, partitioning the cohort into 24 MSI-H tumors and 82 MSS tumors. MSI-H tumors had increased A>G/T>C transitions and decreased G>C/C>G transversions and were enriched for mismatch-repair-pathway, POLE, and BRAF mutations.
  • Significantly mutated genes: Eight in the non-hypermutated group (all previously reported by TCGA); nine in the hypermutated group, six of which were not reported in TCGA. Four newly identified genes — CASP5, RNF43, LTN1, and BMPR2 — were mutated in >50% of hypermutated samples.
  • Proteomic readout of mutations: Tumors with stop-gain/frameshift APC mutations had reduced abundance of phosphosite APC-T2451 downstream of the truncation. TGFBR2 frameshift mutations reduced TGFBR2-S553 phosphorylation. TP53-S315 phosphorylation was increased in tumors over matched NATs (p=0.001, paired t-test); three tumors with the R273 hotspot mutation had >10-fold increases — corroborated by reanalyzing CPTAC breast and ovarian cancers where R273-mutant tumors had 3.4–83× TP53-S315 phosphorylation above cohort medians.
  • SOX9 re-classification: Mutated in 19 tumors (6 stop-gain, 8 frameshift, 5 nonsynonymous SNVs). The 20/20 rule classifies it as a tumor suppressor, but SOX9 protein was significantly overexpressed in tumors versus matched NATs (p = 1.02×10⁻¹⁰, paired t-test) — even within the subset bearing truncating mutations (p=0.04). Truncations cluster downstream of the HMG-box and upstream of K398, the conserved ubiquitin-target site for 26S proteasomal degradation; loss of K398 likely stabilizes the protein. The proteomic evidence reclassifies SOX9 as oncogenic, consistent with functional CRC assays.
  • Copy-number drivers prioritized by protein: Arm-level SCNAs matched TCGA (amplifications of 1q, 7p/q, 8p/q, 13q, 20p/q; deletions of 1p, 14q, 15q, 17p/q, 18p/q, 22q). cis-effects of SCNA on protein were stronger than on mRNA. Among focal driver candidates, only 59% showed expected protein-level effects in tumor vs NAT — NAT data substantially refined the driver list. Confirmed amplification drivers included HNF4A and TOMM34 on 20q; confirmed deletion driver SMAD4 on 18q. 17 SMGs + 90 prioritized SCNA genes were enriched for colorectal cancer, proliferation, apoptosis, Hippo signaling, endosome organization, and endocytosis (the latter spanning six genes across multiple focal deletions).
  • Rb phosphorylation as a driver: RB1 was recurrently amplified, and Rb protein was overexpressed in tumors versus NATs (p = 2.10×10⁻¹⁵). Phosphoproteomics quantified four E2F-regulating Rb phosphosites (T373, S807, S811, T826) — average phospho-Rb was 1.84-fold higher in tumors vs NATs (p < 2.2×10⁻¹⁶), significantly exceeding the 1.58-fold increase in total Rb (p=0.01, Wilcoxon). Phospho-Rb change correlated with predicted E2F1 activity (r=0.30, p=3.5×10⁻³), with CDK2 predicted activity (r=0.47, p=1.8×10⁻⁶) more strongly than any other kinase, with histone H3.1 phosphorylation (a proliferation marker; r=0.49, p=2.6×10⁻⁴), and negatively with the apoptosis hallmark gene set (r=−0.28, p=5.3×10⁻³). Conclusion: hyperphosphorylation, not RB1 loss, drives proliferation and dampens apoptosis in colon cancer — a vulnerability addressable by CDK2 inhibition.
  • Cancer-associated proteins/phosphosites: Among 6,422 proteins quantifiable in ≥50% of samples, 2,217 (35%) were significantly increased and 2,527 (39%) significantly decreased in tumors versus paired NATs (adj. p < 0.01). 31 proteins increased >2-fold (defined as “colon-cancer-associated”); 8 were elevated in >95% of pairs (DDX21 100%, S100A11 100%, RSL1D1 99%, S100P 97%, RPL36A 97%, PLOD2 96%, SERPINH1 95%, GPRC5A 95%). Among 7,295 quantifiable phosphosites, 63 were upregulated >2-fold (mapping to 50 proteins); only four proteins overlapped the protein and phosphosite lists. Only 7 of the combined protein/phosphosite-defined cancer proteins are in the Cancer Gene Census — most are invisible to mutation-based discovery.
  • Druggable kinases: Activity prediction (kinase-activating site phosphorylation + target-site enrichment) flagged CDK1, CDK2, CDK4, CDK7, MELK, PFKFB3, and PI4KB as elevated in tumors. CDK4 has FDA-approved inhibitors; CDK1/2/7, MELK, and PFKFB3 are targeted by clinical-trial drugs. PI4KB is a novel candidate.
  • Tumor antigens: Customized-database proteomics search recovered 173 proteomics-supported somatic mutations; 88 mutant 9–11mer peptides had high predicted HLA-I binding affinity (putative neoantigens). One or more neoantigens were identified in 38% of tumors. 16 cancer/testis antigens were detected; three were >2-fold elevated in tumors in >5% of pairs: IGF2BP3 (51%), SPAG1 (14%), ATAD2 (8%). Proteomics-supported neoantigens or CT antigens were found in 78% of tumors. Neoantigens were enriched in MSI-H tumors and patient-specific; CT antigens were MSI-status-independent and shared across patients.
  • Unified molecular subtypes (UMS): Network integration of consensus molecular subtypes (CMS1–4; cms-classifier), the previously published proteomic subtypes (ProS A–E), and MSI status collapsed the heterogeneity into three subtypes — MSI, CIN, and Mesenchymal. 87/110 tumors assigned. CMS3 dissolved (its molecular boundary was vague). Tumors in the CIN subtype had higher chromosome instability and higher RB1 copy number; Rb-S811 and S807 were 1.61- and 1.51-fold higher in CIN vs other subtypes (much greater than total Rb at 1.13-fold), suggesting CDK2 inhibition may be most effective in CIN. The Mesenchymal subtype had elevated stromal infiltration and suppressor immune cells (MDSCs, macrophages, Tregs); MSI was enriched in cytotoxic immune cells (NK, activated CD8).
  • Glycolysis–immune-evasion axis in MSI: Despite cytotoxic-cell enrichment, MSI tumors universally upregulated glycolytic enzymes at the protein level (also at mRNA, broadly) while TCA-cycle enzymes were decreased only at the protein level — a protein-level Warburg effect. Almost all glycolytic enzymes correlated negatively with CD8 infiltration in MSI tumors; median glycolytic-enzyme abundance versus CD8 infiltration: Spearman ρ = −0.61, p = 0.02. This anti-correlation was absent in other subtypes or when all tumors were pooled. SRM validation confirmed SLC2A3 (1.85-fold) and PKM2 (1.4-fold) elevation in MSI vs other UMS subtypes (p=0.007 and 0.003, t-test), and showed PKM2 (not PKM1) is the predominant isoform measured.

Genes & alterations

  • APC: stop-gain and frameshift mutations; reduced abundance of downstream phosphosite APC-T2451 in mutant tumors.
  • TP53: R273 hotspot missense mutations associated with >10-fold elevation of TP53-S315 phosphorylation (paired t-test p=0.001 for global tumor-NAT increase); confirmed in CPTAC breast and ovarian cohorts.
  • KRAS, BRAF, POLE: classic colon-cancer SMGs; BRAF and POLE mutations enriched in MSI-H/hypermutated tumors.
  • SMAD4: confirmed deletion driver in 18q focal-deletion region.
  • HNF4A, TOMM34: confirmed 20q amplification drivers via integrated copy-number/mRNA/protein prioritization.
  • TGFBR2: frameshift mutations reduce TGFBR2-S553 phosphosite abundance.
  • CASP5, RNF43, LTN1, BMPR2: newly identified hypermutated-group SMGs (>50% mutation frequency in hypermutated samples), not reported in the TCGA colorectal study.
  • SOX9: 19 mutated tumors (mostly truncating, downstream of HMG box, upstream of K398 ubiquitin site). Despite genomic prediction of tumor-suppressor status (20/20 rule), protein is significantly overexpressed in tumors vs NATs (p=1.02×10⁻¹⁰); truncations stabilize the protein. Reclassified as oncogene.
  • RB1: recurrently amplified (CIN subtype enriched); protein overexpressed (p=2.10×10⁻¹⁵); hyperphosphorylation at T373/S807/S811/T826 (E2F-regulating sites) drives proliferation and suppresses apoptosis.
  • E2F1: activity (inferred) positively correlated with total Rb (r=0.40, p=5.1×10⁻⁵) and phospho-Rb (r=0.30, p=3.5×10⁻³) — supports an E2F1–cyclin-E–CDK2–phospho-Rb feedback loop.
  • CDK1, CDK2, CDK4, CDK7, MELK, PFKFB3, PI4KB: elevated kinase activities in tumors; targetable therapeutic candidates.
  • CEACAM5: confirmed clinical CRC biomarker, recovered among cancer-associated proteins.
  • IGF2BP3, SPAG1, ATAD2: recurrent CT antigens (in 51%, 14%, 8% of tumor-NAT pairs respectively). IGF2BP3 has demonstrated in-vitro/in-vivo CTL induction in esophageal cancer.
  • SLC2A3, PKM: glucose transporter and pyruvate kinase elevated in MSI tumors (PKM2 is the predominant isoform); central to the MSI glycolytic phenotype and inversely correlated with CD8 infiltration.
  • CD8A: marker for CD8 T-cell infiltration; reduced in MSI tumors with high glycolysis.
  • DDX21, S100A11, S100P, PLOD2, SERPINH1, GPRC5A: most uniformly upregulated proteins across tumor-NAT pairs (≥95%), candidates for biomarker/therapeutic follow-up.

Clinical implications

  • CDK2 inhibition for colon cancer: Phospho-Rb is the principal Rb-pathway lesion in COAD; CDK2 inhibitors should be evaluated, particularly in CIN-subtype tumors where RB1 copy gain and Rb phosphorylation are highest. This opportunity is invisible to RB1 mutation/deletion analysis used in other cancers.
  • Glycolysis inhibition + checkpoint blockade in MSI-H CRC: The negative correlation between glycolytic activity and CD8 infiltration specifically in MSI tumors supports combining glycolysis inhibitors (e.g., PKM2- or SLC2A3-targeting agents, PFKFB3 inhibitors) with immune checkpoint blockade as a strategy for MSI-H tumors that fail to respond to checkpoint inhibition.
  • Personalized neoantigen vaccines: Proteomics-supported filtering (vs. mRNA-only filtering) identified high-affinity-HLA-binding neoantigens for 38% of tumors. The authors argue this is a more effective prioritization because neoepitopes are themselves peptides.
  • CT-antigen vaccines for MSS tumors: IGF2BP3, SPAG1, and ATAD2 are MSI-status-independent recurrent CT antigens — potential immunotherapy targets for MSS colon tumors that are poor candidates for checkpoint blockade or neoantigen vaccines.
  • Druggable kinases beyond CDKs: CDK4 (palbociclib-class inhibitors approved); CDK1, CDK2, CDK7, MELK, and PFKFB3 inhibitors in clinical development. PI4KB is a novel target proposed by this work.
  • Biomarkers: 15 of the 31 cancer-associated proteins have known clinical utility (diagnostic, outcome, or therapeutic), including CEACAM5 (standard-of-care CRC marker). 19 of the 31 are detectable in plasma — candidate blood biomarkers.

Limitations & open questions

  • Cohort scope: Colon-only, not colorectal — rectal tumors were excluded because untreated rectal cancers are difficult to obtain. Generalization to rectal cancer requires separate validation.
  • Mechanistic question: Why is RB1 recurrently amplified in colon cancer if the protein’s inhibitory activity is then negated by phosphorylation? The authors flag this as mechanistically inefficient and unresolved; the antiapoptotic role of phospho-Rb (negative correlation with apoptosis hallmark, r=−0.28) is a partial answer but motivates further work.
  • MSI subtype heterogeneity: Three MSI-H tumors with relatively fewer MS INDELs were assigned to the Mesenchymal UMS subtype — boundary cases the unified classification absorbs but does not fully explain.
  • No therapeutic validation: All therapeutic hypotheses (CDK2 inhibition for COAD, glycolysis inhibition for MSI checkpoint resistance, PI4KB targeting, CT-antigen vaccines) are generated computationally from omics data; in vivo/clinical validation is explicitly outside the scope.
  • Sex-stratified analyses not performed: Sample size insufficient after stratification (65F/45M).
  • Sample sizes for some readouts: 96 tumor/NAT pairs for global and phospho-TMT; 100 tumors for label-free proteomics; 105 for SCNA; 106 for RNA-Seq/WXS — minor non-overlap across modalities reduces power on a per-feature basis.
  • SNP array data discarded: Illumina SNP array data had low dynamic range and were dropped in favor of WXS-derived SCNAs — limits comparability with array-based studies.
