TL;DR

Tang et al. integrated ATAC-seq, RNA-seq, and DNA sequencing across a biobank of 22 patient-derived organoids, 6 PDXs, and 12 cell lines of metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) and identified four chromatin-defined molecular subtypes: CRPC-AR (androgen receptor–dependent), CRPC-NE (neuroendocrine; see PRSCC), CRPC-WNT (Wnt-driven), and a newly described CRPC-SCL (stem cell–like) subtype driven by AP-1 family transcription factors (notably FOSL1) cooperating with YAP1/WWTR1 (TAZ) and TEAD. Applying transcriptomic signatures to 366 CRPC patients across the SU2C and WCM cohorts, CRPC-SCL was the second-most-common subtype (~28%) and was associated with shorter time on next-generation ARSI (enzalutamide/abiraterone). Verteporfin and the c-Fos/AP-1 inhibitor T-5224 selectively reduced growth of CRPC-SCL models, nominating the YAP/TAZ/AP-1 axis as a therapeutic vulnerability.

Cohort & data

  • 40 metastatic prostate cancer models profiled by ATAC-seq and RNA-seq: 22 patient-derived organoids (15 newly derived MSKPCa8–MSKPCa24 + 7 prior), 6 patient-derived xenografts (PDX), and 12 cell lines (7 with new ATAC-seq + 5 NEPC models from Park et al.) (PMID:35617398).
  • Mutational/CNV profiling of organoids and 10/15 matched tumor biopsies with MSK-IMPACT; copy-number landscape compared against the SU2C metastatic CRPC cohort.
  • Patient classification cohort: 366 CRPC patients with polyA-enriched RNA-seq — 266 from the published SU2C cohort (Abida et al. 2019) and 100 from a Weill Cornell Medicine (WCM) cohort.
  • Dataset of record: organoid biobank deposited as prad_organoids_msk_2022 on cBioPortal.
  • Functional validation: ChIP-seq for FOSL1/TEAD/YAP/TAZ in MSKPCa3 and DU145; CRISPR sgRNA cell-competition assays; siRNA knockdown of YAP/TAZ; exogenous FOSL1 overexpression in LNCaP.

Key findings

  • Four chromatin-defined CRPC subtypes identified by consensus k-means clustering of ATAC-seq peaks (validated by hierarchical clustering and UMAP): CRPC-AR, CRPC-NE, CRPC-WNT, and a novel CRPC-SCL (stem cell–like). Signature peaks were enriched at enhancers vs. promoters (one-tailed Fisher’s exact P = 4.18e-126).
  • 861,195 reproducible ATAC-seq peaks across 35 newly profiled models + 5 published NEPC models, mostly at distal intergenic/intronic regions.
  • Tumor-suppressor loss burden: TP53 was the most frequently altered gene with putative driver mutations or deep deletions in 23/35 (66%) samples; RB1 bi-allelic loss in 20% and PTEN bi-allelic loss in 43%. Including transcript/protein-level loss, RB1 loss was observed in additional 11/35 (31.4%) and PTEN loss in 10/35 (28.5%) samples.
  • RB1 loss enriched in AR-negative/low samples (14/24) vs CRPC-AR (2/12) (one-tailed Fisher’s exact P = 0.0200); no difference for TP53 or PTEN — consistent with prior data linking RB1 (but not TP53/PTEN) to shorter CRPC survival.
  • CRPC-AR enriched for AR amplification and/or AR mutation (one-tailed Fisher’s exact P = 7.01e-06); top key TFs were AR and FOXA1.
  • CRPC-WNT (4/35 models): all carry Wnt pathway alterations; 3 with hotspot CTNNB1 mutations, 1 with shallow APC deletion + RSPO2 gain; top TF TCF7L2 (TCF-4), with LEF1/TCF7/TCF7L1 also high-ranked.
  • CRPC-NE: top TFs NEUROD1 and ASCL1 (the same master TFs in SCLC); high SYP/CHGA/DLL3, enriched for RB1 deep deletion.
  • CRPC-SCL (newly identified): enriched for mammary stem cell signature, cancer stem cell markers CD44 and TACSTD2 (TROP2A), basal signature, FGFR signaling, and TGF-β/EMT/TNF-α/IFN response pathways. Top key TFs were AP-1 family members, with FOSL1 the highest-ranked.
  • AP-1 + YAP/TAZ/TEAD co-operate in CRPC-SCL: TEAD motifs were the second-most-enriched (after AP-1) in CRPC-SCL accessible peaks. ChIP-seq in MSKPCa3 and DU145 showed significant overlap of FOSL1/TEAD/YAP/TAZ peaks (Fisher’s exact P < 0.001), all enriched at CRPC-SCL ATAC-seq peaks but not CRPC-AR peaks (P < 0.0001).
  • Functional dependency: sgRNA depletion of FOSL1 in MSKPCa3 and DU145 reduced GFP+ fraction (positive-control-like) but not in CRPC-AR organoid MSKPCa2. siRNA double-knockdown of YAP/TAZ significantly decreased growth of MSKPCa3 and DU145 (two-tailed unpaired t-test P < 0.0001) but not of MSKPCa2 or 22Rv1.
  • FOSL1 acts as a pioneer factor: stable FOSL1 overexpression in AR-high LNCaP cells (alone or with YAP/TAZ) increased chromatin accessibility at CRPC-SCL-specific sites and decreased CRPC-AR-specific accessibility (permutation P < 0.001), with concordant upregulation of CRPC-SCL signature genes (FDR < 1e-5) — direct evidence of lineage transformation.
  • Positive feedback loop: YAP/TAZ double KD depleted FOSL1 at RNA and protein levels in MSKPCa3 and DU145; FOSL1 itself was a target of FOS/JUN and TEAD in the inferred regulatory network, with a CRPC-SCL-specific FOSL1 enhancer co-bound by TEAD/YAP/TAZ/FOSL1.
  • Patient subtype distribution (Nearest Template Prediction on RNA-seq from 366 CRPC patients): 206/266 SU2C and 82/100 WCM patients assignable to one of four subtypes; CRPC-AR largest, CRPC-SCL the second-most-common (~28% across cohorts), then CRPC-NE, then CRPC-WNT. NTP and SVM classifiers agreed on 92% (WCM) and 85% (SU2C) of samples.
  • CRPC-SCL is ARSI-refractory: among 56 SU2C patients with time-on-ARSI data (enzalutamide/abiraterone), CRPC-SCL had significantly shorter time on treatment (Cox log-rank).
  • Therapeutic vulnerability: verteporfin (YAP/TAZ inhibitor; FDA-approved photosensitizer) and T-5224 (c-Fos/AP-1 DNA-binding inhibitor) preferentially inhibited proliferation of CRPC-SCL models MSKPCa3 and DU145 vs. CRPC-AR models MSKPCa2 and 22Rv1.

Genes & alterations

  • AR — amplification and/or mutation enriched in CRPC-AR; AR signaling lost or attenuated in the three AR-negative/low subtypes.
  • FOXA1 — co-top TF with AR in CRPC-AR.
  • FOSL1 — highest-ranked key TF for CRPC-SCL; acts as pioneer factor whose overexpression in AR-high LNCaP cells reprograms chromatin toward CRPC-SCL.
  • YAP1 and WWTR1 (TAZ) — transcriptional coactivators upregulated in CRPC-SCL; double knockdown selectively suppresses CRPC-SCL cell growth; co-bind with FOSL1/TEAD at CRPC-SCL enhancers.
  • TP53 — putative driver mutations or deep deletions in 23/35 (66%) of CRPC models, no subtype-specific enrichment.
  • RB1 — bi-allelic loss in 20% of models genomically; combined with RNA/protein loss, present in 14/24 (58%) AR-negative/low samples vs. 2/12 CRPC-AR (P = 0.0200); deep deletion enriched in CRPC-NE patients in the SU2C/WCM cohorts.
  • PTEN — bi-allelic loss in 43% of models; not subtype-specific.
  • CTNNB1 — hotspot mutations in 3/4 CRPC-WNT models.
  • APC, RSPO2 — combined shallow deletion + gain in the fourth CRPC-WNT model.
  • TCF7L2, LEF1, TCF7, TCF7L1 — top TFs in CRPC-WNT.
  • ASCL1, NEUROD1 — top TFs in CRPC-NE, mirroring the master regulators of SCLC.
  • SYP, CHGA, DLL3 — high NE markers in CRPC-NE.
  • CD44, TACSTD2 (TROP2A) — stem cell markers elevated in CRPC-SCL (with CD44 also elevated in CRPC-SCL patients).
  • CCN1 (CYR61), AXL, CCN2 (CTGF), AJUBA, ANKRD1, CCND1 — canonical YAP/TAZ targets downregulated upon YAP/TAZ double knockdown in MSKPCa3 and DU145.
  • AXIN2 — elevated in CRPC-WNT patients (Wnt target).

Clinical implications

  • Subtype-directed therapy stratification: the authors propose that transcriptomic NTP/SVM classifiers using their derived signatures can assign CRPC patients to one of four subtypes from bulk RNA-seq, with potential to inform treatment selection.
  • ARSI resistance biomarker: CRPC-SCL patients have significantly shorter time on next-generation ARSIs (enzalutamide, abiraterone), suggesting subtype identification could deprioritize ARSI escalation in these patients.
  • YAP/TAZ/AP-1 pathway as a CRPC-SCL vulnerability: verteporfin and the AP-1/c-Fos inhibitor T-5224 selectively reduce CRPC-SCL cell growth in preclinical models, nominating the pathway for clinical development in this subtype. (Authors caution that verteporfin’s clinical translation may be limited by YAP-independent effects.)
  • Lineage plasticity as a unifying mechanism: CRPC-SCL tumors lack neuroendocrine features but share basal/stem-like reprogramming with NEPC, suggesting a broader model in which loss of AR dependence proceeds via distinct master-TF-driven trajectories (FOSL1/AP-1 for SCL, ASCL1/NEUROD1 for NE).
  • CD44 (and to a lesser extent AXIN2) are proposed as candidate IHC/RNA-level biomarkers for CRPC-SCL and CRPC-WNT respectively.

Limitations & open questions

  • CRPC-SCL identification leans heavily on in vitro/PDX models — the four subtypes are defined epigenetically in 40 models and then projected onto bulk RNA-seq from patients, which may miss intra-tumoral heterogeneity or sample composition effects.
  • Authors note 60/266 SU2C and 18/100 WCM patients remained unassigned by NTP, with unassigned samples showing more heterogeneity and a tendency toward CRPC-SCL — possibly representing transitional states.
  • Verteporfin’s clinical potential is explicitly hedged due to known YAP-independent off-target effects; T-5224 has not yet been tested in prostate cancer trials.
  • ARSI-treatment analysis was limited to 56 SU2C patients with time-on-treatment data; CRPC-WNT and CRPC-NE arms had <5 patients each, so direct comparison across all four subtypes was not possible.
  • In vivo durability and specificity of YAP/TAZ/AP-1 inhibition in CRPC-SCL await longer-term PDX/xenograft experiments.
  • Mechanistic basis for lineage choice (why some AR-loss tumors become CRPC-NE vs. CRPC-SCL despite shared TP53/RB1 co-loss) remains unresolved.
