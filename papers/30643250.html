Advancing cancer research through 3D cell culture models

Authors

Panez-Toro I

Mountford J

Muñoz-Garcia J

Heymann D

Doi

10.17179/excli2025-8563

PMID: 30643250 · DOI: 10.17179/excli2025-8563 · Journal: EXCLI Journal (2025)

TL;DR

This is a methodological review of three-dimensional (3D) cell culture systems for cancer research. It argues that monolayer cultures fail to recapitulate intra- and inter-tumoral heterogeneity, drug resistance, and tumor microenvironment biology, and surveys four families of 3D models — scaffold-free spheroids/tumorspheres/tumoroids, scaffold-based hydrogels and electrospun/porous scaffolds, 3D bioprinting, and microfluidic platforms including droplet systems and organ-on-a-chip — with worked examples in breast, glioblastoma, osteosarcoma, ovarian, pancreatic, Ewing sarcoma, head and neck, and other cancers (PMID:30643250).

Cohort & data

  • Review article — no primary patient cohort. Examples cited come from cell-line and tumoroid experiments using lines such as MCF-7 and MDA-MB-231 (breast), HCT-116 (colon), 143B/MG63/MNNG-HOS/SAOS-2/U2OS/SJSA1 (osteosarcoma), U87/U87MG (glioblastoma), OVCAR-5 (ovarian), A673 (Ewing sarcoma), MRC5 fibroblasts, B16-OVA/B16-F0 (murine melanoma), and H4-II-EC3 (hepatoma) (PMID:30643250).
  • Cancer types referenced as illustrative test beds for 3D models: BRCA, OS, GB, NBL, ES, PAAD, COAD, HNSC, NPC, MEL, CHS, LUNG, OVT.
  • Methods surveyed: 3d-cell-culture-models, tumorsphere-formation-assay, patient-derived-xenograft.

Key findings

  • Spheroid size limits. Cancer spheroids rarely exceed 1000 µm diameter, far below human tumor sizes — e.g. colon tumors ≈ 4 cm median across 300,386 patients; osteosarcoma 9.14 cm mean length in ≤14-year-olds (n=140) vs. ≈ 200–1000 µm spheroid diameters with 143B/MG63/MNNG-HOS/SAOS-2/U2OS/SJSA1; 38.2% of 4,265 primary breast tumors ≥ 2 cm vs. ≈ 200–900 µm MCF7 spheroids (PMID:30643250, Table 1).
  • Hypoxia recapitulation. 3D models develop hypoxic cores similar to tumors and recapitulate the drug-resistance phenotype observed in patients; monolayers cannot model the spatial oxygen gradient. Phenotypic and genotypic effects of hypoxia can irreversibly yield malignant subclones in tumoroid models (Kumano et al., 2024) (PMID:30643250).
  • Drug-resistance amplification. IC50 values typically rise 3–5-fold in spheroids vs. monolayers (e.g. therapeutic peptide testing in breast cancer cell-line spheroids, Cavaco et al., 2021). Co-culture of ovarian cancer cell lines with fibroblasts using liquid overlay (Flörkemeier et al., 2024) increased cisplatin susceptibility, demonstrating stromal modulation of drug response in 3D (PMID:30643250).
  • Stemness phenotype emergence in 3D. Breast cancer spheroids upregulate Nanog and OCT3/4 (Dhandapani et al., 2023); glioblastoma cells in chitosan-hyaluronic porous scaffolds upregulate CD44, ABCG2, and MGMT in a pore-size–dependent manner and show greater temozolomide resistance vs. monolayers (Zhou et al., 2023); gelatin-foam glioblastoma tumoroids resist irinotecan and fluorouracil but are more sensitive to the hypoxia-activated agent tirapazamine, coincident with HIF-1α upregulation (Yang et al., 2015) (PMID:30643250).
  • Electrospun PCL scaffolds reveal mechanobiology. Aligned electrospun poly(ε-caprolactone) fibers induce CAV1-driven F-actin polymerization, focal adhesion formation, and nuclear YAP translocation in breast cancer cells; CAV1 silencing reduces invasion into murine tissue (Li et al., 2024) (PMID:30643250).
  • High-throughput droplet microfluidics. MCF-7 spheroid generation in droplet-based chips achieves >1000 droplets/min at 16–20 Hz with droplet diameters up to 250 µm after 2 weeks (Kwak et al., 2018). U87MG glioblastoma spheroids in droplets dropped viability from 91% to 55% under near-infrared irradiation with nanocomposites, supporting photothermal therapy screening (Lee et al., 2020). Anchored droplet chips screen cisplatin + etoposide combinations on A673 Ewing sarcoma spheroids without IC50 bias vs. 96-well plates; synergy is greater when etoposide precedes cisplatin (Fevre et al., 2023) (PMID:30643250).
  • Organ-on-a-chip vascularization. A neuroblastoma-on-a-chip model using collagen biomaterial at 36 dynes/cm² (143 µL/min) shear stress supports endothelial–NB co-culture capillary formation, while static cultures produce cell death (Villasante et al., 2024). Skin/skin-cancer-on-a-chip platforms achieve uniform 600 µm penetration of doxorubicin-loaded GelMA microneedles to melanoma cells beneath the epidermis (Barros et al., 2025) (PMID:30643250).
  • Co-culture in droplets enables immune-tumor study. Anchored droplet co-cultures of T cells with B16-OVA spheroids show that tumor destruction reflects cooperative cytotoxic T-lymphocyte accumulation at the spheroid edge rather than the sum of individual T-cell activity (Ronteix et al., 2022) (PMID:30643250).

Genes & alterations

  • No primary gene-level findings — this is a review. The paper references signaling and stemness genes (HIF-1α, CAV1, YAP, Nanog, OCT3/4, OCT4, CD44, ABCG2, MGMT, Ki67, IL18, IDO-1, CXCL12, FAP, BMI1, BCL2, NF-κB, vimentin, N-cadherin, E-cadherin, Zeb1, Twist2, Snail1, αSMA, LMP1, CK14) only as readouts in cell-line experiments cited from other primary studies (PMID:30643250). Canonical gene pages are not warranted from this review alone.

Clinical implications

  • Personalized medicine framing. The authors position patient-derived tumoroids (notably from 3D bioprinted decellularized scaffolds and porous-scaffold-grown tumoroids) as a potential drug-screening platform for individualized therapy selection, especially for cancers where the tumor microenvironment drives drug response (e.g. pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, osteosarcoma) (PMID:30643250).
  • Minimal residual disease modeling. Droplet-based microfluidic encapsulation of OVCAR-5/RFP cells enables transcriptomic characterization of chemotherapy-residual vs. chemotherapy-resistant ovarian-cancer populations, supporting MRD-specific therapeutic strategies (Yang et al., 2025, cited herein) (PMID:30643250).
  • Drug-screening readiness. Anchored droplet chips deliver IC50 measurements consistent with conventional 96-well plates for combination chemotherapy (cisplatin + etoposide) in Ewing sarcoma spheroids, suggesting microfluidic formats are viable for translational drug screening (PMID:30643250).
  • Transdermal drug delivery. Skin-cancer-on-a-chip enables localized doxorubicin delivery via GelMA microneedles with significantly higher efficiency than diffusion through media — a model for testing transdermal melanoma therapy (PMID:30643250).

Limitations & open questions

  • No standardized 3D nomenclature. Terms “spheroid”, “tumorsphere”, “tumoroid”, and “organoid” are inconsistently used across the literature; the authors adopt “tumoroid” for patient-tumor-derived 3D cultures to disambiguate (PMID:30643250).
  • Size mismatch with patient tumors. Spheroids rarely exceed 1000 µm even after 20+ days with serial transfer, limiting extrapolation to clinical disease (PMID:30643250).
  • MatrigelTM batch variability. Composition is poorly defined (≈1851 proteins identified across batches; growth-factor-reduced batch similarity may be as low as 53% between manufacturers), confounding mechanistic interpretation; PubMed shows a downward trend in MatrigelTM publications since 2013 (PMID:30643250).
  • Rotating wall vessel introduces microgravity artifacts. Simulated microgravity alters cell-cycle progression, cell death, and migration in ways that do not reflect in-vivo shear stress experienced by circulating tumor cells (PMID:30643250).
  • Magnetic levitation toxicity. SPIONs and gold nanoparticles can induce reactive oxygen species, apoptosis, and cytoskeletal perturbations, complicating phenotype interpretation (PMID:30643250).
  • Scalability vs. resolution trade-off. Dynamic suspension techniques generate large spheroid batches (e.g. up to 841 spheroids/run) but make it difficult to track individual spheroids over treatment time (PMID:30643250).
  • Bioprinting cost and access. Bioprinter cost, patient-sample availability, and inability of simple bioinks to fully reproduce the tumor microenvironment limit the throughput and fidelity of bioprinted tumor models (PMID:30643250).
  • Frontmatter mismatch in source record. The raw extraction lists study_id: gbm_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018; this appears to be a stale extractor artifact — the paper makes no use of TCGA or any cBioPortal study and is not a cohort analysis. The PMID also does not align with the publication date (paper published August 2025) and may be misassigned at the source.
