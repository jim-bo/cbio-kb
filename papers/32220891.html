Oncogenic Genomic Alterations, Clinical Phenotypes, and Outcomes in Metastatic Castration-Sensitive Prostate Cancer

PMID: 32220891 · DOI: 10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-20-0168 · Journal: Clinical Cancer Research (2020)

TL;DR

Stopsack and colleagues at Memorial Sloan Kettering profiled paired tumor–blood samples from 424 patients with metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer (mCSPC) using the MSK-IMPACT panel between May 2015 and September 2018, asking which tumor-genomic features distinguish clinical phenotypes (high- vs. low-volume disease; de-novo metastatic vs. metastatic recurrence) and which predict castration resistance and overall survival. High-volume disease had higher fraction genome altered and more frequent alterations in the NOTCH, cell cycle, and epigenetic-modifier pathways; de-novo disease was distinguished primarily by more frequent CDK12 alterations. AR, TP53, cell cycle, and MYC pathway alterations were associated with worse prognosis, while SPOP and WNT pathway alterations were associated with better prognosis. PI3K-pathway alterations (including PTEN) were not prognostic after multivariable adjustment.

Cohort & data

  • N = 424 patients with metastatic castration-sensitive prostate adenocarcinoma; median age at sampling 65.6 years (IQR 59.0–71.8); 88% white.
  • Phenotype split: 213 (50%) high-volume disease vs. 211 (50%) low-volume; 275 (65%) de-novo metastatic vs. 149 (35%) metastatic recurrence.
  • Dataset: prad_mcspc_mskcc_2020 — paired tumor–blood sequencing from a tertiary referral center (Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center).
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT panel, an FDA-authorized hybridization capture clinical assay covering 341–468 genes depending on assay version.
  • Sample tissue distribution: prostate 204 (48%), lymph node 93 (22%), bone 61 (14%), lung 26 (6%), other soft tissue 25 (6%), liver 15 (4%).
  • Disease-volume definition: modified — visceral metastases or ≥4 bone metastases (no extra-axial requirement, unlike CHAARTED).
  • Follow-up: median 27.2 months for castration resistance and 30.5 months for overall survival.
  • Analytic methods: FACETS for allele-specific copy-number and clonality; tumor purity ≤20% excluded (6.7% overall); fraction genome altered as a measure of genomic instability; OncoKB annotation (Aug 1, 2019) for actionability; pathway grouping per Sanchez-Vega et al. 2018 (11 pathways, 144 genes; HIPPO and TGF-β excluded because altered in <5% of samples); Cox proportional hazards regression and three-year restricted mean survival times; gene-level Fisher’s exact test with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR for genes altered in ≥5% of samples; Kaplan-Meier curves for time-to-event endpoints.

Key findings

  • Disease volume drives prognosis: of 213 high-volume patients, 139 developed castration resistance vs. 101 of 211 low-volume (adjusted HR 1.84, 95% CI 1.40–2.41); deaths 77 vs. 25 (adjusted HR 3.71, 95% CI 2.28–6.02). High-volume patients lived on average 4.8 months shorter (95% CI 3.3–6.3) over a 36-month restricted window.
  • Timing of metastasis is not strongly prognostic after adjustment: unadjusted HR for castration resistance 1.52 (95% CI 1.11–2.10) became 0.87 (95% CI 0.53–1.43) after adjusting for PSA at diagnosis and time since diagnosis; OS HR 0.95 (95% CI 0.41–2.21).
  • Genomic instability tracks with volume: median fraction genome altered 32% (IQR 24–48); high-volume disease had a 4.6 percentage-point higher mean (95% CI 1.5–7.7) vs. low-volume. Metastatic recurrence vs. de-novo difference was negligible (1.1 pp, 95% CI −3.1 to 5.2).
  • Tumor mutation burden is low: median 2.6 mut/Mb (IQR 1.8–4.4), similar across phenotypes.
  • Actionability: 211 of 424 tumors (50%) carried at least one potentially actionable alteration per OncoKB (as of Aug 1, 2019). Median 3 oncogenic alterations per tumor (IQR 2–5).
  • Pathways enriched in high-volume disease: NOTCH (+4.2 pp, 95% CI 0.1–8.3), cell cycle, and epigenetic modifiers were the top three by adjusted prevalence ratio.
  • Phenotype-discriminating genes: CDK12 alterations were 6.7 pp more common in de-novo metastatic disease vs. metastatic recurrence (95% CI 3.0–10.4; FDR 0.037 in gene-level discovery). AR alterations were 8.6 pp more frequent in metastatic recurrences (95% CI 3.3–13.8) before adjusting for continuous ADT, which nullified the association.
  • Prognostic genomic alterations (castration resistance): AR, TP53, cell cycle, and MYC pathway alterations associated with 1.6–5-fold higher rates; SPOP and WNT pathway alterations associated with ~1.5-fold lower rates. Effects persisted in multivariable models adjusting for all other pathways.
  • Prognostic genomic alterations (overall survival): 2-fold to 4-fold differences by AR, SPOP (inverse), cell cycle, and WNT (inverse) alterations.
  • Negative findings: PI3K, RAS/RAF/MAPK, DNA repair, and epigenetic-modifiers pathway alterations were not associated with castration resistance or OS after multivariable adjustment. BRCA2 trended toward higher castration resistance (adjusted HR 1.60, 95% CI 0.98–2.63) and was inconclusive for OS (HR 1.08, 95% CI 0.50–2.33). PTEN alterations were prognostic only in unadjusted analyses (multivariable CRPC HR 1.27, 95% CI 0.94–1.73; OS HR 0.95, 95% CI 0.58–1.56).
  • TP53 caveat: the OS signal for TP53 alterations attenuated in multivariable adjustment; only the castration-resistance association persisted robustly.

Genes & alterations

  • AR — oncogenic alterations were the strongest predictor of castration resistance and death in this cohort, despite being uncommon in the castration-sensitive setting. AR alterations were enriched in metastatic-recurrent disease (8.6 pp more frequent vs. de-novo), but the effect was confounded by continuous ADT exposure at sampling.
  • SPOP — alterations associated with better prognosis: ~1.5-fold lower rate of castration resistance and 2- to 4-fold longer survival differences in multivariable models. SPOP was less common in de-novo disease (imprecise estimate).
  • TP53 — alterations associated with higher castration-resistance rates (1.5- to 5-fold range across pathway alterations); OS association attenuated after multivariable adjustment.
  • CDK12 — the only single gene with statistically significant (FDR 0.037) differential prevalence by phenotype: 6.7 pp more frequent in de-novo metastatic disease vs. metastatic recurrence (95% CI 3.0–10.4). Authors flag CDK12 as a candidate for further validation alongside SPOP and the epigenetic modifiers pathway.
  • BRCA2 — exploratory sensitivity analysis (top-2 most altered genes per pathway): adjusted HR for castration resistance 1.60 (95% CI 0.98–2.63), OS HR 1.08 (95% CI 0.50–2.33); compatible with but did not establish poor prognosis.
  • PTEN — appeared prognostic in isolation but not in multivariable models (CRPC HR 1.27, 95% CI 0.94–1.73; OS HR 0.95, 95% CI 0.58–1.56), consistent with castration-resistant disease findings.
  • SPEN — cited as one of the individually uncommon NOTCH-pathway genes whose collective alteration the authors interpret as a candidate driver of high-volume disease.
  • CTNNB1 — mentioned as a key WNT-pathway gene; authors note that this study’s finding of better prognosis with WNT alterations is in contrast to two prior smaller mCRPC studies (Chen 2019 PMID:30928160; Wang 2018 PMID:29069303) that linked WNT/CTNNB1 alterations to worse outcomes — recorded here as an open conflict.

Clinical implications

  • Molecular classification of mCSPC: the authors propose that AR, TP53, cell cycle, MYC, SPOP, and WNT pathway status be considered for prognostic stratification in metastatic castration-sensitive disease, complementing the long-established disease-volume axis.
  • Therapy selection signal — NOTCH and docetaxel: NOTCH-pathway alterations are enriched in high-volume disease, the subgroup for which some trials show preferential docetaxel benefit; the authors note that NOTCH alterations have been previously implicated in docetaxel resistance (Domingo-Domenech 2012), suggesting this pathway is a candidate for biomarker-driven studies of docetaxel benefit.
  • PI3K-targeted therapy: PI3K pathway alterations (including PTEN loss) were not associated with prognosis after adjustment, mirroring observations in mCRPC and tempering enthusiasm for PI3K-pathway prognostic stratification in the castration-sensitive state.
  • Timing of metastasis (de-novo vs. recurrence) may not warrant differential systemic therapy based on this dataset, since the prognostic difference disappeared after adjusting for diagnostic PSA and time since diagnosis.
  • AR alterations are predictive of androgen-axis treatment failure even in the castration-sensitive setting — relevant for next-generation androgen-receptor-axis therapies (abiraterone, enzalutamide, apalutamide).
  • Actionability is real: 50% of mCSPC tumors had at least one OncoKB-actionable alteration as of Aug 2019, supporting routine molecular profiling at metastatic diagnosis.

Limitations & open questions

  • Single-center referral cohort: the authors explicitly call out non-representative case-mix (88% white) and possible selection bias since only a subset of mCSPC patients undergoes tumor biopsy. They recommend nesting future genomic studies within population-based cohorts with biorepositories.
  • Targeted panel, not whole-genome: the MSK-IMPACT panel covers 341–468 cancer-relevant genes; broader features (structural variants, non-coding drivers) are unmeasured.
  • Volume definition modified vs. trials: unlike CHAARTED, this study did not require extra-axial bone metastases — limiting direct comparability to phase-3 trial subgroup analyses.
  • Sample size for some pathways: estimates for individually rare alterations (e.g., specific NOTCH genes such as SPEN) were imprecise; authors call for future studies “an order of magnitude larger” and stratified by specific metastatic site.
  • Repeat samples handled by first-sample-only rule: intratumoral heterogeneity and on-treatment evolution were not assessed.
  • Conflict with prior mCRPC WNT findings: WNT pathway alterations associated with better prognosis here, opposite to Chen 2019 and Wang 2018 in mCRPC. Authors flag this as needing replication; the WNT result is a candidate finding for open questions.
  • DNA repair pathway null result: the absence of a DNA-repair pathway prognostic signal may mask gene-specific effects (e.g., BRCA2) lost in pathway-level aggregation.
  • Multivariable attenuation: some pathway associations (NOTCH with castration resistance; TP53 with OS) attenuated in multivariable models, suggesting partial confounding by co-occurring alterations.
