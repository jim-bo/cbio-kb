PIK3CA and MAP3K1 alterations imply luminal A status and are associated with clinical benefit from pan-PI3K inhibitor buparlisib and letrozole in ER+ metastatic breast cancer

Authors

Mellissa J. Nixon

Luigi Formisano

Ingrid A. Mayer

M. Valeria Estrada

Paula I. González-Ericsson

Steven J. Isakoff

Andrés Forero-Torres

Helen Won

Melinda E. Sanders

David B. Solit

Doi

10.1038/s41523-019-0126-6

PMID: 31552290 · DOI: 10.1038/s41523-019-0126-6 · Journal: npj Breast Cancer (2019)

TL;DR

Correlative molecular analysis of a phase Ib trial combining the pan-PI3K inhibitor buparlisib (NVP-BKM-120) with letrozole in ER+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer found that PIK3CA activating mutations and MAP3K1 loss-of-function mutations independently mark patients who derive clinical benefit (CR/PR or SD ≥6 months), with the combination of both alterations conferring the greatest benefit (5/7 = 71% CBR vs 11% in double-wild-type). Mechanistic follow-up in T47D cells showed that PI3K inhibition activates JNK/cJUN signaling in a MAP3K1-dependent manner, but shRNA/siRNA knockdown of MAP3K1 did not sensitize ER+ breast cancer cell lines to buparlisib. Instead, PAM50 analysis of trial tumors and 170/121 PDX models showed that MAP3K1/PIK3CA co-mutation strongly tracks with a luminal A phenotype, and luminal A status itself associates with PI3K-inhibitor benefit — leading the authors to posit that MAP3K1 mutations are a surrogate for luminal A biology rather than a direct causal driver of PI3K dependence.

Cohort & data

  • 33 patients with ER+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer (MBC) refractory to ≥1 prior line of endocrine therapy, enrolled in a phase Ib trial of buparlisib + letrozole (Stand Up to Cancer protocol; Mayer et al. JCO 2014, ref 25).
  • A separate subset of 12 patients from a phase Ib trial of alpelisib + letrozole (Mayer et al. Clin Cancer Res 2017, ref 21) was profiled by PAM50 for cross-validation.
  • Targeted NGS profiling for 341 genes via the MSK-IMPACT panel (IMPACT341) on pre-study biopsies or archival FFPE tissue (Cheng et al. JMD 2015, ref 29).
  • Custom NanoString PAM50 (pam50) codeset on FFPE tissue from 14 buparlisib-trial tumors and 12 alpelisib-trial tumors.
  • Public cohorts: TCGA breast cancer (brca_tcga_pub, n=959 RNA-seq) for PIK3CA/MAP3K1 luminal-correlate analysis and PIK3CA-GS scoring; CCLE for ER+ breast cell-line mutation status.
  • PDX panel from Gao et al. Nat Med 2015 (ref 22): 170 PDX models treated with buparlisib and 121 PDX models treated with alpelisib, stratified by MAP3K1/PIK3CA status and PAM50 subtype.
  • Trial data deposited at brca_mskcc_2019 on cBioPortal.

Key findings

  • No single genomic lesion was associated with objective response (PR/CR), but the cohort contained only one of each.
  • PIK3CA alterations were moderately enriched in patients with CR/PR/SD vs PD (p=0.09, Fisher’s exact); MAP3K1 alterations were significantly enriched in CR/PR/SD (p=0.01, Fisher’s exact).
  • Clinical benefit rate (CBR; defined as CR/PR or SD ≥6 months) by genotype:
    • PIK3CA alteration only: 2/5 (40%)
    • MAP3K1 alteration only: 1/2 (50%)
    • Both PIK3CA + MAP3K1 alterations: 5/7 (71%); two patients had SD lasting 2 years.
    • Neither alteration: 11% CBR (single CR and PR in this group both came off study early due to toxicity).
  • Stratifying PFS (with toxicity-driven study withdrawal as a censor): MAP3K1 alteration improved PFS (p=0.03, log-rank); PIK3CA alteration improved PFS (p=0.009, log-rank); patients harboring truncating/frameshift mutations only (excluding point mutations) continued to show improved outcome (p=0.05).
  • Co-occurrence of PIK3CA + MAP3K1 alterations was associated with the greatest PFS benefit relative to single-alteration or no-alteration patients (p=0.02, log-rank trend test).
  • PIK3CA mutations were primarily hotspots, whereas MAP3K1 alterations were primarily truncating/frameshift (consistent with loss of function).
  • Mechanism: buparlisib treatment of PIK3CA-mutant T47D cells sustained p-AKT (S473) suppression over 5 days but produced reciprocal activation of p-cJUN (S73). shRNA knockdown of MAP3K1 (~70% mRNA reduction) abolished the cJUN induction without changing baseline or buparlisib-suppressed p-AKT — confirming MAP3K1 as the relevant kinase upstream of buparlisib-induced JNK/cJUN activation. However, MAP3K1 knockdown did not sensitize T47D, MCF7, or BT483 cells to buparlisib in full-serum or estrogen-deprived media (SRB, clonogenic, and monolayer assays).
  • In the TCGA breast cohort (n=959), tumors with both MAP3K1 alteration and PIK3CA mutation showed significantly higher correlation to the luminal A vs luminal B PAM50 centroid (p<0.0001, two-tailed t-test); this enrichment was unique to the double-altered tumors.
  • In the trial PAM50 subset, all 14 buparlisib-trial and 12 alpelisib-trial tumors classified as luminal A or B; combined-analysis trend toward enrichment of luminal A among clinical-benefiters (p=0.07, one-tailed Fisher’s exact).
  • In PDX data: PDXs with MAP3K1 alterations were more sensitive to alpelisib (one-tailed p=0.001) but not buparlisib; PIK3CA-mutant PDXs showed a trend toward improved alpelisib response (p=0.07). Luminal A status predicted response to both drugs.
  • PIK3CA-GS (PI3K pathway activation gene signature from Loi et al. PNAS 2010, ref 20) correlated with improved response to both buparlisib (p=0.03) and alpelisib (p=0.02) in PDXs.
  • In TCGA, simultaneous loss of MAP3K1 (or its downstream effector MAP2K4) with PIK3CA alteration did not produce enhanced PIK3CA-GS scores, arguing against MAP3K1 loss acting via increased PI3K-pathway dependence.

Genes & alterations

  • PIK3CA — recurrent hotspot activating mutations in helical/kinase domains; present in ~40–50% of ER+ tumors; in this trial, enriched in clinical-benefit patients and synergized with MAP3K1 alterations for the highest CBR (5/7, 71%).
  • MAP3K1 — primarily truncating/frameshift mutations consistent with loss of function; significantly enriched in CR/PR/SD patients (p=0.01, Fisher’s exact); recurrent L380S point mutation observed in the longest-responding patient (AACR GENIE v4.1 supports recurrence and likely LOF). Authors interpret as a surrogate marker for luminal A subtype rather than a direct PI3K-sensitivity driver.
  • MAP2K4 — downstream effector of MAP3K1 in the JNK pathway; co-loss with PIK3CA alteration in TCGA did not enhance PIK3CA-GS readout.
  • PTEN — deletions/mutations associated with PD at first scan, except for a deep PTEN deletion in the single PR patient.
  • ESR1, FGFR1 — cited as established endocrine-therapy resistance mechanisms (intro context).
  • ERBB2 — patients were HER2-negative by eligibility (HER2-).
  • JUN — p-cJUN (S73) activation by buparlisib confirmed as MAP3K1-dependent escape signaling, but not therapeutically exploitable in vitro.
  • IRS1 — cited as a route by which MAP3K1 loss has been reported to activate PI3K (ref 17, Avivar-Valderas et al. Oncotarget 2018), but this study could not reproduce a corresponding sensitization.

Clinical implications

  • PIK3CA mutation alone is a suboptimal predictor of response to buparlisib + letrozole in endocrine-refractory ER+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer; the authors recommend adding MAP3K1 status and/or PAM50 luminal A classification as candidate biomarkers for patient selection in PI3K-inhibitor + endocrine combinations.
  • Luminal A subtype may itself be a predictive biomarker for benefit from PI3K inhibition + estrogen suppression — supported by both the trial PAM50 data and an independent 170-PDX dataset where luminal A status predicted response to both buparlisib and alpelisib.
  • Consistent with the FERGI trial of pictilisib + fulvestrant (Krop et al. Lancet Oncol 2016, ref 27), where PR+ subset (commonly luminal A) benefitted preferentially.
  • The findings argue for prospective biomarker stratification by PIK3CA + MAP3K1 + PAM50 in larger randomized PI3K-inhibitor trials in ER+ disease.

Limitations & open questions

  • Very small cohort (n=33 NGS-profiled patients in the buparlisib trial; n=14 with PAM50 data) — sample size precludes definitive biomarker claims; authors explicitly call the luminal-A-without-mutation responder observations “anecdotal.”
  • The mechanistic hypothesis that MAP3K1 loss directly sensitizes cells to PI3K inhibition was not supported: shRNA/siRNA knockdown in T47D, MCF7, and BT483 did not enhance buparlisib sensitivity in either full-serum or estrogen-deprived conditions; clonogenic and proliferation assays confirmed the null result.
  • No ER+ breast cell line in CCLE harbors both MAP3K1 loss and PIK3CA mutation — likely because luminal A tumors poorly tolerate cell-line or PDX establishment — leaving the prototypic genotype unmodeled.
  • TCGA data show MAP3K1 truncating mutations are not associated with reduced mRNA (no nonsense-mediated decay), raising three open possibilities: untranslated mutant mRNA, translated but nonfunctional protein, or neomorph function not captured by knockdown.
  • Luminal A tumors have low intrinsic proliferation; prolonged SD could reflect indolent biology rather than treatment effect — a placebo-controlled randomized trial would be needed to disentangle this.
  • PDX endocrine therapy was not administered, unlike the clinical trials, complicating direct extrapolation; however, both trials enrolled endocrine-refractory patients where endocrine benefit was debatable.
  • Buparlisib itself has been largely deprioritized clinically (BELLE-2 / BELLE-3 toxicity profile), so the biomarker hypothesis most relevant to current practice is PIK3CA + MAP3K1 + luminal A as predictors of alpelisib benefit — independently suggested by Dawson et al. ASCO 2018 (ref 26).
