Cell-of-Origin Patterns Dominate the Molecular Classification of 10,000 Tumors from 33 Types of Cancer

TL;DR

Hoadley and colleagues performed an integrative multi-platform molecular analysis of ~10,000 tumors from 33 cancer types in The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) PanCancer Atlas, combining chromosome-arm aneuploidy, DNA methylation, mRNA, miRNA, and reverse-phase protein array (RPPA) data. Using iCluster across the four most complete platforms (copy number, DNA methylation, mRNA, miRNA) on 9,759 samples, they identified 28 integrated molecular subtypes. Cell-of-origin and tissue/organ-system patterns dominated the classification, but the analysis also surfaced cross-tissue groupings (pan-GI, pan-gyn, pan-squamous, pan-kidney) that motivate organ-system and stemness-based pan-cancer companion analyses.

Cohort & data

  • Samples: 11,286 tumors across 33 cancer types with data on at least one of five assay platforms; 9,759 with complete data across four platforms (aneuploidy, DNA methylation, mRNA, miRNA); RPPA available for 7,858 samples across 32 tumor types (LAML lacked protein data).
  • Patients: 11,188 TCGA patients with at least one molecular platform — 5,769 females, 5,282 males, 137 unknown; median age at diagnosis 60 (range 10–90).
  • Cancer types covered: LAML, DLBCLNOS, THYM, OV, UCEC, CESC, BRCA, BLCA, PRAD, TGCT, KIRC, KICH, KIRP, THCA, ACC, ESCA, STAD, COAD, READ, LIHC, PAAD, CHOL, HNSC, LUAD, LUSC, MESO, GBM, LGG, SARC, UCS, PCPG, SKCM, UVM.
  • Datasets (cBioPortal study mirror): the 33 TCGA PanCancer Atlas studies, e.g. gbm_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, brca_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, luad_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, coadread_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018, and the other PanCancer Atlas study IDs listed in frontmatter.
  • Assays / platforms: Affymetrix SNP 6.0 arrays for somatic copy number (affymetrix-snp6) processed with GISTIC 2.0 and ABSOLUTE; Illumina Infinium HM27 and HM450 DNA methylation arrays merged across 22,601 shared probes; mRNA sequencing (rna-seq) on Illumina GAII/HiSeq; miRNA sequencing (mirna-seq); reverse-phase protein arrays (RPPA).
  • Integrative analytics: iCluster on copy number + DNA methylation + mRNA + miRNA over 9,759 tumors; COCA (Cluster-Of-Cluster-Assignments) to compare platform-specific cluster memberships; PARADIGM to infer activity of ~19,000 pathway features; TumorMap for 2D visualization of iCluster latent-variable distances.

Key findings

  • 28 integrative iClusters were identified from joint clustering of 9,759 tumors across four platforms. Copy-number alterations contributed 47% of the integrated signal, mRNA + miRNA 42%, and DNA methylation 11%.
  • Single-platform clustering recapitulated cell-of-origin: aneuploidy yielded 10 groups, DNA methylation 25 groups (from 3,139 CpGs hypermethylated in ≥1 tumor type), mRNA 25 groups (≥40 samples each), miRNA 15 groups, and RPPA 10 groups — with squamous (LUSC/HNSC/CESC/ESCA), pan-GI (COAD/READ/STAD/non-squamous ESCA), and pan-kidney (KIRC/KIRP) tumors repeatedly co-clustering.
  • 16 of 33 tumor types had >80% of samples in a single iCluster; 8 iClusters were dominated by one tumor type (C24: LAML, C11: LGG IDH1 mut], C6: OV, C8: UCEC, C12: THCA, C16: PRAD, C26: LIHC, C14: LUAD).
  • Cross-tissue iClusters: pan-kidney mapped almost entirely to C28 (KIRC+KIRP); KICH co-clustered with ACC in C9 (both hypodiploid). Pan-GI separated into C1: STAD (EBV-CIMP, hypermethylated EBV-associated tumors), C4: pan-GI (CRC, COAD/READ with CIN), and C18: pan-GI (MSI, mostly STAD/COAD). Pan-squamous formed C10, C25 (chr11 amp), and C27: pan-SCC (HPV+, including most CESC, HPV+ HNSC, and some BLCA).
  • C2: BRCA (HER2 amp) comprised mostly ERBB2-amplified tumors across BRCA, BLCA, and STAD, demonstrating an actionable cross-tissue amplification subtype.
  • Mutation burden by iCluster: C15:SKCM/UVM (UVB signature) and C14:LUAD (smoking signature) showed the highest mutation rates; POLE signatures appeared in hypermutated C8: UCEC and C4: pan-GI (CRC); C18: pan-GI (MSI) was enriched for mismatch-repair-deficient mutational signatures.
  • Stromal and immune content: C20 had the highest median stromal fraction (relabeled C20: mixed [stromal/immune]); C3 had the highest leukocyte-to-stromal ratio and was relabeled C3: mesenchymal (immune). Both shared high PDCD1 (PD-1), CTLA4, and T-cell/B-cell activation gene programs (GP2).
  • Pathway features: PARADIGM and gene-program analyses showed C28: pan-kidney enriched for hypoxia, retinoid metabolism, PPAR-RXR, and immune checkpoints PD-1/CTLA4; pan-squamous iClusters (C10, C25, C27) shared dNp63/TAp63 squamous signaling, proliferation, hypoxia, and basal signaling; C3 and C20 shared upregulated JAK2/STAT1,3,6 signaling with C14:LUAD and C10:pan-SCC.
  • Stemness: TGCT showed strong enrichment of both mRNA- and DNA-methylation-based stemness signatures (Malta et al., 2018 companion); LAML was strongly enriched only for the mRNA-based stemness signature.
  • CUP relevance: authors note that this iCluster classification could improve management of the 1%–3% of cancer patients newly diagnosed with cancer of unknown primary, where RNA- or DNA-methylation-based profiling has already improved outcomes.

Genes & alterations

  • IDH1 — defines C11: LGG (IDH1 mut), one of the most homogeneous and high-silhouette iClusters; IDH1-wildtype LGG co-clusters with GBM in C23: GBM/LGG (IDH1wt).
  • ERBB2 — focal amplification defines C2: BRCA (HER2 amp), which spans BRCA, BLCA, and STAD — a clear cross-tissue alteration subtype.
  • POLE — POLE-mutation signature found in hypermutated subsets of C8: UCEC and C4: pan-GI (CRC).
  • ESR1 (ER-α), AR, IGFBP2 — high in P3/P4 RPPA groups containing luminal BRCA, UCEC, OV, and some LIHC; estrogen-signaling gene program (GP7) high in C2: BRCA (HER2 amp) and C19: BRCA (luminal).
  • PDCD1 (PD-1), CTLA4 — immune checkpoint programs enriched in C3: mesenchymal (immune), C20: mixed (stromal/immune), and C28: pan-kidney; basis for the authors’ suggestion of immunotherapy susceptibility.
  • JAK2, STAT1, STAT3, STAT6 — JAK-STAT pathway upregulated in C3, C20, C14:LUAD, and C10:pan-SCC, raising hypothesis of repurposing JAK-STAT inhibitors approved in non-malignant settings (rheumatoid arthritis, myelofibrosis, polycythemia vera).
  • ALK, EGFR, BRAF, KIT, ABL1 — cited as cancer-driving targets that motivate tissue-agnostic basket/umbrella trials (e.g. NCI-MATCH); the authors caution that the same BRAF mutation yields unpredictable clinical responses across diverse malignancies, illustrating why tissue-of-origin context still matters.

Clinical implications

  • The 28-iCluster classification has potential utility for cancer of unknown primary (CUP) management, where DNA-methylation- and RNA-based profiling have already improved patient outcomes.
  • Cross-tissue ERBB2-amplified C2 (BRCA + BLCA + STAD) supports use of HER2-directed agents beyond breast cancer.
  • Immune-enriched iClusters (C3 and C20) with high PD-1/CTLA4 and T-cell/B-cell activation gene programs are nominated as candidates for immune-checkpoint blockade across diverse histologies.
  • Shared JAK-STAT activation across C3, C20, C14:LUAD, and C10:pan-SCC suggests repurposing of JAK inhibitors currently approved for non-malignant inflammatory and myeloproliferative diseases.
  • Findings support basket/umbrella trial design (e.g. NCI-MATCH) but argue that integrated molecular tumor profiling should consider both targetable mutations and oncogenic-pathway context plus tissue of origin — illustrated by the heterogeneous response of BRAF-mutant tumors across cancer types.

Limitations & open questions

  • Mutation data were excluded from iCluster due to sparsity of mutations across the platform-aligned cohort; mutation burden and signatures were used only for downstream characterization, so mutation-driven subtypes may be under-represented.
  • Aneuploidy was weakly concordant with other platforms because more than a third of samples (AN7) had few arm-level events, limiting COCA’s resolution.
  • Heterogeneous iClusters remain hard to interpret — C20: mixed (stromal/immune) contains 25 of 33 tumor types and 6 tumor types had <50% of samples in any single iCluster (BLCA, UCS, HNSC, ESCA, STAD, CHOL), indicating substantial residual molecular heterogeneity.
  • TCGA cohort biases: adult tumors only (median age 60), predominantly treatment-naive primary specimens with high RIN/DNA-quality thresholds — results may not generalize to pediatric, metastatic, or treatment-resistant cancers.
  • Translation to clinical actionability is hypothesis-generating: PARADIGM/gene-program activations identify candidate vulnerabilities (PD-1/CTLA4, JAK-STAT) but trials are needed; the authors’ own BRAF example shows that pathway activation does not guarantee response.
