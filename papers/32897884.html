Germ cell tumors and associated hematologic malignancies evolve from a common shared precursor

TL;DR

Taylor et al. assembled a 15-patient MSK cohort (1998–2019) of young men with primary mediastinal nonseminomatous germ cell tumors (PM NSGCT) and concomitant hematologic malignancies, and performed targeted (msk-impact-panel) and whole-exome sequencing on the paired solid tumors, blood/marrow malignancies, and fingernail-derived germline DNA. Phylogenetic reconstruction in 5 WES-profiled patients showed that the GCT and the hematologic malignancy in each individual evolved from a shared ancestral clone, nearly always harboring an allelically imbalanced TP53 and/or RAS-pathway (KRAS, NRAS, RRAS2) driver, plus frequent isochromosome 12p. The associated leukemias/sarcomas lacked the canonical de novo AML/MDS drivers (FLT3, DNMT3A, TET2, NPM1) and were not detectable in pre-transplant autologous stem cell products, arguing that they arise from a germ-cell-lineage precursor rather than from clonal hematopoiesis or from differentiated GCT components.

Cohort & data

  • 15 patients with concurrent germ cell tumor and hematologic malignancy, identified at MSKCC over a 21-year period (1998–2019); all males aged 18–33 (median 25) with PM NSGCT (PMID:32897884).
  • GCT histology: teratoma component in 73% (predominant in 60%); yolk sac tumor component in 53% (TT, TYST).
  • Hematologic malignancies (MNM) included AML (53% of patients, nearly half AMKL M7), MDS, CMML, and histiocytic sarcoma (HS); one-third of patients developed multiple temporally distinct hematologic subtypes.
  • Sequencing: targeted MSK-IMPACT on 11 patients with available tissue; whole-exome sequencing on a 5-patient subset, with fingernail DNA as patient-matched normal control.
  • Reference cohorts for genomic comparison: 51 PM mediastinal GCTs without secondary malignancy (10 seminoma + 41 nonseminoma) — corresponds to gct_msk_2016 lineage — and 200 de novo AMLs from the TCGA AML cohort (PMID:32897884).
  • Allele-specific copy number and clonality: FACETS v0.5.6; only clonal CNAs (>=80% cellular fraction) considered. Oncogenic-alteration annotation via OncoKB. Phylogenetic trees constructed manually by most-parsimonious sequence of mutations and CNAs.
  • Data deposition: WES at dbGaP phs2231.V1; IMPACT mutation calls deposited via cBioPortal as study gct_msk_2020.

Key findings

  • Median overall survival of the dual-cancer cohort was 6.3 months (95% CI 4.6–25.2 months); only one patient was alive at the time of analysis (PMID:32897884).
  • Median time between GCT and blood-cancer diagnosis was 4.8 months; 4 of 15 patients developed both malignancies concurrently, before any anticancer therapy — incompatible with a purely therapy-induced second neoplasm.
  • Isochromosome 12p [i(12p)] was common in both mediastinal GCTs and their associated hematologic malignancies but was never observed in de novo AML; in several patients i(12p) appeared as an isolated clone by conventional cytogenetics, marking it as an early event.
  • TP53 mutations were shared between the GCT and the blood cancer in 91% (10/11) of paired patients, versus 61% in mediastinal GCTs without hematologic malignancy and only 9% in de novo AML.
  • Activating mutations in KRAS or NRAS were present in 63% of mediastinal GCTs with associated hematologic malignancies, 37% of mediastinal GCTs without, and only 13% of de novo AML.
  • Absence of canonical myeloid drivers: the associated hematologic malignancies lacked mutations in FLT3, DNMT3A, TET2, and NPM1 — distinguishing them molecularly from de novo myeloid neoplasms and from clonal hematopoiesis.
  • Shared ancestral precursor identified in every WES patient (n=5). Examples:
    • 24-year-old PM NSGCT + CMML: shared i(12p), KRAS G12S, and 8 additional truncal mutations, followed by branching divergent evolution.
    • 28-year-old PM NSGCT + AML: shared TP53 R248L with copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity (CNLOH) defining the ancestral clone.
    • Patient with PM NSGCT and high-risk MDS: shared KRAS G12D precursor with identical chromosomes 13 and 17 CNAs; each malignancy independently acquired distinct TP53 hotspot mutations with CNLOH (mutually exclusive on aligned reads).
    • 19-year-old with successive GCT → histiocytic sarcoma → CMML → AML within 18 months: all 4 malignancies derived from a precursor bearing TP53 Q144Rfs*26 with CNLOH plus a PIK3CD mutation; daughter precursors then split GCT+AML from histiocytic sarcoma+CMML, with the histiocytic sarcoma further acquiring NRAS Q61H. The inferred clonal tree did not match the order of clinical diagnosis. Notably this patient’s GCT lacked i(12p), so karyotype alone would have missed the clonal relationship.
  • Autologous stem cell products from 5 high-risk PM GCT patients showed no clonal hematopoiesis by sequencing, even though one of them later developed a clonally related MDS and histiocytic sarcoma sharing a somatic BCOR G943D mutation and an RRAS2 activating mutation with CNLOH (with each subsequent disease acquiring its own distinct TP53 mutation with independent LOH). This argues the precursor is not a hematopoietic stem cell circulating during mobilization.
  • 2 of the 15 PM NSGCT patients had clinically diagnosed Klinefelter syndrome; none of the 41 PM NSGCT comparators without hematologic malignancy were known to have it (germline confirmation not done in comparators).

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — Shared truncal mutations (including hotspots R248L, Q144Rfs*26, Q144Rfs*28) with CNLOH defined the GCT-blood-cancer ancestral clone in multiple patients; mutually exclusive secondary TP53 hits accumulated independently in each lineage. Shared in 91% of GCT/hematologic-malignancy pairs vs 61% of GCT-only and 9% of de novo AML.
  • KRAS — Activating mutations (e.g., G12S, G12D) seen as truncal precursor alterations; KRAS or NRAS mutations together in 63% of GCT+hematologic-malignancy cases vs 13% of de novo AML.
  • NRAS — Q61H observed as a late, lineage-specific event in a histiocytic sarcoma; part of the activating RAS-pathway driver set together with KRAS.
  • RRAS2 — Activating mutation with CNLOH identified as a shared truncal alteration in a patient whose GCT, MDS, and post-transplant histiocytic sarcoma were all clonally related.
  • BCOR — G943D shared across the GCT, MDS, and post-allogeneic-transplant histiocytic sarcoma of one patient, supporting host (not donor) origin and shared precursor.
  • PIK3CD — Mutation present in the ancestral precursor of the 19-year-old with 4 clonally related malignancies (text also writes “PI3KCD” in one figure callout; the gene symbol is PIK3CD per OncoKB/HUGO).
  • PTEN — Referenced in prior reports (Lu et al., 2016) describing concurrent GCT/AMKL with shared TP53 and PTEN mutations; not a primary driver in this 15-patient cohort.
  • Copy-number / cytogenetic features — Isochromosome 12p shared between GCT and hematologic malignancy in this cohort, never seen in de novo AML; complex karyotypes common in the blood malignancies; copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity at TP53 a recurrent mechanism of biallelic inactivation.

Clinical implications

  • Hematologic malignancies arising in PM NSGCT patients should be recognized as a distinct clinical syndrome — not therapy-related AML/MDS — and treated with that biology in mind: high TP53 alteration burden and absence of canonical AML drivers (FLT3, DNMT3A, TET2, NPM1) likely explains the documented refractoriness to standard MDS/AML therapy and contributes to the 6.3-month median survival.
  • The same TP53 alteration burden in the GCT precursor provides a mechanistic basis for cisplatin resistance, consistent with prior work linking p53 pathway alterations to cisplatin failure in advanced germ cell tumors. Treatments listed in this cohort included cisplatin, etoposide, ifosfamide (VIP regimen), and paclitaxel plus high-dose carboplatin and etoposide (TICE regimen), followed by autologous SCT.
  • Autologous stem cell transplant products were not contaminated with clonal hematopoiesis in any of the 5 PM GCT patients tested, including one who later developed a clonally related MDS — so the relapse hematologic malignancy is not attributable to mobilized CHIP. This is useful biology when counseling about auto-SCT for relapsed mediastinal GCT.
  • Karyotype-only screening is insufficient to identify clonally related hematologic malignancy in GCT patients: i(12p) was absent in at least one clonally related case.
  • Klinefelter syndrome should be evaluated (including for mosaicism / subclinical disease) in PM NSGCT patients who develop hematologic malignancies, given that 2 of 15 in this cohort had known Klinefelter syndrome.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small cohort (n=15 total; WES in 5) — phylogenetic conclusions rest on a handful of patients given the rarity of the syndrome.
  • WES patient 2’s post-allogeneic histiocytic sarcoma required filtering of donor-derived contamination using preallogeneic variant allele frequencies; that sample was excluded from copy-number analysis.
  • Germline predisposition is not characterized — the authors call for systematic germline studies to evaluate for predisposition (including occult Klinefelter mosaicism).
  • The basis for the specific association between PM NSGCTs and somatic-type hematologic malignancies remains unexplained; no testicular or seminomatous GCT patient in this series developed a clonally related hematologic malignancy.
  • The proposed cell of origin — embryonal carcinoma cells reprogrammed from primordial germ cells — is a model, not a directly demonstrated lineage; further single-cell or fate-mapping studies would be needed.
  • Comparator cohorts (51 PM mediastinal GCTs; 200 TCGA AMLs) are MSKCC-internal and TCGA respectively; differences in age distribution, sequencing assay, and treatment era introduce confounding.
