Cell-free DNA profiling in retinoblastoma patients with advanced intraocular disease: An MSKCC experience

TL;DR

Kothari et al. asked whether plasma cell-free DNA (cfDNA) can recover the somatic RB1 mutations that drive retinoblastoma, at a time when ophthalmic artery chemosurgery has dropped enucleation rates below 10% and removed tumor tissue from most molecular workups. Working from 10 pediatric patients with advanced intraocular unilateral retinoblastoma who underwent primary enucleation at MSKCC, the authors profiled matched tumor by MSK-IMPACT and matched plasma/buffy-coat by a custom hybrid-capture panel tiling all 27 RB1 exons. Tumor-guided genotyping recovered 10/13 expected somatic RB1 mutations in 8/10 patients (median variant allele frequency [VAF] 4.9%, range 0.7%–12.6%), and a tumor-blind de novo calling pipeline still recovered 7/13 mutations in 6/10 patients. The two patients with the highest cfDNA VAFs (12.6% and 8.1%) went on to develop metastatic disease, suggesting cfDNA VAF tracks disease burden. The cohort is deposited in cBioPortal as rbl_cfdna_msk_2020.

Cohort & data

  • n=10 treatment-naive pediatric patients with advanced intraocular unilateral retinoblastoma at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, all undergoing primary eye enucleation (PMID:32633890).
  • Median age at diagnosis: 30.8 months (range 0.67–50.2 months).
  • 9/10 patients were treatment-naive at plasma collection; P19 had received 1 cycle of systemic carboplatin/etoposide/vincristine at an outside institution prior to presentation.
  • 3/10 patients developed metastatic disease on follow-up (P11, P16, P23) — a rate elevated above the general retinoblastoma population because the MSKCC enucleation cohort is enriched for advanced disease.
  • Tumor assay: MSK-IMPACT — FDA-cleared targeted panel covering >400 cancer-associated genes including all coding exons of RB1.
  • Plasma assay: Custom hybrid-capture panel tiling all 27 exons of RB1 plus heterozygous-SNP regions, sequenced on Illumina HiSeq 2×100 bp paired-end (rb1-cfdna-custom-capture-panel). Average unique coverage ~1530×; cfDNA yields 5.5–27.4 ng (mean 17.3 ng).
  • Variant callers: Tumor-guided genotyping via Waltz 2.0; tumor-blind de novo calling via VarDict, with manual review in IGV v2.3.36.
  • Public release: cBioPortal study rbl_cfdna_msk_2020.

Key findings

  • Tumor-guided cfDNA genotyping: Detected 10/13 expected somatic RB1 mutations in 8/10 patients; median cfDNA VAF 4.9% (range 0.7%–12.6%); average VAF 3.78% across detected mutations (PMID:32633890).
  • Tumor-blind de novo calling: Recovered 7/13 expected RB1 mutations in 6/10 patients with high confidence using VarDict + custom VAF/buffy-coat filters (VAF >0.5%, ≥10 mutant reads, VAF >2× matched buffy coat, VAF > mean+2SD across 9 unmatched buffy coats).
  • De novo calling also flagged 2 additional RB1 variants not reported in tumor (P22 p.G509E at 1.56% VAF; P26 p.P781Q at 0.99% VAF) — interpreted as either false positives or possible mosaic mutations from a subpopulation of retinal cells absent from the bulk tumor sample.
  • VAF tracks metastatic risk: Of 3 patients who developed metastatic disease, two (P23, P11) had the cohort’s highest cfDNA RB1 VAFs at 12.6% and 8.1% respectively. P23 recurred at 3 months with right orbit and bone marrow disease.
  • Subthreshold cases: In the 2 patients where tumor-guided genotyping was negative, P16 showed evidence below detection threshold (X702_splice: 5/5666 reads; Q736*: 2/2286 reads) while P19 (the only patient with prior systemic chemotherapy) showed no signal despite 1596× coverage — consistent with chemotherapy reducing circulating tumor cfDNA.
  • Technical replicates (5 samples with leftover library) showed VAF concordance with Pearson r²=0.993.
  • Germline-vs-somatic discrimination: Patient P01 carried a clinical germline RB1 mutation; tumor and cfDNA identified a different RB1 mutation at 1.37% VAF that was absent from matched buffy coat — interpreted as the somatic “second hit” rather than the germline allele (which would appear near 50% VAF in cfDNA).

Genes & alterations

  • RB1 — biallelic loss-of-function is the canonical retinoblastoma driver; this study targets somatic RB1 mutation detection in plasma cfDNA. The 13 tumor-discovered RB1 variants in the cohort include nonsense (R579, R320, R255, R445, E315, R556, Q736), frameshift (N623Qfs15, D338Vfs*11), in-frame deletion (N480del), and splice variants (X474_splice, X702_splice) (PMID:32633890).
  • MYCN — discussed but not assayed here. The authors note that ~1% of retinoblastomas are driven by MYCN amplification without RB1 loss-of-function, and flag incorporation of copy-number analysis on cfDNA as a future extension to broaden the addressable patient population.

Clinical implications

  • Noninvasive somatic RB1 testing in unilateral retinoblastoma. Intraocular biopsy is contraindicated due to tumor-seeding risk, and ophthalmic artery chemosurgery now spares most tumors from enucleation — so plasma cfDNA offers the only realistic route to somatic RB1 confirmation. Demonstrating a somatic RB1 mutation in cfDNA that is absent from buffy coat is sufficient to declare the disease non-heritable, which avoids serial eye exams under anesthesia, lifts secondary-cancer screening for the patient, and removes inherited risk counseling for siblings/offspring (PMID:32633890).
  • Potential prognostic signal. Highest cfDNA VAFs were observed in patients who developed metastases. Authors explicitly caveat that n=3 metastatic events cannot establish a quantitative correlation and call for a larger validation cohort.
  • Timing of blood draw matters. Systemic chemotherapy appears to suppress circulating tumor cfDNA (P19); for diagnostic somatic-RB1 testing, plasma must be drawn before systemic therapy.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small n=10 cohort, all with advanced intraocular disease at enucleation — not representative of the broader retinoblastoma population that is now treated with eye-sparing intra-arterial chemotherapy and never reaches enucleation. Feasibility in earlier-stage disease (lower tumor burden, lower expected cfDNA VAF) is unproven.
  • False positive rate at sub-1% VAF is uncharacterized. Authors call for profiling a large panel of healthy-donor plasma cfDNA to establish the error floor; without this, the two “extra” RB1 variants identified by de novo calling (P22 G509E, P26 P781Q) cannot be confidently classified as mosaic vs technical artifact.
  • No unique molecular identifiers (UMIs) in the current assay; authors flag UMI + deeper sequencing as the obvious sensitivity improvement.
  • Single-timepoint analysis. Longitudinal cfDNA sampling to test the hypothesis that rising VAF predicts metastatic recurrence is proposed as future work but not done here.
  • MYCN-amplified retinoblastomas are excluded by the RB1-only capture design; incorporating copy-number analysis on cfDNA is flagged as the path to covering the ~1% MYCN-driven, RB1-wildtype subset.
  • Conflicts of interest disclosed: MFB, DWYT, JP, MH, and FM are co-inventors on a provisional patent for cfDNA cancer-screening systems (PCT/US2019/027487); DT and FM are co-inventors on a separate patent for distinguishing pathological from clonal-hematopoietic mutations in plasma by fragment size.
