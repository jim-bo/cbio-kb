Microsatellite Instability-High Endometrial Cancers with MLH1 Promoter Hypermethylation Have Distinct Molecular and Clinical Profiles

TL;DR

Manning-Geist et al. interrogated 184 microsatellite-instability-high (MSI-H) endometrial carcinomas (ECs) from >1,100 patients prospectively sequenced on MSK-IMPACT at Memorial Sloan Kettering (2015–2020), stratifying tumors by the underlying mechanism of mismatch-repair deficiency (MMR-D): germline MMR mutation (n=25, Lynch), somatic MMR mutation (n=39), or epigenetic MLH1 promoter hypermethylation (MLH1ph; n=120). MLH1ph ECs constituted a clinicopathologically distinct entity — older, more obese, higher-stage, with more lymphovascular space invasion — and exhibited lower tumor mutational burden (TMB) and lower tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) scores than germline/somatic MMR-D ECs, despite enrichment for JAK1 frameshift mutations. Germline (Lynch) ECs were instead enriched for ERBB2, ERBB3, and FBXW7 hotspot mutations. Two-year progression-free survival in stage I/II MLH1ph ECs was 70% versus 100% in germline and somatic groups, supporting sub-stratification by MMR-D mechanism in future trials.

Cohort & data

  • Patients: 184 MSI-H or pathogenic-germline-MMR ECs filtered from 1,157 consented patients undergoing prospective tumor-normal sequencing at MSK between 03/2015–07/2020 (PMID:35849120). Cohort was uploaded to cBioPortal as study ucec_ccr_msk_2022.
  • MMR-D groups: 25 germline (14%), 39 somatic (21%), 120 MLH1ph (65%).
  • Cancer type: Endometrial carcinoma — UCEC / UEC. Tumors were primarily endometrioid (82–92% per group); other histologies included carcinosarcoma, clear cell, mixed, and undifferentiated/dedifferentiated.
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT tumor-normal targeted sequencing covering 341 (n=1), 410 (n=13), or 468 (n=170) cancer-related genes (IMPACT410 / IMPACT468). Median depth 637× (range 129×–1,368×).
  • Survival cohort: 119 patients with initial care at MSK and sequencing within 6 months of diagnosis were retained for survival analyses; 18 patients with recurrent disease treated with on-label pembrolizumab (16 MLH1ph, 1 germline, 1 somatic) contributed to the exploratory ICI-response analysis.
  • Analytic tools: MSI status by MSIsensor (≥10 MSI-H; 3–<10 indeterminate; <3 MSS); copy-number/LOH by FACETS; mutational signatures (COSMIC v3.1) by deconstructSigs; survival by Kaplan-Meier and Cox proportional-hazards models. Pembrolizumab response was scored by independent blinded radiology review per RECIST 1.1.
  • Orthogonal validation: Four-marker MMR immunohistochemistry (MLH1/MSH2/MSH6/PMS2) on all 184 ECs; MLH1ph status by bisulfite-based methylation assay in IHC-MLH1/PMS2-deficient cases.

Key findings

  • Clinicopathologic distinctness of MLH1ph. MLH1ph patients were older (median 64 vs 54 years germline/somatic; p<0.001), more often obese (BMI ≥30: 58% vs 42% germline, 26% somatic; p=0.001), diagnosed at higher FIGO stage (p=0.025), and had higher rates of lymphovascular space invasion (61% MLH1ph vs 56% somatic vs 32% germline; p=0.033). Within MLH1-altered ECs, MLH1ph patients were older (64 vs 50 years; p<0.001) and more obese (58% vs 9%; p<0.001) than MLH1-mutated patients.
  • Distinct somatic mutational landscape. Germline (Lynch) ECs were enriched for hotspot mutations in ERBB2 (29% vs 3% MLH1ph), ERBB3 (25% vs 2%), and FBXW7 (46% vs 10%) (all p<0.05). MLH1ph ECs were enriched for JAK1 alterations — predominantly frameshift indels — at 45% vs 4% in germline (p<0.05). Frequencies of ARID1A, PTEN, and PIK3CA mutations were comparable across MMR-D mechanisms.
  • Lower TMB in MLH1ph. Median TMB was 32 mt/Mb (range 13–302) in MLH1ph, 44 mt/Mb (1–74) in germline, and 48 mt/Mb (25–89) in somatic MMR-D ECs (p<0.001).
  • MSI/IHC discordances skew toward Lynch. All MLH1ph and somatic ECs were MSI-H by MSIsensor, but 83% (10/12) of MSH6-germline ECs and 31% (4/13) of MLH1/PMS2/MSH2-germline ECs were classified MSS or MSI-indeterminate — biallelic loss was observed in 5/14 such tumors and low tumor content in 9/14, suggesting an MSIsensor sensitivity floor for Lynch-associated ECs. Concordantly, 28% of germline ECs were MMR-proficient/equivocal by IHC versus none of the somatic or MLH1ph groups.
  • Mutational signatures. Aging-related COSMIC signature 1 dominated 62% (15/25) germline and 54% (20/39) somatic ECs but only 23% (27/120) of MLH1ph ECs (p<0.001). Conversely, MMR-D signatures 6, 15, 20, or 26 dominated 77% (88/120) of MLH1ph versus 38% germline and 46% somatic (p<0.001).
  • Lower immune infiltrate in MLH1ph. TIL scores were significantly lower in MLH1ph than germline/somatic MMR-D ECs across all three compartments (intratumoral p=0.002; stromal p=0.002; peritumoral p=0.001).
  • Survival. Among 119 survival-analyzed patients (29 progression events; 11 deaths; median PFS follow-up 25 months), MLH1ph was associated with inferior univariate PFS (p=0.005), but in multivariate models including MMR-D mechanism, age, stage, and LVSI, only stage was independently associated with PFS (p<0.001). In an exploratory stage I/II subgroup, two-year PFS was 70.3% for MLH1ph versus 100% for both germline and somatic MMR-D ECs.
  • Response to ICI. In an exploratory analysis of 18 patients with recurrent EC on on-label pembrolizumab (16 MLH1ph, 1 germline, 1 somatic), 4/16 (25%) MLH1ph patients had disease progression while neither germline nor somatic patients progressed. Among the 16 MLH1ph patients: 2 stable disease, 8 partial response, 2 complete response — clinical-benefit rate 75%, overall response rate 63%. Median TMB in MLH1ph patients receiving ICI was 33.8 mt/Mb (range 22.8–66.7).

Genes & alterations

  • MLH1 — defines the MLH1ph subgroup via promoter hypermethylation (n=120); biallelic MLH1 alterations also drove 3 germline and 10 somatic MMR-mutant tumors. MLH1ph patients were clinically and molecularly distinct (older, obese, lower TMB, lower TILs, worse stage-I/II PFS).
  • MSH2 — accounted for 8 (32%) germline and 19 (49%) somatic MMR-mutant ECs.
  • MSH6 — accounted for 12 (48%) germline and 7 (18%) somatic MMR-mutant ECs; 83% of MSH6-germline ECs were MSS or MSI-indeterminate by MSIsensor, suggesting reduced sensitivity of molecular MSI assays for MSH6-driven Lynch.
  • PMS2 — 2 germline (8%) and 3 somatic (7.7%) MMR-mutant ECs.
  • EPCAM — no pathogenic EPCAM alterations were identified in the cohort.
  • JAK1 — enriched in MLH1ph ECs (45%) versus germline (4%), predominantly frameshift indels (p<0.05). Authors invoke JAK1/2 as a putative ICI-resistance mechanism (citing MMR-D colon cancer and melanoma literature) to motivate the worse ICI responses observed in MLH1ph ECs.
  • ERBB2 — hotspot mutations enriched in germline (Lynch) ECs (29% vs 3% MLH1ph); proposed as a potentially targetable alteration via irreversible HER2 tyrosine-kinase inhibition for Lynch-associated EC.
  • ERBB3 — hotspot mutations enriched in germline (Lynch) ECs (25% vs 2% MLH1ph).
  • FBXW7 — hotspot mutations enriched in germline (Lynch) ECs (46% vs 10% MLH1ph).
  • PTEN, PIK3CA, ARID1A — frequencies similar across MMR-D groups (background EC alterations).
  • POLE — pathogenic POLE-mutated ECs (n=6) were excluded a priori to focus the analysis on MSI-H/MMR-D molecular subclass.

Clinical implications

  • MMR-D EC should not be treated as a monolith. MLH1ph ECs are a clinically higher-risk subgroup (older, obese, higher stage, more LVSI) with lower TMB, lower TIL infiltrate, and worse stage-I/II PFS than germline or somatic MMR-D ECs.
  • The lower TMB/TIL infiltrate and JAK1 frameshift enrichment in MLH1ph ECs raises the possibility that this subgroup may benefit less from single-agent PD-1 blockade than germline or somatic MMR-D ECs — authors observe 25% progression on pembrolizumab in MLH1ph versus 0/2 in germline/somatic, and explicitly speculate about JAK1-driven ICI resistance.
  • Authors recommend that future trials of upfront ICI + radiation (NRG-GY020, NCT04774419), dual immune-checkpoint blockade (NRG-GY025), and ICI + chemotherapy (NRG-GY018) sub-stratify by MMR-D mechanism rather than pooling all MMR-D ECs.
  • Lynch-associated ECs harbor an enrichment of ERBB2 hotspot mutations, motivating evaluation of irreversible HER2 tyrosine-kinase inhibitors in this group (e.g., extending the SUMMIT-style approach to Lynch ECs).
  • Diagnostic implication: IHC-based MMR and molecular MSI assays are not interchangeable in EC — 28% of MMR-germline-mutated ECs were MMR-proficient/equivocal by IHC, and 56% of germline MMR-D ECs were MSS or MSI-indeterminate by MSIsensor. Both assays should be performed in EC diagnostic workups.
  • FDA-approved ICI agents for MMR-D/MSI-H EC (pembrolizumab, dostarlimab) currently include all MMR-D ECs regardless of mechanism; the heterogeneity demonstrated here suggests label refinement may be warranted.

Limitations & open questions

  • Retrospective study, single-center cohort (MSK), restricted to patients undergoing tumor-normal sequencing — may exclude patients screened only by germline panels and thus underestimate the germline (Lynch) component.
  • MSK-IMPACT targets 341–468 cancer-related genes; differences in non-targeted regions could underpin additional unobserved differences across MMR-D mechanisms.
  • TIL scoring used H&E semi-quantitative density × distribution scoring; multiplex IHC distinguishing CD4/CD8 populations and PD-L1 expression would refine the immune-microenvironment characterization.
  • The ICI-response analysis is exploratory (n=18; only 1 germline and 1 somatic case) and imaging was non-protocolized; conclusions about differential pembrolizumab response by MMR-D mechanism require prospective validation.
  • The stage-I/II PFS difference between MLH1ph and germline/somatic groups is hypothesis-generating; “fewer than 3 events” qualifications apply to several subgroup contrasts.
  • IHC/MSIsensor discordance in MSH6-germline ECs (83% MSS or MSI-indeterminate) suggests molecular MSI tools under-detect Lynch — confirms previously reported MSI sensitivity floor in EC vs colorectal cancer.
  • MMR-D mechanism was not an independent multivariate predictor of survival after adjusting for stage; whether the stage-I/II MLH1ph PFS deficit reflects a true biological risk or unmeasured confounding remains open.
