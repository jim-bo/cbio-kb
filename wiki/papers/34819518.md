**PMID:** 34819518 · **DOI:** 10.1038/s41467-021-27106-w · **Journal:** Nature Communications (2021)

## TL;DR

Alatise et al. profiled colorectal cancer ([COAD](../cancer_types/COAD.md)/[READ](../cancer_types/READ.md)) in 380 Nigerian patients enrolled through the African Research Group for Oncology (ARGO), comparing 64 tumors sequenced with [MSK-IMPACT](../methods/msk-impact-panel.md) against MSKCC ([crc_nigerian_2020](../datasets/crc_nigerian_2020.md)) and TCGA reference cohorts. Microsatellite instability was markedly enriched in the Nigerian cohort (28.1% MSI-H vs. 14.2% TCGA and 8.5% MSKCC, P<0.001); among microsatellite-stable tumors, [APC](../genes/APC.md) mutations and [WNT](../themes/wnt-pathway.md) pathway alterations were depleted while RAS pathway alterations and [PIK3R1](../genes/PIK3R1.md)/[PIK3CB](../genes/PIK3CB.md) mutations were enriched. The findings argue that West African colorectal cancer follows a partially distinct molecular trajectory and motivate region-specific MSI/MMR testing and treatment guidelines.

## Cohort & data

- **Clinical cohort**: 380 Nigerian colorectal cancer ([COADREAD](../cancer_types/COADREAD.md)) patients enrolled prospectively through the African Research Group for Oncology (ARGO; Obafemi Awolowo University + 12 Nigerian tertiary care facilities) from April 2013 to November 2018, compared to 458 MSKCC patients diagnosed January–June 2013.
- **Molecular cohort**: 157 Nigerian tumors profiled with some combination of [immunohistochemistry](../methods/immunohistochemistry.md), [MSK-IMPACT](../methods/msk-impact-panel.md) targeted next-generation sequencing, and DNA methylation analysis.
  - 64 Nigerian + 1,145 MSKCC tumors underwent MSK-IMPACT paired tumor-normal sequencing.
  - 94 Nigerian tumors had MMR protein [IHC](../methods/immunohistochemistry.md) ([MLH1](../genes/MLH1.md), [MSH2](../genes/MSH2.md), [MSH6](../genes/MSH6.md), [PMS2](../genes/PMS2.md)).
  - 25 of 64 MSK-IMPACT-profiled Nigerian tumors had genome-wide DNA methylation by reduced representation bisulfite sequencing ([RRBS](../methods/rrbs.md), Zymo Methyl-MiniSeq).
- **Ancestry**: Genetically determined ancestry by [ADMIXTURE v1.3](../methods/admixture.md) against 1000 Genomes references. 100% (64/64) of Nigerian specimens were of African ancestry; MSKCC cohort was 77.2% European, 12.7% African.
- **Comparator dataset**: 1,145 MSKCC colorectal MSK-IMPACT specimens (April 2014–September 2016) plus TCGA colorectal data.
- **Public release**: Nigerian sequencing data deposited in cBioPortal as [crc_nigerian_2020](../datasets/crc_nigerian_2020.md).

## Key findings

- **Phenotype shifts**: Nigerian patients were younger (median 55.8 vs. 60.0 years, P<0.001), more likely to present with rectal disease (50.8% vs. 33.7%, P<0.001), and far more likely to present with stage IV disease (53.8% vs. 36.2%, P<0.001). Lung (10.5% vs. 29.3%) and liver (26.8% vs. 41.0%) metastases were less common but peritoneal metastases more common (30.3% vs. 18.1%, all P<0.001).
- **MSI enrichment**: 28.1% (18/64) of Nigerian tumors were MSI-H by [MSIsensor](../methods/msisensor.md), vs. 14.2% (65/459) in TCGA and 8.5% (97/1,145) in MSKCC (P<0.001). MMR protein deficiency by IHC concordantly reached 21.3% (20/94). The MSI excess persisted in patients ≤45 years (18% Nigerian vs. 9.5% MSKCC) and exceeded the rate among MSKCC self-identified African Americans (7.2%, 7/97).
- **MSI-H molecular profile**: [BRAF V600E](../genes/BRAF.md) was absent in all 18 Nigerian MSI-H tumors versus 25.8% (25/97) at MSKCC (P=0.01); [KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md) mutations trended higher (61.1% vs. 41.1%, P=0.20). [APC](../genes/APC.md) alteration frequency was similar across MSI-H cohorts (55.6% Nigeria vs. 52.2% MSKCC, P=1.0). [TCF7L2](../genes/TCF7L2.md) was more frequently altered in Nigerian MSI-H tumors (64.3% vs. 34.2% MSKCC, P=0.04).
- **Germline findings**: Among 18 Nigerian MSI-H patients, 22.2% (4/18) carried a high-penetrance pathogenic/likely-pathogenic germline variant; 16.7% (3/18) had germline DNA MMR mutations consistent with Lynch syndrome (two [MLH1](../genes/MLH1.md), one [MSH2](../genes/MSH2.md)); a fourth had an incidental [BRCA1](../genes/BRCA1.md) variant. In the 46 MSS Nigerian tumors a single pathogenic germline [APC](../genes/APC.md) (familial adenomatous polyposis) and an additional [MLH1](../genes/MLH1.md) variant were identified.
- **Methylation**: Only 28.6% (2/7) of MSI-H Nigerian tumors that underwent methylation analysis showed [MLH1](../genes/MLH1.md) hypermethylation, and only 8% (2/25) were CpG-island methylator phenotype high (CIMP-H) — substantially lower than the dominant somatic MSI mechanism in HIC cohorts.
- **MSS molecular profile**: In MSS tumors (Nigeria n=46, MSKCC n=1,040), [APC](../genes/APC.md) was depleted in Nigeria (36.9% vs. 76.0%, P<0.01) and WNT-pathway alteration overall was 47.8% vs. 81.9% (P<0.001). [CTNNB1](../genes/CTNNB1.md) frequencies were similar (4.4% vs. 3.7%, P=0.68), so the WNT deficit was not compensated by β-catenin activation. RAS-pathway alteration was higher in Nigerian MSS tumors (76.1% vs. 59.6% non-AA MSKCC, P=0.03). [PIK3R1](../genes/PIK3R1.md) (8.7% vs. 2.2%, P=0.02) and [PIK3CB](../genes/PIK3CB.md) (4.3% vs. 0.5%, P=0.04) were enriched. [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) frequency did not differ (71.7% vs. 77.5%, P=0.370).
- **Outcomes**: Median [OS](../cancer_types/OS.md) was 11.9 months for the Nigerian cohort versus not-reached at MSKCC (P<0.001); stage IV OS was 7.3 vs. 34.3 months. Recurrence-free survival was 12.5 vs. 60.4 months. Differences reflected presentation, treatment access (57.5% Nigerian vs. 83.8% MSKCC neoadjuvant chemotherapy; 71.0% vs. 87.3% surgery), and follow-up duration (16.9 vs. 64.8 months).

## Genes & alterations

- **[APC](../genes/APC.md)** — Depleted in Nigerian MSS tumors (36.9% vs. 76.0% MSKCC, P<0.01); decoupled from [CTNNB1](../genes/CTNNB1.md) compensation, indicating non-canonical WNT activation paths.
- **[BRAF](../genes/BRAF.md)** — V600E entirely absent (0/18) in Nigerian MSI-H tumors (vs. 25.8% MSKCC, P=0.01), arguing against the serrated/sporadic CIMP–MLH1-methylation route as a major MSI driver in this cohort.
- **[KRAS](../genes/KRAS.md)** — Enriched in Nigerian MSS tumors (RAS pathway 76.1% vs. 59.6%, P=0.03); 61.1% in Nigerian MSI-H (vs. 41.1% MSKCC, P=0.20).
- **[TP53](../genes/TP53.md)** — Similar overall frequency in MSS tumors (71.7% Nigeria vs. 77.5% MSKCC), but skewed away from right-sided tumors in Nigeria (25% right vs. 81.6% left-sided).
- **[MLH1](../genes/MLH1.md)** — 2 germline pathogenic variants among MSI-H Nigerian patients (Lynch syndrome) plus 1 in MSS; promoter hypermethylation in only 2/7 MSI-H methylation-profiled tumors.
- **[MSH2](../genes/MSH2.md)** — 1 germline pathogenic variant in an MSI-H Nigerian patient.
- **[MSH6](../genes/MSH6.md)**, **[PMS2](../genes/PMS2.md)** — Assayed by IHC as part of the MMR panel.
- **[PMS1](../genes/PMS1.md)** — Included in the MMR oncoprint of MSI-H Nigerian specimens (Fig. 1d).
- **[BRCA1](../genes/BRCA1.md)** — Incidental high-penetrance germline mutation in 1 MSI-H Nigerian patient.
- **[CTNNB1](../genes/CTNNB1.md)** — No enrichment despite low [APC](../genes/APC.md); 4.4% vs. 3.7%, P=0.68.
- **[TCF7L2](../genes/TCF7L2.md)** — Enriched in Nigerian MSI-H tumors (64.3% vs. 34.2%, P=0.04).
- **[PIK3R1](../genes/PIK3R1.md)** — Enriched in Nigerian MSS tumors (8.7% vs. 2.2%, P=0.02).
- **[PIK3CB](../genes/PIK3CB.md)** — Enriched in Nigerian MSS tumors (4.3% vs. 0.5%, P=0.04).

## Clinical implications

- **MSI/MMR testing policy**: The roughly 2–3× excess of MSI-H tumors in Nigerian CRC strengthens the case for routine MSI/MMR testing in West African and other LMIC settings, where it directly affects chemotherapy and immunotherapy decisions.
- **Chemotherapy benefit**: Authors note that up to a quarter of Nigerian CRC patients (the MSI-H subset) may derive only marginal benefit from systemic [fluorouracil](../drugs/fluorouracil.md)-based chemotherapy; this supports earlier consideration of immunotherapy (PD-1 blockade) for MSI-H disease.
- **Generalizability of trials**: The mutation profile cautions against generalizing therapeutic-trial results from US/European populations to West African patients, particularly for WNT-, RAS-, and PI3K-pathway-directed regimens.
- **Lynch syndrome**: 16.7% of Nigerian MSI-H tumors carried germline MMR variants — within the HIC expected range (11.0–18.5%) — supporting clinical germline testing in this population.

## Limitations & open questions

- **Sample size and validation**: Only 64 Nigerian tumors had MSK-IMPACT data and 25 had methylation profiling, limiting statistical power for sidedness and ancestry-stratified analyses; no external validation cohort exists in the public domain.
- **Staging discordance**: MSKCC tumors received pathologic staging (surgery) or clinical staging (neoadjuvant therapy), whereas all Nigerian tumors were staged clinically, complicating stage-for-stage outcome comparisons.
- **Comparator bias**: MSKCC is a specialized tertiary center and TCGA is predominantly of European descent, so both comparators may understate variation across the broader US population.
- **MSI mechanism**: Low MLH1 methylation and CIMP-H rates among Nigerian MSI-H tumors imply an alternative (e.g., biallelic somatic MMR inactivation) pathway that this study could not resolve and that future West African cohorts should target.
- **Outcome attribution**: Shorter Nigerian OS reflects later-stage presentation, less neoadjuvant therapy, and shorter follow-up — disentangling biological from access-of-care contributions requires longer prospective follow-up.
