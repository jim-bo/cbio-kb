Landscape of somatic alterations in large-scale solid tumors from an Asian population

Authors

Liqun Wu

Herui Yao

Hui Chen

Aodi Wang

Kai Wang

Minghui Wang

Doi

10.1038/s41467-022-31780-9

PMID: 35871175 · DOI: 10.1038/s41467-022-31780-9 · Journal: Nature Communications (2022)

TL;DR

Wu et al. report the largest pan-cancer somatic-mutation landscape of an Asian (predominantly Chinese, 92% Han) cohort to date: 10,194 solid-tumor patients across 25 principal tumor types profiled with the OrigiMed CSYS 450-gene NGS panel under CLIA/CAP. They compare allele frequencies, TMB, MSI, gene fusions, and OncoKB-defined actionable variants against the MSK-IMPACT 2017 cohort (n=2,820 per arm after propensity-score matching) and TCGA, finding broadly similar mutation spectra but disease-specific differences (notably enrichment of EGFR in LUAD, KRAS in IHCH, and depletion of KEAP1 in LUAD in the Chinese cohort). 64% of patients harbored at least one OncoKB Level 1–4 actionable variant. Data are deposited as the pan_origimed_2020 cBioPortal study.

Cohort & data

  • Patients screened: 11,553 across 25 principal tumor types and >100 subtypes from China; 31 ethnicities represented (92% Han) PMID:35871175.
  • Final analyzed cohort: 10,194 (88%) tumor + matched-blood pairs after excluding insufficient tumor content, low DNA yield, or sequencing failure.
  • Geography: 41% East China, 29% South China.
  • Stage: 55% advanced (stage III/IV), 35% early (pre-cancer or stage I/II).
  • Treatment status: 76% treatment-naive, 16% pretreated, 8% unknown.
  • Principal tumor types (% of cohort): NSCLC 20%, COADREAD 12%, LIHC 11%, STAD 8%, ESCA 6%, STS 6%, IHCH 5%, PAAD 5%, EHCH 3%, BRCA 3%.
  • Assay: OrigiMed CSYS 450-gene NGS panel targeting ~2.6 Mb of coding exons of 450 genes plus the TERT promoter and select introns of 39 rearrangement-prone genes; CLIA-certified, CAP-accredited.
  • Sequencing: Median tumor unique coverage 1,202×; matched normal blood mean unique coverage 300×; Illumina NextSeq 500 / NovaSeq 6000.
  • Variant calling: SNVs via MuTect, InDels via Pindel, CNVs via EXCAVATOR; fusions via in-house algorithm; all variants manually reviewed in IGV.
  • PD-L1: Immunohistochemistry with anti-PD-L1 clone 28-8 (Abcam ab205921, 1:300); TPS ≥1% positive (analog to but distinct from 22C3).
  • Comparator cohorts: MSK-IMPACT 2017 (n=10,366; 266 common genes; aOM vs MSK = 2,820 per arm after PSM on primary/metastatic, sampling, gender, smoke) and TCGA PanCancer Atlas + ovarian cancer (aOM vs aTCGA = 1,008 per arm; SNV/InDel/truncation only).
  • Data availability: Deposited on cBioPortal as study pan_origimed_2020.

Key findings

  • Variant inventory in the analyzed cohort: 80,703 SNVs/InDels; 19,192 truncations; 17,779 gene amplifications; 1,688 homozygous deletions; 3,111 gene fusions/rearrangements PMID:35871175.
  • Top recurrently altered genes (pan-cohort frequency): TP53 58%, KRAS 18%, TERT 14%, EGFR 13%, APC 13%, CDKN2A 12%, PIK3CA 11%. Most common point hotspots: KRAS G12, EGFR L858, TP53 R273.
  • TP53 hotspots: R273, R175, R248 are the top three. R175H/L most common in COADREAD (8%) and PAAD (6%); R249S in LIHC (10%) and IHCH (3%).
  • Significant pairwise co-occurrence: EGFR and KRAS at the SNV/InDel and CNV level — an unusual pattern relative to the canonical mutual exclusivity reported in Western LUAD.
  • CNV peaks: High-frequency CDKN2A/CDKN2B deletion; MYC amplification; ERBB2 amplification; EGFR amplification; chr 11q13.3 (CCND1/FGF3/FGF4/FGF19) amplification.
  • Tumor-type CNV enrichment vs other types: ERBB2 amplification in BRCA (24 vs 2%, FDR=7.645×10⁻¹⁰⁵); 11q13.3 amplification in ESCA (43 vs 4%, FDR=3.553×10⁻³⁰¹).
  • Fusions detected: 513 fusion events spanning 31 driver genes. ALK (n=139), ROS1 (n=51), RET (n=50), FGFR2/FGFR3 (n=50), NTRK1/NTRK3 (n=30), BRAF (n=12) were broadly distributed; EWSR1 restricted to sarcomas; TFE3 restricted to KIRC. Novel partners reported include GRIK2-ROS1, PARP12-BRAF, KIF13B-MET, LRRC28-NTRK3, and new exonic boundaries in KIF5B-ALK and EML4-ALK relative to the Quiver database.
  • Clinical-feature associations (FDR<0.05): In COADREAD, 270 / 100 differentially mutated genes between younger/early-stage vs older/advanced (consistent with MSI-H and POLE-associated hypermutation in young early-stage CRC). In NSCLC, female nonsmokers with early-stage disease enriched for EGFR mutations; male smokers with advanced disease enriched for TP53, CDKN2A, PIK3CA, KRAS. Younger STAD patients had more CDH1; older more TP53/NOTCH1/FAT4. Younger LIHC patients had more TP53; younger KIRC more TFE3; younger bone sarcoma more VEGFA; older counterparts had CTNNB1, TERT, and TP53 respectively.
  • aOM vs MSK (PSM-matched, n=2,820 each, 15 tumor types, 266 shared genes): Only 12 tumor-type:gene pairs were significantly different (FDR<0.05). Most differences localized to lung adenocarcinoma and hepatobiliary tumors — notably LUAD: EGFR (higher in aOM), LUAD: KEAP1 (lower in aOM), and IHCH: KRAS. COADREAD: APC was virtually identical (71.9% vs 72.7%, FDR=1); SCLC: RB1 84.2 vs 71.1%, FDR=1.
  • aOM vs aTCGA (PSM-matched, n=1,008 each, 9 tumor types): 6 tumor-type:gene pairs differed (FDR<0.05); 3 trended consistently with the aOM-vs-MSK comparison (higher CRC:TP53, higher LUAD:EGFR, lower LUAD:KEAP1 in aOM).
  • TMB: TMB-H defined as ≥10 Muts/Mb per KEYNOTE-158. Overall density similar between aOM and MSK; a long tail of 119 samples with TMB≥40 in aOM. Significant tumor-type differences vs MSK for 7+ tumor types (p<0.05).
  • MSI: 186 MSI-H samples (~2% of cohort); 55% (102/186) in COADREAD, 26% in STAD, 7% in IHCH.
  • Combined IO biomarker positivity (MSI-H OR TMB-H OR PD-L1 TPS≥1%) in 2,723 evaluable tumors: 30.3% (824/2,723); highest in SCLC (48%, 24/50), NSCLC (46%, 298/648), ESCA (34%, 80/235).
  • CD274 amplification: 85 (1%) tumors had CD274 amplification (copy ≥6); co-occurred with PDCD1LG2 (89%) and JAK2 (82%) amplifications on 9p24.3-9p22.2; 30 evaluable cases had a 70% PD-L1 IHC positive rate; 78% harbored concurrent TP53 mutations.
  • OncoKB actionability: 64% of patients (n=6,498) harbored at least one Level 1–4 actionable variant when TMB-H was included (Level 1: 32%; 2: 1%; 3A: 1%; 3B: 13%; 4: 16%). Excluding TMB-H, 58% (Level 1: 17.9%; 2: 1.5%; 3A: 1.5%; 3B: 17.8%; 4: 19.2%). Removing TMB-H drops Level 1 to 18%. Of 3,696 patients without Level 1–4 variants, 4% were PD-L1 IHC positive — candidates for checkpoint blockade outside the OncoKB framework.
  • TMB-H × fusion in NSCLC: Negative association — 3% fusion frequency in TMB-H vs 13% in TMB-L (P=1.31×10⁻¹¹), driven mostly by ALK. MSI-H × fusion: positive association (6% vs 1%, P=0.04), driven mostly by NTRK1/NTRK3.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — 58% pan-cohort SNV/InDel; hotspots R273, R175, R248; R249S enriched in LIHC (10%) and IHCH (3%) consistent with aflatoxin signature.
  • KRAS — 18% pan-cohort; G12 hotspot; significantly elevated in IHCH vs MSK; G12C in 2.7% of NSCLC (sotorasib Level 1).
  • EGFR — 13% pan-cohort; elevated in LUAD vs MSK and TCGA; L858R 20% and exon 19 deletion 19% within NSCLC; G719 mutations in 3% of NSCLC; pairwise co-occurrence with KRAS noted (atypical for Western cohorts).
  • APC — 13% pan-cohort; 72% of COADREAD, indistinguishable from MSK.
  • CDKN2A — 12%; deep deletion peak in CNV analysis; LoF in 20% of PAAD (CDK4/6 inhibitor candidate per OncoKB).
  • PIK3CA — 11%; canonical hotspots (C420R, E542K, E545A/D/G/K, H1047L/R/Y, Q546E/R) actionable to alpelisib in 31% of BRCA and 7.4% of CRC.
  • ERBB2 — amplification in 24% of BRCA (vs 2% in other types); HER2-amp BRCA actionable with lapatinib, trastuzumab+pertuzumab, neratinib, margetuximab, and trastuzumab+tucatinib. ERBB2 GoF mutations in 3% of NSCLC actionable with T-DM1 (ado-trastuzumab-emtansine) and trastuzumab-deruxtecan.
  • ALK fusions — 6.7% of NSCLC; actionable with crizotinib, ceritinib, alectinib.
  • ARID1A truncating mutations — 7.9% LIHC, 9.9% IHCH, 7.2% STAD; PLX2853 / tazemetostat (Level 4).
  • BRAF V600E — 4.4% of COADREAD; encorafenib+cetuximab Level 1.
  • TSC2 LoF — 4.9% of LIHC; everolimus Level 4.
  • CDK4 amplification — 10.9% of STS; abemaciclib/palbociclib Level 4.
  • KEAP1 — significantly lower in aOM LUAD vs MSK and TCGA.
  • CD274/PDCD1LG2/JAK2 — co-amplified 9p24.3-9p22.2 cluster; CD274 amplification in 1% of tumors, 70% PD-L1 IHC positive.
  • TERT — promoter mutations captured by the panel; 14% pan-cohort.
  • TFE3 — fusion-restricted to KIRC (Xp11 translocation RCC).
  • EWSR1 — fusion-restricted to sarcomas.
  • FGFR2, FGFR3, NTRK1, NTRK3, RET, ROS1, MET — pan-cancer fusion drivers with multiple novel partner genes catalogued.

Clinical implications

  • Actionability framework for Asian patients: ~64% of Chinese solid-tumor patients harbor an OncoKB Level 1–4 variant including TMB-H — a baseline against which institutional precision-oncology programs in China can benchmark.
  • NSCLC: erlotinib ± ramucirumab for EGFR L858R / exon 19 del; afatinib for G719; sotorasib for KRAS G12C; ALK-fusion TKIs; pembrolizumab for TMB-H. TMB-H × fusion mutual exclusivity argues against combining IO with ALK-TKIs in many TMB-H tumors.
  • CRC: pembrolizumab for MSI-H (8.3% of cases); nivolumab±ipilimumab for MSI-H; encorafenib+cetuximab for BRAF V600E; MEK inhibitors (binimetinib, cobimetinib, trametinib) for KRAS GoF; alpelisib for PIK3CA GoF.
  • HER2-amp BRCA (24%): dual HER2 blockade (trastuzumab+pertuzumab), lapatinib, neratinib, tucatinib.
  • PAAD: abemaciclib/palbociclib/ribociclib for CDKN2A LoF (20%); MEK inhibitors for KRAS (74% of PAAD have KRAS GoF actionable to MEKi at Level 3B/4).
  • LIHC: everolimus for TSC2 LoF; tazemetostat for ARID1A truncation; pembrolizumab for TMB-H (15.8% of LIHC are TMB-H Level-1 candidates).
  • Triple-biomarker IO triage (MSI-H ∨ TMB-H ∨ PD-L1+): captures 48% of SCLC, 46% of NSCLC, 34% of ESCA — argues for broad biomarker-panel testing rather than single-marker IHC.
  • CD274 amplification as an orthogonal IO biomarker: ~1% of tumors qualify even when MSI/TMB/PD-L1 IHC are negative; consider when first-line biomarkers fail.

Limitations & open questions

  • Confounders only partially controlled. PSM balanced primary/metastatic status, sampling method, gender, smoke, age, stage and grade but the authors acknowledge unmeasured confounders persist (sequencing depth, tumor purity, subtype heterogeneity, ancestry beyond Han).
  • Methodological heterogeneity vs TCGA. Only SNVs/InDels/truncations were comparable to TCGA (different platforms, callers, and variant criteria); CNVs and fusions could not be benchmarked against TCGA in this study.
  • TMB algorithm. No consensus pipeline for clinical TMB; authors used in-house calculation against KEYNOTE-158 thresholds — TMB-H values are panel-specific and not portable between assays.
  • MSI method. In-house bioinformatic MSI caller (>15% unstable loci) rather than the canonical Bethesda panel or MSIsensor — cross-study MSI-H rate comparisons require caution.
  • PD-L1 antibody clone. Clone 28-8 (used here) is not the regulatory companion diagnostic (22C3 / Dako is); cross-trial PD-L1 numbers will differ.
  • Coverage of the panel. Targets 450 cancer-related genes (~2.6 Mb), so signature analysis (mutational signatures, HRD, large-scale rearrangements) is limited compared to WGS/WES studies.
  • No outcome data. The cohort is cross-sectional somatic landscape; survival, response to therapy, and longitudinal resistance trajectories are not reported.
  • Open question: How much of the EGFR–KRAS co-occurrence in this cohort is biological (Asian-specific Han subset selection on EGFR-driven LUAD) versus tumor-heterogeneity / subclonal admixture artifact requiring single-cell or multiregion sequencing to resolve.
  • Open question: Whether the lower KEAP1 frequency in Chinese LUAD translates to differential sensitivity to checkpoint blockade or KEAP1-pathway therapeutics relative to North American patients.
