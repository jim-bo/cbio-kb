**PMID:** 34667026 · **DOI:** 10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-21-2577 · **Journal:** Clinical Cancer Research (2022)

## TL;DR

In a single-institution Memorial Sloan Kettering cohort of 2,069 men with prostate cancer who received clinical-grade [MSK-IMPACT](../methods/msk-impact-panel.md) tumor–normal sequencing (1,841 self-reported White, 165 Black, 63 Asian), Stopsack et al. show that somatic genomic differences by self-reported race largely persist after adjusting for age, PSA, disease extent, stage, and sample type. Tumors from Black men carried more [AR](../genes/AR.md) alterations and chromosome 8q gains and fewer [PTEN](../genes/PTEN.md) and [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) alterations than those from White men; tumors from Asian men were enriched for class-1 forkhead-domain [FOXA1](../genes/FOXA1.md) mutations and [ZFHX3](../genes/ZFHX3.md) alterations. Genetic ancestry (inferred from 5,072 1KGP SNPs and supervised ADMIXTURE) tracked self-reported race, but area-level household income was independently associated with chr8q gain, arguing that ancestry differences cannot be interpreted as purely germline-driven.

## Cohort & data

- **Cohort:** 2,069 men with histologically confirmed [prostate adenocarcinoma](../cancer_types/PRAD.md) sequenced at MSK (1,841 White, 165 Black, 63 Asian by self-report).
- **Dataset:** [prad_msk_stopsack_2021](../datasets/prad_msk_stopsack_2021.md) — clinical and genomic data available on cBioPortal at `https://www.cbioportal.org/study/summary?id=prad_msk_stopsack_2021`.
- **Assay:** FDA-cleared [MSK-IMPACT](../methods/msk-impact-panel.md) targeted hybridization-capture panel, 341 to 468 genes depending on version ([IMPACT341](../methods/IMPACT341.md), [IMPACT410](../methods/IMPACT410.md), [IMPACT468](../methods/IMPACT468.md)); captures SNVs, indels, CNAs, and rearrangements. [ZFHX3](../genes/ZFHX3.md) was only on later panel versions, so only 1,924 samples (93%) were profiled for it.
- **Germline:** 1,781 men (86%) consented to germline analysis, with no difference in consent by race.
- **Oncogenicity annotation:** [OncoKB](../methods/oncokb-annotation.md) v2.8 (Sep 17, 2020).
- **Ancestry inference:** 5,072 bi-allelic autosomal SNPs from 1000 Genomes Project within MSK-IMPACT468 baits; supervised [ADMIXTURE](../methods/admixture.md) v1.3 to [estimate](../methods/estimate.md) AFR, EUR, EAS, NAM, SAS proportions; arm-level CNAs called with [ASCETS](../methods/ascets.md).

## Key findings

- **Clinical baseline differences ([PMID:34667026](../papers/34667026.md), Table 1).** Black men were diagnosed 3.0 years younger (95% CI, 0.6 to 5.4) than White men, had higher median PSA at diagnosis (17 vs. 9 ng/mL; difference 8.2 ng/mL, 95% CI 0.1 to 16.3), and lived in zip codes with average household income 36% lower (95% CI 30 to 42%). Current smoking was twice as prevalent in Black men (13%) as in White men (6%; difference 7 percentage points, 95% CI 2 to 12). Asian men had a more advanced stage at sample procurement (32% castration-resistant vs. 19% White; difference 13 pp, 95% CI 1 to 24).
- **[AR](../genes/AR.md) alterations (Fig. 2).** Observed in 18% of Black, 19% of Asian, and 12% of White tumors. After adjusting for clinical features, the Asian–White difference attenuated fully (0 pp, 95% CI −5 to 6) whereas the Black–White difference persisted (5 pp, 95% CI 1 to 8).
- **[FOXA1](../genes/FOXA1.md) alterations.** 27% of Asian-men tumors vs. 13% White (adjusted difference 15 pp, 95% CI 6 to 26), driven by class-1 forkhead-domain mutations (21% Asian vs. 8% White; Supplementary Fig. 3A).
- **ZFHX3 alterations.** 17% Asian vs. 7% White (adjusted difference 8 pp, 95% CI 1 to 17).
- **[TP53](../genes/TP53.md) alterations.** Less common in Black men (19%) than White (30%; adjusted difference −10 pp, 95% CI −17 to −3), driven by fewer missense mutations (11% Black vs. 18% White).
- **[PTEN](../genes/PTEN.md) alterations.** Less common in Black (14%) and Asian men than in White men (20%; adjusted Black–White difference −7 pp, 95% CI −12 to −1), particularly fewer mutations (8% Black vs. 3% White — note the paper reports the directionality as "fewer", values per Supplementary Fig. 3C; adjusted difference −4 pp, 95% CI −7 to 0).
- **No difference in DNA-repair alterations** ([ERCC5](../genes/ERCC5.md), [MRE11](../genes/MRE11.md), [TP53BP1](../genes/TP53BP1.md), [POLE](../genes/POLE.md), [RAD21](../genes/RAD21.md), [MSH2](../genes/MSH2.md), [MSH6](../genes/MSH6.md), [BRCA1](../genes/BRCA1.md), [BRCA2](../genes/BRCA2.md), [ATR](../genes/ATR.md), [ATM](../genes/ATM.md)) by race, contrasting with the prior Project GENIE-based analysis by Mahal et al. (NEJM 2020). MSI prevalence and TMB also did not differ by race (Supplementary Table 1).
- **Chr8q gain (Fig. 4).** 49% of Black, 47% of Asian, and 37% of White tumors had chr8q gains. After clinical-feature adjustment the Asian–White difference attenuated (5 pp, 95% CI −6 to 17) but the Black–White difference persisted (11 pp, 95% CI 4 to 18). 100% AFR ancestry vs. 0% AFR was associated with +13 pp chr8q gain (95% CI 4 to 23).
- **Chr8q gain is independently prognostic.** Among White men, chr8q gain HR for death = 1.61 (95% CI 1.33 to 1.96); among Black men, HR = 2.00 (95% CI 1.00 to 4.01), each adjusted for disease extent at sequencing.
- **Income is independently associated with chr8q gain.** Each $50,000/yr increase in area-level median household income reduced chr8q-gain prevalence by 2 pp (95% CI −4 to 0) after adjusting for self-reported race and inferred ancestry. Adjusting for income reduced the Black–White chr8q-gain difference from 13 to 10 pp (95% CI 0 to 20).
- **Germline contribution is not the driver.** Of the genes with race/ancestry-associated prevalence differences, none had more than a single germline mutation among the 1,781 germline-consented patients (Supplementary Table 2).
- **Overall survival.** Among Black men with follow-up (n=156), HR vs. White = 1.15 (95% CI 0.85 to 1.57); not significantly different but cannot exclude an effect.

## Genes & alterations

- [AR](../genes/AR.md) — alterations enriched in tumors from Black and Asian men; only the Black-vs-White difference persists after clinical adjustment (adjusted difference +5 pp, 95% CI 1 to 8).
- [BRAF](../genes/BRAF.md) — modestly higher in Black men, consistent with prior reports; targeted-therapy relevance noted by authors.
- [FOXA1](../genes/FOXA1.md) — class-1 forkhead-domain mutations enriched in Asian men (21% vs. 8% White); same structural class previously described as early-occurring activating events.
- [ZFHX3](../genes/ZFHX3.md) — mutations and CNAs enriched in Asian men (17% vs. 7% White); profiled in 93% of samples due to later panel inclusion.
- [TP53](../genes/TP53.md) — fewer alterations in Black men (19% vs. 30% White), specifically fewer missense mutations; R273 hotspot prevalence is only 2% in White men.
- [PTEN](../genes/PTEN.md) — fewer alterations in Black and Asian men, particularly fewer mutations; relevant to PI3K-inhibitor strategies in prostate cancer.
- [MYC](../genes/MYC.md) — copy-number gains enriched in Black men; co-located on chromosome arm 8q whose gain is the dominant CNA difference.
- [TMPRSS2](../genes/TMPRSS2.md) / [ERG](../genes/ERG.md) — [TMPRSS2](../genes/TMPRSS2.md):[ERG](../genes/ERG.md) fusions intentionally excluded from race comparisons due to suboptimal panel sensitivity for fusions; known to be more common in European-ancestry men.
- DNA-repair genes ([ERCC5](../genes/ERCC5.md), [MRE11](../genes/MRE11.md), [TP53BP1](../genes/TP53BP1.md), [POLE](../genes/POLE.md), [RAD21](../genes/RAD21.md), [MSH2](../genes/MSH2.md), [MSH6](../genes/MSH6.md), [BRCA1](../genes/BRCA1.md), [BRCA2](../genes/BRCA2.md), [ATR](../genes/ATR.md), [ATM](../genes/ATM.md)) — no significant prevalence difference by race, contradicting one prior Project GENIE-based report.

## Clinical implications

- The persistence of AR and [BRAF](../genes/BRAF.md) alteration differences in Black men, even after controlling for healthcare-access proxies, supports the case that race-aware reporting of tumor genomics may inform pre-test expectations and treatment selection.
- Lower PTEN alteration rates in non-White men may translate into smaller [absolute](../methods/absolute.md) benefit from PI3K-pathway inhibitors (e.g., ipatasertib), which had reached phase 3 in mCRPC at the time of publication.
- Higher chr8q-gain prevalence is associated with worse survival in both Black and White men; therapeutic targets on chr8q beyond [MYC](../genes/MYC.md) may be especially relevant for Black patients.
- Authors explicitly argue against attributing tumor-genomic racial differences solely to germline genetics: at most one germline mutation per race-associated gene was observed across 1,781 consented patients, and area-level income independently predicted chr8q gain.

## Limitations & open questions

- Single-institution hospital-based cohort with sequenced patients only — denominator of men at risk is unknown, precluding causal attribution.
- Black men (n=165) and Asian men (n=63) are underrepresented; the study cannot resolve Hispanic ethnicity, EAS vs. SAS, or within-AFR substructure (Northern/Eastern/Western African ancestry collapsed).
- TMPRSS2:ERG fusions and other rearrangements excluded due to panel sensitivity limitations; relevant given known ancestry differences in fusion prevalence.
- ZFHX3 only on later MSK-IMPACT versions, leaving 7% of samples unprofiled.
- Some patients overlap with the prior Mahal et al. Project GENIE analysis, so findings are not a fully independent replication of prevalence-difference estimates.
- Asian-men cohort (n=58 with follow-up, 15 deaths) was too small for survival analysis.
- Area-level (zip-code) household income is a crude, ecological proxy for socioeconomic status; cannot pinpoint which specific modifiable factors (education, pollution, food insecurity, violence) drive the chr8q association.
