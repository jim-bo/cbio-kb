Molecular Subtypes of Diffuse Large B-cell Lymphoma are Associated with Distinct Pathogenic Mechanisms and Outcomes

Authors

Bjoern Chapuy

Chip Stewart

Andrew J. Dunford

Jaegil Kim

Atanas Kamburov

Robert A. Redd

Mike S. Lawrence

Donna S. Neuberg

Gad Getz

Margaret A. Shipp

Doi

10.1038/s41591-018-0016-8

PMID: 29713087 · DOI: 10.1038/s41591-018-0016-8 · Journal: Nature Medicine (2018)

TL;DR

Chapuy and colleagues performed whole-exome sequencing with a spiked-in structural-variant bait set on 304 primary diffuse large B-cell lymphomas (DLBCLNOS) — 85% of whom received R-CHOP-like therapy with long-term follow-up (median 78.5 months) — and integrated recurrent mutations, somatic copy number alterations (SCNAs) and structural variants (SVs) via non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) consensus clustering. They defined five robust genetic subtypes (C1–C5) plus a driver-poor C0 group, identified 98 candidate cancer genes including 40 previously undescribed drivers, and showed that the coordinate genetic signatures predict progression-free and overall survival independently of the International Prognostic Index (IPI). The framework refines transcriptional cell-of-origin (COO) classification by splitting ABC-DLBCL into a favorable extrafollicular C1 and an unfavorable MYD88L265P/CD79B C5 subset, and GCB-DLBCL into a favorable histone/BCR-mutant C4 and an unfavorable BCL2-SV/EZH2/PTEN C3 subset, with a fifth C2 subset characterized by biallelic TP53 inactivation and CDKN2A loss PMID:29713087.

Cohort & data

  • Patients: 304 newly diagnosed primary DLBCLs that passed QC (out of 351 collected); 85% (259/304) received R-CHOP-like therapy with median follow-up 78.5 months PMID:29713087.
  • Sources: 129 RICOVER-60 prospective trial samples (Pfreundschuh et al., Lancet Oncol 2008), 103 DFCI/Brigham & Women’s, 67 Mayo Clinic/University of Iowa SPORE (including 51 previously published WES), and 5 University of Göttingen samples.
  • Paired-normal status: 44% (135/304) had patient-matched normals; 55% (168/304) were FFPE-derived. New computational filters (Panel-of-Normals, ExAC frequency cutoff, germline-somatic log-odds, OxoG/FFPE orientation-bias filtering) were developed to call somatic events on tumor-only samples.
  • Cancer type: DLBCLNOS; the cohort includes occasional cases with extranodal/PCNSL or TT (primary testicular lymphoma) involvement that cluster with C5.
  • Dataset: dlbcl_dfci_2018. Raw WES deposited in dbGaP under accession phs000450.v1.p1. Affymetrix U133plus2 expression data in GEO under GSE98588 PMID:29713087.
  • Assays/pipeline: Whole-exome sequencing with Agilent SureSelect Human All Exon v2.0 + spiked-in SV bait set; median tumor coverage 87.6× (range 39–206.8×). Targeted SV capture sequenced to mean depth 221.4× on 296/304 samples. Reads aligned with BWA to GRCh37 via Broad’s Picard/Firehose pipeline. Variants called with MuTect and Indelocator, annotated with Oncotator. Significantly mutated genes called with MutSig2CV (q ≤ 0.1); spatial mutation clustering with CLUMPS. Significant SCNAs called with GISTIC2.0. Purity/ploidy/CCF estimated with ABSOLUTE. Mutational signatures discovered with Bayesian NMF via SignatureAnalyzer. SVs called by four-algorithm consensus: BreaKmer, Lumpy, dRanger, SvABA, with Breakpointer validation.

Key findings

  • 98 candidate cancer genes (CCGs) identified by MutSig2CV at q < 0.1, of which 40 were previously undescribed drivers in DLBCL; an additional 7 CCGs (including MAP2K1) were captured only by CLUMPS 3D protein-structure clustering PMID:29713087.
  • Mutational signatures: Three dominant processes — spontaneous CpG deamination (“Aging”) accounting for ~80% of mutations, canonical AID (cAID) at RCY motifs, and AID2 at WA motifs (resembling COSMIC9/non-canonical AID). cAID activity localized to known aberrant somatic hypermutation targets (BCL2, SGK1, PIM1, IGLL5).
  • SVs detected in 64% (189/296) of tumors. Most frequently rearranged genes: IGH (40%), BCL2 (21%), BCL6 (19%), MYC (8%), CD274/PD-L1 and PDCD1LG2/PD-L2 (5% combined), TBL1XR1 (4%), TP63 (3%), CIITA (3%), ETV6 (2%).
  • SCNAs: GISTIC2.0 detected 18 arm-level and 18 focal copy gains, 2 arm-level and 32 focal copy losses (q ≤ 0.1, frequency ≥ 3%); SCNA frequencies ranged 5–32%. CCGs were highly enriched within focal SCNAs (Fisher’s exact P = 1×10⁻⁴⁴).
  • Five coordinate genetic subtypes (C1–C5) identified by NMF consensus clustering on a gene-sample matrix of 158 driver alterations, plus a C0 driver-poor group of 12 tumors:
    • C1 (n=56): ABC-DLBCLs with BCL6 SVs, NOTCH2 PEST-domain activating mutations, SPEN truncations, BCL10/TNFAIP3/FAS mutations, immune-escape via B2M/CD70/PD-L1/PD-L2; features of extrafollicular/marginal-zone origin; low cAID activity; favorable outcome.
    • C2 (n=64): COO-independent; biallelic TP53 inactivation (mutation + 17p loss), CDKN2A loss, 13q14.2/RB1 loss, MCL1 (1q23.3) gain, genomic instability with elevated whole-genome doubling (P < 0.001), highest driver SCNA burden (P < 0.0001).
    • C3 (n=55): GCB-DLBCLs with BCL2 mutations concordant with BCL2-IgH SVs (Fisher P = 3.3×10⁻³⁵), chromatin modifier mutations (KMT2D, CREBBP, EZH2), MEF2B/IRF8 mutations, TNFSF14/HVCN1/GNA13 mutations, dual PTEN inactivation (10q23.31 loss and truncating mutations); unfavorable outcome.
    • C4 (n=51): GCB-DLBCLs with H1 linker and core histone mutations (H1-2, H1-3, H1-4, H1-5 and HIST1H2 family), CD83/CD58/CD70 immune-escape mutations, RHOA/GNA13/SGK1 BCR-PI3K mutations, NFKBIE/NFKBIA/CARD11 NF-kB mutations, BRAF/STAT3 RAS/JAK/STAT mutations; significantly higher mutational density (P < 0.0001); favorable outcome.
    • C5 (n=64): ABC-DLBCLs with near-uniform 18q gain (BCL2/MALT1), concurrent MYD88L265P (50%, 30/60) and CD79B (48%, 29/60) mutations (co-occurrence P = 0.036), additional ETV6/PIM1/TBL1XR1/BTG1 mutations; highest cAID contribution; 96% (45/47) of cases with available COO designation were ABC (P < 0.001); 8/9 testicular cases and 1/2 CNS cases fell in this cluster (P < 0.001); unfavorable outcome.
  • Median 17 genetic drivers per tumor (range 0–48).
  • Prognostic individual alterations (PFS, R-CHOP cohort n=259): 1q42.12 loss, MYC SVs, 18q21.33/BCL2 gain, 13q31.3/miR-17–92 gain, and 18p gain were independently predictive of inferior PFS and retained significance when added to IPI risk groups; MYC SVs, 13q31.3 gain and 1q41.12 loss were also associated with shorter OS. The prognostic individual events were predominantly SCNAs or SVs rather than mutations (Fisher’s exact PFS P = 0.007; OS P = 0.02).
  • Cluster outcomes: C0/C1/C4 patients had favorable PFS/OS; C3/C5 had unfavorable outcomes; C2 patients exhibited a distinct steady rate of progression over time. In multivariate models combining IPI and genetic signature with low-risk-IPI + favorable-signature as reference: C5 features alone HR = 2.01, high-risk IPI alone HR = 3.44, and the combination HR ≈ 6.91. Genetic signature contributed independently of IPI PMID:29713087.
  • Clonality/timing: 74% of mutations, 49% of SCNAs and 57% of SVs were clonal (CCF ≥ 0.9). In C1, NOTCH2/SPEN/BCL10/CD70/B2M mutations were largely clonal whereas BCL6 SVs and PD-1 ligand SVs were often subclonal — supporting BCL6 rearrangement as a late, potentially transforming event. In C2, 17p loss was clonal and preceded TP53 mutation. In C5, CD79B/MYD88/TBL1XR1 mutations were clonal whereas 18q gain and PIM1/BTG1/ETV6 mutations were more often subclonal.
  • Immune escape: 74% of DLBCLs carried at least one genetic basis of immune escape — MHC class I loci, B2M, CD70, CD58, CD274/PD-L1, PDCD1LG2/PD-L2 or CIITA alterations.

Genes & alterations

  • MYD88 — L265P mutations enriched in C5 ABC-DLBCL (P < 0.001 vs C1), often co-occurring with CD79B mutations; non-L265P MYD88 mutations occur in C1 and signal extrafollicular biology.
  • CD79B — mutated in 48% (29/60) of C5 tumors; defines BCR-pathway addiction.
  • CD274 (PD-L1) and PDCD1LG2 (PD-L2) — SVs juxtaposing intact ORFs to multiple regulatory elements drive PD-L1/PD-L2 overexpression, identified in ~5% of DLBCLs, enriched in C1.
  • BCL2 — IgH-juxtaposed SVs in C3 GCB-DLBCL (97% partner IgH); 18q21.33/BCL2 focal gain in C5 ABC-DLBCL is independently prognostic for inferior PFS.
  • BCL6 — SVs in 19% of cohort; clonal-to-subclonal timing analysis supports BCL6 rearrangement as a late event in C1.
  • MYC — SVs in 8%; independently prognostic for inferior PFS and OS (multivariable Cox).
  • TP53 — biallelic inactivation by mutation + 17p copy loss defines C2; CLUMPS analysis localizes mutations to the DNA-binding site and Zn²⁺-coordinating residues.
  • CDKN2A — 9p21.13 copy loss is a defining feature of C2 alongside 13q14.2/RB1 loss; together perturb chromosomal stability and cell cycle.
  • KMT2D, CREBBP, EP300, EZH2 — chromatin-modifier mutations enriched in C3 GCB-DLBCL with concordant upregulation of EZH2 targets by GSEA.
  • PTEN — focal 10q23.31 loss and truncating mutations in C3, with N- and C-terminal mutations distinguishing roles in PI3K/AKT antagonism and genomic stability.
  • NOTCH2 and SPEN — activating PEST-domain NOTCH2 mutations and truncating SPEN mutations characterize C1 with marginal-zone–like biology; clonal events.
  • BCL10, TNFAIP3, FAS — NF-kB pathway and apoptosis-mediator mutations in C1.
  • RHOA — mutations cluster at ARHGEF-binding interface (CLUMPS/EMPRINT), keeping RHOA inactive and de-repressing PI3K/Gα-migration; enriched in C4 GCB tumors.
  • GNA13, SGK1, TNFSF14, HVCN1 — BCR/PI3K signaling modifiers in C3/C4.
  • CARD11, NFKBIE, NFKBIA — NF-kB modifiers in C4.
  • BRAF, KRAS, MAP2K1, STAT3 — RAS/JAK/STAT pathway mutations enriched in C4; non-canonical BRAF mutations perturb the P-loop/activation-loop autoinhibitory interaction.
  • B2M, CD58, CD70, CIITA — immune-escape genes with mutations and copy loss; collectively altered in 74% of DLBCLs.
  • H1-2, H1-3, H1-4, H1-5 — linker histone mutations (also recurrent in HIST1H2 core histones) define C4; globular/C-terminal domain mutations likely reduce chromatin association and raise local mutation rate.
  • PIM1, IGLL5 — aberrant somatic-hypermutation targets with predominant AID signatures; recurrent in C5.
  • TBL1XR1, BTG1, ETV6 — recurrent in C5; clonal TBL1XR1 with subclonal BTG1/ETV6.
  • TP63 — recurrent SV partner (TBL1XR1/TP63 fusion) in 3% of cohort.
  • CCND3 (6p21.1), JAK2/CD274/PDCD1LG2 (9p24.1), MALT1 (18q) — examples of CCGs simultaneously perturbed by mutation, copy gain and SV.
  • FBXW7 — CLUMPS identified clustering at the acceptor groove that impairs CCNE1 recognition; previously described tumor-suppressor mechanism in other cancers.

Clinical implications

  • Genetic signatures predict outcome independent of the IPI (multivariable Cox: C5 HR = 2.01 vs favorable-signature reference for low-risk IPI patients; combination with high-risk IPI HR ≈ 6.91). The authors propose coordinate genetic signatures as a new axis for DLBCL risk stratification alongside IPI.
  • Targeted-therapy hypotheses by cluster raised by the authors PMID:29713087:
    • C5 (ABC, MYD88L265P/CD79B/18q-BCL2): BCR/TLR-proximal inhibition (BTK, IRAK4) and BCL2 antagonism (e.g. venetoclax-like agents).
    • C1 (ABC, marginal-zone–like, BCL6/NOTCH2/SPEN): NOTCH inhibition, BCL6 inhibition, and immune-checkpoint–targeted approaches given frequent PD-L1/PD-L2 SVs and B2M/CD70/FAS loss.
    • C3 (GCB, BCL2-SV/EZH2/PTEN): BCL2 inhibition, PI3K inhibition, EZH2 and CREBBP-targeted epigenetic therapy.
    • C4 (GCB, BCR/PI3K/JAK-STAT/BRAF + histone): JAK/STAT and MEK/BRAF blockade.
    • C2 (TP53/CDKN2A, genomic-instability): no immediately actionable target highlighted; the steady progression trajectory suggests a distinct treatment paradigm is needed.
  • Refining “double-hit”/“triple-hit” DLBCL: Co-occurring BCL2 and MYC SVs were significantly enriched in C3 (Fisher P = 0.003), but multiple clusters carried alternative mechanisms of BCL2/BCL6/MYC deregulation (e.g. C5 18q-BCL2 gain, C1 BCL6 SVs, C2 13q31.3/miR-17–92 gain). The authors argue current double-/triple-hit definitions based solely on SVs are insufficiently precise.
  • MYD88L265P + extranodal tropism: Concentration of testicular (8/9, P < 0.001) and CNS (1/2) lymphomas in C5 generalizes prior targeted-sequencing observations of PCNSL and primary TT lymphoma genetics (Chapuy et al. Blood 2016, PMID:26702065) to systemic DLBCLs with extranodal involvement.

Limitations & open questions

  • 55% of samples (168/304) lacked patient-matched normals and 55% were FFPE-derived. Although the authors developed an extensive tumor-only filtering pipeline (PoN, ExAC, germline-somatic log-odds, OxoG/FFPE orientation filters) and showed empirically that residual germline events are randomly distributed (beta-binomial P = 0.41) with no FFPE-biased drivers after FDR correction (one 21q22.3 focal amp removed), some sensitivity loss for LOH detection in tumor-only samples is acknowledged.
  • No chromothripsis detected, despite the high C2 SCNA burden; this is noted as a discrepancy with other cancer cohorts and may reflect cohort composition or detection-method sensitivity.
  • C0 driver-poor cluster (12 samples) is enriched in T-cell/histiocyte-rich LBCLs (P < 0.001) but the absence of detectable drivers may reflect lower tumor purity rather than a genuinely distinct biology — open question.
  • Cluster boundaries vs. transcriptional COO: Although C1 and C5 are both predominantly ABC and C3 and C4 are both predominantly GCB, the framework implicitly assumes the genetic axis is the more clinically informative one. Independent validation of cluster outcomes in non-R-CHOP-treated cohorts and modern triplet/quadruplet regimens (e.g. R-CHOP + targeted agents) is needed.
  • Clinical actionability of cluster-specific therapy hypotheses is asserted but not tested. The C2 trajectory of steady progression over time is unexplained mechanistically.
  • Coverage of non-coding drivers: WES misses non-coding regulatory mutations (e.g. BCL6 promoter SHM) that may further refine subtypes.
