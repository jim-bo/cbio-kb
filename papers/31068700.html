TL;DR

Ghandi et al. expand the Cancer Cell Line Encyclopedia (CCLE) with a next-generation multi-omic characterization of 1,072 cancer cell lines, layering whole-exome (326 lines), whole-genome (329 lines), deep RNA-seq (1,019 lines), reduced-representation bisulfite methylation (843 lines), histone H3 mass-spec profiling (897 lines), microRNA profiling (954 lines) and RPPA protein/phospho-protein quantification (899 lines) on top of the original genetic and expression backbone. Integrating these data with Project Achilles/DRIVE shRNA and CRISPR–Cas9 dependency screens reveals new paralogue synthetic lethalities (RPP25/RPP25L, LDHA/LDHB), identifies MDM4 exon-6 inclusion (regulated by RPL22/RPL22L1) as a predictor of sensitivity to MDM2 inhibitors such as nutlin-3a, and nominates phospho-SHP2 (pY542) as a marker of ponatinib sensitivity in AML and CML.

Cohort & data

  • 1,072 human cancer cell lines spanning multiple lineages and ethnicities (MIXED pan-cancer cohort, with disease-specific findings in AML, CML, and SKCM melanoma).
  • Profiling matrices: whole-exome seq (326), whole-genome seq (329), deep RNA-seq (1,019), RainDance targeted sequencing (657, plus 950 in total per Methods), Sanger GDSC WES (1,001; 667 overlapping), RRBS promoter methylation (843; 17,182 promoter regions covered), Nanostring miRNA profiling of 734 miRNAs (954 lines), mass-spec global chromatin profiling on 42 H3 peptide marks (897 lines), and RPPA for 213 antibodies (899 lines).
  • Dataset slug: ccle_broad_2019 (this study’s release); related cell-line resource cellline_ccle_broad.
  • Functional perturbation data integrated from Project Achilles and DRIVE (shRNA / sgRNA dependency screens).
  • Companion metabolomic dataset of 225 metabolites across 928 lines reported in parallel work.

Key findings

  • Cell-line concordance and drift. Comparison of germline variant calls between CCLE and Sanger GDSC for 667 overlapping lines showed high concordance (Pearson r = 0.95 for allelic fractions); 3 of 667 (0.4%) overlapping lines had mismatching germline calls suggestive of mislabelling. Cancer hotspot somatic variants correlated at r = 0.92, but non-hotspot somatic variants only at r = 0.8; 3–10% of lines (at cut-offs 0.60 or 0.75) showed substantial somatic-variant differences indicative of genetic drift.
  • TERT promoter mutations detected in 16.7% (84/503) of cell lines surveyed by WGS and targeted sequencing — the most common non-coding somatic mutation in the panel.
  • Mismatch repair / MSI. Among 65 inferred-MSI lines, 17 had MLH1 hypermethylation and 38 had genomic alterations in MSH2 or MSH6. MSH6 protein loss tracks with truncating MSH2 mutations, consistent with MSH2–MSH6 heterodimer co-stability. COSMIC signatures 6, 21, 26, 15 (MSI) and 5, 1 (clock-like) correlated with higher genetic drift in cell lines.
  • Fusion-driven dependencies. ESR1–CCDC170 and AFF1–KMT2A fusions identified as drivers conferring dependence on ESR1 and AFF1, respectively (cross-checked against RNAi loss-of-function data).
  • Lineage methylation → dependency. Promoter hypomethylation of lineage transcription factors SOX10, PAX8, HNF1B, and HNF4A correlates with selective dependence on those factors; SOX10 hypomethylation and expression are restricted to melanoma lines and predict SOX10 knockdown sensitivity.
  • Paralogue synthetic lethalities driven by methylation.
    • RPP25 promoter methylation silences RPP25 expression in bladder, ovary, endometrium, and glioma lineages and creates dependence on the paralogue RPP25L; RPP25 silencing also predicts sensitivity to POP7 knockout (both components of RNase P / RNase MRP complexes).
    • LDHB promoter methylation marks vulnerability to LDHA knockout, and conversely LDHA methylation marks LDHB dependence — a reciprocal paralogue synthetic-lethal pair. The same methylation patterns are observed in primary tumours (TCGA).
  • VHL inactivation by methylation. Three renal clear cell lines lack VHL mutation/deletion of both alleles but instead carry one VHL deletion and one methylated VHL allele, extending the “VHL-null” genotype in renal clear cell.
  • Histone-modification clusters. Mass-spec H3 profiling separated lines enriched for EZH2 and NSD2 mutations (as previously reported) and revealed a novel cluster with elevated H3K18ac/H3K27ac enriched for truncating EP300 and CREBBP mutations downstream of the HAT domain — proposed as the first cancer-associated gain-of-function alterations of p300/CBP.
  • MDM4 splicing predicts MDM2-inhibitor response. Among genes whose splicing correlates with dependence, MDM4 dependency tracked only with the MDM4-FL isoform (exon 6 retained), not total MDM4 mRNA. In wild-type TP53 lines, low MDM4-FL expression abrogated MDM4 dependence, and nutlin-3a (an MDM2 inhibitor) was the small molecule most strongly correlated with MDM4 exon-6 inclusion. Exon-6 quantitation outperformed total MDM4 mRNA for predicting response.
  • RPL22 loss → RPL22L1 → MDM4 splicing. RPL22L1 was the top correlate of MDM4 exon-6 inclusion. RPL22 K15fs frameshift mutations (common in MSI tumours) were found in 68% (67/99) of inferred-MSI CCLE lines; RPL22 loss-of-function or deletion was associated with higher RPL22L1 expression and increased MDM4 exon-6 inclusion in both CCLE and TCGA. High RPL22L1 expression marks RPL22L1 dependence — extending paralogue synthetic lethality logic to ribosomal proteins.
  • microRNA / lineage signalling. mir-215 expression correlated with CTNNB1 (β-catenin) dependence, especially in stomach and colon lineage lines; mir-215 also correlated with WNT-pathway and gastric-cancer gene sets in TCGA.
  • Phospho-SHP2 as a ponatinib biomarker. RPPA dependence modelling found pSHP2 (Y542) was a stronger predictor of PTPN11 (SHP2) dependence than PTPN11 mRNA. Among tyrosine-kinase inhibitors, ponatinib was the drug whose sensitivity prediction was most improved by adding RPPA data, and SHP2.pY542 was the top RPPA predictor. In a validation set of AML lines plus CML BCR–ABL positive controls, 4/5 previously untested AML lines (CTV1, NKM1, EOL1, MonoMAC1) with high pSHP2 were ponatinib-sensitive; HEL9217 was the lone exception. In 7/9 ponatinib-sensitive AML lines the authors detected alterations in FLT3, PDGFRA, FGFR1, or KIT. In vivo, AML primagrafts with high pSHP2 (CBAM-87679, NVAM-61786 — carrying BCR–ABL and FLT3-ITD respectively) showed extended survival on ponatinib relative to a low-pSHP2 primagraft (DFAM-68555).

Genes & alterations

  • SOX10, PAX8, HNF1B, HNF4A — promoter hypomethylation drives lineage-restricted dependence.
  • RPP25 (methylation/silencing) → RPP25L and POP7 dependence.
  • LDHA ↔︎ LDHB — reciprocal paralogue synthetic lethality gated by promoter methylation; replicated in TCGA primary tumours.
  • VHL — biallelic inactivation in renal clear cell can include one deleted + one methylated allele (3 cell lines identified).
  • EZH2, NSD2 — known histone-mark cluster drivers.
  • EP300, CREBBP — truncating mutations distal to the HAT domain define a new H3K18/H3K27 hyperacetylation cluster; nominated as gain-of-function.
  • MDM4 exon 6 inclusion (MDM4-FL) — predictive biomarker for MDM4 dependence and for MDM2 inhibitor nutlin-3a sensitivity in TP53 wild-type lines.
  • RPL22 K15fs frameshift (68% of MSI lines) → elevated RPL22L1 → MDM4 exon-6 inclusion; RPL22L1 over-expression itself marks RPL22L1 dependence.
  • TERT promoter mutations — 16.7% of 503 lines (most common non-coding somatic alteration in cancer cell lines).
  • MSH2 / MSH6 — truncating MSH2 mutations cause concordant MSH6 protein loss; MLH1 hypermethylation in 17/65 MSI lines.
  • ESR1–CCDC170 fusion → ESR1 dependence; AFF1–KMT2A fusion → AFF1 dependence.
  • CTNNB1 — dependence correlates with mir-215 expression in stomach/colon lineages.
  • PTPN11 (SHP2 pY542 phospho-protein) — dependency biomarker; phospho level explains sensitivity to ponatinib in AML and CML, including lines with FLT3, PDGFRA, FGFR1, KIT, or BCR–ABL1 alterations.

Clinical implications

  • MDM2 inhibitor patient selection. MDM4 exon-6 inclusion and RPL22/RPL22L1 status are proposed as biomarkers for clinical response to MDM2 inhibitors (e.g. nutlin-3a class) beyond TP53 mutation status alone.
  • Phospho-SHP2 as an RTK-activation screening biomarker. Because clinical detection of the full spectrum of RTK fusions and activating mutations remains difficult, pSHP2 (Y542) abundance is proposed as a unified screening biomarker for tumours likely to respond to RTK inhibitors such as ponatinib, with direct relevance to AML, CML, thyroid, and rhabdoid sarcoma lineages identified in the screen.
  • LDH-pathway therapeutics. Targeting lactate dehydrogenase in cancer should take into account paralogue methylation status (LDHA vs LDHB) to select sensitive tumours.
  • Cell-line reproducibility. The 3–10% of CCLE lines with substantial somatic drift and the 0.4% mislabelling rate detected against GDSC argue for routine identity / drift QC when reusing cell-line panels for therapeutic studies.

Limitations & open questions

  • Cell-line model only. All molecular and dependency findings derive from cancer cell lines (with limited primagraft / xenograft validation for the ponatinib axis); clinical validation in patient tumours is left for future work.
  • Patchy assay coverage across lines. Not every cell line has every data type (e.g., 326 WES, 329 WGS, 843 RRBS, 897 chromatin, 899 RPPA, 954 miRNA), which constrains multi-omic joint models.
  • Drift among passaged lines. 3–10% of lines have substantial somatic-variant divergence between CCLE and GDSC passages, complicating experimental reproducibility.
  • MDM4 splicing axis is correlative. The RPL22 → RPL22L1 → MDM4 exon-6 inclusion model is supported by orthogonal CCLE and TCGA evidence but lacks direct mechanistic perturbation experiments showing causation in human tumours.
  • EP300 / CREBBP truncating variants as “gain of function” is a strong claim grounded in cluster-level histone-acetylation enrichment; functional validation of individual variants is not provided.
  • Drug-sensitivity coverage. Drug correlations rely on the Sanger GDSC and CCLE pharma panels — many clinically relevant agents (e.g., newer MDM2 inhibitors, SHP2 inhibitors beyond SHP099) are not directly tested.
