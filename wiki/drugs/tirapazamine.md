# tirapazamine

## Overview

Tirapazamine (TPZ) is a benzotriazine di-N-oxide hypoxia-activated prodrug that is selectively reduced to a cytotoxic radical species under low-oxygen conditions. Its mechanism exploits the steep oxygen gradients present in solid tumor cores: under hypoxia, tirapazamine undergoes one-electron reduction to generate a highly reactive radical that causes DNA double-strand breaks, whereas in normoxic tissues the radical is rapidly back-oxidized to the non-toxic parent compound. The primary selectivity determinant is the tumor hypoxic microenvironment rather than a specific protein target, though activity is mechanistically linked to HIF-1α upregulation and associated hypoxia-response gene programs. Tirapazamine has been evaluated clinically in combination with [cisplatin](../drugs/cisplatin.md) and radiotherapy in head and neck and cervical cancers but has not received regulatory approval. It is not listed in OncoKB or cBioPortal drug ontologies (corpus-grown slug; `unverified: true`).

## Evidence in the corpus

- In a 3D cell culture review, gelatin-foam glioblastoma tumoroids that were resistant to [irinotecan](../drugs/irinotecan.md) and [fluorouracil](../drugs/fluorouracil.md) showed greater sensitivity to tirapazamine, coincident with HIF-1α upregulation in hypoxic spheroid cores (Yang et al., 2015 cited therein); this illustrates how 3D hypoxic cores can reverse standard drug-response rankings and motivates tirapazamine as a complementary agent in hypoxic niches [PMID:30643250](../papers/30643250.md).

## Resistance mechanisms

- Under normoxic conditions, molecular oxygen back-oxidizes the tirapazamine radical to the non-toxic parent compound, conferring intrinsic resistance in well-oxygenated tumor regions. Tumors with less pronounced hypoxic cores (e.g., small or well-vascularized tumors) are therefore expected to show reduced tirapazamine activity.
