Molecular Changes in Retinoblastoma beyond RB1: Findings from Next-Generation Sequencing

Authors

Jasmine H. Francis

Allison L. Richards

Diana L. Mandelker

Michael F. Berger

Michael F. Walsh

Ira J. Dunkel

Mark T. A. Donoghue

David H. Abramson

Doi

10.3390/cancers13010149

PMID: 33466343 · DOI: 10.3390/cancers13010149 · Journal: Cancers (2021)

TL;DR

Francis et al. profiled 83 enucleated retinoblastoma specimens from Memorial Sloan Kettering using the MSK-IMPACT targeted NGS panel (468 somatic genes; 88 germline cancer-predisposition genes) to characterize the genomic landscape beyond RB1. RB1 inactivation was found in 97.5% (79/81) of patients, with copy-neutral LOH dramatically over-represented at the RB1 locus relative to other tumor suppressors (OR 19.8) and to RB1 in other cancers (OR 44.4). The most common non-RB1 somatic alteration was BCOR (22.9%, 19/83), and BCOR mutations were associated with worse metastasis-free survival (nominal p=0.03). Recurrent arm-level CNAs (1q gain, 16q LOH) independently associated with vitreous seeding (BH-corrected p=0.008 and 0.004; OR=12.6 and 26.7). Five of 42 germline-tested patients (12.0%) harbored pathogenic germline variants in non-RB1 cancer-predisposition genes (NTHL1, ERCC3, MSH3, CHEK2, MITF).

Cohort & data

  • N=83 enucleated retinoblastoma (RBL) specimens, accrued May 2006–August 2019 at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center (IRB #17-049).
  • Dataset: rbl_mskcc_2020 — IMPACT mutation calls deposited at cBioPortal (https://cbioportal.mskcc.org/study/summary?id=rbl_mskcc_2020).
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT hybridization capture targeted NGS — 468 genes for somatic analysis (IMPACT468), 88 genes for germline analysis in 42 consented patients. Detects SNVs, indels, gene-level focal CNAs, and select rearrangements.
  • 74 specimens had matched normal tissue; 9 were tumor-only.
  • Copy-number and clonality called with FACETS v0.5.14; microsatellite instability estimated with MSIsensor; event-free survival modeled with Kaplan–Meier estimators starting from enucleation date.
  • Sample input: 10-µm FFPE microdissected sections, DNeasy extraction, ≥250 ng FFPE DNA per sample.
  • 23 specimens additionally had outside commercial RB1 testing for methylation and germline status.

Key findings

  • RB1 inactivation is near-universal but mechanistically diverse. Pathognomonic RB1 alterations were detected in 97.5% (79/81) of patients PMID:33466343. Biallelic RB1 loss was identified in 79.5% (66/83) of specimens. Of these 66, 10 had somatic homozygous deletion of RB1; 1 had germline heterozygous loss of exons 7–11 followed by somatic LOH; 38/53 (71.6%) of the remainder had a mutation followed by LOH; 15 had two mutations; 2 had promoter hypermethylation plus LOH. Twelve specimens had only apparent heterozygous alterations detected by MSK-IMPACT, suggesting silencing mechanisms not captured by the panel.
  • Two specimens had no detectable RB1 alterations. One had subclonal mutations in BIRC3 (p.A385D, VUS) and PBRM1 (p.G176*, truncating); the other had only a gain of 6p — consistent with the hypothesis that 6p gain may be sufficient for tumorigenesis in wildtype-RB1 retinoblastoma.
  • Copy-neutral LOH (CN-LOH) is the dominant second-hit mechanism at RB1. Of 42 matched specimens with LOH at the RB1 locus, 31 (73.8%) were CN-LOH. CN-LOH at RB1 was significantly enriched vs. other tumor suppressor genes in the same specimens (p<2.2×10⁻¹⁶, OR=19.8) and vs. RB1-mutant tumors in other cancer types from the MSK cohort (~39,846 specimens; p<2.2×10⁻¹⁶, OR=44.4).
  • Intertumoral heterogeneity within bilateral disease. Two patients with bilateral disease and germline RB1 alterations had distinct somatic second hits in each eye (e.g., p.X566_splice vs. p.V654Cfs4; somatic LOH vs. p.S807). Three patients had evidence of >2 RB1 hits, suggesting multiple clones within a single eye.
  • BCOR is the most common non-RB1 somatic gene alteration. Found in 19/83 specimens (22.9%). All BCOR mutations were putative loss-of-function. This frequency exceeds previously published series (~13%).
  • BCOR mutations correlate with worse metastasis-free survival (Kaplan–Meier, nominal p=0.03), but not with high-risk histopathological features.
  • Other recurrently somatic-mutated genes (≥3 specimens): RPTOR (3/83, 3.6%), TERT (3, 3.6%), MSH3 (3, 3.6%), ARID1A (3, 3.6%), MYCN (3, 3.6%), TSC2 (2, 2.4%), CREBBP (1, 1.2%). Among the ten most-frequent non-RB1 alterations, only TERT, MSH3, ARID1A, and HLA-A had any putative-driver mutations. FAT1 and RPTOR mutations were all VUS — non-truncating, non-hotspot.
  • Three patients had MYCN amplification co-occurring with RB1 mutation.
  • Tumor mutational burden was uniformly low (0–3 mutations/Mb).
  • Recurrent arm-level CNAs (n=69 evaluable eyes): 1q gain 67.0%, 2p gain 32.0%, 6p gain 59.0%, 13q LOH 51.0%, 16q LOH 43.0%.
  • Vitreous seeds independently associate with 1q gain and 16q LOH. BH-corrected p=0.008 and 0.004; OR=12.6 and 26.7 respectively. 1q gain and 16q loss were not significantly correlated with each other (p=0.37), and a joint model showed both contribute independently (model p=8.2×10⁻⁸; 1q p=1.3×10⁻⁴, 16q p=2.3×10⁻⁴). 6p gain was not associated with aggressive histopathology in this enucleated-only cohort, contradicting some prior reports.
  • Germline non-RB1 cancer-predisposition mutations in 5/42 (~12%) of germline-tested patients. Variants: NTHL1 c.268C>T (p.Gln90); ERCC3 c.1354C>T (p.Arg452); MSH3 c.1341-1G>T (p.X447_splice); CHEK2 c.470T>C (p.Ile157Thr); MITF c.1255G>A (p.Glu419Lys). Two of these were in autosomal recessive cancer-predisposition genes (NTHL1, MSH3); the MSH3 patient showed no microsatellite instability.
  • No two specimens shared an identical genetic profile across mutations, LOH, or CNVs.

Genes & alterations

  • RB1 — biallelic loss in 66/83 (79.5%); strong preference for copy-neutral LOH as the second-hit mechanism (31/42 LOH events were CN-LOH; OR=19.8 vs. other TSGs; OR=44.4 vs. RB1 in other cancers).
  • BCOR — most common non-RB1 somatic gene; 19/83 (22.9%) putative loss-of-function; significantly associated with worse metastasis-free survival (p=0.03).
  • MYCN — amplified in 3 specimens, all in the presence of RB1 mutation; no MYCN-only “RB1-wildtype + MYCN-amplified” tumors observed in this cohort (in contrast to ~2% frequency reported elsewhere).
  • TERT, MSH3, ARID1A, HLA-A — recurrent at ~3.6% each; only these (among the top ten non-RB1 recurrently altered genes) carried putative-driver variants.
  • CREBBP — single putative-driver mutation; previously implicated by WES studies in retinoblastoma.
  • RPTOR, FAT1 — recurrent but all mutations were VUS in this cohort.
  • TSC2 — mutated in 2 specimens; located at 16p13.3 and corresponds with the 16p region commonly altered in retinoblastoma.
  • CDH11 — candidate gene previously proposed for 16q-loss-associated vitreous seeding; no CDH11 coding mutations in this cohort, so its role remains unclear here.
  • BIRC3, PBRM1 — subclonal alterations in a single RB1-wildtype specimen.
  • Driver candidates discussed but not recurrently mutated in this cohort: KIF14, DEK, E2F3, RBL2, NGFR.
  • Germline pathogenic variants (non-RB1) in 5/42 tested patients: NTHL1 p.Gln90, ERCC3 p.Arg452, MSH3 p.X447_splice, CHEK2 p.Ile157Thr, MITF p.Glu419Lys.

Clinical implications

  • BCOR as a metastasis biomarker. The authors propose that detection of a BCOR loss-of-function mutation — either in an enucleated specimen or via cell-free DNA (cfDNA) — may inform surveillance strategies or preventative measures for metastatic retinoblastoma, potentially marking minimal residual disease or treatment response.
  • 1q gain and 16q LOH as independent biomarkers of vitreous seeding. The two CNAs do not co-occur statistically (p=0.37) but each independently associates with vitreous seeding (the principal indication for enucleation), suggesting they may be useful jointly as predictive markers.
  • Germline screening implications. The 12% prevalence of non-RB1 germline cancer-predisposition mutations is in line with broader pediatric-cancer germline rates (12–17.5%), and the authors argue this supports broader (non-RB1-only) germline testing in retinoblastoma patients to inform lifetime cancer screening — though to date all five carriers in this cohort have shown intraocular disease only.
  • 6p gain in wildtype-RB1 retinoblastoma. One RB1-wildtype tumor had only 6p gain, leading the authors to hypothesize that 6p gain may be sufficient for retinoblastoma tumorigenesis in this setting, though further sensitivity testing for RB1 inactivation is needed.

Limitations & open questions

  • Enucleation-only cohort. All 83 specimens came from eyes removed primarily or secondarily, biasing the cohort toward more advanced or treatment-refractory disease. This may explain why 6p gain did not correlate with aggressive histopathology here, in contrast to prior reports.
  • MSK-IMPACT does not detect all RB1 inactivation mechanisms. Twelve specimens showed only heterozygous RB1 alterations by panel sequencing; promoter hypermethylation and other epigenetic silencing mechanisms are not systematically captured. Not all samples had outside methylation testing.
  • Statistical power for rare alterations. Only BCOR had high enough frequency to test clinical-feature association; recurrent VUS in FAT1 and RPTOR could not be functionally assessed.
  • BCOR–metastasis association is nominal (p=0.03). Not multiple-testing corrected; needs validation in independent cohorts.
  • Germline cohort is small. Only 42/83 patients consented to germline MSK-IMPACT testing; the 12% non-RB1 germline frequency is based on this subset.
  • Functional role of non-RB1 germline variants is unknown. The authors note that whereas germline cancer-predisposition mutations are associated with metastatic disease in other cancers, all five carriers here have shown intraocular disease only — leaving open whether these variants influence tumorigenesis, clinical course, or second-cancer risk.
  • Future work suggested by authors: DNA methylation, RNA expression, and cfDNA-based surveillance to extend the genotype-phenotype map.
