TL;DR

Gounder et al. report comprehensive genomic profiling of 7,494 soft-tissue and bone sarcomas spanning 44 histologies, profiled by FoundationOne Heme (465-gene DNA panel + 333-gene RNA fusion panel) between 2012 and 2018. Sequencing flagged potential diagnostic refinements/reclassifications in 10.5% of cases; 31.7% of patients harbored at least one OncoKB-defined actionable alteration, 2.6% had kinase gene fusions (ALK, BRAF, FGFR1–4, NTRK1–3, RET, ROS1), 3.9% had TMB ≥10 mut/Mb, MSI-H was rare (<0.3%), and 15% had high genome-wide LOH (gLOH ≥19.3%) only partly explained by HRD-gene alterations. A clinically annotated subset of 118 MSK patients demonstrated anecdotal responses to matched targeted and immune therapies. The cohort is registered in cBioPortal as sarcoma_msk_2022.

Cohort & data

  • N = 7,494 patients with 44 sarcoma histologies; samples profiled from 2012–2018 (PMID:35705558).
  • Tissue composition: 81.0% soft tissue (n=6,067), 14.7% bone (n=1,105), 4.3% other (n=322).
  • Most common subtypes: sarcoma NOS 17.2%, leiomyosarcoma 12.7%.
  • Demographics: median age 53 (range <1–89); 53.4% female; pediatric-adolescent-young-adult (P-AYA, ≤30 years) = 21.8% (1,636/7,494).
  • Categorization: translocation-associated n=1,724 (23.0%) vs. genomically complex/other n=5,770 (77.0%).
  • Assay: FoundationOne Heme targeted panel — hybrid-capture DNA sequencing of 465 genes (median 704× coverage, IQR 515–798×), select introns of 31 rearrangement genes, plus RNA-seq of 333 commonly rearranged genes. Tumor-only profiling (no matched normal). Reads aligned to hg19 via BWA v0.5.9.
  • Annotation: Actionability assessed using OncoKB levels (data cutoff June 8, 2021). Mutational signatures decomposed against COSMIC v2 (30 signatures) and aggregated into APOBEC, aging, BRCA, MMR, smoking, UV, POLE, and alkylating classes (dominance threshold ≥0.4).
  • Clinical sub-cohort: 118 adult sarcoma patients at Memorial Sloan Kettering (MSK) with linked outcomes; median age 60 (range 18–89); 60% metastatic; median 4 prior systemic therapies (range 0–12).
  • Dataset: sarcoma_msk_2022.

Key findings

  • Diagnostic reclassification. Sequencing prompted potential refinement or reassignment of diagnosis in 10.5% (789/7,494) of cases, based on presence/absence of pathognomonic alterations or fusions; reclassifications were not re-reviewed by expert sarcoma pathologists (PMID:35705558).
  • Recurrent short-variant / CNV drivers. TP53 37.1%; RB1 17.2%; CDKN2A 16.7%; CDKN2B 12.8%; MDM2 10.7%; CDK4 10.6%; CDK6 0.7%; MDM4 0.5%; CCNE1 2.6%; CCND1 1.0%; CCND2 1.4%; CCND3 1.8%.
  • p53 + Rb pathway dominance. The p53 pathway (TP53, MDM2, MDM4) was altered in 47.8% of sarcomas and the Rb pathway (RB1, CDKN2A/B, CDK4/6, CCND1/2/3, CCNE1) in 46.8%; co-occurrence between the two pathways was highly significant (OR=4.2, p<1.3×10⁻¹⁹⁶, FDR-adjusted), while alterations within each pathway were largely mutually exclusive.
  • Complex vs. translocation-associated sarcomas. p53 pathway 58.2% vs. 12.9%; Rb pathway 56% vs. 15.8%. TMB also higher in complex sarcomas (median 2.4 vs. 1.6 mut/Mb; p<4×10⁻⁶¹).
  • Histology-enriched alterations. 4q12 amplicon (KDR, PDGFRA, KIT) in osteosarcoma 10.7%, liposarcoma NOS 8.0%, UPS/MFH 7.3%; 11q13 amplicon (CCND1, FGF2, FGF3, FGF19) in UPS of bone 4.3%; CDKN2A/B deletion in MPNST 47.3% and chordoma 36.8%; SMARCB1 in epithelioid sarcoma 56% and malignant rhabdoid tumor 60%; PIK3CA, PTEN, and AKT E17K in myxoid liposarcoma 36.3%, 17.8%, 3.7%; IDH1 in chondrosarcoma 22.9%; PBRM1 in chordoma 15.8%; MYC amplification in embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma 18.4%, clear-cell sarcoma 13.5%, osteosarcoma 15.0%.
  • Kinase fusions actionable in 2.6% (196/7,494). Recurrent in-frame fusions of ALK, BRAF, FGFR1–4, NTRK1/2/3, RET, and ROS1. Histology prevalence: IMT 62.1%, MPNST 4.9%, UPS of bone 4.3%, extraskeletal osteosarcoma 4.0%, UPS 3.6%, sarcoma NOS 3.8%, LMS 1.9%.
  • Novel TNS1–ALK fusion in LMS. TNS1–ALK rearrangements (breakpoints in introns 16 and 18; fusions of exons 17–29 / 19–29) detected in 16 cases, primarily uterine and non-uterine leiomyosarcomas — suggesting reclassification of some LMS as kinase-driven.
  • Recurrent fusion-driver gene rearrangements. EWSR1 fusions with FLI1, WT1, NR4A3 and rarer partners; also rearrangements involving RB1 1.5%, TP53 1.4%, LRP1B 0.6%, ATRX 0.5%, ARID2 0.2%, BRCA2 0.3%, NOTCH3 0.3%.
  • MMR-D and MSI. Mismatch-repair deficiency (loss of MLH1, MSH2, MSH3, MSH6, PMS2) in 2.1% of cases; MSI-H in only 18/6,206 evaluable cases (0.29%, most often uterine endometrial stromal sarcoma, leiomyosarcoma, sarcoma NOS); median TMB in MSI-H = 25.8 mut/Mb (IQR 19.8–30.1). 14/18 MSI-H cases had corresponding MMR-gene alterations.
  • HRD signal. Pathogenic HR-pathway alterations (BRCA1/2, PALB2, RAD51 and paralogs RAD51B, RAD51D, RAD52, RAD54L) in 2.5% (184/7,494); 72 confirmed biallelic. Biallelic losses most frequent in uterine LMS 4.8% and chordoma 5.3%.
  • Genome-wide LOH (gLOH). Median gLOH = 8.6% (IQR 3.0–15.7%) across 4,619 evaluable samples. 15% (697/4,619) had high gLOH (≥19.3%, 1 SD above mean); only 5.0% of those had HRD-gene alterations and 3.3% biallelic HRD — i.e. high gLOH is largely not explained by canonical HRD genes. Liposarcoma NOS: 50.0% high gLOH but only 4.0% with HRD-gene alteration; osteosarcoma 30.6% vs. 1.7%; UPS 26.6% vs. 3.6%. gLOH and high TMB were largely non-overlapping (distinct biology).
  • TMB landscape. Median TMB = 2.4 mut/Mb (IQR 0.8–4.0). TMB ≥5, ≥10, and ≥15 mut/Mb in 13.0%, 3.9%, and 2.9% respectively. TMB ≥10 mut/Mb most frequent in angiosarcoma 15.0%, UPS 10.9%, MPNST 10.8%, ossifying fibromyxoid tumor 7.7%. UV signature dominated cutaneous angiosarcoma (35/41 evaluable; 22 from skin) and was present in 7/9 evaluable ultra-high-TMB (≥100 mut/Mb) cases.
  • CD274 (PD-L1) amplification detected in 1.0% (75/7,594) of sarcomas, highest in UPS/MFH 3.6%, myxofibrosarcoma 2.6%, sarcoma NOS 2.2%; CD274-amplified tumors had a median TMB of 3.2 mut/Mb.
  • P-AYA vs. adult differences. P-AYA patients had significantly lower TMB across most histologies (e.g. alveolar RMS 1.6 vs. 2.4 mut/Mb; MPNST 2.4 vs. 3.2; DFSP 0.8 vs. 2.8; all FDR<0.05) and lower median gLOH 3.8% vs. 9.5% (p<4×10⁻⁴³); exception was osteosarcoma where gLOH was higher in P-AYA (16.8% vs. 12.2%; FDR<0.001). In P-AYA osteosarcoma, CNVs more frequent in CCND3, AURKB, CCNE1, GID4, MYC; in adults, more frequent in MDM2, CDKN2A/B, FRS2.
  • Actionability. 31.7% (2,372/7,494) harbored ≥1 OncoKB actionable alteration; only 5.9% (n=439) were Level 1 (FDA-recognized for the given sarcoma type), most being KIT mutations in GIST (observed in 64.4% of GISTs). Majority were Level 3B (clinical benefit in other cancers). Resistance markers also captured: SDH loss, PDGFRA D842V, KIT exon 13 mutations (imatinib resistance), ESR1 mutations (anti-estrogen resistance), and TP53 mutations (MDM2-inhibitor resistance).
  • MSK clinical sub-cohort (n=118). 90% (106/118) sequenced successfully; 47% (50/106) had a treatment-linked alteration; 29% (31/106) enrolled in a biomarker-matched trial or off-label FDA-approved drug; 4% (4/106) had a sequencing-driven diagnostic change; 1% avoided futile treatment.
  • Anecdotal responses (Fig. 6). Durable partial response to tazemetostat in sarcoma NOS with SMARCB1 deletion; rapid near-complete response to vemurafenib+trametinib in BRAF V600E sarcoma; >1-year stable disease on PARP-inhibitor + ATR-inhibitor combination in osteosarcoma with ATM exon 57 truncation; near-complete response to pembrolizumab in UPS with TMB 20 mut/Mb; complete response to nivolumab+ipilimumab in malignant PEComa (TMB 7 mut/Mb); complete response to larotrectinib in IMT with ETV6–NTRK3 fusion.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53: altered in 37.1% of sarcomas (44.9% complex vs. 11.3% translocation-associated); 79.3% short variants; drives p53-pathway co-occurrence with MDM2/MDM4.
  • MDM2: 10.7% overall, 13.5% complex vs. 1.6% translocation-associated; >94% amplifications; pathognomonic of well/dedifferentiated liposarcoma; predicts response to MDM2 inhibitors.
  • MDM4: 0.5% overall, 0.6% complex vs. 0.2% translocation-associated.
  • RB1: 17.2% (21.5% complex vs. 2.8% translocation-associated); also rearranged in 1.5% of cases.
  • CDKN2A / CDKN2B: 16.7% / 12.8% overall; 84.5%/99.0% homozygous deletions; enriched in MPNST (47.3%) and chordoma (36.8%).
  • CDK4 / CDK6: 10.6% / 0.7%; typically amplified; targets for CDK4/6 inhibitors.
  • CCND1 / CCND2 / CCND3: 1.0% / 1.4% / 1.8%; co-occur with CDK4/6 and CDKN2A/B — unusual within-pathway co-occurrence pattern.
  • CCNE1: 2.6%; typically amplified.
  • KIT / PDGFRA / KDR: co-amplified at 4q12 in osteosarcoma 10.7%, liposarcoma NOS 8.0%, UPS/MFH 7.3%. KIT mutations actionable in 64.4% of GISTs (imatinib); resistance markers include PDGFRA D842V and KIT exon 13 substitutions.
  • SMARCB1: inactivated in 56% of epithelioid sarcomas and 60% of malignant rhabdoid tumors; tazemetostat-responsive.
  • IDH1: 22.9% of chondrosarcomas.
  • HRAS: amplified in 20% of granular cell tumors.
  • PBRM1: inactivating alterations in 15.8% of chordomas.
  • BRAF: V600E in 0.5% overall — rare but actionable (vemurafenib + trametinib response shown). Recurrent kinase fusions also detected.
  • KRAS G12X (0.9%), EGFR exon 19 deletions (0.01%) and L858R (0.01%) — extremely rare in mesenchymal tumors; may flag sarcomatoid carcinoma.
  • NF1: co-occurring with UV mutational signature in ultra-high-TMB cases (5/11; may suggest desmoplastic melanoma or radiation-associated MPNST).
  • PIK3CA / PTEN / AKT1: enriched in myxoid liposarcoma (36.3% / 17.8% / 3.7% AKT E17K).
  • TSC1 / TSC2: PEComa — TSC2 loss predicted mTOR-inhibitor responsiveness; subclonal TSC1 documented in PEComa with response to nivolumab+ipilimumab.
  • CD274 (PD-L1): amplified in 1.0% of sarcomas; warrants prospective ICI biomarker evaluation.
  • Kinase-fusion genes: ALK, ROS1, RET, FGFR1, NTRK1, NTRK2, NTRK3, BRAF — 2.6% prevalence; novel TNS1–ALK fusion in LMS (16 cases).
  • Fusion-driver genes (transcription factors): EWSR1 (FLI1, WT1, NR4A3); ETV6–NTRK3; SS18–SSX2 (synovial sarcoma reclassification).
  • DNA damage repair: ATM, BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, RAD51B, RAD51D, RAD52, RAD54L — collectively 2.5% with pathogenic alterations; biallelic in 1.0% (72/7,494).
  • MMR genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 — altered in 2.1%; tightly correlated with MSI-H.
  • ESR1: mutations potentially predict anti-estrogen resistance in endometrial stromal sarcoma (analogous to breast cancer).
  • STAG2: noted as a prognostic marker in pediatric Ewing sarcoma (citing prior work).
  • 11q13 / FGF amplicon: FGF2, FGF3, FGF19 co-amplified with CCND1 in UPS of bone.

Clinical implications

  • Diagnosis. Targeted sequencing identified potential reclassifications in 10.5% of cases by detecting (or excluding) pathognomonic alterations. The authors propose genomic profiling as an adjunct to morphologic, immunohistochemical, and cytogenetic (FISH) workup — especially in centers without specialist sarcoma pathologists.
  • Treatment selection — actionable alterations. 31.7% of sarcomas carry ≥1 OncoKB actionable alteration. Level 1 (5.9%) examples include KIT mutations → imatinib in GIST. Most actionable findings are Level 3B (other-cancer evidence).
  • Treatment avoidance — resistance markers. SDH-loss, PDGFRA D842V, and KIT exon 13 mutations indicate imatinib resistance in GIST; ESR1 mutations predict anti-estrogen resistance in endometrial stromal sarcoma; TP53 mutations confer resistance to MDM2 inhibitors; RB1 deletion confers resistance to palbociclib (CDK4/6 inhibition).
  • Immune checkpoint biomarkers. 3.9% of sarcomas have TMB ≥10 mut/Mb (FDA-relevant threshold for tissue-agnostic pembrolizumab under FoundationOne CDx); MSI-H is rare (0.3%) and routine MSI/IHC testing has low yield. CD274/PD-L1 amplification (1.0%) and gLOH warrant prospective biomarker studies. Anecdotal complete/near-complete ICI responses observed in UPS with TMB 20 mut/Mb (pembrolizumab) and malignant PEComa with TMB 7 mut/Mb (nivolumab + ipilimumab).
  • Kinase-fusion oncoprotein targeting. ETV6–NTRK3 → larotrectinib durable complete response in IMT; novel TNS1–ALK fusions in LMS may identify ALK-inhibitor candidates. NTRK amplifications are not actionable (negative trial finding cited).
  • Epigenetic targeting. SMARCB1 loss → tazemetostat (EZH2 inhibitor): durable partial response in sarcoma NOS.
  • Combined BRAF+MEK. BRAF V600E (rare; 0.5%) → vemurafenib + trametinib.
  • PARP + ATR combinations. ATM loss-of-function osteosarcoma with prior doxorubicin/cisplatin failure → PARP + ATR inhibitor combination (>1-year stable disease).
  • PARP / DNA-damage-repair therapy in HRD/gLOH-high sarcomas. 15% of sarcomas have high gLOH (≥19.3%), with only a fraction explained by HRD-gene alterations; authors invoke the ARIEL3 precedent (rucaparib benefit in BRCA-wildtype, high-gLOH ovarian cancer) to motivate gLOH as a candidate biomarker in sarcoma.
  • Prognosis. In Ewing sarcoma: TP53, CDKN2A, STAG2 alterations carry prognostic weight (citing prior work). Higher TMB is associated with worse outcome in rhabdomyosarcoma (citing Casey et al.).
  • Real-world translation gap. In the MSK cohort, only 29% of profiled patients were matched to a biomarker-driven trial or off-label therapy; barriers included unavailable trials (3.7%), ineligibility (5.6%), late genomic profiling (5.6%), and loss to follow-up (1.8%) — echoing the NCI-MATCH 5%-match rate.

Limitations & open questions

  • No expert sarcoma-pathologist re-review. Reclassifications (10.5%) were not centrally re-confirmed; some may not survive expert review. Cases categorized as “sarcoma, unclassified” were excluded from subtype-specific analyses.
  • Tumor-only profiling. No matched germline DNA — germline variants may be inadvertently included. Biallelic predictions inferred zygosity computationally rather than by phasing against normal.
  • Selection bias. Cohort enriched for refractory/advanced disease (since Foundation Medicine sequencing is reimbursed in that setting), which may inflate observed alteration frequencies relative to all sarcomas.
  • Missing clinical metadata. Stage, tumor grade, treatment outcomes, and whether the WD or DD component of WD/DD-LPS was sequenced were unavailable for the full 7,494-patient cohort; outcomes available only for the 118 MSK patients.
  • Gene-panel scope. FoundationOne Heme does not cover several known/emerging sarcoma genes — explicitly MYOD1, VEGFA, TERT, MTAP, POLE/D, YAP1, TAZ (Hippo pathway) — so the catalog of drivers is incomplete.
  • gLOH mechanism unresolved. High gLOH in 15% of sarcomas is not explained by canonical HRD-gene alterations in the majority of cases; aneuploidy and other mechanisms are invoked but not characterized.
  • PD-L1 IHC unavailable. Authors could not correlate CD274 amplification with PD-L1 protein expression; prospective ICI biomarker validation needed.
  • NCT03784014. A randomized prospective trial in Europe is referenced as the formal test of clinical utility — outcomes pending.
  • Variants with low allele frequency. Some variants were sub-clonal; impact on clonality and tumor heterogeneity not interrogated in this paper.
  • Source data restrictions. Raw sequencing data are not deposited; only variant calls in Supplementary Data 1.
