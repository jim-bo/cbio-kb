## TL;DR

Gorgolewski and Poldrack present a practical, opinionated guide for how individual neuroimaging researchers can improve transparency and reproducibility in their work. The guide is organized around three "pillars of open science" — data, code, and publications — and offers concrete recommendations: include data-sharing language in consent forms; deposit raw data in field-specific repositories such as OpenfMRI or FCP/INDI before manuscript submission; organize data with the [Brain Imaging Data Structure (BIDS)](https://bids.neuroimaging.io); use a version control system (git/GitHub) and apply permissive open-source licenses (MIT, Apache 2.0); share preprints on bioRxiv or arXiv and report all analyses including null results in supplementary materials; and consider preregistration mechanisms such as Registered Reports. The article also discusses emerging trends in post-publication peer review (PubMed Commons, PubPeer, Publons) ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).

## Key findings

- The authors argue that neuroimaging suffers from low statistical power (estimated ~8%) and analytic-flexibility-driven bias, and that open data, open code, and preprints are practical mitigations ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
- They introduce and recommend the **Brain Imaging Data Structure (BIDS)** as a lightweight file/folder convention based on NIfTI, TSV, and JSON, with a browser-based validator at `incf.github.io/bids-validator` ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
- They identify two field-specific repositories accepting general neuroimaging submissions: **FCP/INDI** (resting-state fMRI + T1) and **OpenfMRI** (any MRI). For statistical maps they recommend **NeuroVault.org**; for connectivity matrices, the **UCLA Multimodal Connectivity Database** ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
- For code, they recommend git/GitHub, automation frameworks (Nipype, PSOM, aa, make), and literate-programming environments (Jupyter, RMarkdown, matlabweb). They favor minimally restrictive licenses (MIT) over copyleft licenses (GPL) for maximal reusability ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
- For publications, they advocate posting preprints on bioRxiv/arXiv to claim precedence and solicit feedback, and including all auxiliary analyses and null results in supplementary materials. They note Wiley imposes a 12-month embargo on author self-archiving of accepted manuscripts, while Elsevier permits posting accepted manuscripts on noncommercial sites ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
- They highlight **preregistration** and the **Registered Reports** mechanism (in-principle acceptance before data collection) as a way to distinguish confirmatory from exploratory analyses and reduce publication bias ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
- They recommend an open-access consent-form template (`open-brain-consent.readthedocs.org`) derived from NKI Enhanced and Human Connectome Project consent forms ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).

## Limitations & open questions

- The authors note the recommendations are voluntary and that the scientific incentive structure (publication bias, lack of credit for code/data sharing or reviewing) currently undercuts adoption; cultural change will take years ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
- The guide is explicitly limited to "mechanisms that have been tested and discussed in the community for a number of years" — it does not cover newer tools or computational reproducibility infrastructure (containers, cloud-based pipelines beyond a single VM example) in any depth ([PMID:27389358](../papers/27389358.md)).
