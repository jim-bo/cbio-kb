TL;DR

This Comment article is the first formal publication of the FAIR Guiding Principles — a concise, domain-independent set of guidelines for making scholarly data Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable for both humans and machines. Drafted at the 2014 Lorentz Workshop “Jointly Designing a Data Fairport” in Leiden and refined by a FORCE11 working group, the principles are not a standard or specification but a high-level evaluation rubric for data publishers, repositories, funders, and stewards. The paper articulates 15 sub-principles (F1–F4, A1–A2, I1–I3, R1) and illustrates partial implementations across exemplar resources including Dataverse, FAIRDOM, ISA, Open PHACTS, wwPDB, and UniProt (PMID:26978244).

Cohort & data

  • Not an empirical study. No patient cohort, no sequencing data, no statistical analysis. The paper is a community-consensus position statement.
  • Authorship cohort: 53 co-authors spanning academia, industry, funding agencies, and scholarly publishers (Elsevier, Nature Genetics, GigaScience, Bayer CropScience, Cray, ELIXIR, NIH, BD2K, FORCE11, DTL).
  • Provenance: Drafted at the Lorentz Workshop “Jointly Designing a Data Fairport” (Leiden, Netherlands, 2014); refined by a FORCE11 working group; further revised at the NBDC/DBCLS BioHackathon 2015 (PMID:26978244).
  • Exemplar resources analyzed: Dataverse (Harvard, ~60,000 datasets), FAIRDOM (SEEK + openBIS for systems biology), ISA framework, Open PHACTS (drug-discovery integration), wwPDB, UniProt; emergent initiatives bioCADDIE and CEDAR (NIH BD2K) (PMID:26978244).

Key findings

  • The FAIR Principles are organized in four facets with 15 sub-principles (PMID:26978244):
    • Findable — F1: globally unique, persistent identifier for (meta)data; F2: rich metadata (per R1); F3: metadata explicitly include the data identifier; F4: (meta)data are registered/indexed in a searchable resource.
    • Accessible — A1: (meta)data retrievable by identifier via a standardized communications protocol (A1.1 open/free/universally implementable; A1.2 supports authentication/authorization where necessary); A2: metadata remain accessible even when data are no longer available.
    • Interoperable — I1: (meta)data use a formal, accessible, shared, broadly applicable language for knowledge representation; I2: vocabularies themselves follow FAIR principles; I3: (meta)data include qualified references to other (meta)data.
    • Reusable — R1: rich attributes; R1.1 clear and accessible data-usage license; R1.2 detailed provenance; R1.3 conformance to domain-relevant community standards.
  • The principles explicitly target machine actionability as a continuum, not just a binary state — distinguishing them from peer initiatives that focus on the human scholar (PMID:26978244).
  • The principles are modular and separable: adopters can satisfy any combination incrementally as their publishing environments evolve, and the metadata/data distinction lets sensitive datasets achieve high “FAIRness” through rich discoverable metadata even when the underlying data are restricted (PMID:26978244).
  • The principles precede implementation: they do not prescribe any specific technology, standard, or solution — they act as a rubric for evaluating whether implementation choices yield FAIR digital research artefacts (PMID:26978244).
  • The principles apply not only to data in the conventional sense but also to algorithms, tools, and workflows that produced the data — i.e., all scholarly digital research objects (PMID:26978244).
  • FAIR is positioned as complementary to the Joint Declaration of Data Citation Principles (JDDCP) and the Data Seal of Approval (DSA): JDDCP focuses on citation, DSA on producer/repository conduct, and FAIR on the data itself (PMID:26978244).

Clinical implications

  • None directly. The paper makes no clinical claims and reports no biomarker, drug, or prognosis finding.
  • Indirect relevance to cancer genomics knowledge bases (such as cbio-kb itself): FAIR is the de facto framework that downstream consortia (e.g., NIH BD2K bioCADDIE, CEDAR, ELIXIR) cite when building data-discovery infrastructure for biomedical datasets — including the cBioPortal-style cohort repositories that this wiki indexes (PMID:26978244).

Limitations & open questions

  • The paper is a principles document, not an implementation specification. It deliberately leaves the choice of identifiers, protocols, vocabularies, and serializations to adopters, which means “FAIR-compliant” claims by repositories vary widely in rigor.
  • The authors note that community-endorsed vocabularies often lack the attributes needed for rich annotation, requiring either extensions of existing vocabularies or publication of new ones — itself following FAIR (principle I2). They flag the BioSharing registry (now FAIRsharing) as a partial solution but acknowledge >600 content standards exist in the life sciences alone (PMID:26978244).
  • No measurement framework for FAIRness is provided in this paper — the authors describe FAIR as a continuum but do not define a scoring rubric (subsequent work by the FAIR Metrics group addressed this).
  • The “optimal state” of full machine actionability — where a machine correctly handles a digital object it has never encountered before — is acknowledged as one that “may rarely be achieved” (PMID:26978244).
  • Author disclosures note potential conflicts: M. Axton is Nature Genetics’ Editor in Chief; S.-A. Sansone is Scientific Data’s Honorary Academic Editor and consultant (PMID:26978244).
