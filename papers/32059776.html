Proteogenomic Characterization of Endometrial Carcinoma

TL;DR

Dou et al. report the CPTAC proteogenomic characterization of 95 prospectively collected endometrial carcinomas (83 endometrioid, 12 serous) integrating whole-exome, whole-genome, total and miRNA RNA-seq, DNA methylation, global proteomics, phosphoproteomics, and acetylproteomics. Tumors were classified into the four TCGA-defined genomic subtypes (POLE, MSI, CNV-low, CNV-high). The study uncovers (i) mutation-type-specific cis/trans effects of TP53 and CTNNB1 on the proteome and phosphoproteome, (ii) coupling between Wnt signaling and histone acetylation through BRD3/SIRT1, (iii) a QKI/circRNA/ESRP2 regulatory axis controlling EMT, and (iv) APM (antigen processing and presentation machinery) deficiency — including microsatellite-indel-derived JAK1 truncation mutations — as a potential immune-evasion mechanism that complements TMB for immunotherapy patient selection in EC.

Cohort & data

  • 95 prospectively collected endometrial carcinoma (EC) tumors and 49 normal tissue samples from the CPTAC project, biospecimens collected April 2016–May 2017.
  • Histology: 83 endometrioid and 12 serous tumors. Genomic subtype distribution: 7 POLE, 25 MSI, 43 CNV-low (endometrioid-like), 20 CNV-high (serous-like).
  • Cancer types: UCEC (overall), with UEC endometrioid and USC serous histotypes. The CNV-high subtype contains all serous samples plus the most aggressive endometrioid tumors.
  • Dataset / cohort: ucec_cptac_2020.
  • Assays: whole-exome-seq (Illumina HiSeq4000, paired 76-cycle, min 150× on-target, hg38), PCR-free whole-genome-seq (HiSeqX, min 15× coverage, 151 bp paired-end), total and miRNA rna-seq (mirna-seq), Illumina Infinium MethylationEPIC BeadChip (epic-methylation-array, >850,000 CpG sites), isobaric 10-plex TMT-based global tmt-proteomics and tmt-phosphoproteomics (IMAC enrichment) plus genome-wide acetylproteomics (PTMScan Acetyl-Lysine Motif IAP), run at the PNNL Proteomic Characterization Center under a 1% protein-level FDR cutoff.
  • Data availability: Genomic Data Commons (GDC), CPTAC Data Portal, the cptac Python package, and LinkedOmics.

Key findings

  • Mutation burden by subtype. Roughly 61% of all somatic mutations were found in the 7 POLE tumors (n = 32,340; 32,188 point mutations and 152 indels), 5 of which harbored the recurrent hotspot mutation P286R in the catalytic subunit of DNA polymerase epsilon. MSI tumors carried 88% of all indels (n = 5,061).
  • Significantly mutated genes in MSI tumors. Microsatellite-indel focused analysis revealed higher mutation rates than previously reported for PTEN (92%), ARID1A (76%), and RPL22 (64%) — all FDR = 0. Three putative SMGs were newly identified in the MSI subtype: INPPL1 (56%, FDR = 0), KMT2B (56%, FDR = 0.001), and JAK1 (44%, FDR = 6.4e-07).
  • CNV landscape. Genes frequently altered by copy number variation — ERBB2, CCNE1, FGFR3, and IGF1R — overlapped substantially with TCGA findings.
  • Driver-mutation proteomic impact. Across 18 SMGs studied, 7 had cis and 6 had trans effects on protein/phosphoprotein levels (FDR < 0.05, Wilcoxon rank-sum); 143 trans associations spanning 49 interacting proteins were detected. The largest trans signatures were driven by ARID1A, TP53, and CTNNB1 mutations.
  • CTNNB1 hotspot mutations (23 tumors). All but two were CNV-low or MSI; none were higher than grade 2 and only three were stage III. HotSpot3D analysis showed exon 3 mutations (centered on S33) clustered in 3D with phosphosites Y30, S33, T40 and recurrent mutations at D32 and G34. Tumors with hotspot mutations had higher Wnt-pathway activity than WT MSI/CNV-low counterparts (p = 2.5e-3, Student’s t test) and concurrent upregulation of APC and AXIN1 protein levels.
  • APC/CTNNB1 mutual exclusivity. 11 tumors lacking CTNNB1 hotspot mutations but carrying APC mutations showed reduced APC and elevated β-catenin protein, supporting two mutually exclusive routes to β-catenin overexpression in EC.
  • TP53 mutation-type-specific effects (23 tumors, including all 12 serous). 11 tumors harbored DNA-binding-domain missense hotspots (including R248 and R273), leading to elevated p53 protein and downstream dysregulation of AURKA and XRN2 (p = 7.8e-06, t test for XRN2). 7 truncating-mutation tumors did not alter p53 levels (p = 0.082) but showed increased PLK1-T210 phosphorylation and enrichment of mitotic-marker proteins. A third subset including CDK1, XPO1, and TPX2 was dysregulated regardless of TP53 mutation type. These mutation-type-specific effects were not observed in CPTAC ovarian or colon cancer data.
  • Wnt–acetylation coupling. Upregulation of H2B N-terminal acetylation at K16, K20, K24 in CTNNB1 hotspot mutants, and upregulation of H3K27 and H3K36 acetylation in ARID1A- and KRAS-mutant samples. BRD3 and SIRT1 protein levels were increased in CTNNB1 hotspot mutants; BRD3 correlated positively with multiple H2B N-terminal acetyl sites, while SIRT1, SIRT3, and BRD4 correlated negatively with H3K27 and H3K36 acetylation. 322 tumor-vs-normal acetylation sites differed (216 up, 106 down).
  • DNA methylation. MSI tumors had elevated genome-wide CpG island methylation; methylation-silenced genes include MLH1 and several HOX family members. HOX protein levels were anticorrelated with H2AX phosphorylation, linking methylation to dampened DSB repair.
  • SCNAs and the p53 pathway. 14% of SCNAs had cis effects on transcript/protein (FDR < 0.01). 1q amplification — present in both MSI and MSS tumors — anticorrelated with p53 pathway activity (p < 0.01, Pearson). MDM4 mRNA increased with 1q copy number (p < 0.01, Spearman); SETDB1, SDE2, PARP1, and GATAD2B were the top 1q histone-modifier candidates inversely correlated with p53 pathway target proteins CDKN1A and TNFRSF10B.
  • circRNA/EMT regulator. 234 recurrent circRNAs identified. QKI protein correlated positively with 35 circRNAs and with EMT score and the EMT activators ZEB1 and ZEB2; ESRP2 correlated negatively with 20 circRNAs and negatively with QKI. QKI-correlated circRNAs harbored predicted binding sites for 36 miRNAs (p < 0.02), including miR-200c, miR-221, miR-130a, miR-130b, and miR-183.
  • MSI vs MSS proteomics. MLH1 and EPM2AIP1 were downregulated in MSI at mRNA and protein levels (shared promoter methylation); PMS1 and PMS2 were downregulated only at the protein level. RPL22L1 was upregulated in MSI tumors (highest in RPL22-mutant samples; p = 0.002, rank-sum) — consistent with the previously described RPL22/RPL22L1 synthetic-lethal relationship.
  • Serous vs endometrioid. Serous tumors had increased TP53BP1-S1763 and CHEK2-S163 phosphorylation; both phosphosites correlated with PLK1 protein, which in turn correlated with G2M checkpoint score (R = 0.67, p = 7.9e-14).
  • Tumor immune landscape. Protein-level evidence for putative neoantigens in 49.3% of samples; POLE > MSI in neoantigen burden. 8 known cancer/testis (CT) antigens — including IGF2BP3, ATAD2, PBK — observed in >10% of tumors (>3-fold tumor vs normal). 59% of tumors had ≥1 CT antigen; 78% had either a neoantigen or a high-expression CT antigen.
  • APM-deficient TMB-high tumors. TMB and antigen processing/presentation machinery (APM) efficiency varied independently. TMB-H/APM-L tumors had lower CD8+ T-cell infiltration than TMB-H/APM-H, lower IFNγ and IFNα activity, and were enriched for JAK1 truncation mutations and STAT1 truncation mutations (POLE samples). TMB-H/APM-L tumors without JAK1/STAT1 mutations had lower TAP1, TAP2, and TAP-binding protein levels, plus reduced HLA class I.

Genes & alterations

  • POLE — recurrent catalytic-domain hotspot P286R in 5/7 POLE-subtype tumors; defines the ultramutated subtype (~61% of all somatic mutations).
  • TP53 — mutations in 23 tumors including all serous carcinomas; missense DNA-binding-domain hotspots (R248, R273) elevate p53 protein and dysregulate AURKA, XRN2; truncating mutations increase PLK1-T210 phosphorylation and mitotic-marker proteins.
  • CTNNB1 — exon 3 hotspot region centered at S33 (with phosphosites Y30, S33, T40 and mutations D32, G34) found in 23 tumors; increases Wnt-pathway activity, β-catenin protein, and H2B N-terminal acetylation (K16/K20/K24).
  • APC — loss-of-function mutations in 11 CTNNB1-WT tumors provide an alternative route to β-catenin overexpression; APC and AXIN1 protein are upregulated when CTNNB1 hotspot mutated.
  • PTEN — mutated in 92% of MSI tumors (FDR = 0).
  • ARID1A — mutated in 76% of MSI tumors (FDR = 0); ARID1A-mutant samples show H3K27/H3K36 hyperacetylation; truncating mutations reduce ARID1A protein.
  • KRAS — KRAS-mutant samples show upregulation of H3K27 and H3K36 acetylation.
  • PIK3CA — missense mutations decrease PIK3CA protein levels in this cohort.
  • RPL22 / RPL22L1 — RPL22 mutated in 64% of MSI tumors; RPL22L1 protein upregulated in MSI, especially in RPL22-mutant tumors (p = 0.002), consistent with their synthetic-lethal pair.
  • KMT2B — putative SMG in MSI (56%, FDR = 0.001), not reported in TCGA.
  • INPPL1 — putative SMG in MSI (56%, FDR = 0); truncating mutations reduce protein levels.
  • JAK1 — putative SMG in MSI (44%, FDR = 6.4e-07); all JAK1 frameshifts were in MSI samples and derived from microsatellite indels; associated with higher tumor grade and enriched in TMB-H/APM-L tumors → candidate immune-evasion driver.
  • STAT1 — truncation mutation in a POLE TMB-H/APM-L sample.
  • MLH1 — silenced by promoter methylation in MSI tumors; protein downregulated.
  • EPM2AIP1 — downregulated at mRNA and protein in MSI tumors via shared promoter methylation with MLH1.
  • PMS1 / PMS2 — downregulated only at the protein level in MSI tumors (loss of MLH1 destabilizes them).
  • ERBB2, CCNE1, FGFR3, IGF1R — frequently copy-number altered, consistent with TCGA.
  • MDM4 — mRNA increases with 1q copy number in MSI tumors (p < 0.01, Spearman) — candidate p53-pathway suppressor on amplified 1q.
  • SETDB1, SDE2, PARP1, GATAD2B — 1q-encoded histone modifiers anticorrelated with p53-pathway target proteins (CDKN1A, TNFRSF10B).
  • CDK12 — hyperphosphorylated in CNV-high tumors; modulates ovarian-cancer sensitivity to olaparib (Bajrami et al., 2014) and could enhance immune-checkpoint response.
  • SMARCA4 — hyperphosphorylated in CNV-high tumors; targetable by FDA-approved drugs.
  • DNMT1 — hyperphosphorylated in DDR-high endometrioid and serous tumors; pharmacologically inhibited by azacitidine and decitabine.
  • CDK4 — kinase-activity inference flags CDK4 activation in CNV-high endometrioid vs CNV-low tumors; multiple FDA-approved inhibitors available.
  • QKI / ESRP2 / ZEB1 / ZEB2 — proposed EMT regulatory axis where QKI promotes 35 EMT-associated circRNAs (and is positively correlated with ZEB1/ZEB2), opposed by ESRP2.
  • FBXW7 — circRNA from FBXW7 reported (cited from Yang et al., 2018) to encode a protein product reducing c-Myc half-life.
  • FOXA2 — increased K274 acetylation in CNV-low subtype (deacetylation reduces FOXA2 stability); role in EC remains unclear.
  • ATAD2, PBK, IGF2BP3 — CT antigens observed in >10% of tumors; IGF2BP3 highly upregulated in serous and known immunogenic peptide source.
  • TAP1, TAP2 — reduced antigen-peptide-transporter protein levels in TMB-H/APM-L tumors lacking JAK1/STAT1 mutations.
  • TP53BP1 S1763 / CHEK2 S163 — phosphosites elevated in serous tumors; PLK1-driven inactivation of the DDR and G2M-checkpoint progression.
  • PLK1, CDK1, CHEK1, XPO1, TPX2, KMT2D, MAP3K4 — downstream of TP53 / CTNNB1 / ARID1A mutations in trans.

Clinical implications

  • CTNNB1 hotspot mutations identify a subset of low-grade, low-stage endometrioid tumors with potentially worse prognosis (recapitulating Kurnit et al., 2017; Myers et al., 2014), now mechanistically linked to constitutive Wnt activation and H2B acetylation — refining biomarkers for adjuvant therapy selection.
  • TP53 hotspot missense (gain-of-function) mutations upregulate the Aurora kinase pathway, providing a theoretical basis for AURKA inhibitor evaluation in TP53-hotspot-mutant EC.
  • DDR-high tumors: hyperphosphorylation of DNMT1 suggests potential utility of FDA-approved DNMT inhibitors azacitidine and decitabine — beyond traditional PARP inhibitors — in elevated-DNA-damage EC.
  • CNV-high tumors: CDK12 hyperphosphorylation could sensitize EC to the PARP inhibitor olaparib (analogous to ovarian cancer; Bajrami et al., 2014) and may enhance immune-checkpoint blockade responses. SMARCA4 and CDK4 are additional CNV-high candidates with existing FDA-approved drugs.
  • Immunotherapy selection: TMB alone is insufficient. JAK1/STAT1 truncating mutations and reduced TAP1/TAP2/HLA levels mark APM-deficient tumors that may resist immune checkpoint inhibitors even when TMB is high — and should be considered when selecting EC patients for ICI, in addition to the FDA-approved MSI biomarker.
  • Putative vaccine antigens: 78% of EC tumors harbor either a neoantigen or a high-expression CT antigen, supporting proteogenomics as a vaccine target-discovery tool in EC.

Limitations & open questions

  • Observational study: most mechanistic links (Wnt–acetylation, QKI/circRNA/EMT, APM deficiency, CDK12/PARP-inhibitor sensitivity) require functional validation.
  • Cohort size of 95 tumors limits statistical power, particularly for the 7 POLE and 12 serous subgroups.
  • Demographic and outcome data for this prospective cohort were still maturing at publication; associations between molecular features and recurrence/response/survival remain to be tested.
  • MDM4 protein was not detected (low abundance), preventing direct protein-level confirmation of the 1q-amp / p53-pathway-repression model.
  • Whether the CTNNB1 → histone-acetylation effects are EC-specific or generalize across cancers will require parallel acetylproteomic studies.
  • FOXA2 acetylation has been linked to both proliferation (colon) and metastasis inhibition (lung adenocarcinoma); its role in EC is undefined.
  • Sequencing-platform differences from the TCGA EC cohort may account for some observed mutation-rate increases.
  • Acetylated peptides prepared via IP from the IMAC flow-through likely miss peptides that are both phosphorylated and acetylated; dual-modified PTM stoichiometry was not pursued.
