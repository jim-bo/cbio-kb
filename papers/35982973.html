Whole genome analysis reveals the genomic complexity in metastatic cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma

Bruce Ashford

Jenny Mitchell

PMID: 35982973 · DOI: 10.3389/fonc.2022.919118 · Journal: Frontiers in Oncology (2022)

TL;DR

Thind et al. performed whole genome sequencing (WGS) on lymph node metastases and matched blood from 25 patients with regional metastatic cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (CSCC) of the head and neck. This is the largest WGS study of metastatic CSCC to date and the first to interrogate non-coding regulatory regions (3′UTRs, lncRNAs, promoters) genome-wide. Beyond confirming TP53 and CDKN2A as recurrent coding drivers, they identified C9 (complement component 9) as a novel candidate driver, recurrent 3′UTR alterations in EVC, PPP1R1A, and ABCA4, recurrent mutation of the tumor-suppressor lncRNA LINC01003 (64% of samples), focal copy-number loss of PTPRD, and a novel indel-signature profile (ID8/ID9/ID13) distinct from melanoma. The data were deposited in cBioPortal as cscc_ranson_2022 (cscc_ranson_2022).

Cohort & data

  • 25 patients with resectable regional metastatic CSCC of the head and neck (lymph node metastases), recruited 2015–2019 with matched blood DNA.
  • Median age 69 years (range 30–87); 24/25 (96%) male; 2/25 immunosuppressed (one on long-term azathioprine, one on combination cyclophosphamide and tacrolimus post solid-organ transplant).
  • Nodal stage: predominantly N3b; metastases in neck (n=13) or parotid (n=12). Differentiation: moderately (n=8) or poorly (n=12) differentiated. Extranodal extension in 20/25 (80%).
  • 32 samples sequenced; 25 passed QC (>30% tumor cellularity, range 35–95%). Mean tumor coverage 94.56× (range 64–143×); mean blood coverage 41.08×.
  • Reference genome GRCh38; alignment with BWA-MEM (bwa); GATK 4.1.2.0 (gatk) HaplotypeCaller (germline) and Mutect2 (somatic) for short variants.
  • Cohort deposited in cBioPortal as cscc_ranson_2022 — see cscc_ranson_2022.
  • Variant call files deposited at the European Genome-Phenome Archive under accession EGAS00001006378.

Key findings

  • Extreme TMB. Average tumor mutational burden was 238.7 mutations/Mb (median 166.99, range 32.52–995.66) for SNVs and 2.25 indels/Mb (range 0.63–5.9) at the whole-genome scale — substantially higher than melanoma (~49 mutations/Mb) and much higher than the WES-derived 61.2 mutations/Mb reported by Pickering et al. (the majority of variants fall in non-coding DNA).
  • UV mutational signatures dominate. Over 80% of SNVs were C>T transitions consistent with UV mutagenesis; COSMIC v3.2 SBS7a and SBS7b dominated, with substantial SBS7c representation. One sample showed SBS32, attributable to azathioprine exposure.
  • Novel indel signatures. ID8, ID9, and ID13 dominated across all 25 samples. ID13 was previously reported only in melanoma; ID9 — predominant in soft-tissue sarcoma but rare in melanoma (2/104) — was newly identified as a dominant indel signature in CSCC, defining a novel indel pattern for this UV-driven skin cancer (Figure 7).
  • Three-tool consensus driver list. Filtering genes called significant (p<0.005) by ≥2 of OncodriveFML (oncodrivefml), MutSigCV (mutsigcv), and dNdScv (dndscv) yielded 12 coding drivers: TP53, CDKN2A, C9, C9orf131, SLC22A6, KHDRBS2, COLEC12, LINGO2, CDHR5, ZNF442, PRLR, and DHRS4. TP53, CDKN2A, and C9 were called by all three tools.
  • Non-coding 3′UTR drivers. OncodriveFML identified significantly altered 3′UTRs (Q-value <0.03) in EVC (48% of samples; Q=0.011), PPP1R1A (48% of samples; Q=0.022), ABCA4 (20%), and LUM. A unique recurrent PPP1R1A 3′UTR variant (c.*491C>T; Chr12:54579896 G→A) was observed in 5/25 samples.
  • lncRNA drivers. Four lncRNAs were significantly biased toward high-impact mutations: LINC01003 (64% of cohort; Q=0.0158), LINC01474, RP4-597N16.4, and RP11-61J19.4 (48%).
  • Copy number landscape. Chromosome arm gains at 7 and 5p and losses at 8p, 18q, and 21q were recurrent across the cohort. PTPRD on chr9p was the most frequently deleted gene (24% of samples) and the dominant focus of focal/arm/chromosome-level LOH; PTPRD loss and significant CDKN2A mutation co-occurred in 6 samples but PTPRD deletion and CDKN2A deletion did not co-occur.
  • Rare amplifications. Recurrent amplifications were uncommon; CALR, CCND1, and FGF3 were each amplified in only 2/25 samples (8%). CCND1 and FGF3 co-amplified in 2 cases (CSCC_0132 and CSCC_0134), both with extensive nodal involvement (>50% of lymph nodes harboring tumor). EGFR was amplified in only one sample.
  • Structural variation. Deletions and complex structural variants predominated; despite widespread genomic instability, only two coding–coding gene fusions were detected: STRN–DLG2 (CSCC_0009, the only TP53-wildtype sample) and HEBP2–NTRK2 (CSCC_0011, via unbalanced translocation).
  • The only TP53-wildtype sample (CSCC_0009) had TMB of 122/Mb (51% of cohort average); the 5 CDKN2A-wildtype samples averaged TMB of 470/Mb (201% of cohort average).
  • Pathway enrichment. Among 21 candidate altered genes, top enriched BioPlanet terms included TP53 network, tumor suppressor ARF, CTCF pathway, cell cycle G1/S checkpoint, and “TGF-beta regulation of extracellular matrix” — with LUM, CDHR5, COLEC12, and CDKN2A contributing to the latter.
  • cBioPortal cross-cohort comparison. Using 92 metastatic CSCC and 88 primary CSCC samples already on cBioPortal (drawn from MSK-IMPACT, the Li et al. FFPE cohort, and Pickering et al.), the authors confirmed recurrent mutation of TP53, CDKN2A, C9, COLEC12, and SLC22A6. COLEC12 (primary 25%, metastatic 60%) and SLC22A6 (primary 16%, metastatic 30%) were enriched in metastatic disease.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — recurrently mutated; loss-of-function across 24/25 samples (only CSCC_0009 wildtype). Driver by all three tools. Cohort frequency consistent with the high-TMB UV-driven phenotype.
  • CDKN2A — recurrently mutated; LOF impacting p16INK4a and ARF, dysregulating cell cycle and apoptotic pathways. Driver by all three tools. Deep deletion observed in 1/25 samples.
  • C9 — novel candidate driver (52% of samples carried SNVs). Encodes complement component 9; identified by all three driver tools. cBioPortal shows C9 mutated in 31% primary and 10% metastatic CSCC. Mechanistic link proposed via tumor microenvironment and macrophage biology.
  • PTPRD — most frequently deleted gene (24% of samples) on 9p; tumor suppressor; loss reported to drive STAT3 hyperactivation and resistance to EGFR-targeted therapy in HNSCC.
  • EVC — recurrent 3′UTR mutation (48%); regulator of ciliary Hedgehog signaling.
  • PPP1R1A — recurrent 3′UTR mutation (48%); a unique missense variant (c.*491C>T) in 5/25 samples.
  • ABCA4 — recurrent 3′UTR mutation (20%).
  • LUM — 3′UTR mutation; keratan sulfate proteoglycan implicated in TGFβ/ECM enrichment.
  • LINC01003 — recurrently mutated tumor-suppressor lncRNA (64% of samples); previously shown to act via miR-33a-5p / PIM1 in multiple myeloma.
  • LINC01474 — recurrent lncRNA mutation.
  • KHDRBS2, COLEC12, SLC22A6, LINGO2, CDHR5, ZNF442, PRLR, DHRS4 — candidate coding drivers called by ≥2 tools; KHDRBS2 altered in 48% of cohort; COLEC12 and SLC22A6 also enriched in metastatic vs primary CSCC on cBioPortal.
  • ZNF730 — OncodriveFML coding driver (Q-value <0.1) but not corroborated by all three tools.
  • CALR, CCND1, FGF3 — focally amplified in 2/25 samples each; CCND1 and FGF3 co-amplified in CSCC_0132 and CSCC_0134.
  • EGFR — amplified in a single sample (CSCC_0012).
  • MYC — structural amplification in CSCC_0005.
  • NOTCH1 / NOTCH2 — notably absent from significant or recurrent SNVs in this cohort, in contrast to the primary-CSCC NOTCH-prominent landscape reported by Inman et al.
  • GRHL2 — only a single splice variant observed, suggesting a limited role in metastatic disease here.
  • Structural variant gene list (LINX/PURPLE): SMAD4 (DEL), CDKN2A (DEL), MYC (GAIN), CRLF2, PTPRD (DEL/LOH in multiple samples), CALR (GAIN), EGFR (GAIN), APC (DEL), CREBBP (DEL), CDKN2C (DEL), PARD6G, PTPN13 (DEL), NEGR1 (DEL), RAF1, CCND1, FGF3, MCL1, KDM6A (DEL).
  • Fusions: STRN–DLG2 (STRN exon 1 ENST00000263918 — DLG2 exon 7 ENST00000376104) in CSCC_0009; HEBP2–NTRK2 (HEBP2 — NTRK2) in CSCC_0011 (unbalanced translocation).

Clinical implications

  • Immune checkpoint blockade. Recurrent TP53 and CDKN2A loss-of-function — together with extreme TMB — provides a rationale for the high response rates observed with anti–PD-1 therapy in advanced CSCC (e.g. cemiplimab; Migden et al. 2018, NEJMoa1805131).
  • CDK4/6 inhibition. CDKN2A loss disables p16INK4a-mediated CDK control; the authors propose CDK4/6 inhibitors such as palbociclib — established in metastatic breast cancer — as a candidate therapeutic strategy for metastatic CSCC.
  • MDM2/MDM4 axis. The TP53–MDM2–ARF network (also encoded by CDKN2A) is highlighted as a potential target.
  • TGF-β / ECM axis. TGFβ-regulation-of-extracellular-matrix was a significantly enriched pathway via LUM, CDHR5, COLEC12, and CDKN2A; TGFβ receptor inhibition with chemotherapy or immunotherapy is proposed as a candidate strategy by analogy to other cancers.
  • miRNA potentiation. miR-331-5p was predicted to target six driver candidates including TP53 and C9, suggesting potential as a CSCC-driver–targeting miRNA.
  • Non-coding biomarkers. Recurrent 3′UTRs in EVC, PPP1R1A, ABCA4 and the lncRNA LINC01003 are proposed as candidate non-coding biomarkers of metastatic CSCC — and as concrete examples of regions invisible to WES/targeted-panel platforms.
  • Iatrogenic mutagenesis. SBS32 in one patient is attributable to azathioprine immunosuppression, reinforcing the importance of treatment history in interpreting CSCC mutational landscapes.

Limitations & open questions

  • Cohort size and sex skew. Only 25 patients; 24/25 male. Potential sex differences are unaddressed; the authors note that this skew mirrors the disease burden in NSW, Australia but caution it is a limitation.
  • Metastatic disease only. All samples are nodal metastases; no matched primary tumors. Direct primary-vs-metastasis comparisons rely on external cBioPortal cohorts of differing assay design (WES, targeted NGS) rather than within-patient pairing.
  • 5′UTR / promoter regions excluded. Limitations of current methodology in high-TMB tumors meant 5′UTRs and promoters (other than via prior CCCTC-binding factor analysis) were not analyzed in depth — leaving a sizeable non-coding fraction of the CSCC genome unstudied.
  • Functional validation outstanding. Novel candidate drivers (C9, KHDRBS2, COLEC12, SLC22A6, EVC/PPP1R1A 3′UTRs, LINC01003) lack experimental validation; biological mechanisms are inferred from cross-cancer literature rather than CSCC-specific functional studies.
  • No matched RNA / methylation. Effects of UTR and lncRNA variants on gene expression are not measured in this dataset.
  • Comparator panels are heterogeneous. cBioPortal CSCC cohorts used for cross-validation (Zehir/MSK-IMPACT, Li et al., Pickering et al.) differ in assay (WES vs targeted NGS), tissue preservation (mostly FFPE), and selection — limiting head-to-head frequency comparisons.
