The genomic landscapes of individual melanocytes from human skin

Authors

Jessica Tang

Eleanor Fewings

Darwin Chang

Hanlin Zeng

Shanshan Liu

Aparna Jorapur

Rachel L. Belote

Andrew S. McNeal

Doi

10.1038/s41586-020-2785-8

PMID: 33029006 · DOI: 10.1038/s41586-020-2785-8 · Journal: Nature (2020)

TL;DR

Tang et al. performed single-cell-resolution genotyping of 133 individual melanocytes clonally expanded from 19 sites across 6 light-skinned, elderly donors (ages 63–85) of European ancestry — some with adjacent skin cancer, some without. Using G&T-Seq matched DNA/RNA from each clone plus the UCSF500 cancer panel (509 cancer-relevant genes; subset extended to whole exome), they showed sun-exposed melanocytes carry heavy UV-radiation signature-7 mutation burdens (mean 7.9 mut/Mb, range 0.82–32.3), that melanocytes adjacent to skin cancers are more heavily mutated, and that clinically normal skin commonly harbors melanocytes with weakly oncogenic pathogenic mutations in MAPK-pathway and chromatin-remodeling genes — but conspicuously lacks BRAF V600E and TERT promoter mutations. Phylogenetic analysis revealed clonal fields of related melanocytes spreading invisibly through skin.

Cohort & data

  • 133 individual melanocytes (post-QC) clonally expanded from skin biopsies of 6 donors (cadavers and surgical-discard tissue from UCSF), 19 anatomic sites, ages 63–85, all light-skinned European-ancestry donors.
  • Donors split between those with adjacent skin cancer (peritumoral tissue) and those without skin cancer history; subset adjacent to MEL or BCC lesions.
  • Subset of 5 melanocytes had engineered CDKN2A mutations (CRISPR); removal does not affect conclusions.
  • All 133 cells underwent targeted UCSF500 sequencing of 509 cancer-relevant genes; 48 cells additionally received whole-exome sequencing.
  • Each clone also underwent transcriptome RNA-seq via Smart-seq2 as part of the G&T-Seq protocol for matched DNA/RNA from the same cell.
  • Dataset: normal_skin_melanocytes_2024. Sequence data deposited in dbGaP under accession phs001979.v1.p1. Reference genome: hg19. Sequencing on Illumina HiSeq 2500 / NovaSeq, paired-end 100/150 bp.
  • Mean coverage: 489x unique (targeted), 86x (exome), 7.75M reads/clone (RNA-seq).
  • Variants called with Mutect v3.4.46 after BWA-MEM v0.7.13 alignment; copy number from CNVkit; mutational signatures via deconstructSigs referencing the Petljak et al. 48-signature catalog (focus on UV-associated signatures 7a/7b/7c).

Key findings

  • Mean somatic mutation burden of 7.9 mut/Mb across single melanocytes, ranging from <0.82 to 32.3 mut/Mb. Mean of 37 mutations per cell from targeted sequencing and 790 from exome PMID:33029006.
  • Sun-shielded melanocytes had far fewer mutations and minimal UV signature than sun-exposed melanocytes, where signature 7 (UV) was dominant.
  • Counterintuitive anatomic-site pattern: melanocytes from intermittently sun-exposed skin (back, limbs) carried higher mutation burdens than those from chronically sun-exposed skin (face, neck, bald scalp). Authors note this parallels the disproportionate occurrence of cutaneous melanoma on intermittently-exposed sites.
  • Melanocytes adjacent to skin cancer (donors 6 and 13) had the highest mutation burdens — more than half of melanocytes from these donors exceeded the median mutation burden of melanoma itself, suggesting that field-level mutational damage in normal skin can serve as a biomarker for cumulative sun damage and skin-cancer risk.
  • Bulk melanoma from one donor had a mutation burden comparable to its surrounding individual normal melanocytes — contrasting with colorectal cancers, which exceed surrounding normal cells. No mutation overlap between the melanoma and surrounding melanocytes.
  • Copy number alterations were rare in normal melanocytes, except recurrent Y-chromosome loss and inactive-X loss (consistent with aging-associated mosaic loss reported in normal blood). Supports the model that copy-number instability is acquired late in melanoma evolution.
  • 29 pathogenic mutations identified across 24 melanocytes (Table 1). Pathogenic-mutation burden was concentrated in the MAPK pathway.
  • BRAF V600E was conspicuously absent despite being the most common MAPK mutation in melanocytic neoplasms — supporting the WHO low-CSD vs high-CSD dichotomy in which low-CSD melanomas (driven by BRAF V600E from nevi) originate distinctly from high-CSD melanomas (driven by diverse MAPK mutations arising de novo).
  • No TERT promoter mutations were detected despite their prominence in melanoma, suggesting TERT promoter mutations confer little selective advantage outside the neoplastic context.
  • Phylogenetic analysis identified nine pairs/trios of clonally related melanocytes sharing somatic mutations, demonstrating that melanocytes spread as clonal fields invisibly across skin. Four of these fields had a pathogenic mutation in the phylogenetic trunk.
  • Field-establishing trunk mutations were ruled out as tissue-culture artifacts by growing neonatal melanocytes for months and measuring accumulation — private mutation counts in observed fields exceeded what ~2 weeks of culture could produce, and exhibited UV signature 7 (which does not operate in culture).
  • MDM2 expression positively correlated with mutation burden (DESeq2, adjusted p < 0.01), raising the possibility that heterogeneity in p53 activity (via MDM2-mediated p53 degradation) contributes to the wide range of mutation burdens observed at a single anatomic site.

Authors classify all detected MAPK-pathway mutations as weak activators based on prior functional studies, explaining the absence of clinically visible lesions despite the presence of canonical driver-class mutations. Mutations outside MAPK (chromatin remodelers, cell-cycle regulators) are interpreted as insufficient individually but capable of accelerating progression upon acquisition of a subsequent MAPK driver.

Clinical implications

  • Cumulative sun damage / melanoma-risk biomarker: The authors propose that the mutation burden of normal melanocytes can be harnessed as a measurable biomarker for cumulative UV damage and skin-cancer risk, supported by donors 6 and 13 (both with adjacent skin cancer) carrying the heaviest burdens.
  • Origins of de novo melanoma: The work supports the model that most cutaneous melanomas arise without a precursor nevus from “poised” melanocytes in normal skin that already harbor pathogenic mutations, particularly in high-CSD contexts.
  • WHO low-CSD vs high-CSD distinction reinforced: Absence of BRAF V600E among observed pathogenic mutations is consistent with the high-CSD origin model in which a diverse set of MAPK-pathway mutations (NF1, NRAS, RASA2, CBL, non-V600 BRAF, MAP2K1) drive de novo melanomas, whereas BRAF V600E predominantly drives low-CSD melanomas arising from nevi.
  • TERT promoter mutation timing: Findings imply TERT promoter activation is acquired during neoplastic progression, not in pre-neoplastic melanocytes — relevant to ordering of driver events in melanoma evolution.

No specific drug or therapeutic intervention is evaluated in this study.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small donor cohort (n=6) of uniform demographic — all light-skinned, all European-ancestry, all 63–85 years old. Generalization to other ancestries, skin tones, and ages is not established.
  • Tissue-culture clonal expansion is required, so the population is biased toward melanocytes that proliferate ex vivo (~38% colony-formation rate). Authors argue this is “prevalent and representative” but a proliferation-defective subpopulation could be missed.
  • 5 melanocytes had CRISPR-engineered CDKN2A mutations prior to genotyping; authors state exclusion does not alter conclusions but the CDKN2A p.V43M call in Table 1 is presented as naturally occurring.
  • Only 25.1% of variants could be classified as somatic vs. amplification artifact via expression cross-validation or haplotype phasing — the remainder were classified by VAF, a less specific approach. Some calls in poorly expressed / unphasable regions retain residual uncertainty.
  • Anatomic-site mutation-burden inversion deserves further study — the authors flag the higher burden on intermittently sun-exposed back/limb versus chronically sun-exposed face as needing follow-up on differences in mutation rate, DNA repair, or melanocyte turnover.
  • Mechanism of melanocyte field formation is open — clonal fields could arise from a pathogenic trunk mutation conferring fitness, or naturally during body-surface expansion or homeostasis; the data cannot adjudicate.
  • Tumor adjacency: Only one melanoma was available for paired bulk sequencing, so the conclusion that melanoma mutation burdens match surrounding normal melanocytes (in contrast to colorectal cancer) is based on n=1 and warrants validation.
