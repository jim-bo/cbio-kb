TL;DR

Razavi et al. prospectively sequenced 1,918 breast tumors from 1,756 patients (1,501 hormone receptor-positive) at MSKCC using the MSK-IMPACT panel and linked the resulting genomic profiles to detailed treatment histories. In the 692 tumors collected after exposure to endocrine therapy, activating ERBB2 mutations and loss-of-function NF1 mutations were more than twice as common as in untreated tumors; additional MAPK-pathway lesions (EGFR amplification, KRAS, HRAS, BRAF, MAP2K1, ERBB3) and ER-transcriptional-machinery alterations (MYC, CTCF, FOXA1, TBX3) collectively affected 22% of post-hormonal-therapy tumors and were mutually exclusive with ESR1 hotspot mutations. MAPK-pathway alterations and transcription-factor lesions both predicted significantly shorter progression-free survival on aromatase inhibitors compared with pan-wild-type tumors.

Cohort & data

  • 1,918 breast tumors from 1,756 patients sequenced prospectively (April 2014 – March 2017) at Memorial Sloan Kettering; clinico-genomic dataset published as breast_msk_2018 (PMID:30205045).
  • Hormone-receptor-positive (HR+) tumors comprised 1,501 of 1,918 samples; of these, 692 were collected after exposure to endocrine therapy and 809 were endocrine-therapy-naïve (PMID:30205045).
  • 52% of tumors (1,000/1,918) were from metastatic sites; 52% of samples were collected after exposure to systemic therapy in the (neo)adjuvant (44%) and/or metastatic (27%) settings, with a median of three prior systemic regimens in the metastatic setting (range 1–15) (PMID:30205045).
  • Histologic subtypes included invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC), invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC), and mixed IDC/ILC under the broader BRCA umbrella (PMID:30205045).
  • Assay: MSK-IMPACT hybrid-capture targeted sequencing, using the 341-gene (IMPACT341, n=432), 410-gene (IMPACT410, n=968), or 468-gene (IMPACT468, n=518) panels; mean coverage 771-fold; previously described in Cheng 2015 and Zehir 2017 (PMID:28481359).
  • 30 patients had matched treatment-naïve primary and post-progression tumors sequenced by both whole-exome-seq (median 358-fold coverage) and high-depth MSK-IMPACT; an additional 44 patients had only MSK-IMPACT matched pairs (74 total matched pairs) (PMID:30205045).
  • One research autopsy patient: 10 metastatic tissues subjected to WES (median 296-fold coverage); selected variants confirmed by droplet-digital-pcr on a QX200 system (PMID:30205045).
  • Copy-number inference: FACETS algorithm for allele-specific copy number and purity (PMID:30205045).
  • Hotspot detection: the algorithm of Chang et al. 2016 (cancer-hotspots) applied to a combined cohort of 2,732 breast tumors (this prospective MSK cohort + retrospectively sequenced primaries including TCGA) (PMID:30205045).
  • IRB approval NCT01775072; protocol allowed return of results to patients and physicians (PMID:30205045).

Key findings

  • Across the 2,732-tumor combined cohort, 313 statistically significant hotspot mutations were identified in 72 genes. PIK3CA alone had 35 hotspots affecting 699 tumors (36.4%); 12 previously unknown PIK3CA hotspots were identified as significantly mutated, and 5 of these variants of previously unknown significance induced PI3K pathway activation in MCF10A cells greater than wild-type (PMID:30205045).
  • 32 genes were significantly more frequently mutated in metastases versus primary tumors (q < 0.05); 29 of the 32 (91%) arose in HR+HER2− disease. Metastasis-enriched genes included TP53, epigenetic regulators (ARID1A, ARID2, CREBBP), and MAPK effectors (ERBB2, NF1, FGFR4) (PMID:30205045).
  • ERBB2 activating mutations and NF1 loss-of-function mutations were more than twice as common in specimens acquired after endocrine therapy versus before (p = 3×10⁻⁵ for ERBB2; p = 4×10⁻⁴ for NF1) (PMID:30205045).
  • ESR1 ligand-binding-domain mutations occurred in 18% of post-endocrine-therapy HR+HER2− tumors (128/692), consistent with prior reports from Chandarlapaty 2016 (PMID:27149842) and Toy 2013 (PMID:30205045).
  • ESR1 mutations were mutually exclusive with MAPK-pathway alterations and with transcription-factor lesions (mutual-exclusivity p < 0.0001). The MAPK-pathway alterations and transcription-factor lesions together affected 27% of ESR1-wild-type post-hormonal-therapy HR+HER2− tumors (n = 564) and 22% of all 692 post-hormonal-therapy tumors (PMID:30205045).
  • 16% of post-hormonal-therapy HR+HER2− ESR1-wild-type tumors (n = 90) carried known or presumed oncogenic alterations in MAPK effectors (ERBB2, NF1, EGFR, ERBB3, KRAS, HRAS, BRAF, MAP2K1); 12 patients had focal EGFR amplifications in metastases (all previously treated with tamoxifen; 8 also previously received an aromatase inhibitor) (PMID:30205045).
  • MAPK-pathway lesions predicted significantly shorter PFS on aromatase inhibitors: median 3.5 vs 15.2 months for pan-wild-type tumors (log-rank p = 1.4×10⁻⁹). Transcription-factor-altered tumors had median PFS 6.4 months (p = 1.3×10⁻⁶ vs pan-wild-type); ESR1-mutant tumors had median PFS 4.1 months (p = 3×10⁻⁴) (PMID:30205045).
  • On SERD (fulvestrant) therapy, MAPK-altered tumors had shorter PFS than pan-wild-type (median 3.7 vs 4.8 months; p = 0.029); ESR1-mutant and transcription-factor-altered tumors had PFS similar to pan-wild-type on SERD (PMID:30205045).
  • HER2 status concordance between IHC/FISH and ERBB2 amplification by MSK-IMPACT was 98% (1,778/1,810 samples; κ = 0.9, 95% CI 0.87–0.94) (PMID:30205045).
  • In matched-pair analyses, 60% of post-progression EGFR amplifications and 82% of post-progression ERBB2 mutations were already present in the treatment-naïve primary tumor, whereas oncogenic mutations in downstream MAPK effectors (NF1, KRAS, MAP2K1, BRAF) were typically not detected in the primary and were either acquired or selected by endocrine therapy in 74% of affected patients (PMID:30205045).
  • Research autopsy: a single HR+ patient harbored multiple convergent mechanisms across metastatic sites — pleural disease carried ESR1 Y537, while five spatially distinct lesions harbored independent ERBB2 driver mutations (S310, L755, D769); ddPCR (sensitivity ~0.03%) confirmed mutual exclusivity of ESR1 and ERBB2 across sites (PMID:30205045).
  • Functional validation in MCF7 cells: stable EGFR overexpression conferred fulvestrant resistance, reversed by co-treatment with erlotinib or gefitinib, or by structurally distinct ERK inhibitors (including SCH772984). CRISPR/Cas9-mediated NF1 knockout (4 independent guide RNAs) increased phospho-ERK, MEK, and RSK and reduced fulvestrant sensitivity, which was reversed by ERK inhibition (PMID:30205045).

Genes & alterations

  • ESR1 — ligand-binding-domain hotspot mutations (e.g., Y537) in 18% of post-endocrine-therapy HR+HER2− tumors; mutually exclusive with MAPK and transcription-factor lesions; associated with shorter PFS on AI but not on SERD (PMID:30205045).
  • ERBB2 — activating hotspot mutations (S310, L755, D769) > 2× enriched in post-endocrine specimens (p = 3×10⁻⁵); 82% of post-progression ERBB2 mutations pre-existed in matched primaries; functional resistance driver (PMID:30205045).
  • NF1 — loss-of-function mutations > 2× enriched after endocrine therapy (p = 4×10⁻⁴); typically acquired or selected under therapy (not detected in matched primaries); CRISPR knockout in MCF7 increased MAPK signaling and induced fulvestrant resistance reversible by ERK inhibition (PMID:30205045).
  • EGFR — focal amplifications in 12 post-endocrine-therapy patients (all prior tamoxifen-exposed; 8 also prior AI); 60% of post-progression amplifications were already present in the matched primary; MCF7 EGFR overexpression confers fulvestrant resistance, reversed by erlotinib or gefitinib (PMID:30205045).
  • KRAS, HRAS, BRAF, MAP2K1, ERBB3 — hotspot mutations in MAPK effectors enriched in post-endocrine-therapy tumors; KRAS G12R highlighted as a subclonal-to-clonal switch under endocrine selection in one matched-pair patient (PMID:30205045).
  • MYC — focal amplifications enriched in post-hormonal-therapy specimens; ~half were subclonal-and-selected or pre-existing in matched primaries (PMID:30205045).
  • CTCF — H284 and Y226 hotspots arising exclusively in invasive ductal carcinomas (histology-specific); enriched post-hormonal-therapy; associated with shorter PFS on AI (PMID:30205045).
  • FOXA1 — Forkhead-domain hotspots with histology-specific allele distribution (lobular tumors on wing 2; ductal tumors on wing 1); transcription-factor resistance lesion (PMID:30205045).
  • TBX3 — N297 hotspot exclusive to lobular carcinomas; transcription-factor resistance lesion (PMID:30205045).
  • PIK3CA — 35 statistically significant hotspots; mutated in 36.4% of the combined 2,732-tumor cohort (699 tumors); 12 newly significant hotspots, 5 of which induced elevated PI3K signaling in MCF10A cells when expressed as variants (PMID:30205045).
  • TP53 — significantly more common in metastases of all receptor subtypes except HR−HER2+; biomarker of poor prognosis (PMID:30205045).
  • GATA3 — loss-of-function mutations associated with lung metastasis (organotropism) (PMID:30205045).
  • CDH1 — mutations associated with ovarian metastasis, reflecting lobular-carcinoma predisposition to ovarian spread (PMID:30205045).
  • CCND1, PTEN — recurrently altered in HR+ metastatic cohort with patterns consistent with primary disease (PMID:30205045).
  • ARID1A, ARID2 — SWI/SNF-complex loss-of-function more often acquired post-treatment than pre-treatment in matched pairs (PMID:30205045).
  • CREBBP — epigenetic regulator enriched in HR+ metastatic context (PMID:30205045).
  • FGFR4, FGFR1 — FGFR4 enriched in HR+ metastases; FGFR1 focal amplification noted as a clonal event in a representative matched-pair patient (PMID:30205045).
  • SPOP — E78K mutations in 3 HR+HER2− patients post-AI therapy, affecting a MATH-domain region distinct from prostate-cancer SPOP hotspots and analogous to ER-stabilizing loss-of-function alleles in endometrial cancer (PMID:30205045).
  • RHOA — L22R subclonal hotspot present in a treatment-naïve primary but lost at post-progression, illustrating subclone replacement under endocrine therapy (PMID:30205045).

Clinical implications

  • The authors propose a four-class taxonomy of endocrine-resistant breast cancer: (1) ESR1-mutant, (2) MAPK-pathway-altered, (3) ER-transcriptional-machinery-altered (MYC, CTCF, FOXA1, TBX3), and (4) pan-wild-type tumors with unknown resistance mechanism (PMID:30205045).
  • HR+ tumors carrying ESR1, MAPK, or transcription-factor lesions are unlikely to benefit from continued aromatase-inhibitor therapy and should be considered for alternative regimens (PMID:30205045).
  • HER2 kinase inhibitors are active in ERBB2-mutant breast tumors (citing Hyman et al. 2018); erlotinib and gefitinib reverse fulvestrant resistance in EGFR-amplified MCF7 cells, suggesting EGFR inhibitors warrant prospective evaluation in EGFR-amplified endocrine-resistant HR+ disease (PMID:30205045).
  • MAPK-pathway inhibitors (selective MEK and ERK inhibitors) are flagged as candidate combination partners with hormonal therapy in MAPK-altered HR+ tumors (PMID:30205045).
  • The autopsy data — convergent independent ERBB2 driver mutations across metastatic sites, with ESR1 and ERBB2 in distinct subclones — argue for sensitive cfDNA profiling rather than single-site biopsy to capture coexisting resistance mechanisms (PMID:30205045).
  • Fulvestrant (SERD) outperforms aromatase inhibitors in ESR1-mutant and transcription-factor-altered settings (no PFS deficit vs pan-wild-type), but MAPK-altered tumors remain resistant on SERD as well (PMID:30205045).

Limitations & open questions

  • Single-institution dataset with retrospective clinical annotation; the authors call for independent multi-institutional confirmation (PMID:30205045).
  • 87.5% of biopsied metastatic tumors had prior adjuvant or metastatic therapy exposure, making it analytically impossible to fully disentangle metastasis-intrinsic from therapy-selected alterations; the treatment-naïve metastatic subgroup (n = 125) was underpowered for separate enrichment analysis (PMID:30205045).
  • Single biopsies cannot capture the multi-clonal, spatially heterogeneous resistance phenotypes evident from the autopsy case; cfDNA studies suggest ESR1-mutation rates in tissue underestimate true prevalence (PMID:30205045).
  • WES sensitivity (~5% CCF) and even higher-depth MSK-IMPACT (~1.3% CCF) cannot exclude rare pre-existing resistance subclones; mutations called “acquired” may simply have been below detection in the primary tumor (PMID:30205045).
  • Pan-wild-type endocrine-resistant tumors (the fourth taxonomy class) remain unexplained; the authors note non-coding transcription-factor mutations and broader profiling as plausible future avenues (PMID:30205045).
  • The SERM PFS analysis showed no significant difference among the resistance subgroups (Figure S4B); whether SERMs are differentially affected by these mechanisms is not resolved (PMID:30205045).
  • Differential PFS impact of transcription-factor alterations on AI vs SERD therapy is hypothesis-generating and requires mechanistic validation in models (PMID:30205045).
