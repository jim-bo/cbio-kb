TL;DR

Miao, Margolis, Vokes and colleagues pooled whole-exome sequencing from 249 microsatellite-stable tumors (melanoma, NSCLC, bladder, HNSCC, anal, sarcoma) drawn from seven previously-published immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) cohorts plus 78 newly-sequenced Dana-Farber samples, and uniformly re-analyzed them against clinical response by RECIST 1.1. Beyond confirming that tumor mutational burden (TMB) is associated with complete/partial response (CR/PR) vs progressive disease (PD), they identify clonal driver alterations in PIK3CA, KRAS, and PBRM1 enriched in responders, EGFR hotspots enriched in non-responders, biallelic PTEN loss occurring exclusively in PD, and 11q (PAK1/YAP1/CCND1) and 12q (MDM2/CDK4) amplifications enriched in PD. Dominant mutational signature (UV in melanoma, smoking in NSCLC, APOBEC in bladder/HNSCC) is highly correlated with TMB and intratumoral heterogeneity, and in melanoma TMB ceases to predict response after adjusting for dominant signature. Power calculations indicate that detection of common (~10% prevalence) response-associated variants requires ~300 samples and rare variants thousands.

Cohort & data

  • N = 249 tumors with matched germline (from 314 starting samples after QC) across six cancer types:
    • SKCM melanoma — N=151
    • NSCLC non-small cell lung cancer — N=57
    • BLCA bladder cancer — N=27
    • HNSC head and neck squamous cell carcinoma — N=12
    • ANSC anal cancer — N=1
    • SARCNOS sarcoma — N=1
  • Treatment classes: anti-PD-1 (N=74), anti-PD-L1 (N=20), anti-CTLA-4 (N=145), combination anti-CTLA-4 + anti-PD-1/L1 (N=10), other combinations (N=7).
  • Assay: tumor/normal whole-exome-seq; average mean target coverage 150x tumor / 119x germline; mean tumor purity 58% (range 10%–97%).
  • Dataset: mixed_allen_2018. Raw BAMs at dbGaP phs000694.v3.p2, phs000980.v1.p1, phs001041.v1.p1, phs000452.v2.p1, phs001075.v1.p1, and new accession phs001565.v1.p1.
  • Computational pipeline: mutect (SNVs), strelka (indels), oncotator (annotation), absolute (clonality, purity, ploidy), polysolver (HLA typing), netmhcpan v3.0 (neoantigen prediction), non-negative matrix factorization for mutational-signatures deconvolution against COSMIC signatures.

Key findings

  • TMB as a single variable is weakly predictive. CR/PR patients had significantly higher TMB than PD across the pooled cohort (p<0.05, Mann-Whitney U), but the distributions overlap and ROC AUC = 0.66 for CR/PR vs PD (PMID:30150660, Supplementary Fig. 4).
  • Clonal TMB is stronger than total TMB. Mutational clonality inferred by absolute showed that clonal nonsynonymous burden predicts CR/PR vs PD across cancer types and response categorizations. Patients with >50% subclonal mutations (high intratumoral heterogeneity) were enriched for PD (p=0.0014, Fisher’s exact).
  • Driver gene enrichment. Clonal driver alterations in PIK3CA, KRAS, and PBRM1 were enriched in CR/PR; clonal driver mutations in EGFR were enriched in PD (p<0.05 for all; FDR q=0.18 for KRAS and PIK3CA). KRAS and PIK3CA remained associated with CR/PR after correcting for TMB (p<0.05, logistic regression), though without surviving FDR correction.
  • PIK3CA cancer-type context dependence. Patients with clonal hotspot PIK3CA and CR/PR were melanoma, HNSCC, anal, or bladder; most PIK3CA-mutant PD patients were lung. KRAS hotspots associated with CR/PR across multiple cancer types.
  • Interferon-γ pathway CNAs. Copy number alterations expected to disrupt interferon-γ signaling (deletions of interferon genes or amplifications of SOCS1, SOCS3, PIAS1, PIAS4) were enriched in PD vs CR/PR (19/123 vs 3/70; p=0.019, Fisher’s exact).
  • Focal CNAs in PD. Amplifications of PAK1, YAP1, CCND1 on 11q, and MDM2, CDK4 on 12q were seen predominantly in PD patients.
  • Biallelic PTEN loss is intrinsic-resistance-only. Homozygous deletion of PTEN occurred exclusively in PD (N=4, p=0.30); truncating clonal biallelic loss was also seen in resistant tumors from mixed-response anti-CTLA-4 patients.
  • PBRM1 / SWI/SNF in non-ccRCC settings. Truncating mutations in PBRM1 and other PBAF SWI/SNF members were over-represented in responders, with biallelic loss almost exclusive to CR/PR — generalizing prior ccRCC findings to melanoma, lung, bladder, and HNSCC.
  • Mutational signature dominates in melanoma. After stratifying by dominant signature (UV S7, alkylating S11, other S1/S5), TMB was no longer a significant predictor of CR/PR vs PD in melanoma (p>0.05 for all). The non-UV/non-alkylating melanoma subgroup (mucosal, uveal, acral) had a higher PD rate.
  • APOBEC in bladder and HNSCC. APOBEC signatures S2/S13 correlated with higher TMB (p=0.002 bladder, p=0.03 HNSCC, Mann-Whitney U) and CR/PR (p=0.019), and may confound the observed PIK3CA–response association.
  • Smoking signature in NSCLC. High smoking-signature NSCLC tumors had low intratumoral heterogeneity, high TMB, and frequent KRAS. EGFR-hotspot tumors had subclonal-skewed mutations (p=0.035), low TMB, were enriched in never-smokers (p=0.00017, two-tailed Fisher), and had poor ICB response. TMB remained a significant predictor of response after controlling for smoking history.
  • Neoantigens are highly collinear with TMB. On average each nonsynonymous mutation yielded 2.24 predicted neoantigens (R²=0.99, p<0.00001). 871 of the predicted neoantigens arose from driver mutations; 8 of these driver-derived neoantigens recurred in CR/PR but not PD in an HLA-dependent manner, and were clonal in all carrier samples.
  • Power analysis. With Bonferroni correction over 116 driver genes and assumed 40% CR/PR rate, detection of a common (~10% prevalence) responder-specific variant requires sample sizes ~300; detection of rare (~1%) variants requires thousands.

Genes & alterations

  • PIK3CA — clonal hotspot mutations enriched in CR/PR (FDR q=0.18); response association is cancer-type dependent and may be confounded with APOBEC signatures.
  • KRAS — clonal hotspot mutations enriched in CR/PR across cancer types; remains associated after TMB correction (p<0.05, logistic regression) but does not survive FDR.
  • PBRM1 — truncating / biallelic loss-of-function enriched in CR/PR, generalizing prior ccRCC findings to melanoma, lung, bladder, HNSCC. PBAF SWI/SNF family alterations show the same pattern.
  • ARID2 — PBAF SWI/SNF member; loss implicated alongside PBRM1 in responder enrichment.
  • EGFR — clonal hotspot mutations enriched in PD; tumors are subclonal-skewed, low-TMB, and over-represented in never-smokers.
  • PTEN — homozygous deletion occurred only in PD patients (N=4); biallelic truncating loss seen in mixed-response anti-CTLA-4 cases.
  • MDM2 / CDK4 — 12q focal amplifications enriched in PD.
  • PAK1 / YAP1 / CCND1 — 11q focal amplifications enriched in PD.
  • Interferon-γ pathway — combined deletions of interferon genes or amplifications of SOCS1, SOCS3, PIAS1, PIAS4 enriched in PD (p=0.019).
  • B2M, JAK1, JAK2, STAT1 — antigen-presentation and JAK/STAT pathway loss-of-function events were rare in this pre-treatment cohort, possibly reflecting intrinsic- vs acquired-resistance differences.

Clinical implications

  • TMB alone is an imprecise pre-treatment biomarker (AUC=0.66 in this cohort) — integrating clonality, dominant mutational signature, and specific driver/CNA events yields better stratification.
  • For SKCM melanoma, the dominant mutational signature is a stronger correlate of response than TMB; TMB may be a proxy for UV-driven biological processes that both raise immunogenicity and accumulate mutations.
  • For NSCLC, KRAS-mutant smoker-signature tumors are a favorable subgroup for ICB; EGFR-hotspot never-smoker tumors are unfavorable — consistent with prior reports.
  • For BLCA bladder and HNSC head and neck SCC, APOBEC-signature dominance is associated with both high TMB and CR/PR.
  • Biallelic PTEN loss flags intrinsic resistance to ICB; loss of intact interferon-γ signaling via CNA also flags poor response and may be a candidate exclusion criterion.
  • Loss of PBRM1 / PBAF SWI/SNF components predicts response across multiple histologies, not only ccRCC.
  • Drug-class context: cohort spans ipilimumab, tremelimumab, nivolumab, pembrolizumab, atezolizumab; anti-CTLA-4 use was concentrated in melanoma while anti-PD-1/L1 dominated other histologies, limiting drug-class-by-tumor-type inference.

Limitations & open questions

  • Pooled retrospective cohort with heterogeneous original sources and FFPE-derived sequencing in the Dana-Farber additions; sample size still under-powered for most single-gene tests after FDR correction (e.g. PIK3CA/KRAS at q=0.18).
  • Anti-CTLA-4 vs anti-PD-1/L1 effects are confounded with cancer type (anti-CTLA-4 predominantly melanoma).
  • Computational neoantigen prediction is highly collinear with TMB (R²=0.99); in-silico methods cannot disentangle the two with this cohort.
  • Loss-of-function alterations in JAK/STAT and antigen-presentation machinery (B2M, JAK1, JAK2, STAT1) were rare pre-treatment; intrinsic vs acquired-resistance mechanisms may differ.
  • Patients with stable disease (SD) by RECIST 1.1 are handled separately from the main CR/PR-vs-PD comparison; alternative response definitions stratifying SD by PFS/OS give different classifications.
  • Mixed-response patients (tumor shrinkage at some sites, progression at others) are excluded from main analyses but were enriched for PTEN truncating events at progressing sites.
  • Authors call out that ccRCC, the original setting for PBRM1 loss as a response biomarker, is not represented in this cohort; the generalization to other histologies needs further validation.
  • Power calculations indicate that detection of rare (~1%) response-associated variants is infeasible without sample sizes in the thousands, motivating hypothesis-driven validation from experimental work.
