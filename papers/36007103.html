TL;DR

This prospective proof-of-principle study from Memorial Sloan Kettering tested whether high-sensitivity, UMI-based cell-free DNA (cfDNA) sequencing can capture the mutational repertoire of primary endometrial cancer (EC) and enable longitudinal disease monitoring. In 44 newly diagnosed EC patients with serial pre- and post-surgical plasma collections over 24 months, primary tumors were profiled by MSK-IMPACT (468 genes) and matched cfDNA by MSK-ACCESS (129-gene high-depth, molecular-barcoded assay). Baseline cfDNA mutations were detected in 22% (8/36) of patients — almost all with advanced stage or aggressive histology — and 35/38 (92%) of primary-tumor mutations covered by MSK-ACCESS were detected in baseline plasma. Changes in ctDNA fraction during follow-up tracked treatment response and disease progression, with a lead time over imaging in two cases. Detectable ctDNA at baseline (p<0.001, HR 11.14) and immediately post-surgery (p=0.014, HR 15.56) were significantly associated with reduced progression-free survival.

Cohort & data

  • N = 44 prospectively accrued, biopsy-confirmed newly diagnosed endometrial cancer (UCEC) patients consented at MSK between 09/2017 and 07/2018 (PMID:36007103).
  • Stage distribution: FIGO stage I in 34/44 (77%); advanced stage III/IV in 10/44 (23%).
  • Histology: endometrioid (UEC) 24/44 (55%); serous carcinoma (USC) 7/44 (16%); carcinosarcoma (UCS) 6/44 (14%); remaining cases included high-grade carcinomas of no specific type and an adenosarcoma (CD37).
  • Tissue NGS: 42 primary ECs profiled with MSK-IMPACT 468-gene panel (median tumor depth 618x, range 101–1030x; normal 504x). Six stage III/IV cases also had microdissected extra-uterine sites (sentinel lymph nodes, peritoneal implants, fallopian tube) sequenced.
  • cfDNA NGS: 36 patients with adequate plasma input (10–20 ng cfDNA) were sequenced on MSK-ACCESS, a 129-gene UMI-based liquid-biopsy assay (median raw coverage 25,611x; median duplex coverage 1,340x post-collapsing). Variant calling was tumor-informed (“genotyping”) via Waltz, requiring ≥2 duplex consensus reads.
  • Longitudinal: Blood drawn at baseline (pre-op), post-op (median 22 days, range 9–57), and every 6 months to 24 months. 229/258 (89%) intended draws were collected despite COVID-19 disruption. Longitudinal serial samples from 25 patients were sequenced.
  • Dataset: Targeted sequencing data deposited at cBioPortal as ucec_ccr_cfdna_msk_2022 (“Endometrial Carcinoma cfDNA (MSK, Clin Cancer Res 2022)”).
  • Follow-up: Median 33 months (range 0.3–44); 11 patients had recurrent or persistent disease.

Key findings

  • cfDNA concentration correlates with stage. Median pre-operative cfDNA concentration was 2.71 ng/mL (range 0.66–20.62). Patients with stage III/IV disease had significantly higher cfDNA than stage I patients (Wilcoxon rank sum, p=0.028). cfDNA was numerically higher in carcinosarcoma (UCS) and high-grade carcinomas of no specific type but did not reach significance (p>0.1) (PMID:36007103).
  • Primary-tumor mutational landscape recapitulates TCGA. Median 7.5 non-synonymous mutations per primary tumor (range 1–92). Most frequently mutated genes: TP53 52%, PTEN 50%, PIK3CA 50%, ARID1A 36%, FBXW7 29%. TCGA molecular subtype distribution: 10/42 (24%) MSI, 12/42 (29%) copy-number low, 20/42 (48%) copy-number high.
  • Baseline ctDNA detection enriched in advanced/high-risk disease. Tumor-derived mutations were detected in baseline cfDNA in 8/36 (22%) patients sequenced; 6/8 had stage III/IV disease, and the remaining two were a stage IA high-grade adenosarcoma and a stage IB high-grade mixed endometrioid/serous carcinoma with isolated tumor cells and lymphovascular space invasion. No low-grade stage I endometrioid (UEC) patients had detectable baseline ctDNA.
  • High concordance between primary tumor and baseline cfDNA. Of 38 somatic mutations in primary tumors covered by MSK-ACCESS, 35 (92%) were detected at baseline and 38 (100%) at some follow-up timepoint. All mutations clonal in the primary tumor and within the panel were detected in pre-operative cfDNA, with the exception of patient CD28 (subclonal TP53 detected, but clonal CDKN2A E61Pfs*54 and TERT C228T promoter mutation only detected at later timepoints).
  • Baseline ctDNA fractions varied. Most ctDNA-positive cases had baseline ctDNA fraction 0.7–2.0%; outliers were CD22 (stage IVB serous EC, 5.8%) and CD23 (stage IVB carcinosarcoma with 2,608 cm³ tumor burden, 74.6%). Both patients died of disease 30–31 months post-baseline.
  • Tumor–lymph node heterogeneity. In stage III/IV cases with sequenced extra-uterine sites, mutations could be private to the primary or to specific sentinel lymph nodes. Notable: in CD20, ARID1A truncating, PIK3CA and KRAS hotspot mutations subclonal in the primary became clonal in sentinel lymph nodes; a clonal PTEN mutation plus RNF43, TSC1, SMARCA4 frameshifts were shared with only one of two SLNs; AGO2 and FOXA1 missense mutations were SLN-restricted. cfDNA at baseline captured the primary repertoire more faithfully than the lymph node repertoire.
  • Serial cfDNA mirrors clinical course. In 6/25 patients with serial sampling, changes in MAFs and ctDNA fraction tracked imaging-confirmed response or progression. Examples: CD20 (stage IIIC2 dedifferentiated EC) — MAFs rose with progression at 8–9 months, fell during salvage chemotherapy, rose again at 18–24 months with retroperitoneal adenopathy; CD26 (stage IIIC1 carcinosarcoma) — ctDNA cleared on therapy then rose with bony/nodal recurrence; CD22 (stage IVB serous) — ARID1A/PIK3CA/TP53/PPP2R1A MAFs fell post-treatment then rose with lung and perirectal metastases.
  • Lead time over imaging in selected cases. In CD28 and CD36, rising ctDNA preceded imaging-detected recurrence by 6 months. In CD22 and CD28, ctDNA MAF rises preceded elevated serum CA125 by 5.8 and 17 months, respectively, suggesting ctDNA may be more sensitive than CA125 for surveillance in EC.
  • Baseline ctDNA is prognostic. Detectable baseline ctDNA was significantly associated with worse progression-free survival (PFS): p<0.001, HR 11.14 (95% CI 2.72–45.59). 7/8 patients with baseline ctDNA recurred within follow-up (the exception was CD37 adenosarcoma).
  • Post-surgical ctDNA is prognostic. Landmark analysis (landmark = 51 days post-surgery) showed detectable post-surgery ctDNA was associated with disease progression: p=0.014, HR 15.56 (95% CI 2.16–112.16). All 4 patients with detectable post-surgery ctDNA had recurrent or persistent disease.
  • Uterine sarcoma feasibility. The CD37 adenosarcoma harbored 12 somatic mutations including pathogenic DICER1, FGFR2, PTEN, and ARID1A mutations; the 3 covered by MSK-ACCESS were detected in baseline cfDNA, suggesting liquid biopsy may extend to uterine sarcomas.

Genes & alterations

  • TP53 — mutated in 52% of primary ECs; the most common driver in this cohort. Subclonal TP53 detected in CD28 baseline cfDNA; clonal TP53 tracked in CD20, CD22, CD26, CD28 longitudinal series.
  • PTEN — mutated in 50%. Clonal PTEN shared between primary EC and one of two SLNs in CD20 (heterogeneity); pathogenic PTEN detected in CD37 adenosarcoma cfDNA.
  • PIK3CA — mutated in 50%. Hotspot PIK3CA subclonal in CD20 primary became clonal in sentinel lymph nodes; serial monitoring of PIK3CA MAFs tracked progression in CD22, CD26, CD28.
  • ARID1A — mutated in 36%; truncating ARID1A mutations recurrent in cfDNA-positive cases (CD20, CD22, CD37).
  • FBXW7 — mutated in 29%. Tracked in CD36 longitudinal series (stage IIIA grade 1 endometrioid EC).
  • KRAS — hotspot mutations subclonal in CD20 primary, clonal in SLN.
  • CTNNB1 — listed among genes interrogated in hotspot cfDNA EC literature reviewed by the authors.
  • PPP2R1A — shared mutation in CD22 (primary, mesenteric implant, three lymph nodes, cfDNA).
  • CDKN2A — E61Pfs*54 clonal in CD28 primary; not detected at baseline but detected at 6-month follow-up.
  • TERT — C228T promoter mutation clonal in CD28 primary; detected later in cfDNA, not at baseline.
  • PIK3R1, APC, ERBB3 — among the 5 primary-tumor mutations in CD36 (stage IIIA grade 1 endometrioid), all detected in baseline cfDNA.
  • RNF43, TSC1, SMARCA4 — frameshift mutations co-occurring with PTEN in CD20, present in primary + one of two SLNs.
  • AGO2, FOXA1 — missense mutations restricted to sentinel lymph nodes in CD20 (private to nodal compartment).
  • DICER1, FGFR2, NTRK3 — pathogenic mutations in CD37 adenosarcoma (DICER1, FGFR2) and CD26 carcinosarcoma (NTRK3).

Clinical implications

  • Prognostic biomarker at diagnosis. A single pre-operative blood draw analyzed by MSK-ACCESS yields a strong prognostic signal in newly diagnosed EC (HR 11.14 for PFS), supporting baseline ctDNA as a candidate risk stratifier beyond stage and histology — particularly in high-risk histologies (USC, UCS).
  • MRD detection post-surgery. Detectable ctDNA within ~51 days post-surgery is associated with very poor PFS (HR 15.56), identifying patients who may benefit from intensified adjuvant therapy. Conversely, ctDNA-negative low-grade stage I endometrioid (UEC) patients had no detectable cfDNA signal, consistent with their favorable prognosis and potentially supporting de-escalation studies.
  • Treatment response monitoring. Serial ctDNA tracking on adjuvant or salvage paclitaxel/carboplatin and radiation showed real-time correlation with imaging response and progression, with ctDNA rises preceding clinically detected recurrence by up to 6 months and elevations in serum CA125 by 5.8–17 months.
  • Targetable alteration capture. Because EC has a high number of recurrently mutated cancer genes (TP53, PIK3CA, PTEN, ARID1A, FBXW7), gene-panel-based tumor-informed cfDNA assays are well suited for longitudinal monitoring without requiring personalized tumor-bespoke panels.
  • Sarcoma extension. Detection of DICER1, FGFR2, PTEN, and ARID1A mutations in cfDNA of the CD37 adenosarcoma motivates larger cfDNA studies in uterine sarcomas.

Limitations & open questions

  • Small sample size. N=44 with stratification across stage and histology yields very small subgroup numbers (some histology categories have only 1–2 patients), and PFS analyses are based on limited events; multivariate analysis was not feasible.
  • Non-consecutive enrollment. Cases were selected to represent histologic and FIGO stage diversity, not a consecutive series, so prevalence estimates of baseline ctDNA positivity may not generalize to an unselected diagnostic cohort.
  • Limited sensitivity in low-stage, low-grade disease. MSK-ACCESS at 0.1% (tumor-informed) / 0.5% (de novo) detection threshold could not detect ctDNA in any low-grade stage I endometrioid (UEC) patient. Whether more sensitive personalized MRD assays would identify clinically meaningful ctDNA in this subgroup remains open.
  • Panel coverage gaps. MSK-ACCESS covers a 129-gene subset of MSK-IMPACT; the 38 primary-tumor mutations interrogated represent only the panel-overlapping subset, so true tumor-to-plasma concordance for the full mutational repertoire is not measured.
  • COVID-19 disruption. 11% of intended follow-up draws were missed, particularly at the 18- and 24-month timepoints.
  • CA125 comparison is exploratory. Only two patients had serial CA125 measurements; broader comparison of ctDNA vs. CA125 sensitivity in EC requires prospective studies.
  • Therapeutic decision impact unproven. The study demonstrates prognostic and monitoring value but does not test whether ctDNA-guided treatment intensification improves outcomes — larger interventional trials are warranted.
  • Heterogeneity between primary and lymph node sites suggests baseline cfDNA may miss mutations that emerge in nodal metastases, complicating any future use of cfDNA as a tumor-genotyping surrogate for therapy selection.
