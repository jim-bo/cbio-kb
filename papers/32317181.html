Pan-cancer Analysis of CDK12 Alterations Identifies a Subset of Prostate Cancers with Distinct Genomic and Clinical Characteristics

Authors

Jose Mauricio Mota

Subhiksha Nandakumar

Konrad H. Stopsack

Emily Weg

PMID: 32317181 · DOI: 10.1016/j.eururo.2020.03.024 · Journal: European Urology (2020)

TL;DR

Nguyen et al. performed a retrospective single-institution pan-cancer analysis of 26,743 MSK-IMPACT-sequenced tumors across 25 solid tumor types to characterize CDK12 alterations. CDK12 alterations were rare overall (1.5%) but most frequent in prostate (5.3%) and ovarian (4.2%) cancers, where they were predominantly truncating, clonal, and biallelic. CDK12-biallelic (CDK12-Bi) prostate cancers exhibited a fragmented copy-number profile suggestive of a tandem duplicator phenotype, distinct oncogenic pathway involvement (cell-cycle pathway enrichment; depletion of TP53 and TMPRSS2-ERG fusions), and were associated with more aggressive clinicopathologic features and shorter overall survival (median 64.4 vs 74.9 mo; aHR 1.80, 95% CI 1.12–2.89; p = 0.024) independent of standard prognostic factors and tumor copy-number burden.

Cohort & data

  • Total cohort: 26,743 patients across 25 solid tumor types sequenced by MSK-IMPACT between July 2014 and April 12, 2019. Excludes 1,965 cases with MSI sensor score >10 or TMB ≥20 mut/Mb to limit passenger CDK12 alterations.
  • Sequencing panels: IMPACT341, IMPACT410, and IMPACT468 — all included CDK12.
  • Prostate cancer subcohort: 1,875 prostate tumors profiled; 100 with CDK12 alterations. Clinical outcomes evaluable for 1,465 prostate cancer patients (100 CDK12-altered, 1,365 CDK12-WT).
  • Ovarian cancer subcohort: 1,034 patients; 43 with CDK12 alterations.
  • Dataset on cBioPortal: prad_cdk12_mskcc_2020.
  • Key analytic tools: FACETS for allele-specific copy number / zygosity / clonality; MutSigCV v1.4 for significantly mutated genes; OncoKB (Aug 28, 2019) for oncogenic annotation.

Key findings

  • Pan-cancer prevalence. CDK12 alterations identified in 404/26,743 patients (1.5%) overall. Highest in prostate (100/1,875; 5.3%) and ovarian (43/1,034; 4.2%) cancers. MutSigCV showed CDK12 significantly recurrently mutated only in prostate (FDR < 0.001) and ovarian (FDR = 0.056) cancer.
  • Mutation type localization. In prostate cancer, truncating mutations dominated (101/105; 96% within or upstream of the kinase domain), while missense mutations clustered within the kinase domain (25/30; 83%; p < 0.001 vs expected by chance). 18/24 (75%) prostate patients with kinase-domain missense mutations had a concurrent truncating mutation, suggestive of biallelic inactivation.
  • Biallelic inactivation. Putative CDK12-biallelic inactivation: 61/84 (73%) prostate, 26/28 (93%) ovarian. In prostate, the dominant biallelic mechanism was multiple concurrent CDK12 mutations (49/61; 75%); in ovarian, it was LOH (20/26; 77%).
  • Clonality. CDK12 mutations were more frequently clonal than other mutations in both prostate (105/116; 91% vs 140/196; 71%; p < 0.001) and ovarian cancer (26/30; 87% vs 82/130; 63%; p = 0.016).
  • Temporal stability. Of 14 prostate patients with longitudinal tumor sequencing, 13 showed shared CDK12 alterations across matched tumors (one outlier had a CDK12 rearrangement prone to hybrid-capture false negatives), with one patient followed up to 7.8 yr. CDK12-Bi frequency did not differ between primary (34/48; 70%) and metastatic (27/36; 75%) tumors — suggesting CDK12 alteration is an early event.
  • Genomic architecture. TMB and overall FGA were similar across CDK12-WT/Mono/Bi groups, but CDK12-Bi prostate cancer had a higher fraction of genome gain, a higher number of breakpoints, and smaller median copy-number-altered segments — consistent with a tandem duplicator phenotype. 13% of the evaluated genome was differentially altered between CDK12-WT and CDK12-Bi.
  • Differential oncogenic alterations (CDK12-Bi vs CDK12-WT; FDR < 0.05). More frequent in CDK12-Bi: CCND1, MCL1, MYC amplifications; FANCA loss; FOXA1 mutations. Less frequent in CDK12-Bi: TP53 mutations and TMPRSS2–ERG fusions. 11q13.3 amplification (which spans CCND1, FGF19, FGF4, FGF3) was enriched in CDK12-Bi tumors.
  • Pathway-level alterations. Cell-cycle pathway was more frequently altered in CDK12-Bi vs CDK12-WT; p53, PI-3-Kinase/Akt, and β-catenin/Wnt pathway alterations were less frequent in CDK12-Bi.
  • Clinical features. CDK12-altered prostate cancer patients had higher rates of de novo metastatic disease (40% vs 26%), higher median PSA at diagnosis (14.8 vs 9.0 ng/mL), and higher Gleason ≥8 (80% vs 57%). Patients with CDK12-Bi had the worst baseline characteristics.
  • Overall survival. From metastatic diagnosis, CDK12-altered patients had shorter median OS (64.4 vs 74.9 mo); aHR 1.80 (95% CI 1.12–2.89; p = 0.024) after adjusting for age, Gleason ≥8, PSA, visceral metastasis, de novo metastatic status, and FGA.
  • Time to castration resistance. CDK12-altered patients reached castration resistance faster (median 10.8 vs 13.1 mo; aHR 1.49, 95% CI 1.09–2.03; p = 0.017; 522 total events).
  • Time on first-line CRPC therapy. No significant difference between CDK12-altered and CDK12-WT patients on first-line abiraterone or enzalutamide: median 9.7 vs 8.7 mo (aHR 1.08, 95% CI 0.57–1.51; p = 0.8).

Genes & alterations

  • CDK12 — primary focus. Truncating and kinase-domain missense mutations, rearrangements, and deep deletions. Biallelic inactivation (CDK12-Bi) defined a clinically and genomically distinct prostate cancer subtype.
  • CCND1 — amplified more frequently in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer (11q13.3 locus).
  • MCL1 — amplified more frequently in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer.
  • MYC — amplified more frequently in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer.
  • FANCA — lost more frequently in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer.
  • FOXA1 — mutated more frequently in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer.
  • TP53 — mutated less frequently in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer.
  • TMPRSS2–ERG fusions — observed less frequently in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer.
  • FGF19, FGF4, FGF3 — within 11q13.3 region of recurrent copy-number gain in CDK12-Bi prostate cancer.

Clinical implications

  • Prognostic biomarker. CDK12 alterations define a distinct subset of metastatic prostate cancer with somewhat more aggressive disease at diagnosis, shorter OS, and shorter time to castration resistance, even after adjustment for standard prognostic factors (FGA, Gleason, PSA, age, visceral/de novo metastatic status).
  • Treatment response. Time on first-line abiraterone / enzalutamide in castration-resistant disease was not significantly different between CDK12-altered and CDK12-WT patients (~9 mo).
  • Therapeutic hypothesis. Authors note prior literature suggesting CDK12-altered prostate cancers may be enriched for fusion neoantigens and respond to immune checkpoint blockade; this hypothesis is being tested in a multi-institutional phase 2 trial combining nivolumab with ipilimumab (NCT03570619).
  • No DDR co-occurrence. No significant co-occurrence between CDK12 alterations and DNA-repair gene alterations was detected, consistent with limited PARP inhibitor efficacy in CDK12-altered mCRPC reported by prior trials (TRITON2, TOPARP-B).

Limitations & open questions

  • Retrospective single-institution design with potential selection bias from a tertiary referral center.
  • Targeted-panel assay. MSK-IMPACT can miss genomic rearrangements; one patient’s discordant longitudinal CDK12 status was attributed to a rearrangement event prone to hybrid-capture false negatives. Tandem duplications are difficult to characterize directly on targeted panel sequencing.
  • Homozygous CDK12 deletion could not be definitively demonstrated via copy-number alteration alone; CDK12 deep deletions were nonetheless included in the CDK12-altered group.
  • Mechanism of impact on disease behavior independent of broad copy-number burden (FGA) remains unresolved — possibly via specific pathway alterations or impaired intronic polyadenylation affecting HR gene expression.
  • Therapeutic vulnerability of CDK12-altered prostate cancer to immune checkpoint blockade remains untested in this dataset and is the subject of ongoing clinical trials.
